# (updated) Sign language is another language version of the website
> 

> **Update from 2024-07-07:** [Kateřina Pešková from Deaf Friendly](https://www.deaffriendly.cz/nas-tym) wrote a great [comment under the newsletter #26](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/26/comment/60909936). I have supplemented the article with her quotes.

Deaf people communicate with each other using sign language. It has its own vocabulary and grammatical rules, which are different from Czech. Therefore, deaf people perceive written Czech as a foreign language.

During simultaneous interpretation from Czech into sign language, the interpreter often retains Czech grammatical structures at the expense of sign language grammar. The interpretation is more literal and less understandable. Unlike spoken language, interpretation leaves less room for facial expressions, making the interpreted speech flat. As with any interpretation, a secondary joke, pun, or reference to cultural context is occasionally lost.

> **Kateřina Pešková:** *“\[…\] when making websites accessible, we would rather talk about translation, which is a very different process, and in translation, something ‘slipping through’ should not happen at all, because in translation the translator has time to prepare, and their work is checked both linguistically and content-wise by revisers \[…\]”*

![Example of sign writing for SignWriting.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/f6a986a3-2b56-44aa-a2a5-5bd4ccf7387e_686x899.png "686x899")

The characters for SignWriting are part of the [Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sutton_SignWriting_(Unicode_block)) standard.

On websites, we primarily use written communication. Deaf people also have difficulties with this. There is no common written form for sign language. There are ways to write sign language using Czech words, using the [Hamburg notation system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamburg_Notation_System), or as [SignWriting](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Znakopis), but think of sign language more as a sign script – our Latin alphabet based on phonemes is not very compatible with the sign language of the deaf.

Not all deaf people know SignWriting, so video remains the most suitable way to communicate with deaf people.

> **Kateřina Pešková:** *“SignWriting has only been used by a few enthusiasts or sign language researchers in the Czech Republic, and most Czech deaf people have not even heard of it.”*

Generative AI and video avatars bring hope that deaf people might soon have tools for quickly translating information from Czech and other languages into sign language. For now, however, this is music of the future.

Signing in video significantly limits deaf people's access to information. Imagine that you could not use Google for searching because all information would only be in video. In that video, no one would speak aloud, so there would be no transcripts. You could not search in transcripts or captions. Video is linear, so forget about structured headings, scanning content, and jumping to keywords in text, forget about hypertext links in the text.

The most suitable way to make a website accessible to deaf people seems to be to create a distinct language version of the pages with video in sign language supplemented by Czech subtitles, so that the videos can be full-text searched.

> **Kateřina Pešková:** *“It is true that videos are searched differently than written text, and this must be taken into account, for example by having headings in videos, short content descriptions (ideally in easy to read / accessible Czech), and a fast playback function. The necessity of subtitling to make videos full-text searchable does not make sense if deaf people poorly understand Czech texts.”*

If you are preparing a website or application for the European Accessibility Act, look for more information on the project pages of [Deaf Friendly](https://www.deaffriendly.cz/blog/jak-ma-vypadat-webova-stranka-pristupna-neslysicim), [Ruce](http://ruce.cz/znakopis/o-znakopise), or the excellent [Stéphanie Walter](https://stephaniewalter.design/blog/accessibility-resources-tools-articles-books-for-designer/).
