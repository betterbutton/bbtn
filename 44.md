# Linkodrome

**The job market for UX designers is in a paradoxical situation.** On one hand, a record number of UX designers with experience in designing applications and information systems are looking for work, on the other hand, agencies are struggling to hire web designers with experience in designing large-scale websites. When designing websites, designers spend more time with spreadsheets than in Figma, and they are more likely to generate diagrams from data than to just connect rectangles on a canvas in Mir. A quarter of a century ago, [Jesse James Garrett](https://jessejamesgarrett.com/) visualized [the difference between designing software and hypertext systems](http://jjg.net/elements/pdf/elements.pdf). Web designers work with [content inventories](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/content-audits/), for page templates they must assume that in a modern [CMS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Content_management_system) site administrators will want to assemble layouts from components themselves, so the design is a much more living organism than with applications and is also influenced by people without design knowledge. Specific object-oriented UX frameworks like [ORCA](https://www.ooux.com/resources/introducing-orca-the-third-diamond-in-your-ux-process) are applied in the design.

At the same time, content websites are often already being built in their fourth or fifth version, so the design is not created on a green field. Websites are often merged from several sub-sites due to company and brand consolidation. It must be considered that the CMS is not a monolith, but besides content management, there are other systems for e-commerce, email marketing, and websites connect via APIs to various other systems. On top of that, the revolution of AI tools for web design, like [Relume.io](https://www.relume.io/), is entering the picture.

The state of the job market in UX design is an opportunity for application designers to retrain for web design. But who should they learn from?

**Can you understand accessibility without contact with disabled users?** Next year, the [European accessibility law](https://www.zakonyprolidi.cz/cs/2023-424) will take effect. On LinkedIn, web consultants are starting to put [certification acronyms](https://www.accessibilityassociation.org/s/certified-professional) after their names, [e-books on accessibility](https://silktide.com/installed-silktide-toolbar/) are sprouting like mushrooms after rain, and we could wallpaper with [checklists](https://www.figma.com/community/file/1233184772650050315/accessibility-checklist-for-designers). However, what we all often lack is real experience with disabled users. It's like loading up on best practices instead of user research and doing heuristics instead of user testing.

Soon, the [INSPO conference](https://www.inspo.cz/) will take place, where experts on accessibility and assistive technologies have been meeting with users with specific needs since 2001. It's a suitable place where you can first get acquainted with the wide range of limitations users struggle with, but also with the tools that make it easier for them to work with the internet and information systems. [Tickets are already on sale](https://goout.net/cs/listky/konference-inspo-2024/rqgbb/) and will cost a few hundred crowns.

Soon you will also find that accessibility, like SEO, is not a project that ends with the website launch. Once you start with it, it will need to be included in further activities.

**Don't look at offers only by price, but by total value**

When evaluating offers, focus on overall economic advantage, not just price. The main goal of business is not cost reduction at any cost, but primarily profit maximization and added value.

Look for a supplier who has already proven the ability to deliver quality results – check their past projects and see if they were successful and completed on time for the agreed price. It is also important who will be involved in your order and what experience they have with similar projects.

From a well-prepared offer, you should learn something new, because quality suppliers already inject part of their know-how into the offer phase.
