# Why Does No One Read Your Design Documentation?
> 

Designers face the issue that their carefully prepared documentation is read by no one. And it doesn't matter whether "no one" means fellow designers, coders, or QA testers. Simply no one.

> "The best sign of documentation's value is when it serves as a starting point for constructive discussions among team members."\
> — **Slava Shestopalov**

According to designer Slava Shestopalov, [the problem stems from several reasons](https://medium.com/design-bridges/design-documentation-2-b03e270c2d5b): The most common cause is documentation created without the participation of other team members or texts that are too long and complex. For documentation to be truly useful, it must be part of the daily operations of the product team, it must support discussions, and allow contributions from others.

It is also important to distribute documentation continuously, place it in relevant locations, and constantly update it. Sometimes, however, it is better to skip documentation altogether and start talking to people from delivery. A handout is not a document, but a process.
