# Inclusive Sans – what makes a font accessible
> 

Typographer Olivia King deals with font accessibility. While the WCAG accessibility standard focuses primarily on text size and color contrasts, experiments show that [accessibility is already influenced by the shape of characters of the chosen font](https://medium.com/the-readability-group/a-guide-to-understanding-what-makes-a-typeface-accessible-and-how-to-make-informed-decisions-9e5c0b9040a0).

![Sample of Inclusive Sans font on Google Fonts](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/97d49441-1da8-49c9-8246-c3edce1131ab_1746x867.png "1456x723")

Olivia King decided to create an accessible font based on academic articles and the experiences of users with specific needs. This is how the Inclusive Sans font was created – a linear sans-serif static font (neo-grotesque) designed for accessibility and text legibility. Olivia King was inspired by Sophie Beier's book: [Reading Letters: Designing for Legibility](https://sofiebeier.dk/?page_id=390).

The font has clear distinction between I, l and 1, Characters d, b and q, p are not mirror images of each other, the difference between O and 0 is easily noticeable, wider and more open shapes for letters c, o, a and e, higher middle height (x-height) and wider default spacing between letters. Development took place in Sydney and includes support for indigenous languages.

The font in static and italic styles can be [obtained on GitHub](https://github.com/LivKing/Inclusive-Sans/tree/main/fonts) or [Google Fonts](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Inclusive+Sans/about).
