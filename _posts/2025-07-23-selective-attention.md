# Selective Attention

[Selective Attention](https://wikisofia.cz/wiki/Pozornost,_jej%C3%AD_druhy_a_vlastnosti) ([selective attention](https://www.simplypsychology.org/attention-models.html)) is the ability to focus on one task while simultaneously ignoring other stimuli. It is the same phenomenon that causes people watching a video with basketball passes to often completely miss a person in a gorilla costume walking through the scene. When we concentrate, we simply overlook many things.

This has a crucial impact in application and web design. Users do not perceive everything you display to them. They primarily focus on what will help them complete their current task. A button, call-to-action, or notification outside their field of view will easily go unnoticed – and it doesn't matter how prominent it is.

Selective attention has been studied in psychology since the 1950s. Current knowledge stems primarily from the 1963 work *[Attention: Some theoretical considerations](https://deutsch.ucsd.edu/pdf/Psych_Rev-1963_70_80-90.pdf)* by [J. Anthony Deutsch](https://psychology.ucsd.edu/people/profiles/jadeutsch-in-memoriam.html) and [Diane Deutsch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diana_Deutsch). The Deutsches hypothesized that the brain processes all sensory information and selection occurs later.

Designers and developers often overestimate how much users see. Just because something is on the page doesn't mean someone will notice it. Good interfaces account for this. They should be as simple as possible, clearly structured, and key information should be emphasized in multiple ways – not just visually, but also through sound or motion.

Testing is essential. Observe where people actually look while performing a task. Don't rely on rules or intuition – context decides.

Designing with selective attention in mind means having humility. We are not designing for ideal users who read every word. We are designing for busy people trying to solve something quickly. And we must clearly show them the way.
