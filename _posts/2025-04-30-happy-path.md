# Happy day scenario
> Happy path – an ideal scenario in digital product design where everything goes smoothly. It often contrasts with reality full of errors and unexpected user behavior.

Happy path scenario ([happy path](https://www.ramotion.com/blog/happy-path-in-ux-design/), [happy day scenario](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Happy_path)) means that everything works. The user does exactly what is expected of them. The system responds correctly. Nothing breaks. This is where most designs start. Designing the happy path helps determine the main flow, core features, and what should be the focus of attention.

A common mistake of beginning designers is quickly moving to handling edge cases, errors, and exceptions. Those will also occur and need to be addressed in the design. But first, design the system as if everything should work on the first try.

However, you must not end the design with the happy path. Real people are not perfect. They make mistakes. They click somewhere by accident. They have old phones. They forget passwords. If the system doesn't account for these, the user will get stuck. They won't know what to do next. And they'll leave. Good design is therefore not only based on how things should work, but also on [what can go wrong](https://www.pixelmatters.com/blog/designing-beyond-the-happy-path).

Sometimes people also talk about the best-case scenario. For example, when the product is used without problems, everything is understandable, and it even brings joy. Such a scenario is a nice goal. But it must not be the only plan. Because when something goes wrong – and it will go wrong – the person must have a way to get out of it.

The happy path is like a travel plan. But life also has detours. And if you don't have them mapped out, you risk people getting lost. A great product isn't one where nothing goes wrong. But one where it doesn't matter.
