# Skeleton screen
> Skeleton screens improve user-friendliness during content loading by displaying element outlines instead of blank screens or rotating loading indicators.

While waiting for content to load, the user experience can be significantly influenced by how this wait is visually processed. **[Skeleton screens](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/skeleton-screens/)** display outlines of elements that mimic the final page appearance, instead of showing blank screens or rotating loading indicators. This helps users better understand what to expect and gives them a sense that the page is responding faster.

As content loads, the skeleton screen shows placeholder elements for text, images, or buttons. Once data is available, the actual content appears gradually. This approach feels more natural than the sudden display of a full page.

One of the main advantages of skeleton screens is [better perceived speed](https://blog.logrocket.com/ux-design/past-present-skeleton-screen/). Users feel the page loads faster because they see its structure, even if it's not fully functional yet. This effect reduces the frustration that might arise from watching a traditional rotating indicator.

When designing skeleton screens, it's important to avoid several mistakes. Placeholders should match the actual content; otherwise, users might become confused. Also, overly complex animations should be avoided, as they could slow down the application's performance. Accessibility is also crucial to ensure the solution is usable for users with limited vision.

Compared to traditional spinners, skeleton screens offer clearer visual feedback and keep users engaged. When implemented correctly, they can significantly enhance the user experience and reduce the feeling of waiting.

---

**Academic Note:** The 2018 study *[The effect of skeleton screens:](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3232078.3232086) Users’ perception of speed and ease of navigation* [available on ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/326858669_The_effect_of_skeleton_screens_Users'_perception_of_speed_and_ease_of_navigation) concluded that the subjective speeding up of website loading is not statistically provable, even though users claimed to perceive the website as faster and easier to navigate.
