## From The Designer

![On the left (incorrect), graduated sizes and font cuts do not distinguish heading hierarchy. On the right (correct), a typographic scale subtly grades heading levels.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/7e19e7e3-4a8a-47ff-a06a-8e556e91c0d4_2160x1080.png "1456x728")

**Set up a typographic scale and font size hierarchy.** Prepare several size levels (headings, subheadings, body text) and use them consistently.\
\
❌ Headings and paragraphs in the same size without distinction.\
✅ H1, H2, H3, and body text with a clear size difference.\
\
👉 Readers quickly understand the importance of the content.\
👉 Visual hierarchy makes orientation easier.\
\
A typographic scale brings order to your design.

## Jakub Goldmann: New Lion – No Cause for Jubilation

I'm really glad we're finally moving towards a **unified state visual identity**. The era when any minister would have their office logo whipped up by a buddy in tragically poor quality was taking us somewhere between improvised post-Bolshevik punk and bureaucratic hobby circles. So: **good job that it's happening at all!**

But I'm grossly dissatisfied with the new **Czech lion** (the graphic mark).

### What struck me in the eye – and it hurt

1.  **The crown that levitates\**
    It looks like it doesn't sit on the head. What used to clearly form a mane now rather evokes an unfinished "Kč" symbol somewhere between a crown and typography.

2.  **An eye without a uniform stroke\**
    Geometrically straight at the top, organically, asymmetrically round at the bottom. A stylistic contradiction that creates a strange, unharmonious impression.

3.  **Mouth & tongue like from a Kofola can\**
    Violent stylization, very strong negative space – one starts searching with their eyes for another motif in the inverse space.

4.  **Front paw\**
    It looks chopped off. The shape of the claws and transitions to the body look rushed, as if without craft certainty; generally, I feel the negative outlines are too strong, as if the dismembered lion, according to a fairy tale, is waiting for dead water to grow back together.

5.  **Anatomy in the crotch area\**
    Either there shouldn't have been a genital detail at all, or it should have remained as pronounced as before. Now it looks indecisive – as if the tip is missing.

### And that red...

Sure, red is the national color. But at this saturation and without visual counterbalance, it feels aggressive. Compared to more modestly conceived state symbols (e.g., the British crown), it's a visual slap.

### I expect more from state symbolism

It's not just about the technical quality of curves. The state coat of arms must carry **strength, dignity, continuity, and timelessness**. It has to work on a facade, a coin, a stamp, a phone, and a billboard. It must survive generations of redesigns of everything around it – precisely because it should be stronger than trends.

I don't feel that certainty here.

### What do you say about the state administration's visual identity?

Agreement? Disagreement? Do you see qualities in the new lion that I missed? Let me know. I'm happy to be convinced by an argument, not by the studio's reputation. Discussion is welcome – professionally, calmly, sharply.

## Martin Kopta: Second Place is Equally Good

All attention is focused on [the winning design](https://www.najbrt.cz/aktuality/vizualni-identita-statni-spravy-ceske-republiky) of the Czech state administration's visual identity by Studio Najbrt – and rightly so. But I'd like to direct you [to the design that placed second](https://klarakvizova.graphics/projekt/jednotna-prezentace-statni-spravy-ceske-republiky/). Klára Kvízová and František Štorm were the authors.

In my opinion, it is equally good as Studio Najbrt's design. While the other designs placed the lion on a shield, unnecessarily creating a collision between the graphic mark and the small state coat of arms, Kvízová and Štorm, like their colleagues at Najbrt, understand that the graphic mark is not a heraldic device.

They handled the distinction between the hierarchy of central and lower state administration bodies very well. Where Studio Najbrt's design distinguishes subtly only with a blue stripe, Kvízová and Štorm introduce a much more pronounced differentiation of the lion in a circle for lower state administration. By using two shapes, their design can manage with just one multicolored version in its minimal variant, potentially reducing printing and sign-making budgets.

The lion strongly bears Štorm's handwriting. His historicizing drawing of the lion is much bolder than that of the other competitors. For the identity, they offered two variants of the Bohemica typeface – an antiqua and a linear one. Both in a well-blending dynamic variant that complements the cheerful graphic mark with the lion well.

Unlike the designs that placed lower, Kvízová and Štorm do not introduce another multicolored element like [Superior Type](https://x.com/PragerGroup/status/1946041289557873026/) with a golden crown on the lion. Their design contains no decorations, such as the potentially problematic footer with the state flag that [Dynamo](https://www.dynamodesign.cz/cs/novinky/navrh-sjednoceni-identity-statni-spravy-cr) came up with. Kvízová and Štorm, with their experience, know that not only seasoned professionals will always work with the identity. The fewer opportunities for error, the better.

If they're so good, why didn't they win? I don't know. I wasn't on the jury. I guess, however, that the jury preferred the geometric linear typeface and the simpler drawing of the lion with regard to better readability in small sizes and low resolution. Also, introducing only one typeface instead of a choice between a humanist antiqua and a linear typeface limits the possibility of blunders during unprofessional application.

Competitions like this are a great opportunity to get acquainted with designers' approaches to a brief. When you choose a visual identity for your company, event, or product, you'll know where to turn.

## Linkodrome

**How to make a website accessible to AI agents:** Accessibility is [a competitive advantage in the AI era](https://mareklecian.cz/pripravite-ai-agentum-bezbarierovy-vchod-a-nebo-je-poslete-ke-konkurenci/), writes Marek Lecián. AI agents decide for users and only understand well-structured websites. If your website uses HTML incorrectly, lacks image labels and video captions, it will simply disappear from search radar. Besides compliance with the law, accessibility thus improves SEO, conversions, and long-term brand growth.

![An XKCD comic panel titled "Machine Learning Captcha." **What if you could develop websites by conversing in the browser?** The [Stagewise](https://stagewise.io/) project offers a plugin for development environments like Cursor, VS Code, Windsurf, and others, allowing you to contextually prompt development agents directly over the generated website in the browser. A client can pass their comments like: *What if those buttons were yellow? Could it be done without rounded corners?* and similar directly to the AI.

**How learning UX advanced me professionally and personally.** Edijs Bogomolovs describes how teaching UX design transformed him into a better professional. Although teaching is time-consuming and ineffective from a scaling perspective, for him it represents a way to maintain mental fitness and [learn by teaching others](https://edijsbogomolovs.medium.com/how-ux-teaching-made-me-a-better-designer-thinker-and-an-ultralearner-4d3c586cb355). He understands learning as personal training that forces him to constantly simplify, update, and refine his thinking.
