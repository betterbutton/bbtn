# 1 MB of data represents up to 20 g of CO₂ emissions
> 

> **Updated 2024-09-01:** After readers' notifications, we checked the data presented in Chiara Santella's article with the book and other sources. Calculating emissions from data is complicated and different sources disagree on what to include. Similarly, to find out emissions for transferring gigabytes, you cannot simply multiply the emissions for transferring megabytes. Direct proportionality does not work here.
>
> Mike Berners-Lee in the book, for example, estimates emissions for an email that ends up in spam at 0.3 g CO₂, but for an email that someone opens and reads, it's already 4 g CO₂. [You can also find the book in Czech libraries](https://aleph.nkp.cz/F/?request=how+bad+are+bananas&func=find-b&find_code=WRD&local_base=SKC) or you can [buy a paper copy](https://www.heureka.cz/?h%5Bfraze%5D=how+bad+are+bananas) or [electronically](https://profilebooks.com/work/how-bad-are-bananas/).

Do you know the book [How bad are bananas](https://howbadarebananas.com/)? It was written by [Mike Berners-Lee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mike_Berners-Lee). Yes, he is the brother of [Sir Tim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tim_Berners-Lee). In that book, he tries to convert anything into CO₂ emissions. It was first published in 2010, but three years ago it was revised. So when we now tell you that every 1 MB of data transferred over the internet creates 20 grams of CO₂ emissions, then even knowing that renewable sources are increasingly contributing to electricity production, it will probably still be true.

> „Today we *recognize* the environmental impact of throwing a plastic bottle on the ground, but not of doing an 1 hour long video-call or sending a 7-minute voice note.“\
> **— Chiara Santella, [The environmental impact of our messaging habits](https://medium.com/user-experience-design-1/the-environmental-impact-of-our-messaging-habits-84b5a737e9b3#:~:text=And%20sadly%2C%20that%E2%80%99s%20the%20issue.)**

Sending one SMS represents 0.014 g CO₂, an average email causes emissions of 4 g CO₂, but with a large attachment it can easily be up to 50 g CO₂. Chiara Santella in the article [The environmental impact of our messaging habits](https://medium.com/user-experience-design-1/the-environmental-impact-of-our-messaging-habits-84b5a737e9b3#:~:text=And%20sadly%2C%20that%E2%80%99s%20the%20issue.) asks the question what we are causing to the environment with endless teleconferences that we got used to during the lockdowns. But you know what? Probably better than driving to the office and business flights.

If it now occurred to you that you will help the planet by starting to grow houseplants at home, you will probably need a lot of them. An average houseplant consumes 5—10 g CO₂ per day. So two emails and we're done. A grown tree can consume up to 110 grams of CO₂ per day, so we should all get a nice piece of forest.
