# Jakob Nielsen's Usability Heuristics
> Nielsen's heuristics are rules that help design interfaces that don't frustrate users – and often prevent costly mistakes before they happen.

In 1994, he formulated [ten basic rules for designing user interfaces](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/191666.191729). They have stood the test of time surprisingly well. They are not dogmas, but guidelines – and when you look at them while designing a website or application, you often start to see things that would otherwise remain hidden until someone breaks a finger over them.

> [Heuristics](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heuristika) (from Greek *heuriskó*, *εύρίσκω* – to find, to discover) are proven rules based on informed guesswork and experience that help solve problems when we don't know exact algorithms or methods.

**Visibility of system status**

The user should always know what's happening. Is the page loading? Is the submission in progress? Is the system waiting for confirmation? A well-placed spinner or a "saved" message does more than the fanciest animation.

**Match between system and real world**

Words, icons, and structure should correspond to how people think. If you want a cart, don't call it an "object repository." Speak the users' language, not the developers'.

**User control and freedom**

People make mistakes. The interface must allow them to revert from errors. A "back" button, confirmation before destructive actions, the ability to cancel or undo – all of these reinforce a sense of security.

**Consistency and standards**

What works in one place must work elsewhere. If you have a "save" button in the bottom right, it shouldn't be in the top left somewhere else. Use established conventions – not out of laziness, but for orientation.

**Error prevention**

The best error message is the one the user never sees. Forms should check inputs before they are submitted. Contextual help reduces the risk of errors even before they occur.

**Recognition rather than recall**

The interface should not rely on users remembering things. It's better to offer choices than to expect them to remember an exact name or path.

**Flexibility and efficiency of use**

Experienced users appreciate shortcuts. Keyboard shortcuts, pre-filled forms, or quick actions can significantly speed up work – without confusing beginners.

**Aesthetic and minimalist design**

Less is often more. Remove clutter. Every element on the page should have a reason to be there. The more ballast, the harder it is to find what's essential.

**Help users recognize, diagnose, and recover from errors**

An error message must be understandable. Ideally, it says what happened, why it happened, and what the user can do about it. "Error 143X" is useless; "Connection failed. Check your internet connection" is helpful.

**Help and documentation**

Yes, the interface should be intuitive. But sometimes it's simply not enough. In such cases, help should be available quickly, easily searchable, and practical.

[Heuristic Evaluation of User Interface](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/97243.97281) (Nielsen & Molich, 1990)

> Nielsen's ten heuristics were preceded by [nine heuristics](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/77481.77486). Nielsen published these four years earlier together with his Danish colleague [Rolf Molich](https://www.dialogdesign.dk/about-rolf-molich/).

[Nielsen's heuristics](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/97243.97281) are not a checklist to simply tick off. They are a way of thinking. They help you advocate for users when they need it most – before they even reach for your product. And that's where their strength lies.
