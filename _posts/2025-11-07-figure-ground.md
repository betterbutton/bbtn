# Figure-Ground Principle
> A perceptual rule that determines what steps forward and what fades into the background.

The figure-ground principle ([law of figure‑ground](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/law-of-figure)) describes how people distinguish between what is the main subject in a visual field and what forms the mere background. In design, this ability determines visual hierarchy and guides user attention. Strong contrast, clear depth, and distinct edges help people understand where to focus and where to act.

![Figure-ground illusion: Are we looking at columns or at silhouettes of people?](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/0c120429-020e-44fd-b6ff-21634041b5f1_1200x750.jpeg "1200x750")

[People trapped in the wall](https://www.flickr.com/photos/shashachu/443215138) or columns? (photo: [Sha Sha Chu](https://www.flickr.com/photos/shashachu/443215138), CC BY-NC-SA 2.0)

When the separation between figure and ground is weak, confusion arises. Text gets lost in an image, buttons blend into the background, or a modal window appears without dimming the surroundings. Such an interface feels noisy, and users grope, hesitate, or click on the wrong spots.

The term's origin dates back to [Gestalt psychology](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/gestalt). The most famous example is [Rubin's Vase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubin_vase), where the shape alternately transforms into a vase or two face profiles depending on what we perceive as the foreground. Designers use the same mechanism to guide attention in digital interfaces. They are aided by cues such as *contrast*, *size*, *borders*, *symmetry*, and *depth*.

In practice, the figure-ground principle manifests in many familiar situations: a darkened background behind a modal window, a gradient under text in a [hero image](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/hero-section), elevated cards on a dashboard page, or a prominent primary button next to subdued secondary ones. Even an active form field functions as the figure while others recede into the background.

Well-designed interfaces continuously maintain this relationship. When hierarchy is clear, interaction feels natural and effortless. When it breaks down, users lose confidence. Understanding the figure-ground principle means learning to direct attention—the most valuable resource of any user experience.
