## Linkodrome

**Outcome-focused metrics – measuring the real benefit of UX.** Traditional metrics like time on page or NPS cannot capture whether people's lives have truly improved. Jared Spool presents a [framework for outcome-focused UX metrics](https://articles.centercentre.com/what-are-outcome-driven-ux-metrics/) that measure the specific impact of UX work on users and the organization. It's based on three types: *Success Metric* (goal completion and *party moment*), *Progress Metric* (tracking sub-steps toward the goal), and *Problem Cost Metric* (the monetary cost of poor UX).

**Product design as a stack of knowledge-based decisions,** inspired by Jesse James Garrett's twenty-year-old diagram [Elements of user experience](http://www.jjg.net/elements/pdf/elements.pdf). It's not a linear process but interconnected layers: from user research through conceptual models to aesthetic details. Every change in a lower layer affects higher ones, and neglect leads to UX debt. The key is to clearly separate the problem space and solution space and leverage team members' specializations. Jamie Mill points out that the conceptual model [Elements of product design](https://jamiemill.com/blog/elements-of-product-design/) is often underestimated and recommends consciously designing it, for example using [OOUX methods](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/ooux).

**Marty Cagan: AI does not mean the end of custom software or SaaS.** User programming was once limited to spreadsheets and low-code tools. AI opens the possibility to program in natural language. Yet SaaS solutions won't just disappear. Their value lies in thousands of embedded rules and logic that aren't easily replicated. [Marty's vision of the future](https://www.svpg.com/article-build-vs-buy-in-the-age-of-ai/) therefore doesn't lie in replacement but in combination. Companies will buy robust components while building their own workflows with AI's help. The new [MCP protocol](https://www.marigold.cz/ai/mcp/) is important, as it allows software to be understood by machines.

**Meeting a website's accessibility checklist isn't enough.** [Míša Vranková in the Na hraně kódu podcast](https://www.vas-hosting.cz/blog-o-pristupnosti-webu-s-michaelou-vrankovou) explains that an accessible website doesn't mean just a site for the visually or hearing impaired, but for everyone – for example a mother with a child in her arms or a senior. Accessibility is a gradual process, not a one-time action – it starts with keyboard testing, continues with adjusting contrasts or descriptions, and requires the cooperation of everyone who creates the website.

**Peter Merholz: Weak leadership destroys design teams.** Weak leaders pretend to have control even when they don't, thereby losing trust and harming the people around them. Peter calls for confidence, self-reflection, and working on oneself, because [leadership is not a reward but a responsibility](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/dont-weak-ass-design-leader-peter-merholz-p7qoc/).

**Craig Abbott criticizes the use of the term** ***best practice*** **in accessibility audits.** According to him, it creates the impression that these are generally valid recommendations. But according to Craig, [best practice is a subjective term](https://www.craigabbott.co.uk/blog/best-practice-is-just-your-opinion/). It changes with technological development and can come across as arrogant. Craig emphasizes that problems must be clearly named and action triggered.

Don't limit maximum password length.

**Don't limit password length.** Stéphanie Walter lamented on LinkedIn that [PayPal limits password length to 20 characters](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7364235005687910400/). According to current [recommendations from the US National Institute of Standards and Technology](https://pages.nist.gov/800-63-4/sp800-63b.html#appA), we should leave maximum password length up to users: *"Since the size of a hashed password does not depend on its length, there is no reason to prohibit the use of long passwords (or passphrases) if the user so desires."* Instead of complex rules, you can [motivate users to choose a stronger password](https://www.w3.org/WAI/tutorials/forms/examples/password/) with appropriate validation.

## Marissa Mayer wanted a simple and fast web

![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/fa5e7b65-1b41-4809-bae2-15f1293d558e_1536x1024.webp "606x404")

[Marissa Mayer](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/marissa-mayer) insisted on speed, simplicity, and global availability of services

[Marissa Mayer](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/marissa-mayer) (*1975) was the first female engineer at Google and later CEO of Yahoo. She shaped the web as we know it today. She was behind the simple Google homepage, Gmail, Maps, and News. She championed three principles: speed, simplicity, and global availability.

At Google, Marissa mentored a generation of product leaders who now run successful companies. As Yahoo's CEO, she faced criticism and controversies but left her mark on the shift toward mobile design. Her legacy lies in user-friendliness, which we now take for granted.

## Toolbox

[DNS.Surf](https://DNS.Surf) is an online service that shows how DNS servers around the world return records for your domain. Unlike a standard resolver, it monitors responses from various locations and networks, so you can easily find out if DNS changes have already propagated, if georouting is working, or if users are reaching the correct server. For developers, CDN administrators, e-shops, or multilingual websites, it's a valuable tool that saves time and uncertainty. It provides a view of the network from the perspective of real visitors – whether they're in Tokyo, Paris, or Brno.

## So we understand each other

[Assistive technologies](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/assistive-technology) like screen readers, captions, or voice control open the digital world to people with various disabilities. However, to function properly, developers must ensure correct code structure, logical navigation, and readable interfaces.

Proper [color contrast](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/colour-contrast) helps not only people with visual impairments or color blindness, but also anyone using a mobile phone in sunlight or with eye fatigue.

A [content priority guide](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/content-priority-guide) is a simple list that determines what information should be on a page and in what order. Unlike wireframes, it doesn't focus on spatial layout but on user needs. Thanks to its austere form, it helps avoid unnecessary changes in later design phases and keeps development focused on essential things.
