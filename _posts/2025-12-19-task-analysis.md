# Task Analysis
> A method that describes what users actually do to achieve a goal and helps design digital systems based on real work, not on features.

Task Analysis ([task analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Task_analysis)) focuses on how people achieve their goals in practice, not just on what the screens look like. It helps reveal where digital products make users' work easier and where they unnecessarily complicate it. Designers who skip task analysis often improve interfaces but leave the actual work slow, fragile, or error-prone.

A task is a meaningful piece of work with a clear goal, such as publishing an article, paying an invoice, or regaining account access. It involves a sequence of steps, decision-making, memory load, and error handling. If teams confuse tasks with interface steps, they design better buttons but don't solve the real problem.

Task analysis originates in ergonomics and human factors, where it was used in designing tools, training, and safety-critical systems. UX adopted it because digital products today mediate complex cognitive and organizational work. Forms, administrative interfaces, or editorial systems are always part of a broader context that includes interruptions, decisions, and returns to unfinished work.

In practice, task analysis combines observation, interviews, and artifact study. Designers select an important task, observe how people perform it in a real environment, and create a model of its structure. These models are validated with users and serve as a basis for simplifying steps, reducing cognitive load, and meaningful usability testing.
