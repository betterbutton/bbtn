# Principle of Common Region
> A Gestalt principle stating that people perceive elements as related if they are enclosed within the same visual area – it aids design comprehension.

The principle of common region ([law of common region](https://lawsofux.com/law-of-common-region/)) is one of the fundamental principles of Gestalt psychology that we use when designing user interfaces. It states that elements placed within one visually enclosed area—such as a frame, colored field, or panel—are perceived as a whole, even if they are different in size or color.

Unlike the principle of proximity, which works with the distance between elements, the principle of common region uses visual containers. For example, when you place several related form fields inside a single frame, the user immediately understands that they belong together. This makes it easier to navigate the page and speeds up decision-making.

This principle helps designers organize information in a clear and structured way. The user can then focus on the task they want to accomplish on the page, instead of trying to figure out what belongs to what.

However, if the law is applied incorrectly—for instance, by putting unrelated elements into one area or, conversely, by splitting a logical whole into multiple areas—it can lead to confusion. The user then loses confidence in the interface's functionality.

The law of common region works best in combination with other principles, such as similarity or alignment. When used thoughtfully, it creates an interface that is clear, user-friendly, and easy to use.
