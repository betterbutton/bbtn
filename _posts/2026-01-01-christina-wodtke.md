# Christina Wodtke and Designing Systems People Can Live With
> A designer, author, and educator who understands human-centered design as designing systems so that people can understand the world, act in it, and thrive in it over the long term.

![Christina Wodtke (illustration generated from a photo)](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/38674dbc-b929-44f4-b9e4-7fe93e55a9e9_795x533.webp "602x403")

“Software has a point of view. Even people in Silicon Valley often think software is neutral, and there is no software that’s neutral. Period.” — [Christina Wodtke](https://understandinggroup.com/architecting-information/christina-wodtke-february-9#:~:text=And%20I%27ve%20always%20thought%20that%20software%20has%20a%20point%20of%20view.%20Even%20people%20in%20Silicon%20Valley%20often%20think%20software%20is%20neutral%2C%20and%20there%20is%20no%20software%20that%27s%20neutral.%20Period.)

*[Christina R. Wodtke](https://cwodtke.com/)* (October 22, 1966) is one of the figures who expanded the concept of human-centered design. She did not stop at interface usability but systematically showed that design shapes behavior, decision-making, and culture. Her main contribution lies in the consistent emphasis on responsibility for how systems influence human action over time.

She entered the awareness of the professional community thanks to [a book on information architecture](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/224130.Information_Architecture). She understood structuring information, naming things, and ensuring findability as an act of empathy. Good structure reduces cognitive load and respects the limits of human attention. It is not a technical detail but a human-sensitive intervention into the environment in which people orient themselves.

She built on this foundation with the concept of social architecture. According to her, rules, incentives, and feedback function like an interface. They influence behavior just as strongly as buttons or navigation. From this perspective, ethics is an inseparable part of design because every system carries values and consequences, whether its creators are aware of them or not.

She also transferred these ideas into the field of organizational management. In her work with goals and key results, she emphasized that the purpose is not performance control but focusing attention and strengthening team autonomy. Without trust and psychological safety, even well-intentioned tools turn into alienating mechanisms.

Her work on building communities and institutions also complements Christina's legacy. She co-founded the *[Information Architecture Institute](http://archive.iainstitute.org/)*, was involved in the creation of the magazine *[Boxes and Arrows](https://web.archive.org/web/20250807002314/https://boxesandarrows.com/)*, and founded the initiative *[Women Talk Design](https://womentalkdesign.com/)*. In all these cases, she designed systems that give people a voice and create fairer conditions. Her work thus shows that human-centered design is long-term care for the environment in which people work, make decisions, and belong to each other.
