[10 Usability Heuristics](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/heuristic-evaluation)

- **Visibility of system status\**
  Provide the user with continuous information about the system's state and what is happening in it. Is the document saved? When was it last saved? Are we online? Is our microphone muted? Can everyone see the screen we're sharing?

- **Match between system and real world\**
  Design the system to work in accordance with how users imagine it works. Does the user understand the language the system uses?

- **User control and freedom\**
  Give the user control over the system's behavior and reduce the impact of poor decisions. Does the browser's back button work? Can the user open a link in a new tab?

- **Consistency and standards\**
  Use icons, words, interactive components, and color palettes uniformly across the entire system so the user can more easily predict how the system will respond to their inputs. Don't forget that according to Nielsen's Law, "*users statistically spend more time on other websites than on yours, and therefore it's not worthwhile for them to learn the differences in controlling your website from the others*."

- **Error prevention\**
  Design the system's functioning to minimize the negative impact of its operation and user decisions. Does the user confirm a destructive action in a way that prevents them from accidentally dismissing it? Can the last step be undone? And can it be undone with a time delay?

- **Recognition rather than recall\**
  Offer the user actions; don't rely on the user knowing and entering them from memory. This is a weakness of the command line, icons without labels, gesture controls, search fields, and AI prompts and chats.

- **Flexibility and efficiency of use\**
  Allow experienced users to use productive shortcuts and contextually guide new users. Or as John Postel said: "*Be conservative in what you do, and liberal in what you accept from others*."

- **Aesthetic and minimalist design\**
  Don't overwhelm users with complex interfaces; display only relevant information for current tasks. It's certainly good that your complex system can do a lot, but an excess of options can confuse users.

- **Help users recognize, diagnose, and recover from errors\**
  Provide understandable feedback regarding errors and their solutions. Is it an error or a feature? What error occurred and what does it mean? Is reloading the page enough, or is it a deeper problem?

- **Help and documentation**

  Provide the user with meaningful, concise help and allow them to delve deeper when learning the system.

[Heuristic Evaluation of User Interface](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/97243.97281) (Nielsen & Molich, 1990)

Nielsen's ten heuristics were preceded by [nine heuristics](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/77481.77486). Nielsen published these four years earlier together with his Danish colleague [Rolf Molich](https://www.dialogdesign.dk/about-rolf-molich/). So actually, we're celebrating the thirty-fourth anniversary already.

Although the heuristics are thirty years old, they are by no means outdated. They are still a good starting point and foundation for auditing websites and applications from a user-friendliness perspective. Compared to other methods, such as user testing, their advantage is that they don't require any complex preparation or access to users. # Tip From Our Book

**The output of one step is the input for the next.** When planning work, start from the end: What is needed to run the website? You will achieve a more efficient process than if you start planning from the beginning and think about what you can do with the output. You will focus only on those outputs that you need as inputs for subsequent activities that you have already solved.

Dead ends have no place in the process. Cut them off. Designers sometimes create outputs out of habit and inertia, which in your case may not move the project forward. There's a big difference between the necessary amount of outputs for a small website and a website for a corporation where every step needs to be verified with a broad group of decision-making managers.
