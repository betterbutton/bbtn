# Cognitive Blind Spot
> Bias blind spot is a cognitive bias that causes us to overlook our own prejudices even when we see them in others.

We believe we are rational. That our decisions are based on logic, experience, and data. But every designer, developer, or product manager carries a filter in their head—a set of assumptions, simplifications, and cultural patterns that influence what they design. And worse: we are often completely unaware of these biases.

This is called the cognitive [blind spot](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kognitivn%C3%AD_slep%C3%A1_skvrna) ([bias blind spot](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/article/the-bias-blind-spot-and-unconscious-bias-in-design). It is a cognitive bias where we overlook our own biases, even though we easily recognize those of others. This phenomenon was described in the study *[The Bias Blind Spot: Perceptions of Bias in Self Versus Others](https://scispace.com/papers/the-bias-blind-spot-perceptions-of-bias-in-self-versus-58bxfnwqll)*, published by [Emily Pronin](https://psychology.princeton.edu/people/emily-pronin), [Daniel Y. Lin](https://profiles.stanford.edu/194200), and [Lee Ross](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lee_Ross) in 2002. They showed that people systematically underestimate the influence of bias on their own thinking while readily attributing it to others.

In practice, this means we subconsciously design for ourselves—for people who have similar education, devices, language, or abilities as we do. All while believing we are designing for everyone.

Designer's blindness has real impacts. For example, when designers subconsciously assume all users have good vision—and choose a contrast that prevents older people from reading the text. Or when they expect everyone to know the structure of a national identification number because they grew up in the Czech Republic. Or when they use the word "Cancel" in a different meaning than ordinary users perceive it—and don't notice because it makes sense to them. It is precisely these automatic assumptions, which we fail to recognize, that manifest the blind spot of bias.

Cognitive biases in design cannot be completely eliminated. But they can be systematically uncovered. Diversity in the team, testing with real users, and openness to criticism help. Above all, however, humility helps: admitting that our perspective is just one of many. And that what we consider intuitive is often just familiar to us—not universally understandable.
