# It Depends
> It's a rejection of simple answers to complex questions, and that's precisely why this saying is so popular. It reminds us that design answers always depend on users, tasks, context, and constraints.

> *You are opening the 23rd window of BBtn's Advent Calendar for 2025. In this year's advent series, we'll look at popular UX designer proverbs. Today's is: It depends.*

*“It depends”* (it depends) is one of the most common and simultaneously most misunderstood phrases in user experience design. For some, it sounds evasive or uncertain. In reality, however, it is often the most accurate answer to a poorly phrased question.

In UX, almost nothing can be evaluated in isolation. Whether a layout is better, a feature is useful, or a design approach is suitable depends on who the users are, what they aim to achieve, and under what conditions they use the system. Without this context, even the most well-known recommendations and best practices lose their meaning.

It's not about professional caution or personal opinion. User-friendliness is always defined in relation to specific users, specific goals, and a specific context of use. As soon as you omit one of these components, the question ceases to be meaningful. Asking whether something works in general is like asking whether a tool is good without saying what it's supposed to be used for.

This saying has endured because it captures the reality of digital products. Websites and applications exist in an environment full of business goals, technical constraints, legal obligations, accessibility requirements, and human diversity. These are not simple problems with one correct solution.

The problem arises when *“it depends”* is given as a final answer. Used correctly, it should be the beginning of an explanation. It depends on what and why. Naming these dependencies turns ambiguity into a common basis for decision-making.

For website operators and their managers, this phrase has value precisely in that. It doesn't absolve responsibility; it forces decisions to be based on reality, not on empty slogans.
