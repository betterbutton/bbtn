# Which Icons (Don't) Need a Label
> 

When we worked in Seznam's design team, a discussion arose about choosing the right icon for sharing. [Back then, icons varied](https://www.fastcompany.com/3031872/why-isnt-there-a-standard-share-button) from app to app and were often tied to the last redesign. With the shift to a unified visual style, which Jakub's current partner at the agency, David Švejda, was nurturing at the time, we needed to make decisions about which icon was correct. For mobile apps, it was easy: just follow the platform's style. But what about the web? Should the icon on the mobile web change according to the platform's style?

![Overview of share icons for iOS 7 and above, iOS 6, Google Android up to 2011 and from 2011, Windows from version 8 and Windows Phone 7, and finally the open standard](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/ed0734e4-94c0-40fb-b877-19ef3f7e22dd_2384x796.avif "1456x486")

Share icons

In the image, you can see an overview of the icons we considered back then. Missing from it is today's popular swallowtail **⮚** icon, which you'll find on Instagram, for example, or the bent arrow pointing up ⮫, which represents sharing on Facebook and YouTube.

The share icon is still a relatively simple nut to crack. You always have the option to adapt to the platform. But what about other icons? Martin conducted a survey among Seznam users at the time, where they had to assign meanings to the icons.

Surprisingly, not even the users gave designers clear guidance. Often, it depends on subtle differences in context—for example, whether they consider the star icon ⭐ to be a bookmark, a like, or a rating. It's similar with other icons. To make matters worse, even minor stylistic variations of icons can sway interpretations. For instance, the magnifying glass 🔍 icon clearly means search. But when mathematical symbols like plus ➕ and minus ➖ appear nearby, it suddenly means zoom. And returning to the share icon, people associated with different platforms also responded differently here. There are always more Android or Windows users than people with iOS, macOS, and the circus of Linux distributions.

Moreover, preferences change over time, which is nicely illustrated by the different results of similar surveys from [2014](https://elie.net/blog/web/choose-the-right-sharing-icon-to-boost-user-engagement), [2017](https://app.lyssna.com/tests/29259e21a90b/results/12a0760e535a?%7B%22viewState%22%3A%7B%22isExpandedBySectionScreenshotId%22%3A%7B%22206181%22%3Atrue%7D%7D%7D=), and [2018](https://medium.com/@berryverylucky/shortudy-why-use-different-share-icon-in-ios-and-aos-a273be04aacc#:~:text=results%20of%20the%20user%20tests).

Filip Greš, creator of the popular icon set [GriddyIcons](https://www.griddyicons.com/), [divides icons into three categories](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7219671310862352384/): universal, ambiguous, and unique.

Among universal ones, he lists icons like the house, trash can, or magnifying glass, which users undoubtedly associate with returning to the home screen, deleting an item, and searching.

For ambiguous icons, the meaning changes across apps and contexts, and it would be advisable to add a label. Filip classifies icons like the heart ♥️, star ⭐, and thumbs-up 👍 as ambiguous—all can represent a like, but also, for example, saving to a wishlist or rating (boost).

Unique icons, on the other hand, are typically created for a specific action in a given app, and their meaning isn't transferable or is just settling in. Their shape also varies quite a bit across apps, so it's sometimes hard to recognize them even if you've encountered them somewhere before. Filip gives examples like green leaves 🍂, a puzzle piece 🧩, or a light bulb 💡. Users often have no chance to guess the meaning of such icons without a label.

The trouble is that sometimes in the interface, we don't have the opportunity to add a label to an icon without major changes to the layout's appearance.

Sometimes, even with universally recognizable icons, recognition is thrown off by a designer who opts for excessive stylization or simplification. A typical example of such an icon is the trash can from Filip's workshop.

![Trash can icon in Griddy Icons by Filip Greš](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/04486761-4c07-4b10-81d9-c047ada72a70_95x123.png "95x123")

Trash can in Griddy Icons

We recommend approaching all icons as if they were unique. We simply cannot rely 100% on an icon's meaning being recognized. If you must use an icon without a label, [test its comprehensibility](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/icon-testing/) in the appropriate context and observe differing reactions from users on different platforms, cultures, or with varying lengths and scopes of experience.
