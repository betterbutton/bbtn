# Point-and-click
> The fundamental method of computer control that made technology accessible to millions through simplicity and direct manipulation.

The [point-and-click](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_and_click interface, namely pointing and clicking, fundamentally changed how people use computers. Instead of typing commands on a keyboard, it was enough to point the cursor at an element on the screen and click. This simple principle brought technology to the general public.

It was popularized on a mass scale by the [Apple Macintosh](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macintosh_128K in 1984, but the concept first appeared back in the 1970s at the research center [Xerox PARC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PARC_(company) on the [Alto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xerox_Alto computer. It was there that the graphical user interface—abbreviated GUI—was created, replacing text commands with visual elements like icons, buttons, windows, or menus. This ushered in the era of intuitive control.

The click interface also introduced or solidified other key principles of user interface design: for example, affordances (hints about functionality), system feedback in response to user actions, constraints that lead to error-free operation, and the logical organization of elements. These principles help users understand what they can do and what is currently happening. In the book [Design of Everyday Things](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/840.The_Design_of_Everyday_Things), the author [Don Norman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Don_Norman) describes them as the gulf of execution and the gulf of evaluation.

Although today touchscreens prevail and voice control is being promoted, the point-and-click principle remains important. We still find it in websites, office software, and game design. It teaches us that every click is a decision—and that an interface should be designed to make that decision easy, understandable, and without unnecessary effort.
