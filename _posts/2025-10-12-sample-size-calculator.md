# Sample size calculator calculates the required number of respondents for quantitative research
> A practical guide on how to find out how many responses you need for accurate and reliable data.

![Screenshot of the sample size calculator](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/d36c0435-3258-4b27-aaf7-eb74d83ef3e1_1893x1073.png "1893x1073")

The calculator will determine the required sample size

The [Sample size calculator](https://www.surveymonkey.com/mp/sample-size-calculator/) helps determine how many people should fill out a questionnaire for it to accurately reflect the target group. Three key values are used in the calculation: population size, margin of error, and confidence interval.

*Population size* represents the total number of people you want to examine. *Margin of error* indicates by how much the survey results can differ from the actual values in the population. The *confidence interval*, typically 80–99%, expresses how likely it is that your sample truly reflects reality. The smaller the margin of error or the higher the confidence interval you choose, the more responses you will need.

For example, for a city survey among 500,000 inhabitants with a 95% confidence interval and a 5% margin of error, you need approximately 384 responses. Conversely, for a smaller company survey with 300 employees at 80% confidence and a 10% margin of error, 37 responses are sufficient.

The type of survey also has an impact. Medical studies usually require larger, statistically significant samples to provide accurate results. Product or user surveys can work with smaller samples because they focus more on qualitative rather than purely numerical insights.

Before you start collecting responses, define your goal, consider the population size, and set the required accuracy. A larger sample increases reliability but also costs more time and money. Modern tools, such as the [sample size calculator from SurveyMonkey](https://www.surveymonkey.com/mp/sample-size-calculator/), will make your decision easier and help you find a balance between accuracy and efficiency.
