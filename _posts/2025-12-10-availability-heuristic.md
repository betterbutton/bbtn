# Availability heuristic
> A mental shortcut that causes people to judge things as more common or important simply because they can recall them easily.

The [availability heuristic](https://wikisofia.cz/wiki/Heuristika_dostupnosti) ([availability heuristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Availability_heuristic)) is a [cognitive bias](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/cognitive-bias) that explains why designers, users, and teams treat vivid memories as evidence. We tend to believe that what comes to mind easily is also common, important, or true. This principle, [named](https://www.academia.edu/8285377/Availability_A_heuristic_for_judging_frequency_and_probability_1_2) by *Amos Tversky* and *Daniel Kahneman*, helps us in uncertainty, but in digital environments it often [distorts our view of reality](https://houdekpetr.cz/!data/public_html/papers/economics_psychology/Tversky-Kahneman-1974.pdf).

It takes just one unpleasant experience—a frozen payment, a crashed form, or unclear instructions—and the user feels that *"this website is unreliable."* Memory exaggerates what was intense and recent, not what was common. Teams do the same: a last angry tweet or one dramatic video from user testing outweighs months of stable data. Decisions are then guided not by facts, but by what stuck in the head.

In the background is so-called cognitive fluency—we process what is familiar or vividly presented more easily, and therefore believe it more. Familiar icons, predictable navigation, and clear relationships in the interface inspire trust. Conversely, striking reviews or emotional stories shape opinions disproportionately to their weight.

How to get out of this? Make memory easier. Document decisions, show long-term trends, and connect stories with data. Visualize common and reliable moments just as much as the striking ones. You can't turn off availability, but you can direct it. When we design for what users actually experience most often—not for what they remember most—the web becomes fairer and more resilient.
