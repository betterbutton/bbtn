## From The Designer

![Example of bad and good solution for link text: Variant A: Do you want to subscribe to this newsletter? Click here. Variant B: Do you want to subscribe to this newsletter? Subscribe to the newsletter.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/80839b1b-e054-47fa-baff-ced6d6acc522_2160x1080.png "568x284")

Does the link text make sense even without context?

**Today's design tip for better websites** (and higher conversions) is: Don't write "click here" on links. Treat them like small headings – like meaningful messages that must make sense even without context.

The label "Download the guide in PDF" is always better than "Download the guide *here*".\
People will more easily understand where the link leads. It's more accessible for screen readers and search engines. And most importantly: it will increase your link conversion rate.

💡A small change, a big impact. Try it.

## Linkodrome

**Grid instead of Flex: simpler, cleaner, more universal.** Alex Riviere calls for a fundamental change in approach to CSS layouts: instead of the habitual use of flexbox, he recommends reaching for grid first, then block, and only last for flex. Flex often complicates structure and requires redundant JavaScript and complex styling, while [grid brings a cleaner, more predictable, and more maintainable solution](https://alex.party/posts/2025-03-23-grid-first-flex-third/). Using the example of an input field with icons, he shows how simple it can be to replace a complex flexbox layout with a single grid declaration. Alex also criticizes the common use of flex where a simple block-level layout would amply suffice.

**Design tokens are changing the world of design systems.** Jan Six, co-creator of [Token Studio](https://tokens.studio/), [in an interview for UI Breakfast](https://uibreakfast.com/292-using-tokens-design-system-with-jan-six/) describes how the plugin emerged from the need to connect design with code. It has evolved into a comprehensive platform for design system managers. Jan talks about the benefits of consistent visual decision-making, automation, and the importance of living documentation. He also explains basic terms like design system, components, tokens, and the challenges associated with adopting tokens in companies. The main advice is: start simple and grow gradually.

**The same shadows across platforms.** Shadows in web and mobile applications often don't look the same even when they have identical parameters. Marc Edwards compared in detail [the rendering of drop shadow effects in CSS, Android, iOS, Figma, and Sketch](https://bjango.com/articles/matchingdropshadows/) and found precise conversion ratios for blur values. He discovered that the only difference lies in the interpretation of blur radius and that unifying it across platforms is possible. For example, iOS needs half the blur radius value compared to CSS for the same appearance. The results have direct application when generating code for different platforms.
