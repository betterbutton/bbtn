## On extending lines in graphs

Semrush published a [study on the impact of AI on search engine traffic](https://www.semrush.com/blog/ai-search-seo-traffic-study/). It contains a magical graph estimating the development of traffic source value. It expects that in four years, the value of traffic from AI will be four times higher than the value of traffic from search engines today. Similar graphs are tearing the bag.

![The graph shows a rising curve of value for all AI traffic sources and a decline in value of traffic from search engines.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/ca7b92e3-2fba-4e92-9d9b-efde743473b8_1600x1195.png "584x435")

Estimated value of LLM vs. traditional search ([Semrush](https://www.semrush.com/blog/ai-search-seo-traffic-study/))

It reminds me of Jakob Nielsen's 2017 article about a UX outlook for a hundred years. He included a now-legendary graph of the development in the number of UX specialists for the years 1950–2050. The graph shows that from now, in a quarter of a century, there should be a hundred million UXers in the world. This is obviously nonsense, and the publication date suggests how seriously we should take the article.

![The development of the number of UX specialists shows growth from zero in 1950 to a hundred million in 2050.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/726097b6-7535-4261-973b-5f676b3356cc_1547x856.png "576x318")

UX specialists: From zero to 100,000,000 in a hundred years ([Jakob Nielsen](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/100-years-ux/))

With Nielsen's graph, it's clear it's an exaggeration because that number is simply absurd. You can't just extend a trend to project the future. Nielsen has a mathematically precise calculation and clear data for his graph. I assume Semrush's projection is similar. However, in both examples, it's good to show that every growth has a ceiling somewhere.

Where does the value of traffic from AI websites come from? Users bring it. In four years, there will probably be roughly the same number of users as there are now. It can be assumed that the volume of free financial resources people can spend on the internet will not increase extremely either.

For AI websites to bring in four times what search engine traffic brings today in four years, there would have to be a radical reshuffling of our clients' budgets. They would have to, for example, stop buying offline entirely, stop responding to newsletters, stop clicking on ads, abandon social networks, and all their money would have to start being spent as a result of interacting with AI.

How probable is that?

## Linkodrome

**How visions are created.** Jared M. Spool explains [how a truly strong vision emerges](https://articles.centercentre.com/the-diagram-that-shows-where-a-vision-comes-from/): not as an abstract idea from management, but as a concrete story about the problems we see today and a better future where they are solved. The key is to show the difference between what happens if we change nothing and what we can gain if we embrace change. A vision isn't about specific steps, but a shared mental model that inspires others to act despite risks and uncertainties.

**When GTM content harms website speed.** Michal Matuška warns that JavaScript processing in GTM, complex rules, the number of variables (especially custom ones in JavaScript), and an unclear structure can have a significant [impact on performance](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7357825668572741632/). Even without firing tags, you can lose dozens of milliseconds.

**Three roles of microcopy in UX: Information, influence, and interaction.** Microcopy is an essential part of UX. Its main functions are divided into three goals: to inform, to influence, and to support interaction. Informative microcopy facilitates orientation and reduces uncertainty, influential texts drive action or build a relationship with the brand, and interactive [microcopy helps with interface control](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/3-is-of-microcopy/). It's important to perceive context. Texts should always have a clear primary goal that meets user needs.

**Generative AI changes how we search for information** – often we no longer search on Google but directly ask chatbots. warns that although RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation) seems like a salvation, it has fundamental weaknesses: AI can formulate queries poorly, succumb to confirmation [bias](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/cognitive-bias), cite unreliable sources, or sound more expert than it truly is. An illusion of authority arises that deceives. Technical limitations and epistemological problems make RAG a tool that [requires thorough evaluation and cautious use](https://dusanjanovsky.substack.com/p/o-zmenach-vyhledavani-a-problemy).

**How AI overviews affect SEO.** Michal Gališin and Matej Mitterhauszer examined 19,000 non-branded queries 30 days before and 30 days after the introduction of Google AI overviews in Google search. [AI overviews appeared in about 24% of queries](https://visibility.sk/blog/rankmaster3000-potvrdila-analyza-ai-overviews-nase-obavy/), most often in long informational searches, less in commercial ones. In AI overviews, Google cites 3–5 sources, repeatedly displaying pages from established domains. A full 81% of sources were found in the first ten search results. The click-through rate for queries with AI overviews dropped by 18%.

**UX strategy is a compass** that sets the direction for product development and company culture. According to a survey conducted by German UPA among UX professionals, [most companies lack a formally defined UX strategy](https://www.franzidesign.de/post/uxstrategie-umfrage-2025), even though they perceive its importance. Franziska Gronwald was interested not only in whether companies have a UX strategy but also in the areas it should cover. Franziska presents UX strategy as a living tool that helps change not only products but also corporate approaches.
