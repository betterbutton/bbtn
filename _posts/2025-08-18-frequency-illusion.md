# Frequency Illusion
> A cognitive illusion where something you've just noticed suddenly seems to be everywhere.

You've probably experienced it. You learn a new word or notice a detail in an app—and suddenly it seems like you see it everywhere. This is called the [Baader-Meinhof phenomenon](https://edukomforta.cz/clanek/baader-meinhof-fenomen-proc-si-najednou-vsimate-stejne-veci-vsude-kolem/) or [frequency illusion](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/basics/frequency-illusion). It's a [cognitive bias](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/cognitive-bias) where the brain overestimates the occurrence of something simply because you've recently noticed it.

Typically, the frequency illusion manifests when we start noticing mentions of products or services and ads for them after we've talked about them with someone. As a result, people sometimes suspect that mobile phones and apps like Facebook are eavesdropping on spoken conversations and that remarketing apps are tracking them.

> “Just because you’re paranoid doesn’t mean they’re not after you”\
> **— [Joseph Heller, Catch-22](https://www.goodreads.com/quotes/1136532-just-because-you-re-paranoid-doesn-t-mean-they-re-not-after-you)**

Two psychological mechanisms underlie this illusion: [selective attention](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/selective-attention), where the brain focuses on something specific, and [confirmation bias](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Konfirma%C4%8Dn%C3%AD_zkreslen%C3%AD), the tendency to seek evidence that supports what we already believe. When these processes combine, it makes it seem like the thing is appearing more frequently than before—even though it actually isn't.

In digital design, this has consequences. A new feature might seem completely obvious to developers, but the average user might not notice it at all. And if they do notice it, it might seem like it's suddenly everywhere—even if it only appears occasionally.

This is important to understand: perception of frequency is not the same as actual occurrence. Just because something appears somewhere doesn't mean people will notice it. And if they do notice it, it can create an impression of exaggerated presence. Designers should therefore balance the visibility of elements while being careful not to overwhelm users.

The Baader-Meinhof effect reminds us that when designing, it's not enough to just place something—we must also anticipate how people will see and perceive it.
