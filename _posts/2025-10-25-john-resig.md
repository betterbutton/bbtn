# John Resig: inspiration for an entire generation of developers
> Creator of jQuery, who changed JavaScript, championed open source, and enabled millions to build for the web.

![John Resig, illustration generated from a current photograph](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/e3930ed7-bb15-449a-b98a-bd961cafe27b_1536x1024.webp "496x330")

“Write less, do more.” — John Resig

[John Resig](https://johnresig.com/) (*May 8, 1984*) changed web development with one simple idea: *“Write less, do more.”* When he introduced the [jQuery](https://jquery.com/) library in 2006, he offered developers an easy and reliable way to build interactive websites across browsers.

jQuery quickly became the most widely used library of its kind—at its peak powering over 70% of websites, and today it's still used by nearly 40% of the top ten million. Developers on Reddit [call](https://bestofama.com/amas/h42ak#:~:text=Secondly%2C%20sir%2C%20you%20are%20a%20true%20hacker%2C%20a%20gentleman%20and%20a%20scholar.) him *“a true hacker, gentleman, and scholar”* and credit him with making JavaScript more accessible and fun.

On its own, the jQuery library would have secured John a place in the internet pantheon. But two years later, Resig ported the [Processing](http://processingjs.org/) visualization language to JavaScript. This enabled the creation of interactive visualizations, digital art, educational graphs, and games directly in the web browser—no plugins required.

After a stint at [Mozilla](https://www.mozilla.org/cs/), Resig moved to the [Khan Academy](https://www.khanacademy.org/), where he helped design an interactive learning environment for programming. In the field of digital humanities, he merged art and code. On the [Ukiyo‑e.org](https://Ukiyo‑e.org) platform, he made over 220,000 Japanese woodblock prints accessible, and through the [PHAROS](https://pharosartresearch.org/) project, he applied machine vision to art history. Academics today call him *“a citizen of the internet.”*

Resig's philosophy stems from empathy: *“You must put yourself in the shoes of the person trying to use what you've created.”* His success isn't just about technique—it's about listening, teaching, and supporting the community. Proof that the best tools are born not just from code, but from understanding people.
