# Confirmation Bias
> Confirmation bias leads people to search for and interpret information in a way that confirms their existing beliefs. This cognitive bias explains common errors in decision-making, interpretation, and evaluation.

[Confirmation Bias](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Konfirma%C4%8Dn%C3%AD_zkreslen%C3%AD) ([confirmation bias](https://catalogofbias.org/biases/confirmation-bias/)) leads people to search for and interpret information in a way that confirms their existing beliefs. Understanding this cognitive bias helps explain common errors in decision-making, research interpretation, and the evaluation of digital products.

Confirmation bias is the tendency to favor information that confirms our existing beliefs while ignoring or downplaying information that contradicts them ([Nickerson, 1998](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/280685490_Confirmation_Bias_A_Ubiquitous_Phenomenon_in_Many_Guises)). It influences how people search for information, interpret data, and remember past events. Because this mechanism operates predominantly automatically, it affects both everyday decisions and expert judgments.

Research shows that people often actively seek confirmation of their assumptions instead of testing alternative explanations. In a classic experiment, participants had to discover the rule behind a number sequence. Most proposed additional examples that confirmed their hypothesis rather than testing examples that could falsify it ([Wason, 1960](https://archive.org/details/FailureToEliminateHypothesisInAConceptualTask/Failure%20to%20Eliminate%20Hypothesis%20in%20a%20Conceptual%20Task)). Many therefore failed to identify the actual rule. The experiment well illustrates the natural tendency to seek confirmation rather than disconfirmation.

Confirmation bias manifests at several stages of reasoning. People more often seek information sources that align with their views ([Hart et al., 2009](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4797953/)), interpret ambiguous evidence as support for their position ([Lord, Ross & Lepper, 1979](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/232555483_Biased_assimilation_and_attitude_polarization_The_effects_of_prior_theories_on_subsequently_considered_evidence)), and also more easily remember confirming information than information that challenges their beliefs (Nickerson, 1998). This is partly because confirming information requires less cognitive effort and does not evoke uncomfortable tension.

In a digital environment, this effect can be further amplified. Personalized feeds, recommendation algorithms, or search results often prioritize content similar to what users have viewed before. Such mechanisms can unintentionally reinforce existing views and limit exposure to differing perspectives.

Confirmation bias does not only affect users. It also influences those who design and evaluate digital products. Teams may interpret analytics data, user test results, or research findings in a way that confirms their expectations. When evidence is ambiguous, the original assumption often decides.

Mitigating the influence of confirmation bias requires a conscious approach. It is advisable to formulate hypotheses in a way that allows them to be falsified, not merely confirmed. It also makes sense to combine multiple sources of evidence and involve more people in the analysis who can challenge each other's interpretations.

Confirmation bias cannot be completely eliminated. It is a natural part of human cognition. The practical goal is therefore not its elimination, but creating procedures and decision-making processes that can detect it before it subtly influences conclusions.
