# Brenda Laurel and the Birth of Interaction as Experience
> She shifted HCI from interface mechanics to lived experience and fundamentally influenced how UX understands agency, engagement, and meaning.

![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/8a67c44f-9e36-4a4a-bb4f-83befdf5bdcb_800x533.webp "522x347")

*[Brenda Laurel](https://www.linkedin.com/in/blaurel/)* (*November 20, 1950*) brought a change in perspective to [human-centered design](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/human-cetered-design): interactive systems are not just tools for control, but environments for experience. Therefore, UX quality is measured not only by efficiency but by the degree of meaning, engagement, and sense of influence the system provides to people.

She developed this idea most persuasively in her book *[Computers as Theatre](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/239018.Computers_As_Theatre)*, where she uses dramatic theory as a framework for interaction design. Intention, action, feedback, tension, or resolution become design material. The interface is understood as a stage. The user is not an operator but a participant in the action. This perspective foreshadowed today's emphasis on engagement, flow, and experience quality.

Her concept of limited agency, often described by the metaphor of a flying wedge of possibilities, precisely names the essence of interaction design: every choice narrows subsequent possibilities. The designer does not determine behavior but creates conditions in which certain behaviors become more probable. This problem remains at the core of UX to this day.

![Model of a flying wedge. Over time, we move from the realm of the possible toward the probable, and eventually toward the inevitable.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/3535e6c9-7d3b-4afe-95f8-9af66447feaf_1253x987.webp "1253x987")

> "In human-computer interaction, the shaping of potential is influenced by people's choices and actions in real time, by the restriction of possibilities, and by the creation of lines of probability that differ from relation to relation and from person to person. The 'flying wedge' can be oriented in various directions; thus, the program contains potential for many whole actions."\
> **— Brenda Laurel, Computers as Theatre**

Brenda also consistently connected theory with research and practice. At *Interval Research*, she led extensive qualitative studies on girls' relationship with technology and challenged industry myths about their disinterest. Based on these insights, she founded the studio *Purple Moon*, which designed narrative games based on empiricism, not stereotypes. This created a lasting precedent for ethical, research-backed design.

Across VR, ubiquitous computing, and education, she advocated for interaction as a cultural and humanistic discipline. Her legacy is not a method, but an optics: to evaluate design by what kind of person the user can be while using it. And that is a question more relevant today than ever.
