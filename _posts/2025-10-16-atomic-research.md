# Atomic Research
> A modular way to record and share user insights, enabling scalable and evidence-based design decision-making in teams.

[Atomic Research](https://medium.com/design-kisk/atomic-ux-research-trochu-jin%C3%BD-jadern%C3%BD-v%C3%BDzkum-298f8551134e) ([atomic research](https://www.userinterviews.com/ux-research-field-guide-chapter/atomic-research-nuggets)) changes how UX teams work with user insights. Instead of [burying findings in long reports](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Mi-kkFM_SWE), we capture small, individual nuggets—each backed by evidence and tagged for easy reuse. This creates a living [research repository](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/research-repository) that’s simple to search, expand, and leverage across projects.

Each research atom ([nugget](https://www.airtable.com/universe/expShuhNMi0Oc0xpb/polaris-ux-nuggets)) has four parts:

- experiment (what we tried),
- fact (what we observed),
- insight (what we conclude from it) and
- recommendation (what we will do about it).

This structure helps us better track the connection between data and decisions, compare findings across studies, and make informed choices faster and with more confidence.

The inspiration for this method came from [Brad Frost's atomic design](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/atomic-design), [Don Norman's modular approach](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/incremental-modular-design) to problem-solving, or the [DIKW](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/279942958_Data_Information_Knowledge_Wisdom_DIKW_A_Semiotic_Theoretical_and_Empirical_Exploration_of_the_Hierarchy_and_its_Quality_Dimension) knowledge framework. It also draws conceptually from qualitative methods in sociology (e.g., [grounded theory](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zakotven%C3%A1_teorie)), anthropology (e.g., ethnography), or psychology (e.g., [thematic analysis](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tematick%C3%A1_anal%C3%BDza)).

Unlike traditional research archives, the atomic approach allows connecting insights across time, platforms, and teams. It is particularly useful when designers need to quickly find answers from previous studies.

However, discipline is required: consistent tagging, maintaining context, and resisting oversimplification. When we do it well, it’s no longer just about documentation—it’s about the team maintaining and developing a collective understanding of the people they design for.
