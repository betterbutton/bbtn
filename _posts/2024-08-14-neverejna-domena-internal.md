# Private domains will be .internal
> 

The International Authority for Top-Level Domain Names, ICANN, has approved a new [.internal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/.internal) domain intended for private networks, such as intranets. Records in the .internal domain will not be propagated to authoritative root servers, so conflicts between different private networks should not occur. The .internal domain is also designed to prevent conflicts with public domains.

For .internal domains, it is expected that records will point to [private IP addresses](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Priv%C3%A1tn%C3%AD_s%C3%AD%C5%A5), but technically nothing prevents domains under the .internal TLD from pointing to public networks as well.

Until now, it has been common for private network administrators to choose a domain name like .corp, .home, and similar, and later such a domain became a full-fledged domain. This happened, for example, with the .dev domains after the introduction of new top-level domains after 2013.

The .internal domain has not yet appeared among the reserved ones in the IETF technical documents called RFCs. Since 1999, the domains .example, .invalid, .localhost, and .test have been among the [reserved domains](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc6761.html) whose records do not appear in the root DNS servers.
