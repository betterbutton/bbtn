# Hedonic Adaptation
> A psychological effect where users quickly get used to novelties, helping to design experiences that don't get old.

Users get accustomed to digital experiences surprisingly quickly. Initial excitement fades and engagement drops. This is the so-called [hedonic adaptation](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/H%C3%A9donick%C3%A1_adaptace) ([hedonic treadmill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hedonic_treadmill)). It's a psychological phenomenon where people eventually return to their baseline level of happiness, regardless of positive stimuli.

In game design, [hedonic adaptation acts as a design pattern](https://ui-patterns.com/patterns/hedonic-adaptation). In UX, it means that even the most entertaining features quickly become ordinary or even invisible. For designers, this implies [a clear task](https://www.ux-bulletin.com/hedonic-adaptation-in-ux-designing-for-sustained-delight/): to design in a way that the user experience remains interesting even after the novelty wears off.

It helps, for example, to reveal features gradually, at the right time. It's also important to support long-term goals—like learning, health, or creativity. These bring deeper and more lasting satisfaction than immediate pleasure.

Thanks to features that allow for review or reflection—such as progress overviews, memories, or diaries—people value what they've already experienced more. Elements of social interaction bring variability and unpredictability, which slows down adaptation. And well-thought-out gamification, meaning game elements like challenges, rewards, or level progression, can hold attention much longer than an ordinary badge.

A truly sustainable experience, however, arises when design also supports deeper human needs—such as autonomy, competence, and relationships. The [METUX](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/325401567_Designing_for_Motivation_Engagement_and_Wellbeing_in_Digital_Experience) model (Motivation, Engagement and Thriving in User Experience) shows that it is precisely these psychological factors that have the greatest impact on long-term engagement.

Hedonic adaptation isn't a bug—it's human nature. Great UX design learns to work with it. It doesn't chase constant novelty, but grows with the user and creates experiences that have lasting value.
