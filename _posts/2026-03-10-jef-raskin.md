# Jef Raskin and the Idea of the Humane Interface
> He held the view that a humane interface means a way of designing computers that stems from human thinking. The computer adapts to the human, not the human to the computer.

![Jef Raskin (drawing generated from photographs)](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/debd4a93-b082-4fdf-839b-eae11e145e62_1536x861.webp "1536x861")

“A computer shall not waste your time or require you to do more work than is strictly necessary.” — [Jef Raskin](https://www.codefellows.org/blog/the-three-laws-of-interaction-design/)

*Jef Raskin* (March 9, 1943 — February 26, 2005). He was a computer scientist, interface theorist, and also the initiator of the *Macintosh* project at *Apple*. He became most famous for his book *[The Humane Interface](https://archive.org/details/humaneinterfacen00rask)*. In it, he explains that computer systems should respect the limitations of human attention, memory, and decision-making. According to Raskin, poorly designed interfaces unnecessarily delay, cause errors, and force people to expend more mental effort than necessary.

Long before the term user experience came into use, Raskin promoted a field he called *[cognetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jef_Raskin#Cognetics)*. It is the study of the relationship between the interface and human cognition. He was primarily interested in how to reduce the complexity of interaction between humans and computers.

One of his best-known principles is the critique of modes. A mode means that the same action leads to a different result depending on the current state of the system. Such situations often cause errors. Raskin therefore advocated for so-called mode-less interfaces, where the same actions always behave the same way.

His ideas also influenced the early form of personal computers. In 1979, he launched the Macintosh project. The original vision was to create an affordable and easy-to-use computer for ordinary people. Although the final form of the Macintosh later evolved in a different direction, Raskin's vision emphasized accessibility and simplicity.

In later years, he worked on the experimental system *[Archy](https://handwiki.org/wiki/Archy)*, which was originally named *[THE – The Humane Environment](https://arstechnica.com/gadgets/2025/09/your-very-own-humane-interface-try-jef-raskins-ideas-at-home/)*. It was meant to demonstrate how his principles could work in practice. Archy used mode-less commands and an interface based on zooming, which better aligns with how people organize information in memory.

Raskin understood interface design as a fundamental responsibility of technology creators. According to him, computers should not just be functional. They should above all be human-friendly.
