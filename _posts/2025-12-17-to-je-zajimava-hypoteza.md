# That's an interesting hypothesis!
> A way to turn a stakeholder's or client's idea into a testable assumption and shift the debate from opinions and authority to evidence and learning.

> *You are opening the seventeenth window of the BBtn Advent Calendar for 2025. In this year's advent series, we look at popular UX designer proverbs. Today's is: That's an interesting hypothesis!*

In UX design, the exclamation *That's an interesting hypothesis!* is a practical tool for working with unsolicited or problematic client ideas without unnecessary conflict. It neither accepts nor rejects the idea. Instead, it reframes it as an assumption that must be validated based on user behavior and real outcomes.

When used appropriately, this phrase serves two functions simultaneously: It acknowledges the client's contribution, thereby protecting the relationship and trust. At the same time, it defers agreement until data exists. To avoid sounding ironic, it should immediately be followed by clarification. You need to state what would have to be true for the idea to work, how it can be tested, and by what criteria we will know it succeeded. *[Alexander Osterwalder](https://alexosterwalder.com/)* in the book *[Value proposition design](https://www.databazeknih.cz/knihy/vydelavejte-svymi-napady-281622)* formalized hypothesis-driven design using [test cards and hypothesis cards](https://www.strategyzer.com/library/validate-your-ideas-with-the-test-card).

> ***We assume that creating*** `[an experience]` ***for*** `[users]` ***will lead to*** `[outcomes]` ***because*** `[rationale]`***.***

This approach aligns with [hypothesis-driven design](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/evidence-based-design) and Lean UX. Ideas are not truths but educated guesses about the right solution. The same rules apply to designer ideas as to manager ideas. Research decides, not the strength of a voice.

The habit of [turning ideas into hypotheses](https://dataanddesign.substack.com/p/if-you-want-to-make-progress-with) is useful in an environment full of quick opinions, shortcut solutions, and authority-based persuasion. A hypothesis preserves the stakeholder's intent but protects the product from random changes.

Not every idea deserves a test. That is also part of professionalism. But the language of hypotheses helps explain why. In practice, it's not a trick but a habit of thinking in evidence and learning.

The proverb *That's an interesting hypothesis!* comes from designer *[Petr Štědrý](https://www.linkedin.com/in/stedry/)*, who popularized it in the Czech UX subculture through participants of the *UXWell* educational program.
