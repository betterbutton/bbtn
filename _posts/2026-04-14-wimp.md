# WIMP: Windows, Icons, Menus and Pointer
> A graphical interface model based on windows, icons, menus, and pointer control; desktop logic that still shapes the functioning of many websites and web applications today.

WIMP – that is, [windows, icons, menus, pointer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WIMP_(computing), in Czech windows, icons, menus, and pointer – is an old term for a still-living idea. It denotes an interaction model that made computer graphical control accessible to ordinary people and which still significantly influences the shape of the web.

Even though today's web often no longer resembles a classic desktop, it still stands on WIMP-based assumptions. Visible choices are easier than hidden commands. Spatial arrangement aids orientation. Instant feedback reduces uncertainty.

The importance of WIMP lies not so much in the acronym itself as in the usability logic behind it. Research by [Ivan Sutherland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ivan_Sutherland), [Douglas Engelbart](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/douglas-engelbart), *Xerox PARC*, and later [Ben Shneiderman](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/ben-shneiderman) showed that people work better when they can manipulate visible objects, recognize options instead of memorizing commands, and reverse steps without penalty. That is the true legacy of WIMP. Not a nostalgic desktop metaphor, but a lasting principle of interaction.

The web adopted this logic almost automatically. Browser panels and modal dialogs follow windows. Favicons, symbols in toolbars, and button icons continue the role of icons. Navigation, dropdown menus, and context controls develop menu logic. Hover states, dragging, and visual cursor feedback carry forward the pointer model. The browser thus became the most widespread environment where WIMP lives on.

Its limits are equally instructive. WIMP was created for large screens, visual focus, and precise pointer aiming. Touch devices weaken the importance of hover, small screens compress menus, and accessibility rules today correct many pointer-dependent assumptions. They require larger active targets, keyboard alternatives, and more understandable semantics.

This is precisely why WIMP still deserves attention. It is not just a chapter in interface history. It is a practical test of good web design. Make actions discoverable. Clarify controls. And let recognition do more work than memory.
