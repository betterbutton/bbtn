# How Might We
> How Might We – a question used in design processes as a design challenge in the brief.

The question **How Might We** has an important place in [Design Sprint](https://medium.com/svejda-goldmann-webdesign/vyu%C4%8Dujeme-design-sprint-2-0-a-co-%C5%BEe-to-vlastn%C4%9B-je-533e9a4de5e6) or in [Double Diamond](https://www.designcouncil.org.uk/our-resources/the-double-diamond/). In design approaches, it helps describe the brief as a [challenge to ponder](https://kisk.phil.muni.cz/100metod/designova-vyzva).

*"When I worked at a betting company, I came up with the idea to allow editing of already placed bets,"* Martin shares an example from practice. *"Colleagues were slapping their foreheads, what nonsense it is. The best thing about betting is the tension, after all. If bettors have the option to edit their bet slip, all the magic will vanish from betting."*

However, after some time, Martin returned with his idea once more: *"The second time, I formulated the idea as a question: How might we allow bettors to add another event to the slip or remove still-unsettled events from it?"* The result of a brief formulated as a question is that people feel pressure to prove they know the answer: *"I learned that it's a common thing abroad, and we immediately started working on bet slip editing."*

A design challenge stems from the insight we have into user needs and business goals.

**Well-formulated questions:**

- are based on user research,
- are open enough not to presuppose a specific solution,
- describe the situation from the user's perspective,
- are specific in targeting and expected outcome,
- do not describe the problem, but the expected positive benefit, and
- stimulate creativity in finding an innovative solution.

Every designer approaches formulating a How Might We question differently. We have had success working with a template that includes a description of the user and the expected benefit:

> **How might we** `+ {expected benefit} + {for whom} + {with what properties and constraints} +` **?**

Designers advocate various approaches from emotional design, sustainability, inclusion, to minimalism, and participatory design. You can project your values into the HMW question formulation in the form of properties and constraints.

---

**Academic note on historical development:** The creative challenge is first recorded in the form of the How Might We question by Sidney Parnes in the book Creative Behavior Guidebook from 1967. Parnes did not deal with design but with creative problem-solving, and he did not use the phrase How Might We formally methodologically, but as an example of [challenge formulation in a sample exercise](https://www.humantific.com/post/who-owns-how-might-we). In product development, the question How Might We appears in the 1970s when researcher [Marino Basadur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Min_Basadur#:~:text=found%20the%20phrase%20%22-,How%20Might%20We%3F,-%22%20was%20most%20accepted) uses it [at Procter & Gamble](https://www.basadur.com/the-origin-of-how-might-we/). From Basadur, the HMW question was later learned by [Charles Warren](https://www.linkedin.com/in/charleshwarren/details/experience/), who [brought it to the design firm IDEO](https://jdmeier.com/how-might-we/#:~:text=of%20the%20approach.-,The%20History%20of%20How%20Might%20We,-So%20if%20we) and later to Google. Thanks to this, you can find the How Might We question in design frameworks or in Design Sprint.
