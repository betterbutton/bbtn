# Lucy Suchman and Situated Action
> A researcher who fundamentally transformed the field of human-computer interaction by showing that human action is always situated – and that technology simultaneously redistributes responsibility.

![Lucy Suchman, drawing generated from photographs](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/910bd90d-8cb0-4782-8611-b7bfec599579_955x826.webp "446x385")

“Agency is not an attribute but the ongoing reconfigurings of the world.” — Lucy Suchman

*[Lucy Suchman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lucy_Suchman)* (*born February 12, 1954*) transformed thinking about the relationship between humans and technology. In her groundbreaking 1987 book *[Plans and Situated Actions](https://www.cs.colby.edu/courses/J16/cs267/papers/Suchman-PlansAndSituatedActions.pdf)*, she challenged the then-dominant idea that people act according to pre-formed plans which they merely execute. She showed that action does not stem from ready-made scripts but emerges within specific material and social conditions. According to her, plans are not prescriptions but resources—orienting aids that support, rather than determine, action.

> “The designer's project in this sense consists in endowing the machine with reasons for behavior that are responsibly rational, i.e., reasonable or understandable to others, including in interactional situations where it is responding to the actions of others.”\
> **— Lucy Suchman,** ***[Talk with machines, redux](https://interfacecritique.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/Suchman_IC3.pdf)***

During her time at *Xerox PARC*, she developed a method of detailed video analysis of human-technology interactions. Instead of treating user errors as failures of rationality, she interpreted them as key moments revealing how people and technologies jointly construct meaning. This shifted HCI research from laboratory experiments to field studies of actual work practices—and usability came to be understood as a question of context.

In her later book *[Human‑Machine Reconfigurations](https://seeingcollaborations.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/suchman_humanmachinereconfigurations.pdf)*, she extended this perspective to artificial intelligence and automation. Rather than asking whether machines can think, she poses another question: how do claims about machine “intelligence” reshape our concepts of the human and of responsibility? She critically analyzes how AI is presented as an autonomous entity, showing that behind this representation lie complex institutional, political, and labor infrastructures.

> “Thanks to robots, we are becoming increasingly aware that body and mind cannot be separated. Social skills or intelligence are inextricably linked to them.”\
> **— [Lucy Suchman](https://wave.rozhlas.cz/nepodrizujeme-se-potrebam-nasich-technologii-prilis-pta-se-antropolozka-lucy-7910316)**

Her work over the decades returns to one fundamental question: who does what—to whom, under what conditions, and with what consequences? In this way, Suchman shifted design from the plane of technical optimization to the plane of sociomaterial intervention. Contemporary contextual and participatory approaches in UX research bear the clear imprint of her emphasis that technology cannot be understood—nor designed—apart from the situations in which it is actually enacted.
