# Users Hate Change
> An extended proverb in UX that aims to explain user resistance to redesigns—in reality, it often describes resistance to the hassle and losses caused by poorly designed change.

> *You are opening the eighteenth window of the BBtn Advent Calendar for 2025. In this year's advent series, we will look at popular proverbs of UX designers. Today's one is: Users Hate Change.*

*Users hate change.* You hear this phrase in UX so often it sounds like a given fact. It is usually invoked when complaints, confusion, or outright resistance follow a redesign. In its simplest interpretation, it says resistance is inevitable and irrational. But that's precisely what's misleading.

> “Users don’t hate change. Users hate change that doesn’t make things better, but makes everything have to be relearned.”\
> **— [Christina Wodtke](https://eleganthack.com/user-dont-hate-change-they-hate-you/)**

As *[Christina Wodtke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christina_Wodtke)* so succinctly put it, users don't just seemingly hate change. In reality, they might very well hate *you*. This isn't an insult to designers, but a reminder of responsibility. Negative reactions appear when change breaks trust, devalues acquired skills, or forces people to relearn without clear benefit.

Both psychology and information systems research confirm this. People tend to prefer the status quo and experience losses more intensely than gains. When an interface changes, the immediate loss of speed, certainty, and a sense of competence is felt, while the promised benefits remain vague. Familiar things are mentally fluent, and fluent things feel correct and safe. When you disrupt that fluency, users feel it.

> “Studies show, in fact, that there is a mismatch of nine to one, or 9×, between what innovators think consumers want and what consumers truly desire.”\
> **— [John T. Gourville](https://web.mit.edu/mamd/www/tech_strat/courseMaterial/topics/topic4/readings/Eager_Sellers_and_Stony_Buyers/Eager_Sellers_and_Stony_Buyers.pdf/Eager_Sellers_and_Stony_Buyers.pdf)**

Resistance after a change isn't proof that users can't handle novelty. It's a signal that you've created high transition costs for them to adopt the new version. The more you disrupt daily workflows, the stronger the reaction you can expect.

Well-designed change minimizes transition costs. It protects key ways of working, introduces novelties gradually, and can clearly explain why it's worth paying the price for change. It also gives users a sense of control and meaning.

> “Losses loom larger than corresponding gains.”\
> **— [Amos Tversky & Daniel Kahneman](https://bear.warrington.ufl.edu/brenner/mar7588/Papers/tversky-kahneman-qje1991.pdf)**

In this sense, the phrase "users hate change" can serve as a warning. But if you use it as an excuse, it hides the real problem. When it seems users hate *your* change, it's more reasonable to look for the flaw in the design than in human nature.
