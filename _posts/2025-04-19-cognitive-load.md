# Cognitive Load
> Cognitive load represents the amount of mental effort required to use an interface. Design can reduce cognitive load—or conversely, worsen it.

Cognitive load ([cognitive load](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11251-009-9110-0)) is the amount of mental effort a person must exert to understand and use your interface. Often, this very factor decides whether the user experience will be smooth or frustrating. When designing a website or application, your goal is to minimize unnecessary obstacles. This allows people to focus on what's important.

There are three main types of cognitive load. The first is intrinsic load, also known as internal cognitive load ([intrinsic cognitive load](https://data.europa.eu/apps/data-visualisation-guide/intrinsic-cognitive-load)). It relates to how complex the task itself is. For example, entering an address is simply more difficult than clicking a heart icon. The second is extraneous load, or external cognitive load ([extraneous cognitive load](https://data.europa.eu/apps/data-visualisation-guide/extraneous-cognitive-load)). It arises when the design is confusing or cluttered. The third is germane load, sometimes also called essential, relevant, or structural cognitive load ([germane cognitive load](https://data.europa.eu/apps/data-visualisation-guide/germane-cognitive-load)). This is associated with learning and understanding the principles of your system.

When an interface is described as intuitive, it usually means it handles cognitive load well. Clear structure, progressive disclosure of information, and consistency help people orient themselves better. Conversely, unclear elements or chaotic navigation force users to think about what they are actually supposed to do, causing cognitive overload.

A good interface doesn't have to look simple. But it should be simple to use. Primarily because it doesn't consume more attention than is absolutely necessary.

---

**Translation Note** – The types of cognitive load do not have established translations in Czech. Given the lack of authoritative publications that would fix the translation, various translation variants appear, mainly in final theses. Authors are sometimes at a loss; for example, for the term *germane cognitive load*, we have even encountered the translation "germánská kognitivní zátěž" (Germanic cognitive load).

We chose less literal translations—*intrinsic*, *extraneous*, and *germane cognitive load*—which, in our view, better capture the nature of the load categories.
