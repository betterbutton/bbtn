# Larry Tesler left a strong legacy: copy, paste, WYSIWYG, and the end of modes
> He systematically moved complexity from users to information systems. His principles: copy/paste, mode-free interaction, WYSIWYG, and the law of conservation of complexity still determine how we design today.

![Larry Tesler](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/19ad31e4-9e6e-41cc-bba6-41252042b517_1536x1024.png "1456x971")

“I have been mistakenly identified as ‘the father of the graphical user interface for the Macintosh’. I was not. However, a paternity test might expose me as one of its many grandparents.” — [Larry Tesler](https://www.nomodes.com/larry-tesler-consulting/summary#:~:text=I%20have%20been%20mistakenly%C2%A0identified%C2%A0as%20%E2%80%9Cthe%20father%20of%20the%20graphical%20user%20interface%20for%20the%20Macintosh%E2%80%9D.%20I%20was%20not.%20However%2C%20a%20paternity%20test%20might%20expose%20me%20as%20one%20of%20its%20many%20grandparents.)

*[Lawrence Gordon Tesler](https://www.nomodes.com/)* (April 24, 1945 — February 16, 2020) left a legacy that is almost invisible at first glance. His key idea is simple: complexity doesn't disappear from systems. It can only be moved. And the crucial question is: will the user or the system carry it?

Larry Tesler was convinced the answer had to be unequivocal: the system.

He embodied this principle in concrete solutions. At *Xerox PARC*, he contributed to the creation of the *cut, copy, paste* commands, which replaced abstract commands with a natural sequence: first select, then act. This *object–action* model became the foundation of graphical user interfaces.

Just as consistently, he rejected so-called modes. Systems where the same action means something different every time force users to constantly watch the context. This leads to errors and uncertainty. Tesler therefore advocated for mode-free interaction – interfaces that behave consistently and predictably.

> “Unfriendly? That’s an interesting way to think about it. So you want friendly software, software that is friendly to the user.”\
> **— Larry Tesler,** [IEEE Spectrum](https://spectrum.ieee.org/of-modes-and-men#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThat%E2%80%99s%20an%20interesting%20way%20to%20think%20about%20it.%20So%20you%20want%20friendly%20software%2C%20software%20that%20is%20friendly%20to%20the%20user.)

At Apple, these principles became reality. He contributed to the *Lisa* and *Macintosh* projects, where direct manipulation and understandable controls became the standard. Later, he applied the same approach at Amazon and Yahoo, environments of mass digital traffic.

He also influenced the very language of the field. Terms like *user‑friendly* or *[WYSIWYG](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/wysiwyg)* express his ambition: to eliminate the gap between what the user sees and what actually happens.

> “Intelligence is whatever machines haven’t done yet.”\
> **— Larry Tesler,** [AI theorem](https://www.nomodes.com/larry-tesler-consulting/adages-and-coinages?rq=browser#:~:text=Intelligence%20is%20whatever%20machines%20haven%27t%20done%20yet)

Tesler's legacy is almost invisible today. Copying text, editing documents, or navigating interfaces seems obvious. That is precisely where his strength lies – the complexity hasn't vanished, it's just no longer in plain sight.

It remains where, according to Tesler, it always should have been: inside the system.

> “I really didn’t believe in it. I thought cursor keys were much better. We literally took people off the streets who had never seen a computer. In three or four minutes they were happily editing away using cursor keys. At that point I was going to show them the mouse and prove that they could select text faster than with the cursor keys. Then I was going to show that they didn’t like it. It backfired.”\
> **— Larry Tesler,** [IEEE Spectrum](https://spectrum.ieee.org/larry-tesler-the-computer-scientist-who-revolutionized-the-user-interface-dies-at-74#:~:text=I%20really%20didn%E2%80%99t%20believe%20in%20it%2C)
