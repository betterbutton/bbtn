# Prepare for the Worst – Practice Your System Recovery
> 

Perhaps you noticed the [Seznam services outage](https://x.com/seznam_cz/status/1805554256885272985) this summer. Seznam practices data center shutdowns several times a year. This time, the exercise didn't go as expected. Despite the incident, it was a good outcome: Seznam documented what caused the outage and will be better prepared next time.

Hardware failure, a ransomware attack, a natural disaster, or in our parts a potential threat of political restriction or armed conflict can wipe out years of built online business in just a few hours. The mere existence of backups is a huge advantage, but relying solely on having something stored somewhere isn't enough.

This is where training and regular system recovery testing come into play. If you know what to do and how to do it in a crisis, you can minimize losses and quickly bring your website or application back online.

# Why Backups Alone Aren't Enough

Do you feel like you're backing up and that should be enough? What good are backups that you can only restore after several days—or perhaps not at all? Recovery training will reveal weaknesses in your process that you would otherwise only discover at the moment of greatest need.

You might find, for example, that database dumps cannot be imported efficiently without a special tool, that recovery takes disproportionately long, or that you're missing license keys for critical software. Alternatively, you might discover that your backups are on the same machine running your system, so in the event of a natural disaster, you won't be able to access them.

# How Often Should You Practice Recovery?

A good practice is to practice system recovery at least once or twice a year. If your project is undergoing rapid growth, adding new features, or changing infrastructure, you should test more frequently. Regular exercises will help you maintain an overview of the steps to take and ensure that in a real crisis, you'll react more confidently. The better the recovery process is practiced, the faster you'll get a functional environment back.

# From Simulating a Crash to Verifying Functionality

You don't have to perform recovery exercises during full operation like Seznam.cz does. They can take place in a separate and secure environment so you don't jeopardize your business. Create a test instance of a server or virtual machine, upload the latest backups to it, and simulate a crash—for example, by shutting down critical services or deleting directories.

Then, step by step, perform the recovery: import the database, upload files, check configurations. Once you're done, thoroughly verify functionality. It's important to document every step so you can optimize the process later. As you know: [you need checklists](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/advent-2408).

# Tools and Technologies

There are many tools for recovery: from simple scripts for data synchronization ([rsync](https://www.digitalocean.com/community/tutorials/how-to-use-rsync-to-sync-local-and-remote-directories), [rclone](https://tcude.net/setting-up-rclone-with-google-drive/)) through database tools (mysqldump, pg_dump) to comprehensive cloud backup solutions or specialized software. If you use a CMS, plugins focused specifically on backups and recovery can help.

Virtual environments offer quick snapshots and easy migration to another host server, which ultimately reduces the time needed to get a website or application running again.

# Practicing Recovery in Containerized and Virtualized Environments

If you use [Docker](https://www.docker.com/) or [Kubernetes](https://kubernetes.io/), you can easily test recovery using the [Velero](https://velero.io/) tool, which backs up and restores the complete state of a cluster. Virtualization generally allows for quick creation and storage of snapshots and simplifies moving applications between servers. Automation using scripts and tools like [Ansible](https://www.ansible.com/) or [Terraform](https://www.terraform.io/) will help speed up recovery and minimize human error.

# Learning from Mistakes and Continuous Improvement

Once your recovery exercise is over, analyze in detail what went well and what didn't. Did you find that you can't install a key plugin from the command line using automation scripts? Is documentation for restoring licenses missing? Did you neglect updating to a new database backup format? Address these issues as soon as possible and document the process. This way, your next recovery test will be faster, more reliable, and less stressful.

Practicing system recovery isn't a waste of time, but a strategic investment in the stability, security, and credibility of your online business. Through regular training, verification, and improvement of procedures, you'll ensure that even in the event of unexpected outages, you can respond promptly and minimize damage. System recovery is a skill that's better to practice in advance than to learn under the pressure of a real crisis. 🎄09
