# Design is Problem Solving
> A popular UX designer proverb reminding us that we must know whose problems we are actually solving.

> *You are opening the sixth window of the BBtn Advent Calendar for 2025. In this year's advent series, we will look at popular UX designer proverbs. Today's is: Design solves problems.*

Designers love to emphasize that they solve problems. This statement captures the essence of a designer's work, but it simplifies it. In digital products, design turns confusion into clarity, delay into fluidity, and frustration into trust.

Research shows that when about ten percent of a project's budget is allocated to usability, it can [improve performance metrics](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/usability-roi-declining-but-still-strong/) by up to eighty percent. Companies that prioritize [design grow faster and have higher returns on investment](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/growth-marketing-and-sales/our-insights/the-business-value-of-design) than their competitors.

The essence of design is to change the current state for the better. As *[Herbert Simon](https://knihovna.vse.cz/nobel/simon/)* once wrote – design changes existing situations into preferred ones. For web designers or web managers, this means facilitating payments, simplifying navigation, improving accessibility, and measuring user satisfaction.

Processes like *[Double Diamond](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/double-diamond)* or *[Design Thinking](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/design-thinking-klic-k-inovativnim?utm_source=publication-search)* help first to find the right problem, define it precisely, create proposals, and verify if they actually help.

However, most design tasks are not simple. They are so-called *[wicked problems](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/wicked-problems)* – fluid and without a single correct answer. When people leave a website or abandon a form, the cause might be a combination of structure, text, feedback, and trust.

Design not only fixes these situations but also redefines them through research and repeated testing.

Nevertheless, the slogan can be misleading. Design can also create problems – for instance, when it follows only corporate goals and disregards users. Truly human-centered design, as *Don Norman* reminds us, must consider accessibility, ethics, and long-term impact.

Yes, design is problem solving – but responsible designers never forget that every solution changes the world for someone else, too.
