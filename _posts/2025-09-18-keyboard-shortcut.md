# Keyboard Shortcut
> Controlling the application with key combinations increases speed, accessibility, and work efficiency.

[Keyboard shortcuts](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kl%C3%A1vesov%C3%A1_zkratka) ([keyboard shortcuts](https://knock.app/blog/how-to-design-great-keyboard-shortcuts)) are one of the simplest ways to make web applications faster, more productive, and more accessible—and yet developers often forget about them. For creators of digital tools, shortcuts are a way to save users from repetitive clicking, speed up routine tasks, and reduce dependence on the mouse.

In an ideal scenario, shortcuts facilitate learning. A beginner starts with buttons and menus, while an advanced user swiftly [controls the application via keyboard](https://medium.com/@pratikkumar_10506/creating-dashboard-shortcuts-ux-case-study-c88f01985de0). This makes the interface both accessible and efficient.

Shortcuts also enhance accessibility. For users with fine motor difficulties, chronic fatigue, or other limitations, the keyboard offers a reliable alternative.

However, it is important to [design shortcuts thoughtfully](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/previous-versions/windows/desktop/dnacc/guidelines-for-keyboard-user-interface-design). They should be intuitive, easy to remember, and align with what users already know—for example, `Ctrl+S` to save or `Cmd+Z` to undo. If a shortcut is illogical or hidden, it loses its purpose.

Consistency is also crucial. If every part of the application has different control rules, it creates chaos instead of speed. And if shortcuts are not visible anywhere in the interface, users might not even know they exist.

Observe what people actually do in the application. Where do the same steps repeat? That's where a shortcut makes the most sense. Design based on behavior—not on a table.

Shortcuts alone won't fix a poor interface, but they add fluidity to good design. Users will notice—and appreciate.
