# OHIO: Only Handle It Once
> A decision-making rule that helps reduce friction points, maintain order, and improve focus through immediate action for greater long-term productivity.

The [OHIO](https://routine.co/blog/posts/guide-to-the-ohio-method) rule—meaning "handle it now and once and for all"—advises immediate action: read, decide, act, and be done. This means processing inputs, reports, or requests without delay. It reduces context switching and increases efficiency.

It originates from figures like [Ivy Lee](https://workflowy.com/systems/ivy-lee-method/) and [Robert Pozen](https://www.bobpozen.com/) and responds to information overload. The point is not to keep returning to a task. OHIO promotes decisive resolution: reply, delegate, schedule, or discard.

It works particularly well in agile environments. Minor interface bugs, edits, or user queries can often be handled in minutes. OHIO encourages doing them right away, simplifying the workflow.

The method also helps with order in digital tools—like emails, tasks, or documents. Teams then avoid wading through unfinished revisions or notes, working clearly and smoothly.

However, it doesn't always fit. Some complex matters require time or collaboration. OHIO works best where you have an established sorting or planning system.

For authors and digital product managers, OHIO is a simple rule: decide immediately on anything entering your system. Handle what you can in a moment. Schedule what's bigger. But don't let things hang in the air.
