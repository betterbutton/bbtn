# George A. Miller and the Human Limits on Which Usability Stands
> An American cognitive psychologist who showed that human limitations are not a barrier to design, but a key to understanding and respecting human thinking.

![George A. Miller, drawing generated from photographs.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/8c34088c-8760-4c97-b302-0c561265c6ba_1536x1024.webp "548x365")

“Below seven, the subjects were said to subitise; above seven, they were said to estimate.” — George A. Miller

*[George Armitage Miller](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Armitage_Miller)* (February 3, 1920 — July 22, 2012) is most commonly associated with the number seven. His name appears in recommendations, presentations, and design heuristics as the author of [the magical number seven, plus or minus two](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/millers-law). However, his true contribution to human‑centered design is deeper. It does not lie in a numerical rule, but in changing the way we think about the human mind.

George entered psychology at a time when the human mind was understood as an inaccessible black box. He showed that mental processes can be studied systematically and that people are not passive recipients of stimuli, but active processors of information. For design, this means a fundamental shift. Interfaces should not force people to adapt; they should adapt to the way people think, remember, and interpret meaning.

> “If the amount of information in the span of immediate memory is a constant, then the span should be short when the individual items contain a lot of information and the span should be long when the items contain little information.”\
> **— George A. Miller, [The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two](https://labs.la.utexas.edu/gilden/files/2016/04/MagicNumberSeven-Miller1956.pdf)**

His research on memory showed that cognitive load depends not only on the amount of information, but primarily on its organization. People help themselves with grouping, hierarchies, and meaning. This is precisely why structure, naming, and context are key design tools.

Equally important is George's emphasis on language. Interfaces are a form of communication, and their comprehensibility rests on relationships between concepts. His belief that understanding begins with an effort to comprehend the other's perspective today forms the foundation of design research. Human‑centered design thus finds in his legacy not only a scientific foundation but also an ethical compass.
