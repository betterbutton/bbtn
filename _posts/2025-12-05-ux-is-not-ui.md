# UX isn't UI
> The difference between look and feel – nice colors and fonts won't save a website that doesn't make sense to people.

> *You are opening the fifth window of the BBtn Advent Calendar for 2025. In this year's advent series, we will look at popular sayings of UX designers. Today's one is: UX isn't UI.*

Many websites fail with users not because they look outdated, but because they don't understand their users. Development teams often confuse *user experience* (UX) and *user interface* (UI). But they are not the same.

UI is the visible layer – buttons, colors, layout. UX is how easy, useful, and pleasant it is to work with the website. You can change the interface without improving the experience, but without thoughtful UX, no pretty UI will help.

UX encompasses the entire relationship between a person and a product – before, during, and after use. The *ISO 9241‑210 Human-centred design for interactive systems* standard says it is about a person's perceptions and responses resulting from the use or anticipated use of a system. *Don Norman* and *Jakob Nielsen* add that UX concerns all aspects of interaction with a company and its services. UI, on the other hand, is merely the access point through which this interaction occurs.

A well-known example of [a $300 million button](https://articles.centercentre.com/three_hund_million_button/) makes this clear: One e-commerce site forced customers to register before checkout. People left. The only change – the button label from *Register* to *Continue* and the option to pay without registration – an intervention in UX, not graphics – increased sales by hundreds of millions. The interface remained almost the same, but the experience improved radically.

From this follows a simple lesson: invest first in research, information architecture, and testing, only then in appearance. Measure success by ease and satisfaction, not by ornamentation. Accessibility, clarity, and speed are part of the experience, not add-ons.

A beautiful website attracts attention, but only a well-designed experience retains visitors. UX defines the meaning. UI merely mediates it.
