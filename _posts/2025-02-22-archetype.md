# Archetype
> Archetypes are abstract user models defining an entire group of users. They are based on research and help create intuitive digital products.

**Archetypes** in digital design are user models based on research into their behavior, needs, and motivations. These are not Jungian psychological archetypes, but [practical categories created by analyzing user data](https://uxdesign.cc/archetypes-in-design-d110dcb3fd7d). They help designers understand different ways of interacting with digital products and optimize the user experience.

Common archetypes in UX design include the *Beginner*, who needs clear guidance, the *Expert*, who requires advanced features, or the *Skeptic*, who expects credibility and verifiable information. These models allow designers to tailor interfaces to different types of users.

Alongside archetypes, there are other user modeling methods. [Personas](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/article/personas-why-and-how-you-should-use-them) are detailed fictional profiles based on demographic and behavioral data. [Jobs-to-Be-Done](https://jtbd.info/2-what-is-jobs-to-be-done-jtbd-796b82081cca), or JTBD, focuses on specific tasks and user needs rather than characteristics. Behavioral segmentation divides users based on how they interact with the product.

The main [difference between archetypes and personas](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/personas-archetypes/) lies in the level of detail. Archetypes provide a broader view of user behavior and can be applied to various contexts. Personas are more specific, include particular demographic data, and serve for testing specific user scenarios. In practice, both approaches are often combined—archetypes help understand motivations, while personas allow for more detailed user interface design.
