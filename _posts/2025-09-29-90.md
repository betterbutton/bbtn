## The Hidden Cost of Bad UX

Good UX can be expensive, but that's nothing compared to the cost of bad UX.

Bad UX harms business. You lose money, customers, trust, and opportunities because of it. And often, no one notices for a long time. Users usually don't complain. They just leave. A slow app, an unclear form, or a button that isn't where they expect it. And suddenly, they're with the competition, where everything works more simply.

Every second someone spends fumbling in a confusing environment is lost time – and lost profit. And while you're losing conversions, your customer support costs are rising. When users don't know what to do, they call for help. And you need more people on the phone.

Bad UX also damages the brand. People don't say *"the UX is bad"*. They say: *"that company is terrible."* The aftertaste remains. A bad experience transfers to the perception of the entire brand.

But the greatest damage is often invisible. A user who leaves might never come back. They'll never buy a premium plan. They'll never recommend your service to others. And you lose the future you could have had.

Good UX isn't just about looks. It's about people getting what they need. Without unnecessary obstacles. Because when they succeed, your business succeeds too.

## Linkodrome

**You'll fall in love with MCP for Chrome DevTools,** even if you're not a developer. If you regret that [Google released Gemini into Chrome](https://gemini.google/overview/gemini-in-chrome/?hl=cs) only for users in the USA, you don't have to anymore. Into your favorite AI assistant, you can [add an MCP server definition](https://developer.chrome.com/blog/chrome-devtools-mcp) for Chrome DevTools. Through the AI assistant, you'll then be able to open pages, read values from the developer console, but also manipulate the page.

You can tell the AI assistant to click buttons, fill out forms, and the like. This is most useful for QR testing, but it's also a great tool for various automations that you can now command in natural language. It can, for example, click through your OHS training or fill out a timesheet for you. I don't need to tell you that this option will also be appreciated by disabled users, who can thus overcome accessibility barriers.

**UX research is not in crisis, but in a period of transformation.** According to the [The state of user research 2025](https://www.userinterviews.com/state-of-user-research-report) survey, 80% of researchers already use AI, with feelings ranging from fascination to fears of devaluing human judgment. Layoffs have affected researchers similarly to other professions, but the median salary level rose to $105,500. Despite growing doubts about the future, the discipline shows itself as adaptive and seeking new ways to stay relevant.

**A practical and systematic approach to affinity mapping after research** is presented by Slava Shestopalov. Notes must be concise, unambiguous, and interpreted into full sentences. Slava [describes the process step by step](https://medium.com/design-bridges/affinity-mapping-b0b9faddccfb) from creating thematic groups to so-called super-clusters, and shows how to name groups using clear statements according to specific user experiences. Mapping should serve as a basis for team understanding, identifying patterns, and designing specific solutions – not just as decoration from sticky notes.

**A UX scorecard is used to evaluate a product's usability** – it helps compare UX over time and against competition. Nikki Anderson shares an eight-step guide on how to create such a scorecard: from setting goals, selecting metrics and tasks, creating an A–F rating scale, to choosing competition and conducting the actual testing. The scorecard allows for structured evaluation of key tasks from the perspective of user efficiency, satisfaction, and trust. The result is a clear visualization of the UX state, which helps justify the value of research and plan improvements.

## Jason Hoffman Taught the Web to Scale

![Jason Hoffman](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/13768ee3-fea9-491e-b5fc-6fe889db88e7_1536x1024.webp "475x316")

[Jason Hoffman](https://www.linkedin.com/in/jasonhoffman/) – father of containers and Node.js

[Jason Hoffman](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/jason-hoffman) significantly influenced the shape of today's web. At a time when virtual machines dominated the cloud, he advocated for lighter virtualization through containers and an operating system-level approach. This laid the foundation for scalable cloud architectures. By supporting Node.js, he enabled the development of real-time applications and API-based systems. His SmartOS operating system offered observability before it was common.

Jason advocated for openness and portability between clouds. His technological legacy is ubiquitous today – in every Node.js application, in Docker containers, and in Kubernetes.

## So We Understand Each Other

[Insight](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/insight) is not just interpretation of data, but a deeper understanding of why people behave a certain way. In product development, it helps recognize hidden motivations and friction points that raw numbers won't show. It arises from details – unusual patterns, wordings, or silences. It requires concentration, openness, and a culture that welcomes uncertainty. True insight enables more meaningful and empathetic design. Without it, we just optimize without addressing the core problem.

In the world of typography, [hyphen, dash, and minus](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/dash) differ not only in function but also in appearance and usage. Commonly, people use just one character in all three cases – the hyphen-minus. In quality typesetting, however, specialized characters are distinguished: a hyphen for compounding words and line breaks, a dash for text segmentation, and a minus for mathematics. Dashes have variants by length and context (en, em, figure dash), and their usage differs between languages and styles. In Czech, we prefer a medium dash and dashes separated by spaces. [In English, it depends on the style guide.](https://substack.com/@atpok/note/c-159780657)

[Microinteractions](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/microinteractions) are small, seemingly inconspicuous moments in a digital interface: clicks, hovers, animations. They have a crucial impact on the user experience. They consist of four parts: triggers, rules, feedback, and loops. If designed carefully, they add naturalness, understandability, and a sense of care to applications.
