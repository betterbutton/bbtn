**User research is threatened by cognitive bias.** This can distort results and lead to false conclusions. Among the [most common biases](https://uxdesign.cc/10-cognitive-biases-to-avoid-in-user-research-and-how-to-avoid-them-993aa397c8c6) are, for example, confirmation bias ([confirmation of one's own assumptions](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Konfirma%C4%8Dn%C3%AD_zkreslen%C3%AD)), framing effect ([influence of question wording on responses](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sugestivn%C3%AD_ot%C3%A1zka)) or hindsight bias ([retrospective rationalization](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Racionalizace_(psychologie)).

How to defend against cognitive bias? The key is the ability to question your own assumptions and analyze data objectively. It's easy to say, but how to do it? For example, by asking unbiased researchers for a review. Avoiding bias improves research quality and leads to more accurate insights about users.

You can practice recognizing cognitive biases using [flashcards](https://www.brainscape.com/subjects/cognitive-biases). If you prefer the paper version, Richard James Acton offers them [for download](https://github.com/RichardJActon/CognitiveBiasCards/tree/master/png). When printed, you can even [play the board game Pandemic](https://eshop.albi.cz/pandemic-blackfire/#:~:text=Pandemic%20je%20kooperativn%C3%AD%20hra,%20kde%20se%20hr%C3%A1%C4%8Di%20spole%C4%8Dn%C4%9B%20sna%C5%BE%C3%AD%20zastavit) with slightly [modified rules](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/34jf9Z43kBHF7Axz2/biased-pandemic). You can go deeper with David Dylan Thomas's book [Design for Cognitive Bias](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/54866129-design-for-cognitive-bias).

**Microsoft will revive the power plant where usability was born.** [Machal's tweet alerted us](https://x.com/machal/status/1837524968344543656) that Microsoft has contractually secured [electricity for its AI farms](https://edition.cnn.com/2024/09/20/energy/three-mile-island-microsoft-ai/index.html#:~:text=Three%20Mile%20Island,%20the%20site%20of%20worst%20nuclear%20disaster%20in) for twenty years [from the nuclear power plant on Pennsylvania's Three Mile Island](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hav%C3%A1rie_elektr%C3%A1rny_Three_Mile_Island). The nuclear power plant is known to all readers of the cult book [Design for Everyday Things](https://www.restorio.cz/catalog/product/view/id/32794) by Donald Arthur Norman, first published in 1988 and [recently reissued](https://www.nngroup.com/books/design-everyday-things-revised/).

![Generated image showing a view into the control room of a nuclear power plant, where operators are scratching their heads as a large display shows the blue screen of death known from the Windows operating system.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/da746388-8a68-4046-a6a2-296c8621d21c_680x680.jpeg "458x458")

One of the plant's reactors had an accident just a few months after commissioning 45 years ago, and Norman was called in to investigate as a cognitive psychologist. Norman at the time identified [human factors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ergonomics) as the cause, a euphemism for botched ergonomics not only physically but also organizationally. Since then, Norman has focused on interface design and gradually developed ideas of [human-centered design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human-centered_design), [interface usability](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pou%C5%BEitelnost), and [user experience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_experience).

The older reactor was not affected by the accident and remained operational until 2019. The reactor is expected to resume electricity generation in 2028.

**[PayPal's logo redesign](https://www.theverge.com/2024/9/18/24248315/paypal-new-logo-design-2024-update) is causing great passions.** Instead of the two-tone italic font name supplemented with a graphic mark, [PayPal after Pentagram's redesign](https://www.pentagram.com/work/paypal) now presents only the text brand PayPal rendered in a modified [LL Supreme](https://lineto.com/typefaces/supreme) font, which itself is just a variation on the famous sans-serif linear geometrically constructed [font by Paul Renner, Futura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Futura_(typeface).

Last year, Italian designer Emanuele Abrate came up with the [fun Logofonts project](https://www.behance.net/gallery/62589693/Logofonts), where he replaced the names in well-known company logos with the names of the fonts used to render them. Thanks to him, you'll find that PayPal is something like a cousin to Nike, Gillette, RedBull, Cisce, Omega, and generally that Futura is a popular font among logo authors.

**Set clear criteria for evaluating your website's success**

If you have already defined a goal for your website, it's time to think about how you'll know you're actually getting closer to that goal. In business, these indicators are called key performance indicators (KPIs). Each such indicator needs to be supplemented with a method of how you will measure its fulfillment—these become metrics.

Determine in advance which values will be considered successful so it's clear what you want to achieve. The team working on the website will strive to achieve these goals. However, it may happen that you achieve the set values, but the results won't correspond to the success you expected. In such a case, it's advisable to reconsider the metrics you've set. Share the established goals, KPIs, and metrics with designers and other team members, as you will judge the final result—and thus the quality of the work done—based on these criteria.
