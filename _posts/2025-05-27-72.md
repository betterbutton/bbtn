## Linkodrome

**Trust and brand in SEO replace keywords.** Scott Baradell is convinced that with the rise of AI in search, brand determines visibility. [PR and reputation building are becoming key pillars of a modern SEO strategy.](https://www.ideagrove.com/blog/why-seo-is-becoming-brand-marketing-whether-seos-like-it-or-not) This shift, referred to as Entity SEO, means SEO is converging with brand marketing. Technical optimization remains important, but without a strong brand, AI will simply overlook it.

**Why I stopped designing in Figma,** writes Elie Majorel. He describes why he switched to a process where, instead of static designs, functional code is quickly created. Using AI tools like Lovable or Miro, he can create a usable prototype within a few hours that developers immediately process. This approach shortens the development cycle, reduces tension between teams, and emphasizes real functionality. [Elie offers concrete solutions.](https://adplist.substack.com/p/why-i-stopped-prototyping-in-figma)

> “Agile started as a movement driven by builders to «build better», but it is largely dominated now by people who don’t know how to build and look at agile as a management process style.”\
> **— [Patricia Aas](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7332341213074817024/)**

**Users no longer need to speak like computers.** According to Elizabeth Laraki, we are likely the last generation that had to learn to "speak computer" – use keywords, control interfaces, and adapt to machines. With the advent of conversational AI and agents, however, the paradigm is reversing: [technology is learning to understand us](https://elizlaraki.substack.com/p/the-last-generation-to-speak-computer). Interfaces no longer serve as translators between human and machine but recede into the background to allow natural human communication.

**Is humanness fading from life?** [Tomáš Svěrák reflects on Linkedin](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7330874921478193152/) about the article [Navigating by aliveness](https://ckarchive.com/b/zlughnhk8772ma7qrr9qehwzgng00f6) from the newsletter [The Imperfectionist](https://www.oliverburkeman.com/the-imperfectionist) by Oliver Burkeman. Tomáš wonders what happens when people find out an app was created by AI for them. Burkeman believes that even in the age of AI, it makes sense to decide based on what enhances our humanness, even if it sounds naive – that might just be the key to human authenticity.

**Honza Kvasnička presents an approach to analyzing the usability of websites and e-shops.** [In his presentation, he offers a process](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=X-b2TIZKeL4) from user testing, through documenting outputs, prioritizing tasks, to their implementation and impact evaluation. He pays special attention to collaboration between different roles in the team, such as designers, developers, and marketers. In conclusion, he shares the analysis procedure and highlights five key points to watch out for when optimizing a website or e-shop.

> “Good design is a signal that you care.”\
> **— [Guillermo Rauch](https://x.com/rauchg/status/1926188308280864959)**

**When to (not) joke in an error message** advises Lenka Stawarczyk. Every error demands a different tone. Lenka points out that humor has its place in error communication, but only when it's not about serious situations. The article distinguishes four types of errors based on risk level: from minor typos to security failures. The key is to consider the type of error, its impact, the hassle for the user, and the emotions it evokes. A joke can lighten the situation, but must not overshadow the message. When the problem is serious, [it's better to communicate calmly, clearly, and without jokes](https://www.lenkastawarczyk.com/blog/chyby-a-humor).

The easiest way to speed up web page loading is to reduce the data volume of images. New formats like [WebP and AVIF are already widely supported](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/advent-2401-obrazky) and can save a lot of data compared to the classic trio of JPEG, PNG, and GIF, without affecting visual quality, and they also handle transparency. ## To Understand Each Other

On Sunday, [Machal alerted us](https://bsky.app/profile/machal.bsky.social/post/3lpyt6s3qi22r) to the 15th anniversary of the term [responsive web design](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/responsive-design). Flexible page layouts that adapt to any device were already around before, but it was the arrival of `@media` queries that gave designers a tool for better control over element rendering based on viewport width.

You won't need it, says the principle [YAGNI](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/yagni), which guides programmers and which we designers have adopted. When colleagues start barking "yagni, yagni" at you during the next product demo, you know you're spending time inventing something unnecessary.

On the other hand, JTBD is an acronym that tells which jobs need to be addressed. It stands for the [jobs to be done](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/jtbd) framework, which helps uncover user needs during user research.

When searching for solutions, it's good to use [design patterns](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/design-patterns). Just like in football with set plays, penalty kicks, corner kicks, or throw-ins, we have standard situations in design too. Because users are familiar with solutions built on design patterns, it pays to consider them when designing.

William Hick formulated in the mid-20th century the discovery that decision time increases with the number of options. It's one of the basic rules of ergonomics, now known as [Hick's Law](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/hick-law). It puts pressure on us designers not to put users in front of complex choices.
