# Zeigarnik Effect
> The Zeigarnik effect is a psychological phenomenon where people remember unfinished or interrupted tasks better than completed ones. The effect creates mental tension that keeps these tasks in memory.

People often remember things they left unfinished and feel the need to return to them. Digital products leverage this—apps highlight unfilled forms, e-shops remind about abandoned shopping carts, and e-learning platforms work with incomplete lessons. The **Zeigarnik effect** ([Zeigarnik effect](https://lawsofux.com/zeigarnik-effect/)) helps motivate users to return and complete pending tasks.

This phenomenon was described by Lithuanian psychologist [Bluma Zeigarnik](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bljuma_Zeigarnikov%C3%A1) in the 1920s. She observed it by watching a waiter in a Berlin restaurant who perfectly remembered even the most complex orders, but once an order was completed and paid for, the waiter completely forgot both the order and the customer. From this, she deduced that unclosed tasks remain in memory until they are resolved.

In practice, this effect is applied in UX design, where apps and websites use notifications about unfinished processes. In e-commerce, personalized reminders about abandoned carts help increase completed orders. Content marketing builds on gradually published content and the principle of unfinished storytelling, as in *Arabian Nights*, which maintains audience interest and increases user return rates.

The Zeigarnik effect can be a very powerful tool for increasing user engagement and retention. However, it is important to use it sensitively. Too frequent reminders or deliberately unfinished processes can frustrate users and lead to their departure. Proper use of this principle helps improve user experience, support loyalty, and increase the effectiveness of digital products.
