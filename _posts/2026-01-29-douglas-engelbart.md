# Douglas Engelbart: visionary with the mouse
> American engineer who did not strive to invent individual devices, but to systematically enhance human ability to think, collaborate, and solve complex problems using computers.

![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/e4a84286-2d26-493d-8c21-a834d2ea19c6_682x447.webp "682x447")

“…augmentation may well become the single most important factor that will increase intellectual wealth all across the board.” — [Douglas Engelbart](https://blogofcollectiveintelligence.com/2013/07/04/394/#:~:text=augmentation%20may%20well%20become%20the%20single%20most%20important%20factor%20that%20will%20increase%20intellectual%20wealth%20all%20across%20the%20board)

*[Douglas Engelbart](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Douglas_Engelbart)* (January 30, 1925—July 2, 2013) is known as the inventor of the computer mouse. However, this simplifies and obscures the essence of his work. Engelbart's real goal was not new interfaces or technical [implementation](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/vyfikundace), but transforming the way people think and collaborate in an environment of growing world complexity. According to him, computers should not replace humans, but expand their intellect.

He summarized his approach as early as 1962 in the report *[Augmenting Human Intellect](https://www.dougengelbart.org/pubs/augment-3906.html)*. In it, he argued that society's most valuable resource is its ability to solve problems. And this ability can be deliberately increased by connecting people, language, work methods, and interactive tools. He saw the computer as an amplifier of human thinking, not as an automaton for ready-made solutions.

At the *[Augmentation Research Center](https://www.sri.com/)* in the research institute *[SRI](https://www.sri.com/)*, he put this idea into practice. The *[NLS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NLS_(computer_system))* system was created, which already in the 1960s allowed work with on-screen text, structured documents, links, shared editing, and remote collaboration. The computer mouse was only one of its components, designed to control this entire environment.

The public saw Engelbart's vision in 1968 during the famous presentation known as *[Mother of All Demos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mother_of_All_Demos)*. Nevertheless, he remained on the sidelines of the mainstream for most of his life, which preferred simplicity and mass appeal. Towards the end of his life, he spoke about collective IQ, or the ability of groups to think and act together. It is in this unfinished ambition that Engelbart's lasting legacy lies.
