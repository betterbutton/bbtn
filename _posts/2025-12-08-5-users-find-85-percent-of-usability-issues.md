# Five Users Find 85% of Issues
> The 'five-user rule' captures the essence of early usability testing but fails as a universal maxim.

> *You are opening the eighth window of the BBtn Advent Calendar for 2025. In this year's advent series, we look at popular platitudes of UX designers. Today's is: Five Users Find 85% of Issues.*

The claim that testing with five participants will uncover 85% of usability problems is one of the most frequently cited statements in the world of UX. It originated in the 1990s when *Jakob Nielsen* and *Thomas Landauer* [created a mathematical model](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/169059.169166) showing that the yield of testing decreases with each additional user. If each has roughly a one-third chance of encountering a given problem, five participants indeed uncover about 85% of all issues.

The model made sense in small, carefully controlled studies—but its simplicity turned into a slogan. Nielsen's 2000 article *[Why You Only Need to Test with 5 Users](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/why-you-only-need-to-test-with-5-users/)* transformed a statistical observation into a cultural mantra. For teams having to justify time and budget, it sounded like a liberation: testing could be fast, cheap, and effective.

Subsequent research, however, has shown that [the reality is more complex](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/200553186_Testing_web_sites_five_users_is_nowhere_near_enough). *Jared Spool* and *Carolyn Schroeder* found that five users on large e-commerce sites discovered only about a third of problems. *Laura Faulkner* described how [the result depends on chance](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/9032126_Beyond_the_five-user_assumption_Benefits_of_increased_sample_sizes_in_usability_testing)—a group of five can uncover between 55% and 99% of errors. *Alan Woolrych* and *Gilbert Cockton* [criticized the original model for its unrealistic assumption](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/200553185_Why_and_when_five_test_users_aren't_enough) that all problems have an equal probability of being discovered.

[Later studies](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/242156700_Determining_Usability_Test_Sample_Size) and [practical work](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/13515351-quantifying-the-user-experience) have added that five users may be suitable for early, formative tests—not for measuring quantitative metrics or testing multiple target groups.

Today's consensus is: five users are enough to find most common errors in a simple flow—ideal for quick, exploratory tests—but it's insufficient for measuring success rates, accessibility, or products with multiple target audiences.

The five-user rule still carries an important message: start early, learn fast, iterate often. Only the numerical certainty of 85% belongs to the past. A better summary for today's web teams sounds like: a few users are enough to get you started understanding, never enough to finish.
