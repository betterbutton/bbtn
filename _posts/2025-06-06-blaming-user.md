# Blaming the User
> The term 'user error' masks flaws in system design, not actual failures on the part of the user.

When something doesn't work, the first reaction is often to blame the user ([blaming the user](https://medium.com/@melissaperri/stop-blaming-the-user-85dcfcd8f327)). We tell ourselves they did something wrong, that they should have read the instructions or tried harder. But every time someone fails to complete a form, clicks the wrong thing, or simply doesn't understand what to do, it's not their fault. It's a signal that our design has failed.

Sometimes we accompany the label [user error](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_error) with user disparagement. In Czech, the term [BFU](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/BFU) is used, which usually doesn't stand for *běžný Franta uživatel* (average Joe user), but something much spicier. In English, you might hear mocking acronyms like [IBM user, ID10T, or Code 18](https://eu.usatoday.com/story/tech/columnist/komando/2022/04/14/unflattering-codewords-and-acronyms-tech-pros-use-describe-you/9525369002/).

In the field of user experience and digital interface design, the blame is rarely on the user. The problem is usually in how we designed the system. It's not about clumsiness or inattention. Often, clear guidance or feedback is missing, or the entire system is overly complex.

Designers and developers should think differently. They should ask if the next action was obvious. If it was clear what happened when the user did something. And most importantly, if the system works for people who don't have ideal technical skills or use different devices.

As soon as we say "the user didn't understand," we stop looking for what we can improve. And that's the mistake. Because good design accounts for people making mistakes. And it helps them correct those mistakes easily.

Blaming the user means wasting a chance. Instead, we should look for how to make our system more understandable, accessible, and welcoming. Not for perfect users, but for real people.
