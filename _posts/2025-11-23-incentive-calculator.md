# The User Research Incentive Calculator will propose a reward for user research respondents
> A data-backed tool that helps UX researchers establish fair, transparent, and market-appropriate compensation for research participants.

![Screenshot of the reward calculator for user research participants](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/7e88d974-8f64-47a0-a555-6f2cac9b028d_1878x845.png "1456x655")

[Reward Calculator](https://www.userinterviews.com/lp/ux-research-incentive-calculator) from *User Interviews* offers quick and reliable recommendations on how much to pay research participants. It is based on [data from nearly twenty thousand completed studies](https://www.userinterviews.com/blog/research-incentives-report) from 2022 and suggests compensation amounts based on audience type, research format, duration, and participation method. According to last year's survey by Tremendous, [compensation increased](https://www.tremendous.com/blog/ux-research-incentives-charts/) by about 20% compared to 2022. This year's data is not yet available.

An hour-long remote interview with a typical consumer usually corresponds to an amount around eighty dollars, while in-person sessions range from ninety to one hundred twenty dollars, with professional participants receiving more. If you are targeting the Czech market, you are probably used to paying respondents roughly half that amount, so the calculator will be most useful for international research in developed countries.

A [similar calculator is offered by Ethnio](https://ethn.io/incentives/calculator). It allows you to select a target country for research, so for a similar hourly session, it suggests compensation in the range from seven hundred fifty to fifteen hundred koruna. [For survey research](https://www.prolific.com/resources/how-much-should-you-pay-research-participants), Prolific recommends paying at least 250 korun in conversion. Some researchers recommend a simple rule: pay as much as a researcher typically invoices for their work.

Researchers use the calculator to avoid guesswork and justify budgets with data. Just enter a few details – participant type, moderation, meeting format, study format, and duration – and the tool returns recommended minimum, maximum, and average values based on the most common payments for similar projects. This allows research teams to maintain consistency and avoid unethically low compensation that could skew results or deter participants.

However, the calculator's benefits extend beyond mere numbers. Transparent, data-backed compensation settings reinforce fairness and respect for participants – core principles of ethical research. It also serves as an educational tool, reminding that an hour of a participant's time should have comparable value to an hour of a researcher's work. And even though the data is based on the US market, the logic remains universal: European or Czech teams can easily adjust amounts based on local wages and cost of living.

In a field that often relies on intuition, the compensation calculator provides rare certainty – a clear, data-driven compass for establishing fair and justifiable compensation for research participants.
