# 
> 

#### 🛠️ Single-purpose applications

Applications that did **one thing well and fast**. No need for registration, no tutorials, no animated onboarding. You launched and used them. There was no "learning curve" because there was nothing to learn. And yet they were habit-forming because they did exactly what they were supposed to. Today we have more all-in-one monsters and SaaS labyrinths, but maybe we could remind ourselves of the beauty of single-purpose tools.

#### 🗂️ Dual-pane file manager

Total Commander, muCommander, Krusader, Norton Commander… Legends that changed the way we work with files. Two windows, with clear operations between them. No drag & drop with the mouse, no unnecessary gestures – just fast keyboard shortcuts, focused work, and the **flow** that often desperately lacks in today's visual interfaces.

#### 🧾 Text mode

Plain text, no unnecessary formatting. Information density that is hard to find on today's cards, tiles, layouts, and whitespace. 80x25 or 132x43 – and you had everything important in front of you. **No distractions.** Imagine if today's apps could be this "dense" without losing clarity.

#### 🎨 AAS / ASCII art / ANSI style

Design within strict constraints – 16 colors, 256 characters, no images. And yet so many possibilities for expression. A visual language that had style, played with typography and contrast, and was **extremely efficient**. Maybe it's time to look again at what can be designed using just text and colors.

#### ⚡ Speed and responsiveness

Applications started instantly. You didn't wait for them to boot. Press → reaction. **Fast feedback**, no skeletons, no "loading..." animations as an excuse. This is what we should demand today – not just for UX, but also for ecology and focus.

#### ⌨️ Productivity via keyboard

Want to do something? Alt + F5. Move something? F6. Access everything without clicking. **Working without a mouse was often faster**, more precise, and less tiring. Today, keyboard shortcuts are often hidden or don't exist at all. Yet they are key to deeper control of a tool – and to the state where the tool becomes an extension of your thought.
