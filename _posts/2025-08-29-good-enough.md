# Good Enough
> In design and development, 'good enough' means hitting the target without overshooting – it's a deliberate strategy, not laziness.

Good enough ([good enough](https://articles.ux-primer.com/why-good-enough-ux-beats-perfect-ux-every-time-91c5d9bbee12)) is not a sign of complacency, but a conscious and smart approach to building digital products. It means solving the core problem, fulfilling user needs, and resisting the temptation of constant improvement. For web and app creators, this very approach can be the key to a successful launch – instead of endless postponement.

We encounter similar ideas often. The Pareto 80/20 rule states that the majority of results come from a minority of effort. The [KISS principle](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/kiss) encourages simplification. And the saying *"perfection is the enemy of good"* reminds us that in the end, an excessive drive for [perfectionism can be harmful](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/satisficing/).

It works in practice. Gmail was in beta for years. Basecamp gained trust by doing fewer things, but doing them properly. Figma succeeded without trying to mimic Photoshop's complexity. The key was to deliver value quickly and clearly – not everything perfectly.

Users mostly don't want tricks. They want [things to work](https://uxdesign.cc/how-to-embrace-the-good-enough-7d4764a123a8). A login that doesn't frustrate. A button that's clickable. A page that loads immediately. That's the *good enough* that really works.

It's not about cutting corners. It's about intention. About the ability to know when to stop. And when you master that, you get further, faster – and often with a better result. You might know this feeling: *"We're never going to need that feature anyway."* That's exactly what it's about.
