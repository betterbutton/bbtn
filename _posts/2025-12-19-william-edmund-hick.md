# William Edmund Hick Laid the Foundations of Human-Centered Design
> A British experimental psychologist who demonstrated that human decision-making has measurable limits, thereby establishing the cognitive foundation for modern human-centered design.

![W. E. Hick during one of his experiments (drawing based on a contemporary photograph)](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/a4d8292e-9635-430a-9b10-3ab20c7a3574_800x533.webp "570x379")

Hick's Law has a logarithmic form because people divide the total set of options into categories and, at each step, eliminate approximately half of the remaining options, rather than considering each option individually, which would require linear time.

*[William Edmund Hick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W._E._Hick)* is most commonly remembered today as the author of [Hick's Law](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/hick-law). In the field of UX and interface design, this law is cited as an explanation for why a large number of choices slows users down. However, the essence of Hick's contribution was not to devise rules for design. His goal was to show that decision time grows with the amount of information a person must process. It is precisely this insight that remains crucial for human-centered design today, as it stems from human cognitive capabilities and limitations.

Hick formulated his most influential conclusions in 1952 while working at the *Applied Psychology Unit* in Cambridge. Based on information theory, he proved that reaction time grows logarithmically with the number of equally probable choices. This relationship is usually written as RT = a + b · log2(N).

Hick thus described decision-making as an information processing process in a system with limited capacity. For designers, this means the possibility to consider uncertainty, complexity, and the cost of decisions systematically and measurably.

In current practice, Hick's Law is often oversimplified. Hick did not claim that interfaces should have as few options as possible. He did not study finished products or experienced users. His experiments were conducted under controlled conditions and with uniform probabilities. The true significance of his work lies in showing that cognitive limits can be described and modeled.

Hick did not live to see the era of personal computers or UX as a field. He left behind no interviews or popular texts. Yet his influence is fundamental. The idea that systems must respect the limited capacity of human decision-making is among the solid foundations of human-centered design.
