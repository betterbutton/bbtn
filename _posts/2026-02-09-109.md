## Present Your ID

Age verification has moved from a symbolic checkbox to a technical requirement. Legislators in the EU, United Kingdom, Australia, and parts of the USA are forcing web creators to solve a difficult question: how to protect children without turning the internet into a mandatory identification checkpoint.

In major jurisdictions, regulators consider age verification via a simple user declaration to be non-functional and expect demonstrable and auditable measures. The change doesn't just affect pornography, but the very architecture of the website, user experience, and data handling.

Several prerequisites have come together: the most fundamental is technological readiness for such a step. Another prerequisite is an established legal framework that allows for technically precise age verification to be required. Last but not least, there is growing evidence from monitoring internet user behavior, showing how people circumvent existing verification.

In the European Union, the *[Digital Services Act](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2022/2065/oj/eng)* frames the protection of minors as a systemic obligation. Concurrently, the *[European Digital Identity Wallet](https://ec.europa.eu/digital-building-blocks/sites/spaces/EUDIGITALIDENTITYWALLET/pages/694487738/EU+Digital+Identity+Wallet+Home)* indicates a direction for age verification *"I am 18+"* without blanket identification – essentially, your electronic ID does not transfer identity, but only the attribute of adulthood. However – who all finds out where you verified your age?

The United Kingdom is proceeding more forcefully: according to *Ofcom* rules, pornographic services must implement highly effective verification, with real oversight and published results.

Australia already prohibits social media accounts for people under 16 and shifts responsibility to platforms. France is testing privacy-friendly models that separate age verification from content access itself. The USA remains fragmented – shaped by state laws and judicial signals.

Technically, the field is divided into three approaches. Verification uses authoritative sources and brings high certainty at the cost of friction and risks of handling sensitive data. Age estimation, often biometric, lowers barriers but carries bias, error rates, and accessibility issues. The credential-based approach tries to prove only the age attribute and minimize data – assuming interoperable and trustworthy management.

British data shows the trade-offs: after implementing checks, traffic to major websites dropped, while VPN usage increased. Gateways change behavior, but also shift it.

![Graph showing a drop in visits to porn sites from Great Britain, and at the same time a huge increase in VPN usage in Britain.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/464c9a43-a5ec-4d1f-bc41-460035a7ceb7_638x905.png "316x448")

Brits stopped watching porn, but started using VPN. ([The Economist](https://www.economist.com/business/2025/12/10/from%C2%AD%E2%80%91social%C2%AD%E2%80%91media%C2%AD%E2%80%91to%C2%AD%E2%80%91porn%C2%AD%E2%80%91age%C2%AD%E2%80%91checks%C2%AD%E2%80%91are%C2%AD%E2%80%91spreading%C2%AD%E2%80%91across%C2%AD%E2%80%91the%C2%AD%E2%80%91web))

The pragmatic path rejects false dilemmas. Protecting children does not require blanket identification. A good solution is proportionate, data-sparing, accessible, and auditable – and skeptical of both biometrics and the *"upload your ID to every website"* approach. Age verification is becoming web infrastructure; whether it undermines or instead preserves user dignity depends on how we design it today.

## Linkodrome

**How Uber shares insights:** *Uber* once created the *Kaleidoscope* platform as a [tool for sharing and utilizing insights](https://medium.com/uber%C2%AD%E2%80%91design/the%C2%AD%E2%80%91power%C2%AD%E2%80%91of%C2%AD%E2%80%91insights%C2%AD%E2%80%91a%C2%AD%E2%80%91behind%C2%AD%E2%80%91the%C2%AD%E2%80%91scenes%C2%AD%E2%80%91look%C2%AD%E2%80%91at%C2%AD%E2%80%91the%C2%AD%E2%80%91new%C2%AD%E2%80%91insights%C2%AD%E2%80%91platform%C2%AD%E2%80%91at%C2%AD%E2%80%91uber%C2%AD%E2%80%9126f85becc2e6) across the company. The goal was to connect people, processes, and tools for effective decision-making. Kaleidoscope builds on a unified taxonomy of insights, respects the needs of different users, and connects existing systems instead of replacing them. It emphasizes context, collaboration, and the visible impact of insight work.

**Sound as the missing layer of web design.** Sound can significantly improve interface perception. *Raphael Salaja* warns against its poor use – loud autoplay, inappropriate effects, or lack of choice. [Sound can offer faster and more emotional feedback](https://www.userinterface.wiki/sounds%C2%AD%E2%80%91on%C2%AD%E2%80%91the%C2%AD%E2%80%91web) than visuals, thereby increasing both understandability and satisfaction from interaction. Sound must be used sparingly, with respect for user preferences.

**How designers think:** *Anthony Hobday* argues that design is often more work for a curator than a creator, that good design isn't always commercially successful, and that truly high-quality software is more likely created in small teams. All his observations are united by an effort to understand that [people perceive interfaces not only visually, but also culturally and emotionally](https://anthonyhobday.com/blog/20250515.html).

**ChatGPT as a mirror of the past.** *Josef Šlerka* offers a critical view of large language models (LLMs) like ChatGPT, which do not create a new world but conserve the ideology of the past – specifically technocratic liberalism and middle-class "decency." It's not just a tool, but a cultural infrastructure that simulates competence and neutrality, while flattening conflicts and supporting the status quo. LLMs are not objective intelligence, but a statistical mirror of the dominant culture. This gives rise to a new class of *AI overseers* – not creators, but those who [filter, edit, and control its outputs](https://reportermagazin.cz/101745/co%C2%AD%E2%80%91kdyz%C2%AD%E2%80%91je%C2%AD%E2%80%91chatgpt%C2%AD%E2%80%91iluzi%C2%AD%E2%80%91minulosti%C2%AD%E2%80%91vzdyt%C2%AD%E2%80%91je%C2%AD%E2%80%91napomocny%C2%AD%E2%80%91neskodny%C2%AD%E2%80%91a%C2%AD%E2%80%91uprimny/).

*[George A. Miller](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/george-miller)* was an American cognitive psychologist who changed the way we perceive human limits in design. He is known for the [magical number 7±2](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/millers-law), and brought deeper insight: people are not passive recipients, but active processors of information. He emphasized that design must respect how people think, remember, and understand the world – through grouping, language, and context. His approach gave rise to human-centered design not only as a scientific discipline but also as an ethical approach to creating interfaces and services.

*[KPI](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/kpi)* (key performance indicators) are not just numbers, but tools meant to serve decision-making. The difference between a metric and a KPI lies in its connection to a specific goal – only then do data have meaning. An effective KPI system is economical, with clearly defined indicators and responsibilities. Only well-designed KPIs help orient, decide where to act, and where to stay away.

*[Negative space](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/white-space)*, often mistakenly perceived as empty space, is a tool that in UI design determines visual hierarchy, reduces cognitive load, and improves both clarity and accessibility. The perception of proximity between elements helps users quickly understand relationships without needing additional visual cues.

*[Design Spells](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/design-spells)* collect small but significant moments in digital design – animations, micro-interactions, and details that create emotion instead of performance. It is a living archive of joy that arises where technical precision meets a feel for detail.
