## Linkodrome

**Proven three-day process for user testing** shared by designer *Riikka Iivanainen*. From planning, through testing with pilot users, to analysis of results. On the first day, Riika dedicates to preparation and launch of the pilot test. The second day, the main test takes place. And the third day analyzes and summarizes findings. The result is [quick and effective feedback](https://medium.com/design-bootcamp/run-a-full-user-test-faster-than-your-team-can-schedule-a-meeting-0f0937a35068), which allows iteration without unnecessary losses.

**How capable is the company to learn?** *Erika Hall* comes with a simple but eloquent metric: [Continuous Learning Quotient](https://medium.com/mule-design/is-fear-a-factor-37b9d8104980) (CLQ). Measures the percentage of people in the company who feel safe to say 'I don't know' to anyone and at any time. Erika points out that fear of incompetence blocks openness and prevents quality decision-making. In an environment where instead of asking questions, knowledge is only pretended, there is no room for real learning.

**UX strategy is not a plan or a goal, but a path.** *Vitaly Friedman* describes the differences between goal, strategy, and plan. Shows how UX strategy fits into the context of product and business strategy. Strong UX strategy is based on research, defines goals, priorities, risks, and value for users and business. Vitaly offers an overview of six key components of strategy: target state, user segments, priorities, key actions, feasibility, and risks. Successful [UX strategy speaks the language of business](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/practical-guide-ux-strategy-vitaly-friedman-sbjze/) – not just design.

**DesignOps is at the center of attention for companies that perceive design as a competitive advantage.** According to *Stuti Mazumdar*, they strive to create conditions where design can thrive even at scale – without unnecessary friction and loss of focus. They solve team scaling, complex tools, metric tracking, and talent development. Although they operate operationally, their goal is to protect creativity and connect it with business goals. [DesignOps bring system into chaos](https://think.design/blog/rise-of-design-operations-creative-process-meet-business-efficiency/), and thus allow good design not only to be created but also to deliver value faster, more consistently, and more measurably.

**10 basic concepts that explain why and how to measure** according to *John Cutler*. Metrics help us reduce uncertainty, track progress, and make informed decisions. John distinguishes between KPIs, metrics, and measurement. Explains the difference between inputs and outputs. Points out the importance of models (e.g., trees, loops), their adaptation, and stability over time. Important is first to [create a persistent model](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1205M-IyZayiCIGDh9YgEjuT-RsbLOvQ9dWWfuW0Pu6Y/edit?tab=t.0), derive goals from it, and only then define specific hypotheses or experiments.

**A series of guides and recommendations for icon creation** from icon designers from the Streamline library. Authors focus on practicality, optical accuracy, and consistency. There is also a selection of the 12 best books on icon design. The goal is to [improve not only the quality of icons but also their role in visual communication](https://blog.streamlinehq.com/tag/icons/) and graphic design.

## Otto Neurath and his systematic approach to information visualization

![Otto Neurath – drawing generated from a photograph.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/a4eaf84c-9d61-4b96-8348-cff3d9932ee1_1536x1024.webp "1456x971")

“Words divide, images unite,” — Otto Neurath

*[Otto Neurath](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/otto-neurath)*, though a sociologist, influenced the way we work with data visualization today with his approach to visual communication. In the 1920s in Vienna, he developed with Maria Neurath and Gerd Arntz the Isotype system – International System of Typographic Picture Education. Simplified complex data into accessible pictograms. Neurath observed real users and iterated based on their understanding, thus anticipating current UX design methods. His belief that *'design serves people, not aesthetics'* is a challenge and inspiration for contemporary designers.

## To understand each other

[Roadmap](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/roadmap) is not a list of deliveries or a schedule, but a tool for aligning vision, strategy, and team learning. Shows direction based on impact, not deadlines, and connects vision with current steps. Instead of fixed dates, it works with horizons now – soon – later. Acknowledges uncertainty and supports adaptation. Its strength lies in regular updates based on new insights.

[Selection bias](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/selection-bias) is a subtle but serious error where people included in research do not represent the entire target group. Can arise from self-selection, participant dropout, or from observing only those who still use the product. The error creates an illusion of accuracy but distorts reality.

[Spinner](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/spinner) is an animated element that indicates that the system is working – bridges moments of uncertainty and strengthens user confidence. Suitable for short and unpredictable operations, but with longer waiting, it loses effect and can give a frustrating impression. In such cases, it is better to use a progress indicator or a so-called skeleton screen.

[Principle figure-ground](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/figure-ground) explains how people distinguish what is main and what is supportive in the visual field. In design, it determines what attracts attention and what recedes into the background. Contrast, bordering, or dimming the surroundings help users focus and act with confidence. If the figure-ground relationship fails, chaos arises. Properly designed interfaces use this principle across elements – from buttons to modal windows – so that interactions feel natural, understandable, and smoothly guide attention where needed.
