# Mental Models: Why People Develop Superstitions When Using Technology
> 

This is probably the oldest text we have linked to from Jednohubky so far. It is also likely the first link to the `.mil` domain of the US Army. When Donald Arthur Norman concluded the search for the causes of the nuclear power plant accident on Three Mile Island, he continued at the beginning of the 1980s with a project for the Office of Naval Research on the topic of mental models. The project resulted in an eight-page report [Some Observations on Mental Models](http://Some%20Observations%20on%20Mental%20Models) (PDF, 2 MB).

![First page of Donald Norman's research report on Mental Models.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/a9bf4f8a-c0f8-48c3-ac76-b3634ea839da_711x923.png "433x562")

Norman examined the mental models that people develop when using technology. He found that these models are often incomplete, unstable, and superstitious – users adopt unnecessary rituals and superstitions to feel certain. For example, when working with a calculator, users often repeatedly press the `CLEAR` button to be sure they have reset the device, even though it is not necessary.

Mental models thus often emphasize physical action over thinking. This shows that people often sacrifice accuracy and efficiency for the sake of certainty and simplicity.

Norman was already working with the term conceptual model in this report as well. This is the model on which the design is based. The project was created for training sailors and pilots, so Norman dealt with how to easily translate the conceptual model into the correct mental model.

According to him, for every design we must ensure that it is:

- learnable
- functional and
- usable

Only in such a case will users develop mental models that align with the conceptual one.

The term mental model probably originates from the work of Scottish psychologist Kenneth Craik, who in 1943 published the book [The Nature of Exploration](https://archive.org/details/natureofexplanat0000crai/page/n139/mode/2up). If you don't feel like reading old books and research reports, you can get [basic knowledge about the mental model](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/book/the-glossary-of-human-computer-interaction/mental-models?srsltid=AfmBOoqjGMdXru-iUIFPAiRggntT3Raj_Su1D_z7wWYUrganDvN5RAsv) from the dictionary entry on the Interaction Design Foundation.
