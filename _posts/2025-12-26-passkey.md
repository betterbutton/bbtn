# Access Key
> A modern login method that replaces passwords with cryptographic keys and enables secure verification without shared secrets, phishing, or credential reuse.

[Access Keys](https://digitalsecurityguide.eset.com/cz/jsou-uz-hesla-pase-co-jsou-passkeys-a-jake-jsou-jejich-vyhody) ([passkeys](https://www.passkeycentral.org/home)) are quickly becoming the most significant change in web login in decades. They replace passwords with a public-key cryptography model. Users can authenticate without typing a password and without sending anything that could be exploited. This means less phishing, fewer account takeovers, and significantly smoother logins.

The fundamental principle of an access key is a key pair. The website stores only the public key, while the private key remains securely stored on the user's device or in their access key manager. During login, the website sends a one-time challenge, and the device signs it with the private key. The website verifies the result using the public key. No reusable secret is transmitted, and there's nothing valuable for an attacker in the website's database.

This makes access keys inherently phishing-resistant. Authentication is cryptographically bound to a specific website, so a fake page cannot deceive the user. Biometrics or PIN are only used to unlock the key on the device – they are not shared with the website and are not a security factor in themselves.

Access keys are not a panacea. They won't protect against malware on a compromised device, nor do they replace careful session management and account recovery. However, they eliminate the most common and costly attacks associated with passwords, including phishing and mass exploitation of leaked credentials.

For most websites, a gradual transition makes sense. Access keys should first be offered as an optional feature after login or in account settings. Once users adopt them across devices, they become the natural choice – and passwords gradually fade into the background.
