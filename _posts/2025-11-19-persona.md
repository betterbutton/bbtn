# Persona
> A UX persona is a fictional archetype based on real user data – it helps make better design decisions by representing patterns of behavior, not assumptions.

[Personas](https://kisk.phil.muni.cz/100metod/persony) ([personas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persona_(user_experience))) represent hypothetical, but research-backed, user types. They originated in the 1980s thanks to *[Alan Cooper](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/alan-cooper)* and were methodically developed by *[Kim Goodwin](https://www.linkedin.com/in/kimgoodwin/)*. They serve as a tool that allows one to view the product through the eyes of real users. Instead of designing for everyone, designers ask: *“Does this help Peter, our primary persona, achieve his goal?”*

[Personas](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/personas) help avoid designing for the *“elastic user.”* This is a vague figure that changes according to need. Thanks to personas, we build empathy, avoid self-referential design, and align progress across the team. A [persona](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/persona/) includes goals, behavior, frustrations, context, and a human dimension – it gives concrete form to abstract data.

![Meme comparing demographic data about King Charles III and Ozzy Osbourne. Both share the same characteristics, despite being completely different.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/5e0da0b1-60d1-42fd-92a2-62ce51e24175_800x1000.webp "418x522")

A persona does not stand on demographics, but on behavioral dimensions.

How to do it? First, collect qualitative data (interviews, observations), track patterns of behavior (not just demographics), group similar users, and create representative archetypes from them. A good persona is research-backed, targeted, understandable, and regularly updated. You will use it in design, testing, and priority decisions.

What to watch out for: don't create personas without research, don't include unnecessary details, don't confuse them with marketing profiles, and don't let them become outdated. Personas do not replace contact with users, but they facilitate transferring their needs and expectations into every design decision. When based on solid data, they help create websites that people actually want to use.
