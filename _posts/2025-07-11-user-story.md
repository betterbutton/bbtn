# User Story
> A user story explains the meaning and structure of user needs and emphasizes their role in aligning software development with real user requirements.

![User Story: As a (type of user) I want (some goal) So that I can (some action).](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/e20ef3ea-ab0f-48f6-8c1c-04a5f6d30fd0_1536x1024.webp "1456x971")

User stories ([user stories](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/user-stories)) are one of the key tools in agile development. They help both designers and programmers focus on creating features that truly deliver value to people. The story is written from the user's perspective and concisely answers three questions: *Who* is the user, *what* they want to do, and *why* it is important. For example: *"As a frequent traveler, I want to save my passport details so I can book flights faster."* This simple structure keeps development focused on specific needs.

By placing the user's intent at the center of attention, user stories guide us to create value. They are not detailed specifications – they leave room for collaboration and creativity during implementation. This allows teams to jointly find the best solutions.

User stories also improve planning and communication. They are clearly listed in the backlog, can be well estimated, and facilitate sprint planning. And because they are understandable across roles, they connect business requirements with technical implementation.

And most importantly – they foster empathy. They constantly remind us that every software element should help someone specifically solve a problem or make an activity easier.

In conclusion – user stories are not a formality. They are a practical and powerful tool that allows us to create software that truly matters.
