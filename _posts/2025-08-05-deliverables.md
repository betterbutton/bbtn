# Deliverables
> Design Outputs: Visual or textual materials that facilitate collaboration, clarify intent, and reduce risk during digital product development.

In an era of AI tools and so-called [vibe coding](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/vibe-coding), some teams skip classic design deliverables ([design deliverables](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/article/7-ux-deliverables-what-will-i-be-making-as-a-ux-designer)), such as [wireflows](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/wireflow) or Figma mockups, and jump straight to code. At first glance, this may seem efficient, but it often brings more problems than benefits. Deliverables exist to align the entire team on a shared vision *before* implementation begins.

Without artifacts, stakeholder feedback slows down and its integration becomes more costly. When ideas are immediately translated into code, it hinders the exploration of alternatives and can lock in poor decisions. While AI can quickly generate user interfaces, without human guidance it often merely repeats well-worn patterns. It doesn't provide anything that truly responds to specific user needs. Well-prepared deliverables create space for strategy and a thoughtful approach.

Common design deliverables include research findings, personas, user journeys, site maps, user flows, wireframes, visual mockups, interactive prototypes, style guides, specifications, and exported graphic assets. All of these serve to clarify what is being built, why it makes sense, and how to do it well.

Even in fast-paced teams, artifacts do not cause delays. On the contrary — they help prevent errors, facilitate communication, and ensure continuity. It's not about bureaucracy. It's about a quality safeguard.
