# Error Prevention
> Error prevention – a human-centered design principle that helps users avoid mistakes before they happen.

In human-centered design, we aim for human errors to ideally not occur at all. A well-designed system does not give the user the opportunity to make a mistake, and if one does occur, it helps them find their way back quickly and without unnecessary stress.

Instead of dealing with consequences, designers focus on how to prevent errors. This increases confidence, reduces confusion, and improves the overall user experience.

Errors can be divided into two main categories. The first consists of *slips* ([execution errors](https://www.bbc.co.uk/bitesize/guides/zg4j7ty/revision/2#:~:text=An%20execution%20error%20occurs%20when,been%20programmed%20to%20carry%20out.)) – for example, when someone accidentally clicks the wrong button. The second group represents *mistakes* (evaluation errors) – situations where the user misunderstands something and makes an error already in the decision-making phase. Each type of error requires a different approach. Sometimes a visual constraint is enough, other times simple and clear feedback helps.

Imagine a form that cannot be submitted until all fields are correctly filled out. Or a warning that stops you before deleting data. Or validation that alerts you in real-time to an error in an email address. These small things work unobtrusively but significantly contribute to the smoothness and certainty of using the system.

Errors are not made only by beginners. Even an experienced user can be tired, inattentive, or in a hurry. Smart design takes this into account and strives to be not only functional but also considerate.

Error prevention ([error prevention](https://www.zionandzion.com/user-error-prevention-and-recovery-in-ux/)) does not mean our designs are perfect. The point is in designing an environment that does not throw sticks under users' feet – but instead gently guides them in the right direction.
