# Peak-End Rule
> People don't remember experiences as a whole, but based on the most intense and final moment.

The **Peak-End Rule** ([peak-end rule](https://lawsofux.com/peak-end-rule/)) is a psychological principle stating that people evaluate experiences based on two key moments—the most emotionally charged, the *peak*, and the *end* itself. For web and app creators, this means even a perfectly designed process can be ruined by one confusing step—or, conversely, saved by a pleasant ending.

The rule was described by psychologist [Daniel Kahneman](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Kahneman) in the article *[When More Pain Is Preferred to Less: Adding a Better End](https://www.ius.uzh.ch/dam/jcr:5ae9adc9-61ec-4174-b37c-4b752f36c23b/Kahnemann%20et%20al.%20-%20When%20More%20Pain%20is%20Preferred%20to%20Less%20(1993).pdf)*. It explains why users don't remember every step, but recall precisely the strongest and last moment. Memory doesn't calculate the average impression of an experience. It retains what stood out—and how it ended.

In practice, it can look like this: a nice confirmation message or an elegant conclusion to a process can leave a positive feeling, even if a minor hiccup occurred earlier. Conversely, a final error or unpleasant notification can erase all previous impressions.

To leverage the peak-end rule to your advantage, design for strong, pleasant moments—and end the journey clearly, kindly, and without complications. It could be a small animation, a simple confirmation, or a friendly message that makes sense.

At the end of the day, people won't remember every click and finger movement. What stays in their head is the best part—and the absolute end.
