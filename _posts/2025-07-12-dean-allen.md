# Dean Allen – typographer, developer, and blogger
> A visionary who transformed web publishing through comprehensible markup language and elegant design, always on the side of content creators.

![Dean Allen (generated drawing based on photos)](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/8a2fd93c-0dea-48de-be31-b9d1b674befa_1536x1024.webp "1456x971")

Dean Allen, typographer

[Dean Cameron Allen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dean_Allen) (June 2, 1966—January 13, 2018) was among the people who fundamentally influenced the shape of writing and publishing on the internet. He was the creator of the markup language [Textile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Textile_(markup_language) and the publishing system [Textpattern](https://textpattern.com/). Two tools that allowed authors to focus on content instead of technical details.

His approach inspired the development of other markup languages and blogging platforms. He is referenced by John Gruber, author of the popular language [Markdown](https://daringfireball.net/projects/markdown/), and Matt Mullenweg, creator of the editorial system [WordPress](https://ma.tt/2003/04/texturize-finished/). The latter [wrote](https://ma.tt/2018/01/r-i-p-dean/) about Dean Allen: *“Everything he touched, he did with care and craftsmanship, which inspires me to this day.”*.

> “How can you design for the web if you can’t code? How can you direct photography if you’ve never worked in a darkroom? How can you design text if you’re not a careful reader?”\
> — **Dean Allen**, [Reading Design](https://alistapart.com/article/readingdesign/)

Dean Allen created Textile in 2002. It was a way to convert plain text into properly formatted HTML output without the author needing to know complex syntax. The tool was adopted in numerous services. It was followed by the editorial system Textpattern, which Dean Allen designed to support authors and not distract them from writing. His motto was simple: *“Just write.”*

Before that, Dean Allen became known for his blogs [Textism](https://web.archive.org/web/20030203222323/http://www.textism.com/) and [Cardigan Industries](https://web.archive.org/web/20010205000100/http://cardigan.com/). He wrote with ease, irony, and a feel for language and graphic layout. He was a pioneer of an era when the web was not just a technical medium but a space for style and communication.

This Canadian typographer and developer spent the last years of his life primarily in France. He died in 2018 in London. However, his legacy persists—in the quality with which we read and write on the web today, in the languages we use, and in the conviction that technology should serve the written word, not complicate it.
