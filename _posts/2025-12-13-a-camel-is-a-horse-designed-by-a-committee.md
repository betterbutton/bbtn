# A camel is a horse designed by a committee
> A metaphor describing how pressure for consensus transforms a clear design into a disjointed whole.

> *You are opening the thirteenth window of the BBtn Advent Calendar for 2025. This year's advent series looks at popular UX designer proverbs. Today's is:* *One good test is worth more than a thousand expert opinions.*

In the world of digital products, it is often said that *[a camel is a horse designed by a committee](https://quoteinvestigator.com/2023/09/22/horse-camel/)*. It’s a fitting description of a situation where a design stops being guided by people's needs and starts fulfilling the wishes of individual departments. The result is often slow pages, a fragmented interface, and elements that only make sense inside the organization but not to the actual users of the website.

The problem doesn't arise from an unwillingness to collaborate. Committees are formed to gather diverse perspectives. Their nature, however, leads to a shift in focus from a clear goal to compromise. Marketing wants more space, sales wants more calls to action, legal wants additional disclosures, and someone from management pushes a color change. Each adjustment seems minor. But when they all add up, they dilute the original purpose and the design loses its logic.

The decisive moment comes when collaboration turns into a quest for consensus. Collaboration gives people a chance to express themselves. But a committee turns every opinion into a vote with the same weight as research findings or testing results. Decisions then rely on impressions. The strongest voice or the least risky option prevails. Yet prototypes and measurements have one common message. Users get lost in such a design, even if every detail is justified.

The solution doesn't lie in the designer working alone. An effective path leads through a team that bases decisions on evidence. Clear leadership, early-stage research, open criteria, and rapid design verification return it to its essence. People continue to contribute to it, but in an environment that protects its coherence. Only then does a camel stop being created, and a horse—the one you intended from the start—begins to take shape.
