# Dark Patterns
> Dark patterns are design patterns for user interfaces that subtly influence user decisions and undermine their free and informed choice.

[Dark Patterns](https://medium.com/@lenka.stawarczyk/temn%C3%A9-vzory-v-marketingu-stoj%C3%AD-n%C3%A1m-to-za-to-62330c2b1062) ([dark patterns](https://www.deceptive.design/)) are user interface design practices that significantly impair or undermine users' ability to make free, informed, and autonomous decisions. The decisive criterion here is not the intent of the design author, but the foreseeable effect of the interface on user behavior and choices.

The term was coined in 2010 by *[Harry Brignull](https://www.linkedin.com/in/harrybrignull/)* in UX discourse as a critique of manipulative practices—such as hidden costs, forced continuity, or misleading confirmations. Studies have shown that unethical practices are not marginal: they are widespread, structurally embedded, and measurably influence decisions, especially in contexts related to consent, subscriptions, pricing, and account cancellation.

As a result, dark patterns have moved from the realm of professional ethics to the level of law and public policy. In the European Union, manipulative interface design is explicitly addressed in platform regulations, particularly in the *Digital Services Act*, which restricts practices that substantially distort user decisions in [Article 25](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/CS/TXT/HTML/?uri=CELEX:32022R2065#art_25).

Across jurisdictions and taxonomies, the same structural features recur: asymmetric choice architecture, selective friction, delayed or obscured disclosure of material information, emotionally charged framing, and interventions in the interface through visual hierarchy or interaction mechanisms.

Screens, flows, and default settings are assessed based on their observable effects on user autonomy. In this context, clarity, symmetry, and reversibility cease to be mere design ideals—they become fundamental expectations.

### Asymmetric Choices and Default Settings

- *Preselected options* – default settings favoring the service provider.
- *Unequal button prominence* – highlighted "accept," suppressed alternatives.
- *Forced opt-in* – the process cannot be completed without consent to non-mandatory terms.

### Friction and Obstruction

- *Roach motel* – easy registration, difficult exit.
- *Asymmetric effort* – rejection requires more steps than acceptance.
- *Repeated confirmation loops* – questioning a choice already made.

### Hidden or Delayed Information

- *Drip pricing* – mandatory fees revealed late.
- *Obscured renewal terms* – automatic renewals hidden in the fine print.
- *Bundled consent* – multiple purposes under a single "I agree."

### Emotional and Psychological Pressure

- *Confirmshaming* – rejection phrased to induce guilt.
- *Artificial urgency or scarcity* – countdowns and limited availability without a clear basis.
- *Loss framing* – emphasizing perceived negatives of rejection.

### Interface Interventions

- *Misleading toggles or labels* – unclear meaning of on/off.
- *Visual distraction* – diverting attention from substantive choices.
- *Inconsistent interaction patterns* – increasing error rates.

### Patterns Typical for Consent

- *Hidden reject options* – available only in deeper layers.
- *Overloading* – excessive complexity at the decision point.
- *Non-neutral framing* – wording that promotes rather than explains consent.
