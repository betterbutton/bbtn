# IKEA effect
> People value outputs they helped create more highly – which increases engagement and the chance of implementation.

The [IKEA effect](https://uxpsychology.substack.com/p/the-ikea-effect-a-ux-researchers), our tendency to value things we helped build, gives researchers and designers a simple tool to gain client and stakeholder support: involve them in the process. When they contribute to developing insights and formulating solutions, the chance they will continue working with them increases significantly.

Instead of presenting clients with finished conclusions, we gradually involve stakeholders in co-creation. Small things suffice – co-defining research goals, participating in user interviews, or collaborating on data synthesis.

These moments activate psychological mechanisms like effort justification, a sense of competence, and a sense of ownership. This gives stakeholders the impression that the outputs are also their work.

This is not a hypothesis. In experiments, people offered more money for furniture they assembled themselves or for origami they created than for professionally made versions, even though they weren't better quality. One study on this topic is [The “IKEA Effect”: When Labor Leads to Love](https://www.hbs.edu/ris/Publication%20Files/11-091.pdf) by *Michael I. Norton*, *Daniel Mochon*, and *Dan Ariely* from 2012. The implications for UX research are clear: involvement increases value.

But there are conditions. If stakeholder collaboration fails or they lack a clear role, the effect disappears. There's also a risk of overestimating individual problems – for example, based on a single interview experience. Therefore, finding a balance is important. Involvement must not come at the expense of expertise.

Simple methods often suffice. Invitations to interviews, joint workshops, sharing insights, or mentioning contributors in outputs. These small steps create a sense of co-ownership and increase the chance the findings will actually be used.

Gaining support is not about persuasion. It's about jointly building knowledge.
