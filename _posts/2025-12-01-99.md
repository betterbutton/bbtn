## Linkodrome

**Is it time to abandon the Double Diamond?** The *[Double Diamond](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/double-diamond)* model is, according to critics, outdated and confusing. *John V. Willshire* points out that its linear structure does not reflect the complexity of real practice. He doubts its accuracy and usefulness, claiming that the misleading promise of linearity hinders deeper innovation. He warns that [clinging to the old framework undermines designers' ambitions](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7400548352368594945/). He also references [the famous quote](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/audre-lorde-the-master-s-tools-will-never-dismantle-the-master-s-house) by *Audre Lorde* about the inefficacy of the master's tools in dismantling the house he built.

**How to reduce cognitive load in UX:** *Vitaly Friedman* explains why [reducing cognitive load](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/how-reduce-cognitive-load-ux-vitaly-friedman-v0wle/) isn't about simplification at all costs, but about supporting users in orientation, decision-making, and managing complexity. Effective UX should offer users a clear structure, minimize distractions, and provide meaningful context for every step. It's not just about looks, but primarily about functionality, ergonomics, and the software's ability to handle complex work scenarios.

**AI Overviews continue to reduce click-through rates on Google.** New analysis shows that Google [AI Overviews (AIO) significantly reduce the click-through rate (CTR) across both organic and paid results.](https://www.seerinteractive.com/insights/aio-impact-on-google-ctr-september-2025-update) Organic CTR for queries with AIO dropped by 61%, and paid by as much as 68%. Surprisingly, CTR is also dropping where AIO is not present. Citations in AIO bring a significant advantage: up to 91% higher CTR for paid and 35% higher for organic results. Instead of maximizing visits, brands should strive for visibility, authority, and AIO‑citations.

**What Google says about content quality:** SEO expert *Shaun Anderson* connects known principles from the *[Google Search Quality Rater Guidelines](https://services.google.com/fh/files/misc/hsw-sqrg.pdf)* with data from the 2024 *Content Warehouse API* leak. He claims that although we have access to the system's plans, the manual is missing – and that's exactly what his method *Creative Inference Optimisation* addresses. This analytical technique maps human values like effort, originality, and skill to specific system attributes. The result is an evidence‑based framework that helps [understand how Google might actually rate content quality](https://www.searchable.com/blog/decoded-google-quality-rater-guidelines-content-quality).

**There are no limits for alternative text.** The widespread myth that `ALT` text for images should be limited to 100, 150, or 200 characters is not true. There is no technical limit or accessibility perspective restriction. What matters is that [alt text fits the context and correctly conveys the image's meaning](https://web.archive.org/web/20251130224643/https://chrisyoong.com/blog/the-100-150-or-200-characters-alt-text-rule-is-a-myth) – it can be short or comfortably longer. The emphasis should be on clarity and purpose, not length.

**Tools won't make you a better product person.** *Ken Norton* distances himself from product managers' obsession with tools and frameworks. He claims that [tools alone don't improve anyone](https://www.bringthedonuts.com/essays/the-tools-dont-matter/) – real growth comes from self-awareness, experience, and the ability to ask the right questions. He compares it to sports or art: technology is secondary, mastering the craft is key. According to Ken, a good product person doesn't ask what tool, but what way of working is most effective.

**A shared language between design and development determines delivery speed.** Design and engineering are often compared to trains heading toward the same goal – the product. But often, each runs at a different pace without real alignment. *Yann‑Edern Gillet* compares the ideal connection between these disciplines to the Rosetta Stone: instead of overcoming differences, it seeks a common language. A design engineer isn't someone who does everything, but [a living translator between the worlds of design and development](https://yannglt.com/writing/the-rosetta-stone-of-design-engineering), keeping both tracks level. The more shared language, the higher the development speed.

**In a sea of sameness, taste is more important than data.** *Itay Dreyfus* in his essay points out the crisis of authenticity in contemporary design, which is subject to templates, metrics, and automation. Instead of [cultivating taste and a personal perspective](https://productidentity.co/p/in-the-sea-of-sameness), design is reduced to problem-solving and measurable outputs. He calls for a return to intuition, personal style, and the courage to create outside established frameworks.

[Paul Fitts](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/paul-fitts) (1912—1965) was an American psychologist and a pioneer of ergonomics who fundamentally changed the view of the relationship between humans and machines. During World War II as an Air Force officer, he discovered that most pilot errors are not caused by human failure but by poor cockpit design. This insight led to his lifelong credo: *machines should be adapted to people, not the other way around*.

In 1954, he formulated *[Fitts's Law](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/fitts-law)*, which mathematically describes the time needed to target an object – a fundamental principle still used in user interface design. He also created *Fitts's List* (1951), which defined where humans are better and where machines are better, laying the foundation for the theory of function allocation between humans and automation. His work influences UX design, robotics, and artificial intelligence to this day.

## So We Understand Each Other

[Goodhart's Law](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/goodharts-law) warns: once a metric becomes a target, it loses its value as a measure. In UX and product development, this leads companies to chase numbers instead of real value. The solution is to combine quantitative and qualitative approaches, monitor side effects, and focus on user goals, not tables.

[Moravec's paradox](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/moravecs-paradox) describes a peculiar asymmetry: artificial intelligence today excels at abstract thinking but fails at seemingly trivial physical tasks. This phenomenon, named after roboticist *Hans Moravec*, shows that tasks like walking, grasping, or understanding speech in noise are extremely difficult for machines. So far…

The principle of [point‑of‑need](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/point-of-need) builds on the idea that the user should get help exactly when needed – neither earlier nor later. Originally from librarianship and education, this approach now finds application in digital interfaces. Instead of extensive manuals, it offers unobtrusive, contextual assistance: tooltips, smart suggestions, or instant feedback.

[The principle of common fate](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/uniform-connectedness) states that people perceive visually connected elements as a whole – faster and stronger than based on proximity or similarity. Frames, backgrounds, or connecting lines form groups that the brain recognizes before reading text.
