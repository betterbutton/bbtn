# Jakob's Law
> Jakob Nielsen's UX law states that users expect new websites to work similarly to those they already know. Following established patterns makes orientation easier and improves the user experience.

Jakob's Law, also known as [Jakob's Law](https://lawsofux.com/jakobs-law/) ([Jakob’s Law](https://www.nngroup.com/videos/jakobs-law-internet-ux/)), formulated by usability expert [Jakob Nielsen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jakob_Nielsen_(usability_consultant), states that users spend most of their time on other websites and therefore expect your site to work similarly to ones they already know. This principle helps users orient themselves more easily, reduces their cognitive load, and speeds up task completion.

When a person visits a new website, they don't approach it completely without experience. They already have a certain idea of how things usually work. If your site adheres to established patterns, users are more likely to understand it.

The main menu is usually located at the top or on the left because users are accustomed to it. The search field is typically in the top-right corner. The ordering process in e-shops often follows the same pattern – cart, shipping, payment, confirmation. Jakob Nielsen and Marie Tahir even wrote a book about web patterns called [Homepage Usability](https://www.knihovny.cz/Record/ntk.STK01-000168691?sid=24833866).

Following standards doesn't mean UX design has to be boring. It's about finding a balance between what users expect and what makes your product unique. If you change something, always test it with real users.

People like it when things work as they expect. If you make it easier for them to reach their goal, they'll be happy to return to you.
