# Conformity
> The human tendency to follow the majority influences user behavior online – it can help, but also harm, if designers don't use it honestly.

[Conformity](https://josefstepanek.cz/58-kognitivnich-zkresleni-ktera-skodi-vsemu-delame?utm_source=chatgpt.com#:~:text=9.-,Konformita) ([conformity bias](https://atlasti.com/research-hub/conformity-bias), the tendency to align with the majority opinion, significantly influences people's behavior on the internet. Designers often leverage this tendency through social proof elements – for example, star ratings, *most popular* labels, trend rankings, or messages like *“15 people are currently viewing”*.

When used honestly, conformity can improve the user experience. It helps people make decisions faster, find favorite products, or identify trustworthy content. For instance, labeling a product as *best-selling* or displaying the current activity of other users can increase trust and support decisions, as people naturally believe what the majority does.

Problems arise when this effect is misused. Fake messages like *“only 2 items left”*, false numbers such as *“1200 people are currently following”*, or fabricated reviews cross the line of ethics. Even if they may boost short-term sales, in the long run they erode trust and can lead to legal issues. Such practices are monitored by regulatory authorities not only in the USA but also in the European Union.

Creators of digital products should therefore act fairly. Show truthful data. Give people the opportunity to verify information. Don't push, don't manipulate, respect user freedom.

In a digital world dominated by metrics and crowd opinions, conformity is a powerful tool. But trust is even more important. When you use conformity bias honestly, you naturally support user decision-making – and simultaneously strengthen your brand.
