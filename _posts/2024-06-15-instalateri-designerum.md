# Scott Berkun: Even the best designs will remain on paper if you don't secure support for them
> 

It looks like Scott Berkun is writing a new book: Why Design Is Hard. We're peeking over his shoulder and see he's started a chapter: [What designers can learn from plumbers](https://whydesignishard.substack.com/p/what-designers-can-learn-from-plumbers).

https://whydesignishard.substack.com/p/what-designers-can-learn-from-plumbers?utm_source=substack&utm_campaign=post_embed&utm_medium=web

Why Design Is Hard, the book (2024)

What designers can learn from plumbers

Reframing is one of many powers you possess that can make design easier instead of harder. In a previous post, we explained that designing and deciding are often different roles. But if designing isn’t deciding, what else is it? Deciders are busy, so we have to take responsibility for providing answers. Too often we expect other people to do this work. …

Read more

2 years ago · 11 likes · Scott Berkun

Scott writes that we designers often hide behind our made-up specializations and expect someone else to make decisions. In contrast, craftsmen see a problem and propose a solution within their specialization. They don't ask the client about priorities, risks, and constraints.

> "The difference between a good and a great designer lies in the ability not only to solve the problem but also to explain how the design solves it in a way that is convincing and leads to agreement. There are three things every design needs to be successful: it solves the problem, it's easy for the user, and it's supported by everyone."
> **— Scott Berkun, [What designers can learn from plumbers](https://whydesignishard.substack.com/p/what-designers-can-learn-from-plumbers)**

The best work of designers often remains in the design phase because, without securing buy-in and support from the client and stakeholders, it never gets a chance to reach the user.
