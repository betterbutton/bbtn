# Community event organizers are running out of patience with no-shows
> 

If you organize events for the community, you know it all too well: People sign up, but a large portion doesn't show up, and meanwhile you have a full waitlist and other interested people are still writing to ask if there's a spot for them. *„I take it as it is. For events, we do over-booking. For small events for twenty people, it's easily thirty to fifty percent more, for large events for a hundred people, fifteen percent is usually enough. Those few extra people always somehow fit in the worst case,"* says Martin, who has over a quarter century of experience in organizing community events.

However, organizers of other community events are not willing to accept unannounced no-shows or last-minute cancellations. *„There's nothing to be done, there must be penalties for late and unannounced attendance,"* [writes on Twitter](https://twitter.com/PavelUngr/status/1861468559253377352) SEOloger organizer Pavel Ungr. At a completely exclusive event about Seznam's AI, half of the registered people didn't show up.

*„At the last Friends of Figma, there were 70 people on the waitlist. And yet, almost 50 people who didn't show up and didn't even cancel were found,"* [complains on Linkedin](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7267421407808995328/) Šimon Jůn. What can be done about participants' unreliability? Somewhere, they are considering charging for attendance. However, payment might deter, for example, students or parents on parental leave. Elsewhere, they plan to introduce a reputation system. *„What has worked best for me is to ask participants to cancel a day in advance if they can't come. That gives a chance to people from the waitlist. Canceling attendance must be significantly easier than signing up for the event,"* Martin reveals his approach and adds, *„The best is to entrust the event management to specialized services like Meetup.com or Eventbrite.“*

Unannounced absence or last-minute cancellation is not a problem just because someone else doesn't get to the event. Community events are usually paid for by sponsors who expect the opportunity to present to a certain number of people. Based on the expected attendance, catering is ordered and post-event restaurant reservations are made. Sometimes, costs like venue rental are paid from the community budget or are paid directly by the organizers from their own pocket. In such a case, it's a shame when we spend money without effect.
