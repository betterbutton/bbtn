## From The Designer

**Enlarge clickable areas.** Fingers and cursors don't hit pixels.

❌ Tiny links and buttons stuck together\
✅ Target areas of at least 44 × 44 px with spacing

👉 You'll reduce the number of misclicks.\
👉 Touch control will be significantly more comfortable.

Generous target areas = fewer errors and less stress.

## Linkodrome

**How to design irreversible and risky actions.** Besides classic confirmation dialogs, *Victor Ponamariov* [recommends methods](https://whimsical.com/confirm-vs-undo-decision-tree-WCf8q4bTpsUPqFPfCgtZit) like keyword entry, inline confirmation, two-phase verification, a second person for approval, action delay, or the ability to undo. Clarity, visual distinction of critical steps, and knowledge of the target group are important. Victor emphasizes that even [the best practices need to be tested](https://www.smashingmagazine.com/2024/09/how-manage-dangerous-actions-user-interfaces/) in a specific context.

**Eight ways to think without templates.** Designers often experience fatigue from methods where the form is determined before the problem is understood. *Tom Eldridge* points out that the pressure for tangible outputs obscures the real benefit of design thinking: uncovering the invisible, working with time, systems, or people. He proposes an approach based on eight modes of thinking – [from prototyping to considering change](https://www.strategyxdesign.co.uk/tools-for-thinking/) – which better capture the cognitive nature of design and its real value.

**Accessibility cannot be abandoned.** *Declan Chidlow* expresses frustration that despite legislative pressures, we often treat accessibility as an unnecessary cost rather than a fundamental human right. Accessibility is not just for a minority – [it improves the user experience for everyone](https://vale.rocks/posts/accessibility-importance). Declan appeals to personal responsibility – accessibility cannot be added later. It's not a nice-to-have, but a matter of respect for humanity.

**Practical programming principles for self-taught developers.** *Kilian Valkhof*, a self-taught frontend developer with years of experience, summarizes the key programming principles that helped him most to write better, more readable, and more maintainable code. Instead of academic maxims, [he emphasizes practical principles](https://piccalil.li/blog/programming-principles-for-self-taught-front-end-developers/). He warns against the dangers of premature optimization and unnecessary generalization. The main goal is to write code that is easy to read, understand, and maintain. It's not enough that it works.

“We must design for the way people behave, not for how we would wish them to behave.” ― Donald A. Norman, [Living With Complexity](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/8295595-living-with-complexity)

[Don Norman](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/don-norman) (*December 25, 1935) celebrated his ninetieth birthday at Christmas. He shifted the responsibility for errors from users to the design itself. He argues that when people fail, the design failed first – which laid the foundations for the systematic analysis of interaction using concepts like [affordance](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/affordances), signifiers, or feedback.

At Apple, [he introduced the term user experience](https://www.peterme.com/index112498.html) as a comprehensive temporal experience, not just a visual interface. Norman's approach is not a methodology, but an ethical and systemic attitude toward designing the world.

## To Understand Each Other

[User Interface](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/user-interface) connects user intentions with system response. Unlike the broader user experience (UX), UI is concrete, influenceable, and directly measurable. The interface should be clear, understandable, and predictable. However, long-term usability is not given by a one-time design – it requires ongoing care, testing, and adaptation.

[Empathy](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/empathy) is a practical skill grounded in research. Empathetic design therefore builds on understandable structures, accessibility, and tolerance for errors. Decisions should not be based on assumptions, but on conversations, testing, and data. Nevertheless, empathy has its limits – it's not just about compassion, but also the ability to distance oneself and design thoughtfully within real constraints.

[Web browser](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/browser) is the runtime environment for the web. On Thursday, it was [35 years](https://worldwideweb.cern.ch/worldwideweb/) since *[Tim Berners-Lee](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/timbl)* released the first version of a web browser. The dominance of a few browser engines is complemented by fragmentation – an increasing part of interactions takes place in restricted environments like mobile webviews or in-app browsers. Browser history shows how important standardization and interoperability are.

[Passkeys](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/passkey) represent a fundamental shift in login – instead of passwords, they use cryptography with a public and private key, thereby eliminating the risk of phishing and credential misuse. The private key remains securely on the user's device; the web only knows the public key. Login happens without sharing a secret, meaning higher security and a smoother user experience.
