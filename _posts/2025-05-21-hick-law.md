# Hick's Law
> A UX principle stating that the time required to make a decision increases with the number of available options.

Hick's Law ([Hick-Hyman law](https://lawsofux.com/hicks-law/)) states that the more options a person has, the longer it takes them to decide. In practice, this means that if a user on a website or in an application has to choose from too many options at once, they may become overwhelmed, slow down, or decide to leave. For designers, this represents an important rule—don't overwhelm, but organize. Clear, limited choices lead to faster and more confident decisions.

The rule applies most strongly to new users or when all options are similarly important. If the user is experienced, they will orient themselves quickly even in a more complex interface—especially if they rely on familiar patterns or if the system offers them the most probable option. Therefore, it makes sense to combine Hick's Law with other principles, such as progressively revealing options or smart default settings.

The rule dates back to the 1950s, when it was described by psychologists [William Hick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W._E._Hick) and [Ray Hyman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_Hyman). They measured decision time based on the number of stimuli. It turned out that the more stimuli, the slower the reaction—and this still holds true today. In digital product design, Hick's Law thus remains a useful tool for creating clear and user-friendly interfaces.
