# Gulf of Execution and Gulf of Evaluation
> Gulf of Execution and Evaluation – How to easily find out how to use something and what happened to it after use.

We're driving on the highway, and one of the kids in the backseat takes off her headphones: *"Borrow mine,"* I say from behind the wheel, pointing to my own headphones, but I know full well it won't be that easy. I can feel in the air an opportunity to observe the Gulf of Execution in the rearview mirror.

**Gulf of Execution** (English [Gulf of Execution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Design_of_Everyday_Things#Gulf_of_execution)) is the effort a person must expend to figure out how to use something.

She turns on my headphones and discovers they aren't connected to her phone. They are my headphones, after all. On her phone, a Bluetooth device appears: *"Dad, I don't see your headphones in my phone, and I don't see them in unpaired devices either."* Some devices aren't visible to others until you switch them to pairing mode. But the phone doesn't remind you of this in the Bluetooth settings. In the meantime, the headphones have connected to my phone. The headphones give no indication that you can switch them to pairing mode by holding the power button long. A prime example of the phenomenon known as the Gulf of Execution. In this case, without external knowledge supplementation, the gulf is insurmountable.

**Gulf of Evaluation** (English [Gulf of Evaluation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Design_of_Everyday_Things#Gulf_of_evaluation)) is, conversely, the effort a person must expend to understand what happened to something after using it.

When you turn on the headphones, they start searching for paired devices and blink blue rapidly. If they connect to a paired device, they blink blue slowly. When switching to pairing mode, an LED alternates between blue and red. If you don't know these signals, you probably won't recognize what's happening with the headphones. Smart headphones therefore also emit sounds, which has a similar effect on the user as the LEDs do. The smartest headphones will just talk to you. If you don't want to fall into the Gulf of Evaluation, get headphones that will tell you things like "pairing" or "low battery."

The terms Gulf of Execution and Gulf of Evaluation originate from the chapter [Cognitive Engineering](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/235616560_Cognitive_Engineering) in Don Norman's 1986 book [User Centered System Design](https://www.taylorfrancis.com/books/edit/10.1201/9780367807320/user-centered-system-design-donald-norman). Two years later, he popularized them in the bestseller [Design of Everyday Things](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/18012365-the-design-of-everyday-things). The concept of gulfs is important for understanding how to design products and systems that are intuitive and user-friendly. The goal for designers is to reduce the gulfs so people can use things effectively and without unnecessary frustration, as they need to.

---

**Translation Note:** In Czech professional literature, the terms *gulf of execution* and *gulf of evaluation* are usually not translated. In English, the word [gulf](https://www.dictionary.com/browse/gulf) is used in the sense of a bay, but the meaning of an abyss or chasm, including in a figurative sense of a gap or discrepancy, is also common. The word [exekuce](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exekuce) in Czech has a strong association with the exercise of power, so an alternative was needed that would reflect the execution of an intention more accurately.
