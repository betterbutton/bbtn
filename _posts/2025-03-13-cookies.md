# Cookies
> Cookies are small text files stored in the browser that enable personalization, tracking, and user authentication. Cookie regulations are tightening, while new alternatives are constantly being sought.

[Cookies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTTP_cookie) are small text files stored in the browser. The browser can delete them after closing the application (session cookies) or store them long-term (persistent cookies). Some are set by the visited website (first-party cookies), others come from advertising and analytics services (third-party cookies).

Cookies help log users in, remember their settings, track visits, and target advertising. While they improve user comfort, stricter regulations aim to limit their misuse for tracking.

Many countries regulate cookies to protect personal data. The European Union ([GDPR](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obecn%C3%A9_na%C5%99%C3%ADzen%C3%AD_o_ochran%C4%9B_osobn%C3%ADch_%C3%BAdaj%C5%AF)) and the United Kingdom ([DPA 2018](https://www.gov.uk/data-protection)) require user consent for tracking. California ([CCPA](https://oag.ca.gov/privacy/ccpa)) gives users the right to know how their data is used and to prohibit its sale. Similar rules apply in Canada, Australia, Brazil, and China, pushing companies toward greater transparency.

Major browsers like Google Chrome, Safari, and Firefox are gradually phasing out support for third-party cookies. Companies must therefore find new ways to track users that better protect privacy. Websites must implement clear cookie policies, obtain user consent, and use alternative tracking methods to comply with new laws.

Instead of cookies, other technical methods for storing data in the browser are used: local storage, session storage, IndexedDB. New methods, like [Google Topics API](https://privacysandbox.com/intl/en_us/proposals/topics/), track users more anonymously. Modern storage does not address the spirit of privacy laws. The issue is not the technical method of storing user data, but the fair handling of data, user awareness, and the fair acquisition of user consent.
