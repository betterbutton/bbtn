# Fail fast, learn faster
> A principle that prioritizes rapid, low-risk experimentation to expose weak assumptions as early as possible and turn findings into actionable knowledge, not just work speed.

> *You are opening the twenty-second window of the BBtn Advent Calendar for 2025. This year's advent series looks at popular proverbs of UX designers. Today's is: Fail fast, learn faster.*

*Fail fast, learn faster* (*[fail fast, learn faster](https://www.failfastlearnfaster.org/)*) is often cited in UX and product design as a defense of speed. Its true meaning, however, lies elsewhere. It's not a call to err for error's sake. It's an effort to reduce uncertainty as early as possible and with minimal cost—before decisions become expensive and difficult to reverse. The measure of progress isn't the number of features shipped, but how much uncertainty was eliminated.

This distinction is crucial because the saying is often oversimplified. Teams can work quickly, ship changes frequently, and yet learn almost nothing if they don't formulate assumptions, define success signals, and systematically evaluate results. Speed without learning is just motion. Short cycles only make sense when they lead to a deeper understanding of users and their context.

This is well illustrated by comparison with Facebook's former motto, Move fast, break things. In the early days, it expressed an important idea—that excessive caution can hinder innovation. As the company grew, however, "breaking things" meant outages, loss of trust, and technical debt. The later shift to the slogan Move fast with stable infrastructure showed that stability is a condition for sustainable learning.

In UX, therefore, rapid failing should happen where it's safe—in sketches, prototypes, usability tests, and small experiments. Production systems have the opposite purpose. They are meant to protect users from our uncertainty. § In this light, the message is simple. Failing fast is possible. We must learn fast.
