# Design is never finished
> …just rests for a moment between iterations.

> *You are opening the third window of the BBtn Advent Calendar for 2025. This year's advent series looks at popular UX designer proverbs. Today's is: Design is never finished.*

In digital design, the word "done" is a dangerous illusion. The web changes faster than any other medium: browsers update, devices multiply, laws tighten, and user expectations shift day by day. A page that isn't maintained doesn't stay the same—it slowly decays.

Every designer or web manager will realize this sooner or later. What worked at launch soon becomes outdated: links break, frameworks age, loading slows down, and what once delighted becomes ordinary.

Research by *Jakob Nielsen* showed decades ago that [no interface is perfect on the first try](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/iterative-design/)—every cycle of testing and refinement brings measurable improvement. Modern UX, agile, and lean approaches have made this the standard way of working: build, test, learn, repeat.

Continuous design isn't self-indulgent. It's a way to manage risk and care for users. Accessibility standards like *WCAG 2.2* change almost every year. Security patches address new vulnerabilities. Analytics reveal new problems in navigation and content.

Whoever ignores these changes risks not only technical issues but also loss of trust. Treating a website as a living product instead of a finished project means viewing maintenance as a continuation of design—every update improves performance, clarity, and relevance.

The fact that design never ends doesn't mean it shouldn't be launched. The pursuit of perfection often hinders progress. The art lies in releasing the product, observing how it works, and gradually improving it. The first version isn't the end—it's the beginning of learning.

The most successful websites and the most satisfied users come from teams that have adopted this rhythm: launch, observe, improve, repeat. In an environment that never stops, constant change isn't a burden—it's a survival skill.
