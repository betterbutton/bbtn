# Ben Shneiderman creator of a friendly digital world 
> Pioneer who connected computer science with design and influenced how we control technology today.

![AI-generated drawing of Ben Shneiderman based on a photograph](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/b0dc8392-e902-494f-8ecc-200f1604c377_1536x1024.webp "542x361")

Ben Shneiderman is a pioneer of user-friendly interface design.

[Ben Shneiderman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ben_Shneiderman) (*August 21, 1947) is among the most influential figures in the field of human-computer interaction and user experience design. He coined the term *[direct manipulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_manipulation_interface)*, formulated the *[eight golden rules of interface design](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/article/shneiderman-s-eight-golden-rules-will-help-you-design-better-interfaces)*, and designed visualization techniques such as *[tree maps](https://www.cs.umd.edu/~ben/papers/Johnson1991Tree.pdf)*. His ideas influence how we design digital products today.

The concept of direct manipulation means the user can directly influence objects on the screen and immediately sees the result. This simple but fundamental idea laid the groundwork for graphical user interfaces, touch control, and intuitive design.

Another contribution is Shneiderman's *golden rules*. These are recommendations that help design understandable, predictable, and safe interfaces. They include consistency, feedback, reversibility, error prevention, and minimizing the user's memory load. These rules are still used today and continue to form the foundation of quality interface design.

Shneiderman also made a significant contribution to the development of data visualization. He invented tree maps and formulated the principle *"overview first, zoom and filter, then details on demand."* This approach is now commonly used, for example, in designing analytical tools or data dashboards.

In recent years, he has focused on the topic of *human-centered artificial intelligence*. According to him, [artificial intelligence should not replace humans](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aZI-re-tG44), but should support them. He advocates for designing systems that give users control, are understandable, and behave responsibly.

Although Shneiderman is originally a computer scientist, his work is deeply human. He shows that technology can be not only powerful but also friendly, readable, and natural. His ideas inspire developers, researchers, and designers—even today, in the age of artificial intelligence and a rapidly changing digital environment.
