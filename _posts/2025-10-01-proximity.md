# Principle of Proximity
> A fundamental design principle: users assume that elements placed close together are related—and they behave accordingly.

The Principle of Proximity ([law of proximity](https://lawsofux.com/law-of-proximity/)) is a simple rule of visual perception in digital design. When elements are placed close together, users naturally assume they belong together. This psychological shortcut can either enhance clarity or cause confusion—it depends on how you use it.

In practice, the law of proximity helps people orient themselves within an interface. For example, form labels should be placed tightly next to their corresponding fields. Buttons, in turn, must be near the content they influence. If this isn't the case, users can easily get confused—leading to errors, frustration, and a loss of trust in the entire product.

The law of proximity also influences visual hierarchy. A designer doesn't need to draw boxes or use heavy elements. Proper spacing and relationships between interface parts become clear on their own. Slight adjustments to margins or alignment can completely change the impression and understanding.

The Principle of Proximity doesn't just affect aesthetics—it affects usability. Interfaces that ignore it often appear cluttered or disorganized. Conversely, well-arranged elements help people orient themselves quickly, think less, and make fewer mistakes. This is especially true on mobile, where space is tight and it's easy to tap the wrong thing.

The Principle of Proximity might be subtle, but it's absolutely crucial. It influences how well people understand what they see—and how confidently they then interact with it. Once you master it, everything feels natural and trustworthy. And that's exactly what your users want.
