# People Don't Read on the Web
> People don't read on the web. They scan, skip, and search for what they need. If they don't find it immediately, they close the page and go elsewhere.

Open a web page. You glance at the headline, catch a few bolded words, scan a list or the first sentence of a paragraph. If nothing grabs your attention, you scroll further. Or you simply leave.

Research shows that [79% of people just scan websites](https://creativehandles.com/blog-posts/79/4-types-of-eye-tracking-patterns-how-people-don-t-read-on-web). Only 16% read word by word. The most common pattern? [The F-pattern.](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HboNG2m4n7I) First, they read a few lines, then they just skim headlines and the first words in paragraphs. If nothing catches their eye, they stop trying.

In expert studies, we talk about [three stages of reading](https://library.leeds.ac.uk/info/1401/academic-skills/207/reading/2): scanning, skimming, and attentive reading.

During **scanning**, the reader's eyes quickly travel across the page. They perceive the text layout, looking for headings, paragraphs, tables, lists, illustrations, and other visual elements to get a sense of the structure and importance of each section.

**Skimming** is superficial reading. Based on the first impression, the reader goes through headlines, paragraph introductions, highlighted sections, and lists. The goal is to determine if any passages warrant more thorough reading.

**Attentive reading** is true focused reading. The reader dedicates full attention to selected parts of the text and strives to understand them in depth.

What to do about it? [The web must be clear, understandable, and easy to scan.](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/how-users-read-on-the-web/)

- **Strong headings** that make sense on their own.
- **Short paragraphs**, ideally three to four sentences.
- **Highlighted key information** – bold, italics, color.
- **Lists and bullet points**, as they are clearer than long text blocks.
- **The most important information goes at the top**, details further down.

People don't read on the web because they don't have to. If they don't understand the information immediately, they go elsewhere. The goal isn't to force them to read, but to enable them to quickly find what they're looking for.
