# Forgiving Software
> Forgiving software tolerates human errors and allows for easy recovery. The goal is to improve user experience and increase system reliability.

Good design limits the possibilities of failure. Yet, when people interact with a digital interface, errors occur. **Forgiving Software** does not create an indelible stigma from mistakes. No error should end in a dead end without a way back. Making mistakes is a normal part of exploring, learning, and gaining experience.

> Laptops have also gained great popularity due to their independence from the power grid. With a desktop computer, a power interruption results in the loss of unfinished tasks. Before the battery drains, laptops transfer the contents of short-term memory to the hard drive, and after power is restored, you can continue where you left off. This is a typical example of forgiving design.

The principles of forgiving design are important in life-threatening activities. They usually include various compensatory mechanisms and multiple safeguards. It is probably no surprise that the demand for forgiving design emerged with the needs of the military during World War II. On one hand, during general mobilization, people without prior experience were brought into the army to operate complex machines; on the other hand, even in civilian professions, there was a need to replace experienced professionals with untrained workers.

With the first computers came the first computer bugs. That software has bugs was already convinced in 1947 by the team at Harvard University where future Rear Admiral Grace Hopper worked. Their room-sized [computer was taken out of service by a moth](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/prvni-dokumentovany-pocitacovy-bug) stuck on a relay in panel F.

A few decades later, democratization prevailed in information technology, and complex information systems were no longer controlled by trained professionals. Commonly, you have to use a tool you are encountering for the first time. And therefore, it must forgive your errors. The concept of forgiving software appears in 1986 among the eight rules of interaction design in the book [Designing the User Interface](https://www.pearson.com/en-us/subject-catalog/p/designing-the-user-interface-strategies-for-effective-human-computer-interaction/P200000003485) by Ben Shneiderman.

William Lidwell in the book [Universal Principles of Design](https://universalprinciplesofdesign.com/books) offers designers six tools to achieve forgiving software:

1.  [Affordance](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/affordances) – if it is immediately clear from appearance how to use a tool, the risk of error decreases.

2.  Reversibility of actions – the system creates conditions to return to the state before the error.

3.  Safety net – limits the destructive impact of erroneous decisions and actions.

4.  Awareness – for potentially destructive and irreversible actions, ensure the user understands the consequences and acts consciously.

5.  Warnings – alert the user to impending danger and inform them of the current situation.

6.  Support – offers help, system documentation, expert assistance, problem source lookup, and corrective means.

The overview of tools lacks error prevention and [defensive design](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/defensive-design). But that deserves its own entries.
