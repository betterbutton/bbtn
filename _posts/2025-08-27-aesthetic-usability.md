# Aesthetic usability effect
> Users perceive attractive interfaces as more user-friendly, even when there is no functional difference.

The [Aesthetic usability effect](https://www.honzasoukup.cz/blog/aesthetic-usability-effect) ([æsthetic-usability effect](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/aesthetic-usability-effect/)) is a well-known phenomenon in the field of digital interface design. People tend to consider visually appealing applications or websites as easier to use, even in cases where they do not differ in functionality from those with an ordinary appearance.

This effect was first described by Japanese researchers *Masaaki Kurosu* and *Kaori Kashimura* in 1995. In their paper [Apparent usability vs. inherent usability](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/221517663_Apparent_usability_vs_inherent_usability) at the CHI’95 conference, they found that users rated the more aesthetically pleasing ATM interface as more user-friendly, despite its structure being identical to the other versions. Five years later, Israeli scientists *Noam Tractinsky*, *Adi Katz*, and *D. Ikar* revisited the topic in the study [What is beautiful is usable](https://www.academia.edu/3858787/What_is_beautiful_is_usable), which also measured post-use satisfaction and found that respondents perceived the nicer-looking interface as more usable even after interacting with it. Since then, this principle has become an important part of digital product design.

However, a nice appearance is not just about beauty. It builds trust, evokes pleasant feelings, and encourages users to explore the interface further. This allows them to overlook minor errors and adapt to the application's environment more easily.

On the other hand, pretty design can mask fundamental problems. An outwardly successful application might be confusing in practice, difficult to control, or communicate functions incorrectly. Users may then trust the application more than they should.

What does this mean for developers? Visual design must not only attract attention but also guide. It should simplify orientation, reduce mental effort, and promote clarity. Aesthetics in the user interface should not be an ornament but a tool.

The aesthetic usability effect reminds us of a simple truth: people do not perceive appearance and function separately. They perceive them as a single whole.
