# Cognitive Bias
> Hidden mental shortcuts shape how people perceive, judge, and use digital environments – often without them realizing it.

Cognitive bias ([cognitive bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_bias)) are systematic errors in thinking that distort perception and decision-making. They influence how people read information, respond to interfaces, and interact with digital services – even when they feel they are deciding rationally. For creators of digital products, it is important to understand these biases because they can significantly impact user experience and credibility.

Well-known cognitive biases include:

- [Hawthorne effect](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/hawthorne-effect) – When people know they are being observed, they behave unnaturally, and UX researchers must account for this.

- [Availability heuristic](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/availability-heuristic) – A mental shortcut that causes people to consider things more common or important simply because they are easy to recall.

- [Frequency illusion](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/frequency-illusion) – A cognitive bias where something you recently noticed suddenly seems to appear everywhere.

- [Experimenter's bias](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/hawthorne-effect#:~:text=i%20dal%C5%A1%C3%AD%20pojmy.-,Pozorovac%C3%AD%20rozpor,-(observer%E2%80%99s%20paradox) – Encompasses all the ways a researcher can unconsciously influence results through their approach.

- [Survivorship bias](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/survivorship-bias) – A logical fallacy where we draw conclusions from visible successes and overlook invisible failures, which often hold the most valuable information.

- [Bias blind spot](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/bias-blind-spot) – The cognitive bias that causes us to fail to see our own biases, even while we see them in others.

Among the most common biases is confirmation bias, which leads us to favor information that confirms our existing beliefs. Anchoring causes us to give excessive weight to the first piece of information a user encounters. The availability heuristic leads us to perceive rare events as common if they are easily recalled from memory. With hindsight bias, people retrospectively overestimate their ability to have predicted an outcome. The halo effect causes a positive impression of one trait, such as a website's appearance, to influence the evaluation of the whole.

Biases help us orient ourselves faster, but they can also reinforce misinformation or reduce decision quality. Psychologists [Daniel Kahneman](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Kahneman) and [Amos Tversky](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amos_Tversky) described these phenomena as early as the 1970s – today they are worked with in design, economics, and media literacy.

If these errors are to be mitigated, services must be designed with clarity, balance, and diversity in mind. For example, tooltips, feedback, or careful word choice can help users make more conscious decisions.

In a digital environment with an overabundance of information and a minimum of time, every detail matters. And understanding cognitive biases can help make technology fairer, more accessible, and more trustworthy.
