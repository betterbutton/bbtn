## The Paradox of Writing Your Own Tool

![Jakub assembling pieces from the littleBits construction kit. (generated drawing)](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/f0590415-5625-4f4d-b96f-a7d430815603_800x533.webp "444x295")

Jakub and Martin were sitting in The Italians cafe on Saturday morning. *"It's a paradox,"* Jakub suddenly said, *"instead of sitting down at Photoshop or Figma and drawing buttons for an app like before, it pays off for me to write an app in Cursor that generates those buttons based on given parameters."*

The paradox of writing your own tool begins with an unusual degree of clarity: I know exactly what I want. Once the goal is sharp, the main obstacle ceases to be technical difficulty and becomes the friction created by existing tools. Specialized software promises efficiency but often requires adaptation – learning its logic, navigating the interface, and accepting compromises.

AI-assisted programming shifts the balance. Creating a small, purpose-built utility can be faster than setting up, taming, and mastering an off-the-shelf tool. The intent doesn't have to bend to the tool's workflow; it's translated directly into behavior. The resulting script is often fragile and terse, but it's precise. It does one thing – and does it without negotiation. In that sense, it's not a tool in the traditional sense, but an executable decision.

The paradox deepens when the traditionally simpler choice – using the right tool – requires more energy than quickly creating a tool of your own. General tools carry the weight of many users and future scenarios. A custom utility carries a single point of view.

This lightness has its price. Such tools age poorly, create maintenance debt, and tempt work on the meta-level. At the same time, they return authorship, control, and essentially the joy of creation. The real shift isn't technical but mental: the key skill is no longer interface mastery but the ability to precisely determine what is even worth automating.

## Linkodrome

**Three pillars of great UX: context, usability, delight.** *Fred Beecher* explores how delight and usability complement each other when designing user-friendly applications. Using the example of two unit converters for iPhone – Convert and Convertbot – he tested how different levels of delight affect the user experience. In the context of simple and infrequent tasks, intuitive usability wins, while for frequent and free-form use [delight can inspire a greater experience](https://www.fredbeecher.com/writing/2009/playfulness-usability-context-the-three-pillars-of-a-delightful-user-experience).

**How to improve visual design.** *Filippos Protogeridis* summarizes best practices to significantly [improve your visual design skills](https://blog.designary.com/p/the-practical-guide-to-improving): repeatedly study typography, color, and layout; analyze design languages; copy inspirational products in Figma; and also regularly revise your own older work. Diversity is also important – try different styles and product types.

**Dithering: When noise makes sense in an image.** Dithering was originally a practical solution to technical limitations – with limited memory and low color resolution, developers needed to fool the human eye into perceiving more shades. The principle involves adding noise between two colors, creating an [illusion of a smooth gradient](https://typefully.com/DanHollick/why-do-we-need-dithering-Ut7oD4k) through spatial averaging. Today we no longer need dithering, but it has survived as a nostalgic visual style.

**Designers are natural AI orchestrators.** *Benhur Senabathi* writes that designers don't need to know how to program, but they must [master precisely defining what they want and why](https://uxdesign.cc/designers-as-agent-orchestrators-what-i-learnt-shipping-with-ai-in-2025-3b1bf30048a3). He described four phases: blind reliance on AI, conversational tuning, systems thinking, and recognizing when to stop tuning and start testing instead.

**Experience strategy is the new direction for UX.** The strategic [management of user experience at the highest level](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7422058353918554113/) of an organization. Its goal is to align the company's key priorities with what truly improves the lives of customers, users, and employees. Jared Spool says that experience strategists connect user value with business, take leadership, and shape innovation. This approach promises a strong future for UX – ambitious, targeted, and strategically anchored.

**Why do design leaders feel powerless?** *Peter Merholz* argues that many UX leaders have succumbed to [an inability to act in difficult organizational conditions](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/overcome-uxdesign-leaderships-learned-helplessness-peter-merholz-dtnwc/). Leaders often failed at what is their real task: creating an environment where design thrives. Instead of leading, they adopted the role of victims. Peter calls for taking responsibility, becoming more business-oriented, and building credibility.

**Is design review a waste of time?** ** criticizes the wave of vague, aesthetic feedback that doesn't address whether the design meets user and business goals. He explains how psychological phenomena like the HiPPO effect and groupthink lead teams to meaningless consensuses. He proposes three techniques: formulate the question clearly, collect feedback individually, and separate reactions from solution proposals. [Constructive criticism focuses on function](https://balintbogdan.substack.com/p/why-design-reviews-waste-time), not appearance.

**The CSS property corner­‑shape allows for more detailed shaping of element corners** that have `border­‑radius` set. In addition to classic rounding (round), it also supports alternative shapes like scoop, bevel, notch, square, squircle, or the mathematical function `superellipse()`. The property is animatable and allows [different shapes for individual corners](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/CSS/Reference/Properties/corner-shape). Effects like shadow, background, or border adapt to the shape.

*[Douglas Engelbart](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/douglas-engelbart)*. He was not only the inventor of the computer mouse but above all a visionary who wanted to use technology to augment human thinking and collaboration. As early as the 1960s, he promoted the idea of augmentation – connecting people, languages, work methods, and tools to better solve complex problems.

At the center of his work was the NLS system, which was ahead of its time – it enabled structured documents, hypertext, and remote collaboration. His famous 1968 presentation showed his comprehensive vision. Engelbart believed technology should expand human intellect, not replace it.

[Core Web Vitals](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/core-web-vitals) are Google's standard for evaluating website performance based on real user experience. They are not interested in lab speed but in when the main content is displayed to the user (LCP), how quickly the interface responds (INP), and how stable the page layout remains (CLS). They evaluate real data from visits – a page must pass for at least 75% of users.

[Blank page syndrome](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/blank-page-syndrome) describes the paralyzing state at the very beginning of creative or analytical work. Excessive freedom, too many options, and the perceived importance of the first step lead to hesitation, not due to a lack of ideas but due to fear of making a mistake. Psychology shows that constraints increase motivation.

[Matomo](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/matomo) is an open­‑source analytics platform that gives website owners full control over their data. It allows storage on their own server or in the cloud, with an emphasis on privacy protection and GDPR compliance. It offers common and advanced analytics functions, from traffic tracking to user behavior analysis.
