# Visual vocabulary offers simple user flow mapping
> A minimalist visual system for clear, fast, and understandable representation of user journeys even for complex applications.

![Screenshot from a file with visual vocabulary symbols for Figma.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/d4759e6e-9a38-48dc-be36-a050bd264fcc_1051x798.png "620x470")

[Visual dictionary](https://www.figma.com/community/file/842843582488924504/visual-vocabulary) for modeling application flow

[Visual vocabulary](http://jjg.net/ia/visvocab/) – the visual vocabulary of [Jesse James Garrett](https://jessejamesgarrett.com/) is a lightweight and clear way to design user flows. Its power lies in its simplicity – designers and product teams can easily draw clear diagrams of screens, decisions, and logical branches with it, without needing special tools or training.

The basic elements include screens as rectangles, decision points as diamonds, and conditional branching shown with angled lines. Everything is designed to be readable even in black and white. This allows you to work with this language on a whiteboard, in PowerPoint, or directly in Figma.

Specifically for Figma, Gavin McFarland has prepared a modern set of components that precisely match the original design. Just drag them into your file and your diagrams are ready in no time. His library is [freely available in the Figma community](https://www.figma.com/community/file/842843582488924504/visual-vocabulary).

Garrett published his system back in 2000 with the goal of creating a simple, tool-independent language for information architecture and interaction design. It wasn't meant for formal modeling, but to allow everyone on the team – from product manager to developer – to quickly understand each other.

And even though other approaches have emerged in the meantime, like UML or customer journeys, the visual vocabulary remains powerful. Designers reach for it when they want to quickly uncover gaps in an application flow, missing states, or complex logic before writing a single line of code.

Its longevity is actually simple: one page of symbols, no complicated rules, and instantly shared understanding.
