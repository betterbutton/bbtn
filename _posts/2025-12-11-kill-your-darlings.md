# Kill Your Darlings
> Have the courage to abandon what you love when it doesn't serve the whole.

> *You are opening the eleventh window of the BBtn Advent Calendar for 2025. In this year's advent series, we'll look at popular proverbs of UX designers. Today's is: Kill Your Darlings.*

*Kill Your Darlings* is a principle that appears wherever something new is being created – in writing, design, and business. It means the ability to let go of ideas, elements, or details that we love if they do not benefit the outcome. It is a discipline that prioritizes the clarity, coherence, and meaning of the work over the creator's personal ego or emotional attachment.

> “Whenever you feel an impulse to perpetrate a piece of exceptionally fine writing, obey it — whole-heartedly — and delete it before sending your manuscript to press. Murder your darlings.”*\*
> **— [Arthur Quiller­‑Couch](https://www.online-literature.com/quiller-couch/art-of-writing/12/?utm_source=chatgpt.com#:~:text=Murder%20your%20darlings.), On Style**

The phrase *“murder your darlings”* is often associated with *[William Faulkner](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Faulkner)* or *[Stephen King](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_King)*, but it was first used by the British writer *Arthur Quiller­‑Couch* as early as the beginning of the twentieth century. He told his students that when they wrote something they were particularly proud of, they should delete it. He meant that we should write with passion but edit with reason. King later references Quiller-Couch in his book *[On Writing](https://www.databazeknih.cz/knihy/o-psani-memoary-o-remesle-1144)*.

> “Kill your darlings, kill your darlings, even when it breaks your egocentric little scribbler’s heart, kill your darlings.”\
> **― [Stephen King](https://www.goodreads.com/quotes/371112-kill-your-darlings-kill-your-darlings-even-when-it-breaks), On Writing: A Memoir of the Craft**

In literature, killing your darlings means cutting out superfluous plotlines, characters, or ornate sentences that hinder the story. [In design and user experience](https://52weeksofux.com/post/1592164002/kill-your-darlings), it's about removing effects and elements that please the creator but don't help people. In innovation and business, it's about the courage to abandon ideas that sound great but have no real value for the customer.

The meaning is simple: every detail must have a reason for being there. If something doesn't add value, obscures the main idea, or exists just because we like it, it must go. Creating means not only adding but also subtracting. And it is in this uncomfortable part of the process that strength is born – a work that is clear, understandable, and truly effective.
