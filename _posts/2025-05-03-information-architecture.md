# Information Architecture
> Information architecture is a design specialization that organizes content so users can find and understand it quickly, easily, and without unnecessary confusion.

Information architecture ([information architecture](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ia-study-guide/), [IA](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/information-architecture)) determines how easily people can navigate a digital environment. When they open a website, app, or other system, they need to quickly figure out where to find things and how to get where they want to go. If everything is clear and logically organized, the user feels confident. But if they have to search for a long time or backtrack repeatedly, they quickly lose patience. And often, their trust in the entire system.

This invisible structure behind content layout is more important than it first appears. Good architecture makes sense. Bad architecture feels chaotic, even if everything is beautifully designed graphically. The user then doesn't know, for example, whether to look for a desired function in settings or somewhere else. Or they can't tell where a certain type of information belongs.

Proper information architecture isn't created at a desk. It comes from the experiences of people who use the system. We explore how they think, how they name things, what they look for, and how they navigate. Good design also accounts for the system changing. New features will be added, more pages, maybe even other languages.

> [Dona Spencer](https://maadmob.com.au/speaking/books) offers a free download of the second edition of her 2010 book [A Practical Guide to Information Architecture](https://maadmob.com.au/speaking/books/practical-ia) ([PDF, 7.2 MB](https://maadmob.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/PracticalGuideToInformationArchitecture.pdf)). Although it's an older book, its content remains valid and useful.
>
> Similarly useful is the 2014 book [How to Make Sense of Any Mess](https://www.howtomakesenseofanymess.com/) by Abby Covert, which you can read for free on the web. Last year, after ten years, it received an [updated expanded edition](https://abbycovert.com/product/collectors-edition-of-how-to-make-sense-of-any-mess/).

So when you feel like you're getting lost in something, it might not be your fault. Perhaps it's a flaw in the structure itself. That structure ultimately decides whether the user experience will be pleasant or frustrating.
