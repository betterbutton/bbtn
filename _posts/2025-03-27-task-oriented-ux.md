# Task-oriented UX
> Task-oriented UX is a design approach that focuses on user needs and facilitates the completion of specific tasks.

Task-oriented UX ([task-oriented UX](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/task-oriented-design) or TOUX) is a way of designing digital services that focuses on what matters most – what the user wants to do. It's not so much about how the page looks, but whether the user can complete the task they came for.

Imagine you need to apply for a new ID card, for example. You don't want to browse through the site structure or read long texts. You just want to get it done.

This is exactly where the approach that helps people accomplish what they set out to comes in. Sometimes it's a reservation, other times login, payment, downloading a confirmation, or filling out a form. And everything should go as smoothly as possible.

Interfaces designed this way often offer a list of life situations – for example, "I need to change my password" or "I want to submit an application." The user doesn't have to search for anything; they just choose and continue.

To know what should be in the foreground, designers use various methods. They track which tasks people solve most often. They describe specific situations and test real scenarios. They measure whether people can complete the task, how long it takes, and how they feel about it.

The goal is not to impress, but to not delay. If the user doesn't encounter anything and everything goes smoothly, that's the ideal outcome. That's the power of task-focused design.
