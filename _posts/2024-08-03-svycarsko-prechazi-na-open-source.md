# Switzerland is transitioning to open source software in public administration
> 

Switzerland adopted a law (already last March) that requires all government software to be open-source. It aims for transparency, security, and efficiency in public administration. This law also requires public authorities to publish software source code, unless prevented by third-party rights or security reasons.

The law came about after a long political and legal battle that began in 2011, and was approved in 2023. [Professor Matthias Stürmer](https://www.digitale-nachhaltigkeit.unibe.ch/about_us/persons/pd_dr_stuermer_matthias/index_eng.html), who led this initiative, believes that the government, IT industry, and society will benefit from this regulation.

On [Steven Vaughan-Nichols writes on ZDNet](https://www.zdnet.com/article/switzerland-now-requires-all-government-software-to-be-open-source/), that unlike the US, European countries are open to open-source. For example, Germany recently replaced Microsoft products with LibreOffice and Linux, and President [Macron stated in 2023](https://eucloudedgeiot.eu/summary-of-the-open-source-key-areas-for-digital-autonomy-workshop/#:~:text=Macron%20stated%3A%20%E2%80%9CWe%20love%20open%20source%E2%80%9D%20and%20all%20public%20bodies%20should%20use%20open%20source.) that he loves open-source and all public institutions should use it.
