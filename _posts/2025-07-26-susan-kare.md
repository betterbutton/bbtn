# Susan Kare Humanized Computers
> Susan Kare – the designer who transformed computer interfaces with icons like Happy Mac or the Command key symbol, and made technology understandable for everyone.

![Susan Kare (on the right) at an Apple Macintosh Classic computer with the text "hello".](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/c433b8e0-4a20-4c81-84bb-87614b44d686_1536x1024.webp "1456x971")

Susan Kare, [Macintosh Artist](https://priceonomics.com/the-woman-behind-apples-first-icons/#:~:text=her%20business%20card%20read%20%E2%80%9CMacintosh%20Artist.%E2%80%9D)

[Susan Kare](https://kareprints.com/) (February 5, 1954) is a graphic designer behind the icons of the first Macintosh – and through them, behind how we perceive computers today. When Apple approached her in 1982, she had no experience with digital design. Yet she created visuals known worldwide: the smiling Mac, the trash can, or the ⌘ symbol known as the Command key.

Without prior experience in computer graphics, Kare used her artistic skills and began sketching designs on graph paper. Simplicity, clarity, and a human-centric approach were key for her. For example, she found the ⌘ symbol in a Swedish atlas – it marked interesting locations in camps – and it replaced Apple's overused logo at the time.

> “The best surfer in the water is the one having the most fun.”\
> **— [Susan Kare](https://www.playforthoughts.com/blog/susan-kare-design)**

Her approach was clear: an icon should function like a traffic sign. It must be understandable at a glance and memorable forever. This made computers accessible even to people without technical training.

> “In design, there is not one right answer, there's a range.”\
> **— [Susan Kare](https://myhero.com/S_Kare_cvhs_cl_US_2016_ul)**

After leaving Apple, Kare designed playing cards for Solitaire in Windows, icons for Facebook or Pinterest, and fonts like Chicago or Geneva. Her work influenced software design across generations.

The originals of her designs were acquired by the New York museum MoMA, and her influence is still evident – in every click, every touch. Susan Kare showed that design is not just about appearance, but primarily about empathy. And that even within 32 pixels, you can create something that brings a smile to people's faces.
