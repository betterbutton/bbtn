# Elizabeth Feinler – Queen of Internet Domains
> Elizabeth Feinler is a computer scientist who was instrumental in creating the WHOIS system and top-level domains. She transformed the early internet from a technical experiment into a usable and organized system.

![Elizabeth Feinler – drawing generated from a period photograph by Douglas Engelbart.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/6d528ed6-183a-475f-8882-34e3e1efff06_1536x1024.webp "552x368")

Elizabeth Feinler managed the Internet's "phone book".

*[Elizabeth Jocelyn "Jake" Feinler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elizabeth_J._Feinler)* (*March 2, 1931) is one of the most important figures of the early internet. While she did not create any visible invention, her fundamental contribution lies in giving the internet order.

As the long-time director of the *Network Information Center* (NIC) for the *[ARPANET](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/ARPANET)* network, she helped create systems that made it possible to find out who was connected to the network, what services existed, and who was responsible for them. Her work laid the foundations for how we name, organize, and manage the internet to this day.

In the 1970s and 1980s, the internet grew rapidly and threatened to become unwieldy. Computers could communicate with each other, but people were losing their way. Elizabeth understood this problem very early. Under her leadership, the NIC became the network's central point of orientation.

It maintained directories of users and organizations, documented technical rules, distributed specifications, and helped people understand how to use the network. Elizabeth herself later hyperbolically called it a sort of proto-search engine.

One of her most lasting contributions is the *[WHOIS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WHOIS)* system. It allowed people to find out who was behind a specific computer or domain. It was not just a technical service but a tool of trust. In the early internet, it was important to know who you were communicating with and to have the ability to contact them directly. The idea that infrastructure should be transparent and that specific people stand behind it remains part of internet governance to this day.

Her role in the creation of the domain name system had an even greater impact. When it was no longer possible to maintain one central list of all computers, her team proposed general top-level domains. Endings like `.com`, `.org`, `.edu`, or `.gov` were not created as commercial brands but as practical solutions to the problem of growth. They were meant to express the role of an institution, not its prestige. It is precisely this simplicity that explains why this system still works today.

Elizabeth always emphasized, however, that the internet is not just a technical network but, above all, a social space. Thanks to her background in information science, she understood the importance of classification and naming, but she also observed how design influences people's behavior. She liked to recall how students, scientists, and military engineers talked to each other on the network regardless of formal status.

After leaving active service, she devoted herself to preserving the history of the internet. She handed over extensive archives to museums and helped process them so that it would be possible to understand the network's true origins. Her life's work thus combines one main idea. Large systems need not only power but also comprehensibility, accountability, and memory. This is where Elizabeth Feinler's lasting legacy lies.
