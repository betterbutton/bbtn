# Roadmap
> A roadmap is a living tool for aligning vision, goals, and learning—not a list of promised features.

Roadmaps ([roadmaps](https://www.productplan.com/learn/roadmap-basics/)) are often misunderstood. They are not static schedules but dynamic instruments that express strategy through outcomes and knowledge. They show where the project is headed and why. They focus on impact, not on deadlines or a list of deliverables.

At its core, a roadmap connects long-term vision with short-term action. It clarifies priorities, shows main directions, and reveals dependencies across domains. Product teams emphasize business goals, designers explore user problems, and developers plan technical capabilities and debt reduction. Marketing, in turn, works with its own map of campaigns and launches. Each perspective has its audience, but together they form a coherent picture of direction.

Modern product teams prefer time horizons like now—soon—later over fixed calendars. This arrangement openly acknowledges uncertainty and allows for talking about what we already know and what we believe. A healthy roadmap evolves with new insights—and that is a sign of maturity, not instability.

In practice, a roadmap works best as a living document that the team updates regularly, ideally every quarter. It evolves with new information from research, data, and the market to keep efforts aligned with strategy.

A roadmap fulfills its main task: bringing clarity. It captures intent, connects learning with direction, and helps everyone pull in the same direction—even when the path isn't fully visible yet.
