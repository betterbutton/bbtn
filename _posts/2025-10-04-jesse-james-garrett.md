# Jesse James Garrett: The Man Who Gave UX Its Language
> A pioneer of user experience who provided designers with frameworks, a vocabulary, and a name for the web revolution.

![Jesse James Garrett (AI-generated drawing based on a photograph)](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/f152b886-2ea9-41d9-8d8e-0116afbe36d6_1536x1024.webp "1456x971")

Jesse James Garrett

[Jesse James Garrett](https://jessejamesgarrett.com/) is one of the most influential figures in the field of user experience. He co-founded the agency Adaptive Path, wrote the book *[The Elements of User Experience](http://www.jjg.net/elements/)*, and coined the term *[Ajax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ajax_(programming))*. Thanks to his work, UX evolved from a fringe discipline to a globally recognized field.

In 2005, he introduced the term Ajax (Asynchronous JavaScript and XML) in the essay *[Ajax: A New Approach to Web Applications](https://web.archive.org/web/20050227021921/www.adaptivepath.com/publications/essays/archives/000385print.php)*, which became legendary. He thus named a technical breakthrough in web development. Web pages no longer had to load entire screens but could work faster and more interactively by calling data sources in the background. A single word captured the new era of the web.

![The five planes of user experience design: strategy, scope, structure, skeleton, and surface](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/a8ae2255-c9a4-41aa-83b8-ffefa6013c5d_2200x1700.avif "1456x1125")

[The five planes of user experience design](http://jjg.net/elements/) according to Jesse James Garrett

A few years earlier, Jesse had made history in a different way. In 2000, he published the [diagram of the five planes of UX](http://www.jjg.net/elements/): strategy, scope, structure, skeleton, and surface. He later developed this model into the 2002 book *The Elements of User Experience*. It allowed designers to connect business goals with specific interface details. The model became a cornerstone of UX education worldwide.

Jesse also created a [visual vocabulary](http://jjg.net/ia/visvocab/) for information architecture. A system of icons and symbols similar to UML models brought uniformity to the documentation of site maps and user flows. Because it could be used on paper and in various software tools, it spread quickly and helped establish clearer rules for UX practice.

Jesse's legacy lies in his ability to bring order and clarity to a field that was still taking shape at the turn of the millennium. His frameworks are still taught in schools and used in UX design even after a quarter of a century. Although the term Ajax has been superseded by time, its principles have become an integral part of creating digital experiences.
