# Dark Patterns Gallery
> 

In UX design, *dark patterns* refer to practices that intentionally lead users to actions they would not otherwise take and that are often against their interests. You can find a carefully curated gallery of dark patterns on the website [Hall of Shame](https://hallofshame.design/collection/).

Dark patterns are manipulative and include cunning tactics such as:

1.  **Forced Continuity** ([Forced Continuity](https://davidmartinsonnyc.medium.com/ux-dark-design-pattern-forced-continuity-6c7af78682ad)): Users are forced to enter payment details for a free trial and it is difficult to cancel the subscription, leading to unwanted charges.

2.  **Hidden Costs** ([Hidden Costs](https://www.deceptive.design/types/hidden-costs)): During the purchasing process, additional fees appear only at the last stage, which were not previously apparent.

3.  **Nagging** ([Nagging](https://hallofshame.design/tiktok-nagging-dark-patterns/)): Constant notifications and pop-up windows that bother users to subscribe to newsletters or other services.

4.  **Misdirection** ([Misdirection](https://hallofshame.design/tag/misdirection/)): The interface is designed so that users accidentally click on something other than intended, often including buttons for subscription.

5.  **Preselection** ([Preselection](https://ux.stackexchange.com/questions/113871/dark-patterns-preselection)): A pre-checked checkbox that the user must actively uncheck if they do not want to agree to something, such as subscribing to promotional emails.

6.  **Clickbait** ([Clickbait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clickbait)): Intentionally misleading or exaggerated links and buttons that lure users into clicking on something that does not match their expectations.

Dark patterns exploit human psychological tendencies and inattention to achieve goals that are beneficial for the website operator at the expense of users. Dark patterns are generally considered unethical practices because they exploit user trust and manipulate their decision-making.

If you're interested in the topic, check out other definitions of dark patterns in UX design on the website [Deceptive Patterns](https://www.deceptive.design/types).
