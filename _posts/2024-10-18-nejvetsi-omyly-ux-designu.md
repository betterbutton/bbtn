# The Biggest UX Design Myths
> 

Many UX myths circulate that often influence design at the expense of user satisfaction. One of the most common is that *more options are always better*. This fallacy, however, can lead to user overwhelm, [writes Shaheer Malik](https://medium.com/design-bootcamp/8-great-ux-myths-busting-showdown-1714766a4526). [A study by Sheena Iyengar and Mark Lepper](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/12189991_When_Choice_is_Demotivating_Can_One_Desire_Too_Much_of_a_Good_Thing) from 2000 proved that too much choice paradoxically reduces user activity. Instead of an endless array of choices, designers should offer only the most important ones that users will truly appreciate.

Other myths include, for example, the famous rule of 7±2, which does not limit the number of items in navigation. Users know what they want, they just don't always know how to express it precisely, which is why research is necessary. Prototyping is not the end, but the beginning of testing. Users certainly don't reject scrolling, and the number of clicks is not a measure of success. UX design requires a blend of aesthetics and functionality and should not be sacrificed by an exaggerated pursuit of minimalism. Videos are not necessary if they don't improve the experience. More myths can be found in the article [10 popular UX design myths (and the truth they hide)](https://www.markup.io/blog/ux-myths/).
