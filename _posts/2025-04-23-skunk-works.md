# Skunk Works
> Skunk Works – the principles of secret development that show how to innovate outside bureaucratic constraints and corporate policies.

Imagine a team that can try a new idea without waiting. They don't need to ask for approval, they don't need to write reports, and no one is looking over their shoulder. A team that simply gets space, calm, and trust. And they try. They build. They test. And sometimes they also fail. All of this in a rapid sequence. This is how the story of the Skunk Works began – [Skunk Works](https://www.lockheedmartin.com/en-us/who-we-are/business-areas/aeronautics/skunkworks.html).

It was during World War II. Lockheed needed to build a new plane. Quickly. Without unnecessary paperwork. A small team was formed. It was led by the experienced aeronautical engineer Kelly Johnson. He had his own rules. They worked on the side, outside the main company. And within a few months, they were done.

The Skunk Works principle works to this day. In technology, it is used by companies like Google, Apple, or Amazon. The goal is simple: isolate a team, give them a meaningful task, reduce the noise, and trust that the people can do it. And they often surprise.

A similar Skunk Works in unmarked rooms outside the main building ten years ago helped Seznam avoid ending up like its former challengers Atlas and Centrum.

In the world of web services and applications, this is sometimes forgotten. Teams are overwhelmed with operations and organization – plans, sprints, meetings, reviews. And when someone has a good idea, they often have nowhere to try it. Skunk Works says: make space, set aside time, let people create on the side.

Working on developing new ideas is not the kingdom of chaos. It lies in concentration. In trust. And in the fact that new things often arise outside the mainstream. When you set up such a team correctly, something can emerge that would otherwise never have come into being.
