# If you think good design is expensive, try calculating the cost of bad design
> Bad design creates hidden and long-term costs that significantly exceed the investment in quality design.

> *You are opening the fourteenth window of the BBtn Advent Calendar for 2025. In this year's advent series, we'll look at popular UX designer proverbs. Today's is:* *If you think good design is expensive, try calculating the cost of bad design.*

If you think good design is expensive, try calculating the cost of bad design. This sentence belongs to the most quoted statements in the field of UX and digital product design. It is most often attributed to *[Ralf Speth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ralf_Speth)*, former CEO of the car manufacturer *Jaguar Land Rover*, and it spread in professional texts at the beginning of the second decade of this century. We will probably never find out the exact origin of the quote, but its meaning follows an older technical experience: that errors discovered late are exponentially more expensive than those fixed in a timely manner.

> “If you think good design is expensive, consider the cost of bad design.”\
> **— Ralf Speth**

The power of this statement lies in its practicality. Bad design rarely manifests as one big disaster. More often, it turns into a slow and inconspicuous leakage of money. Users [leave websites they don't understand](https://www.toptal.com/designers/ux/ux-statistics-insights-infographic#:~:text=presented%20in%20the-,infographic%20below,-at%20your%20peril), abandon forms they struggle to fill out, and distrust interfaces that don't inspire confidence. Every such abandonment means lost revenue, even if interest in the service or product exists.

Moreover, the costs are not limited to conversions. Unclear interfaces increase the number of support queries, create internal workarounds, and force teams to fix things that could have been solved during the design phase. Research in software development has long confirmed that problems found after launch cost significantly more than those caught during design.

Good design is therefore not an ornament. It is a tool for reducing risk, waste, and uncertainty. And [real case studies](https://www.toptal.com/designers/ux/roi-of-ux-redesign-case-studies) and [research confirm this](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/341318834_Economical_Aspects_of_UX_Design_and_Development). What is truly expensive is not investing in design, but paying for the consequences of its neglect in the long run.
