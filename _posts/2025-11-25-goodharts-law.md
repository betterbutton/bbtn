# Goodhart's Law
> When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.

[Goodhart's Law](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goodhartovo_pravidlo) ([Goodhart’s law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goodhart%27s_law)) states: *When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.* In UX design, this means an obsession with a metric like daily activity or *NPS* can distort the user experience instead of improving it.

![XKCD: Cueball defines Goodhart's Law. White Hat suggests offering a reward to anyone who discovers a metric that has become a target.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/61bc4854-5ef4-4eef-93a5-a7bc4e9c19c8_589x643.png "345x376")

[Goodhart’s Law](https://www.explainxkcd.com/wiki/index.php/2899:_Goodhart%27s_Law) on xkcd. (CC BY-SA)

For example, *Amazon* previously valued growth based on the number of new product pages. The result was a warehouse flooded with unwanted goods. They eventually switched to the *Fast Track In Stock* metric—tracking how many products were immediately available. This better aligned with actual customer needs.

Similarly, *Shopify* found that merchants optimized page speed scores without improving real user performance. Their response was to publish more meaningful performance metrics.

Product teams are under pressure to show results with numbers. But an overly narrow focus can lead to short-term tricks and overlooking quality. Trying to improve NPS can lead to unwanted review prompts. Boosting daily activity might mean annoying notifications. Success becomes a chase for numbers, not value.\
\
**How to counter this?**

- Combine different types of metrics (task success, satisfaction, feature usage)
- Listen to qualitative data (feedback, interviews)
- Build on user goals (Goal — Signals — Metrics model)
- Pair every target metric with a guardrail metric to monitor side effects

Metrics aren't the problem – the problem is blind trust in them. Use them as a compass, not an autopilot. They should serve people, not control them. Because when a metric becomes a mission, a product can succeed on graphs – but fail in the real world.
