# Mechanical Turk
> Amazon's Mechanical Turk platform uses human workers for tasks that artificial intelligence still can't master – a modern echo of an 18th-century illusion.

Amazon [Mechanical Turk](https://www.mturk.com/) is an online platform where people perform simple tasks that are still too complex for computers. These include labeling images, verifying data, or judging text tone. Although it appears technological, in reality, Mechanical Turk relies on human labor. It offers developers and app creators a fast, scalable way to obtain training data or test real user behavior.

The name references an 18th-century hoax. In 1770, inventor [Ján Vlk Kempelen](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_von_Kempelen) – a native of Bratislava – presented a machine in the Schönbrunn Palace in Vienna that was said to play chess. People were enthusiastic, and many long believed it was a truly thinking machine. In reality, a hidden chess player secretly controlled the machine from inside.

Today's Mechanical Turk works similarly. It looks like an automated system but actually relies on human judgment. For developers, it's a useful tool – especially when they need results quickly without hiring staff. But it raises questions: How to ensure quality? How to formulate the task correctly? And what about fair pay, when workers often earn just a few dollars per hour?

For web and app creators, Mechanical Turk is a valuable helper. But it's important to consider how to use it responsibly – so that the spirit of the Mechanical Turk doesn't remain just a deceptive trick.
