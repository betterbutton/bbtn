# SARAH: Shock, Anger, Rejection, Acceptance, Help
> A framework that explains how people emotionally react to change – and how designers can guide them through the entire process.

When a digital product changes, users aren't just learning something new – they are experiencing a loss. The *[SARAH ](https://www.intepeople.co.nz/leading-change-sarah/)– Shock, Anger, Rejection, Acceptance, Help)* model describes how people emotionally come to terms with a new interface, workflow, or technology. For UX designers, it's a practical way to anticipate reactions and ease the transition.

The SARAH model is based on *[Elisabeth Kübler­‑Ross](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elisabeth_K%C3%BCbler-Rossov%C3%A1)*'s theory of the stages of grief, adapted for common changes. The first phase is *shock* – users are confused, disbelieving, and overwhelmed. Then comes *anger*, expressed through complaints and frustration – a natural reaction to the loss of control. This is followed by *rejection*, where people look for logical reasons to resist the change or try to revert to the previous version. Over time, and with support, *acceptance* arrives – users begin to explore how to use the new interface. Finally, *help* comes, sometimes also hope – users get used to the change, help others, and see its benefits.

For designers, SARAH is more of a map than a procedure. Each phase requires a different approach: in shock, people need simple explanations; in anger, empathy; during rejection, patience and hints; during acceptance, encouragement; and in the final phase, recognition. Communication should evolve with emotions – from reassurance to partnership.

The model's power lies in understanding. It teaches us that resistance isn't defiance, but a natural reaction to change. If designers work with it consciously, they can turn resentment into acceptance – and make the change a smooth transition.
