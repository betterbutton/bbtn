## Conversion rate is not a business indicator

Conversion rate is a commonly used metric, but it rarely says anything substantial about business success. [Conversion rate](https://support.google.com/google-ads/answer/2684489?hl=cs) is considered a key performance indicator (KPI). It shows what percentage of visitors completed a desired action – such as registering or ordering. However, it says nothing about actual value, profit, or retention.

Because conversion rate is calculated as a ratio, it can be manipulated. You can achieve higher conversion rates by heavily filtering users coming to your site.

It might not seem that easy. But if you work with the [See-Think-Do-Care](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/See_think_do_care) (STDC) or [Attention-Interest-Desire-Action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AIDA_(marketing)) (AIDA) frameworks, you know you can limit investment in the early stages (See-Think and Attention-Interest) of user acquisition. Short-term, you'll get a better ratio. Long-term, however, you've neglected reaching new potential customers, which over time will manifest as a decrease in visits and, consequently, transactions.

Conversion rate has its place – as a problem-finding tool. It's useful for A/B testing or checking if something stopped working. But that doesn't mean it's a method for measuring company health – you shouldn't tie agency compensation to conversion rate without supplementing it with business metrics. It could easily happen that it will meet targets without having a positive impact on your business.

Real business indicators show value over time – such as customer value, margin, and net revenue. These are the indicators that tell whether a product makes sense, who it serves, and how it performs in the real world.

## Linkodrome

**Common metrics often fail to show the real impact of UX on users.** Jared Spool presents a framework of [outcome-driven UX metrics](https://articles.centercentre.com/what-are-outcome-driven-ux-metrics/) that measures specific improvements in people's lives. The key is to define the desired outcome – how life improves when the work succeeds – and assign three types of metrics to it: success, progress, and cost of poor UX.

**Figma is now designing us.** Figma, which started as a tool facilitating collaboration between designers and developers, is gradually becoming a tool that [dictates the creative process](https://designsystems.international/ideas/when-figma-starts-designing-us/). Design tools should support spontaneity and flexibility, not rigidity. Features like Auto Layout and Dev Mode steer designers toward excessive structure and optimization in the early stages of creation, limiting freedom and experimentation. This trend contributes to the uniformity of digital design and makes it harder to discover new, innovative solutions.

**The difference between listening to and obeying users.** Mark Roudebush [distinguishes between user-driven and user-centered design](https://medium.com/@markroudebush/user-driven-vs-user-centered-1332a0f9eaf0) using Wikipedia as an example. He criticizes that in the website's redesign, the product team yielded to user pressure. Mark defends designers as educated experts who base their designs on research, knowledge, and empathy. According to him, they should have communicated their steps better, but not allowed the Wikipedia community to take control. According to Mark, the process failed not in the design, but in the way change was managed.

**Research is no longer just for researchers.** Democratizing UX research means that designers or product managers also get involved in gathering user feedback – often with the help of AI. While this trend increases the availability of insights and saves researcher capacity, it carries the risk of lower research quality and skewed results. Qiwen Zhao suggests clearly dividing roles, training stakeholders, using AI as an assistant, not a replacement, and maintaining a high level of research. [UX researchers should embrace the role of mentors and managers](https://medium.com/@qiwenz95/learnings-from-the-trend-of-democratizing-ux-research-what-ai-means-for-researchers-d59165df4561) to maintain their influence and the quality of the process.

## Dean Allen – the man who simplified writing on the web

[Dean Allen](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/dean-allen) (1966—2018), a Canadian typographer and developer, fundamentally influenced the shape of writing on the internet. In 2002, he created the Textile markup language, which allowed easy text formatting without HTML knowledge. He subsequently came up with the Textpattern content management system, which emphasized writing itself. Allen's approach also inspired the creators of Markdown and WordPress. His work showed that technology should serve content, not complicate it.

## So we understand each other

[Lateral thinking](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/lateral-thinking) was popularized by *Edward de Bono* with the book [Six thinking hats](https://www.knihovny.cz/Record/mkp.4556895?sid=31562827). It is based on parallel consideration of issues from multiple angles, thereby preventing premature closure on new perspectives. Lateral thinking leads to reducing the complexity of a problem.

The principle of [progressive disclosure](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/progressive-disclosure) is probably well known from computer games. It is also used in user interfaces. It reduces mental load and increases the chance that the user won't get lost in the interface. Beginners appreciate the clarity, while experienced users value the ability to go deeper.

[User story](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/user-story) offers a description from the user's perspective and concisely answers three questions: *Who* is the user, *what* they want to do, and *why* it's important.
