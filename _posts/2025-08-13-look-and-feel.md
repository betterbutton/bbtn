# Look and feel
> Look and feel refers to the combination of visual style and interactive behavior that shapes user experience and brand identity.

The look and feel ([look and feel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Look_and_feel)) of a digital product—its visual appearance and the way it responds to user actions—plays a significant role in usability, brand recognition, and even legal disputes. Look encompasses colors, typography, shapes, and element layout. Feel describes how controls behave during interaction. Together, they influence how users perceive and operate software.

For information systems, a consistent look and feel brings two main benefits: it strengthens the brand and simplifies use. If a user knows one product, they can easily orient themselves in others with the same principles.

The term [look and feel](https://uxdesign.cc/in-defense-of-the-look-and-feel-608d139a60fc) doesn't just apply to graphics: interfaces maintain a consistent handwriting through a consistent visual language and structure, which facilitates their use.

Look and feel were also subject to intellectual property disputes. In the late 1980s and 1990s, Apple, Lotus, and Xerox sued competitors over user interface similarities. Some cases ended without major precedents but demonstrated the commercial value of design.

Whether it's software, documentation, hardware controls, or other interfaces, a well-designed look and feel enhances both quality and trust. A well-designed product is then not only functional but also natural and memorable.
