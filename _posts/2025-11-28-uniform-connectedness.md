# Law of Uniform Connectedness
> Visually connected elements are perceived as one meaningful whole – even without reading the content.

The law of uniform connectedness explains why people immediately group together elements that are visually connected—for example, within a frame, on a shared background, or linked by a line. This connection is stronger than proximity or similarity. It helps quickly understand what belongs together and what is related.

In practice, this means that when filters and an *Apply* button are in the same panel, the user immediately understands they are related. If a label, an input field, and an error message are within one frame, they are perceived as a unit. Tabs in an interface group multiple pieces of data into one clear whole. The same rule applies to highlighted rows in a table or individual steps in a multi-step process.

This principle originates in the psychology of perception. As early as the 20th century, Gestalt psychologists described how the brain groups elements according to patterns. And in the 1990s, *[Stephen Palmer](https://senate.universityofcalifornia.edu/in-memoriam/files/stephen-palmer.html)* and *[Irvin Rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irvin_Rock)* showed in their article *[Rethinking perceptual organization: The role of uniform connectedness](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/225549639_Rethinking_perceptual_organization_The_role_of_uniform_connectedness)* that we first divide a scene into contiguous areas—based on color, brightness, or structure. These areas form the basic perceived units. In user interfaces, this means connected elements are perceived as one whole before we read the text.

However, the law of uniform connectedness also points out errors. If elements sharing a background are unrelated, a false group can form. In responsive design, if frames break or shift, the relationship between elements can become unreadable. A simple test helps: remove the text and see what you perceive as a group.

When designers use connection consciously, the interface is clearer and users make fewer mistakes. The layout speaks for itself.
