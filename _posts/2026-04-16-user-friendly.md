# User-Friendliness
> User-friendly – a design ideal in which the system absorbs complexity so that people can act clearly, confidently, and without unnecessary effort.

The reference *[to Larry Tesler](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/larry-tesler)* can be summed up in a single idea: software should adapt to people, not force people to adapt to the software. This vision shaped his work at Xerox PARC and at Apple. It was there that he helped champion modeless interaction, cut/copy/paste operations, and the broader expectation that digital tools should be easily understandable, safe to use, and as minimally burdensome as possible for a person trying to get something done.

His most enduring insight is this: complexity never completely disappears. In Tesler's law of conservation of complexity, every system has a certain irreducible amount of complication. The real question, therefore, is not whether complexity exists, but who bears it—the user, the developer, or the platform itself. Good design shifts as much of that burden as possible away from the user.

This precisely explains Tesler's opposition to modes. In a modal interface, the same action can mean something different depending on the system's invisible state. The user then has to remember what state they are in to predict the outcome of the next step. Instead, Tesler advocated for modeless interaction: you click where you want to type, select what you want to change, and then perform an action. What today seems obvious in editors and interfaces was once a conscious and fundamental breakthrough.

For the web, this reference remains immediately relevant. The web is not user-friendly just because it looks simple or calls itself intuitive. It is user-friendly when navigation matches people's expectations, forms don't create unnecessary work, editing tools behave predictably, and mistakes can be easily undone. The burden should be carried by the system, not the visitor.

The term *user-friendly* later devolved into a cliché, but its original meaning still holds value. In the best sense, it is not a marketing label. It is an expression of respect for human time, attention, and effort. Tesler helped turn this claim into a norm. The web still often falls short of it, but the benchmark remains correct: the responsibility should primarily lie with those who design and build the system, not with those who merely need to use it.
