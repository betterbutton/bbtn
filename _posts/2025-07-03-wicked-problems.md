# Wicked Problems
> Poorly defined, mutable challenges without a clear solution that require continuous adaptation, not one-time fixes.

Wicked problems ([wicked problems](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/article/wicked-problems-5-steps-to-help-you-tackle-wicked-problems-by-combining-systems-thinking-with-agile-methodology)) are complex and ambiguous challenges that have no single correct solution. They commonly arise in digital product design, especially where technology intersects with social, behavioral, or legal systems. These problems change every time we attempt to solve them.

Features intended to increase clarity or convenience can lead to unintended consequences. Furthermore, user needs evolve over time. What works in one context may exacerbate inequalities in another. And what benefits one user group may hinder access for others.

The term "wicked problem" was introduced by [Horst Rittel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horst_Rittel) and [Melvin Webber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melvin_M._Webber) as early as 1973 in the paper [Dilemmas in a General Theory of Planning](http://magrawal.myweb.usf.edu/phd/articles/planning_wicked_73.pdf). They described situations lacking a clear problem statement, where the correctness of a solution cannot be unambiguously judged, and where every attempted remedy creates new troubles. Software development often exhibits precisely these characteristics—shifting goals, conflicting requirements, and incomplete information.

In 1992, [Richard Buchanan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Buchanan_(academic) connected wicked problems with design thinking. His article [Wicked Problems in Design Thinking](https://web.mit.edu/jrankin/www/engin_as_lib_art/Design_thinking.pdf) presents design not only as the application of known principles but also as the active creation of new meanings and solutions. According to him, design thinking enables understanding problems from various perspectives and driving innovation across disciplines. Buchanan views design as a tool for navigating complex technological culture.

Instead of seeking final answers, product teams should focus on flexible strategies—design thinking, user collaboration, and iterative development. Solutions should be open to change, context-sensitive, and ready for adjustment.

The point is not to eliminate complexity, but to learn to navigate it. A designer should recognize when they are facing a wicked problem—and adapt their approach accordingly.
