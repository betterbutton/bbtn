# Banner blindness
> Banner blindness is a phenomenon where users overlook parts of the interface that resemble advertising, even if they contain important or useful information.

Users often ignore elements that look like ads—even if they contain important content. This phenomenon, known as banner blindness ([banner blindness](https://www.publift.com/blog/reducing-banner-blindness)), is a [well-documented problem](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9128483/) in the field of user experience.

It originated in the 1990s when [eye-tracking](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/eye-tracking) revealed that people automatically skip parts of a page that look like banners. Today, it doesn't just affect ads but also common elements—such as alerts, cookie bars, prompt panels, instructional tips, or even search results ([pdf, 236 KB](https://www.ruf.rice.edu/~lane/papers/banner_blindness.pdf)).

The reason is selective attention. Users come with a specific goal and ignore anything that doesn't support that goal. If an element is in a location they associate with advertising, they often overlook it completely. This effect is stronger on mobile devices, where the screen is small and orientation is fast.

[How to prevent it?](https://blog.logrocket.com/ux-design/avoiding-banner-blindness-designing-attention/) The key is to avoid elements that look like classic banners. Important messages are better integrated into the content itself—where it makes sense and where the user is currently working. Timing, subtle visual distinction, and context also help. Ideally, the message addresses a problem the user is currently solving.

Banner blindness isn't a user error but a sign of their adaptation to a cluttered interface. The designer's goal isn't to shout louder, but to speak at the right moment. The best banner is the one that doesn't look like a banner at all.
