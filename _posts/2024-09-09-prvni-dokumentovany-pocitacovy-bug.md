# First documented computer bug
> 

77 years ago, on Tuesday, September 9, 1947 at 3:45 PM, an entry attributed to mathematician, programmer, and then-lieutenant in the United States Navy [Grace Murray Hopper](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grace_Hopperov%C3%A1) appeared in the [computer laboratory's diary](https://americanhistory.si.edu/collections/nmah_334663) at Harvard University: *"First actual case of bug being found."* The "bug," meaning an error, was literally a beetle. To be precise, a moth. And as proof of the found moth, the American scientists also taped it into the diary.

![Photograph of the aforementioned diary entry with a note about the bug and a taped-in moth.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/ebf184ed-243c-417c-9e76-49de44797aaf_2560x2025.jpeg "1456x1152")

Diary entry about the discovery of the bug

That moment [went down in computer history](https://lunduke.substack.com/p/the-story-of-the-first-computer-bug). Thanks to the careful record, we know the moth got stuck in relay 70 of panel F in the [Mark II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvard_Mark_II) computer. Finding the error was no picnic. Back then, the computer was the size of a small cottage and weighed 27 tons.

In the photograph, you can see the date and time, but the year and the type of computer are not visible, so besides 1947, you will also encounter the attribution of this event to the year 1945. The confusion was likely introduced by an article from the journal *Annals of the History of Computing*, which in the anecdotes [in the third edition from 1981](https://www.computer.org/csdl/magazine/an/1981/03/man1981030283/13rRUy2YLMQ) states precisely the year 1945 and the Mark II computer. However, the Mark II computer did not even exist at that time. The anecdote also states that it was hot then, and on September 9, 1945, it was only [18 °C](https://www.wunderground.com/history/daily/us/ma/cambridge/KBOS/date/1945-9-9) at 3 PM in Cambridge, Massachusetts, whereas in 1947 it was [24 °C](https://www.wunderground.com/history/daily/us/ma/cambridge/KBOS/date/1947-9-9).

The National Museum of American History, which [published the laboratory diary pages](https://americanhistory.si.edu/collections/nmah_334663) on its website, spoils the legend by noting that the handwriting of the note does not match Grace Hopper's handwriting. However, Hopper was one of the key members of the laboratory's staff and [contributed greatly to the popularization of this story](https://web.archive.org/web/19970430003658/http://www.waterholes.com/~dennette/1996/hopper/bug.htm), even though she sometimes described the moth as two inches long, which somewhat exaggerates the bug.

Technical errors, however, have been called "bugs" in English long before that. According to the article [Stalking the Elusive Computer Bug](https://www.computer.org/csdl/magazine/an/1998/04/man1998040005/13rRUwIF6b8), the term "bug" for technical errors was brought to Harvard by engineers from IBM while working on the predecessor of the Mark II computer, the Mark I. The word "bug" in the sense of a technical error also appears in Webster’s New International Dictionary from 1938. Before that, "bug" in the same sense appeared in The Standard Dictionary of the English Language from 1893 and 1895. Edison also used the word "bug" as early as the 1870s.
