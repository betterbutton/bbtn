# Closure Principle
> A Gestalt principle that shows how people perceive incomplete shapes as wholes, enabling the creation of intuitive and minimalist digital interfaces.

The principle of closure ([law of closure](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/law-of-closure?srsltid=AfmBOor2bqzI-GmQ2G1NnExnhsyqesVFLQq-LiVJSr0BhjpmPvg0oAHF)) comes from [Gestalt psychology](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/gestalt) and describes our natural ability to fill in missing parts of images. The brain creates a whole even when it sees only a part of it—a circle with a gap is still perceived as a circle. In design, this explains why simple and incomplete shapes are readable and aesthetically pleasing.

In UX and interface design, closure helps create clearer, faster, and more intuitive environments. Users themselves fill in visual gaps, which reduces mental effort and allows more to be conveyed with fewer elements. This principle underpins modern minimalist design—from icons with suggested outlines to layouts with large areas of white space.

![Material Design 3.0, fastfood icon](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/c9a7eab6-14fb-4a29-96e3-8d3d07b3d088_1024x1024.webp "208x208")

Icons for small display sizes often use Gestalt principles to create an image. (Material Design 3.0, [fastfood symbol](https://fonts.google.com/icons?selected=Material+Symbols+Outlined:fastfood:FILL@0;wght@400;GRAD@0;opsz@24&icon.query=fastfood&icon.size=24&icon.color=%23e3e3e3))

Examples are everywhere: the [WWF panda logo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:WWF_logo.svg) and [IBM stripes](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soubor:IBM_logo.svg) rely on closure; [Material Design icons](https://m3.material.io/styles/icons/overview) use shapes the brain completes; mobile carousels show part of the next card to indicate more content; card layouts often lack visible borders, yet the brain perceives them as separate blocks.

Designers use closure to enhance recognizability, guide attention, and facilitate orientation. But a balance must be found—too much abstraction can confuse users. Incomplete shapes should be familiar and easily understood. Besides visual relationships, contrast, proximity of elements, or supporting text also help. Content accessibility is also important—users with visual impairments must have the same information.

The closure principle shows that perception itself is a design tool. When a design aligns with how people naturally see the world, it feels natural, fast, and understandable. The designer doesn't have to show everything—suggesting and letting the brain fill in the rest is enough.
