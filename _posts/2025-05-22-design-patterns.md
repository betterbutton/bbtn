# Design Pattern
> Standardized solution that simplifies understanding of digital interfaces by standardizing solutions to common usability problems.

UX and UI [design patterns](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/ui-design-patterns) are proven solutions that improve the usability of digital interfaces. They offer familiar ways for users to navigate applications and websites. Examples include saving menus, introductory guides for new users, or smooth content loading without requiring clicks.

The term "design pattern" originates from architecture and urban planning, where it was first systematically described by Christopher Alexander in his 1977 book [A Pattern Language](https://www.patternlanguage.com/). In it, Alexander introduced the idea that complex systems—such as cities, buildings, or homes—can be designed using recurring solutions based on human needs and experiences. This concept later significantly influenced the field of software and digital design.

From a UX perspective, it's about how a user journeys through an entire application. Onboarding screens help understand basic functions. Information displayed progressively reduces cognitive load. And empty states without content can offer guidance on what to do next.

[UI patterns](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/design-pattern-guidelines/) focus on the layout and behavior of individual elements. Buttons, modal windows, scrolling galleries, and navigation bars help create a comprehensible environment. When used correctly, they speed up orientation, facilitate control, and increase user confidence.

However, some patterns can cause problems. For example, hidden menus can make important functions inaccessible. And infinite scrolling makes it harder to reach a page's footer or save content.

Design patterns are not rules that must be followed blindly. They are tools that a designer should choose based on the specific situation. Good selection and user testing will ensure the interface is not only familiar but also user-friendly.
