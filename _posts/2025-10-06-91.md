## Linkodrome

**How to design digital products that an ADHD brain can handle.** Lenka Stawarczyk translates her personal experiences with ADHD into concrete [recommendations for UX designers](https://www.lenkastawarczyk.com/blog/ux-pro-adhd-mozek). She describes six situations that make using digital products difficult for her – from slow page loading and inaccurate notifications to chaotic interfaces. An ADHD brain needs clarity, structure, and immediate feedback. The text advocates for designing accessible UX that helps not only people with neurodiversity but all users.

**The future of design is shaped not by Jony Ive but by Demis Hassabis.** Suff Syed writes that AI is changing the very nature of the user experience. Instead of curves and pixels, the [focus is shifting to designing behavior](https://www.suffsyed.com/futurememo/designers-should-look-to-demis-hassabis-not-jony-ive): human‑machine dialogues, rules, context, and alignment with user intent. Hassabis's arc from games to neuroscience to DeepMind sets a new leadership model. Designers should design intelligence, not just interfaces.

**Over 1200 parallel experiments in Google Search** show that Google isn't switching to new versions but is [constantly testing minor changes](https://searchengineland.com/inside-google-search-system-experiments-ai-agents-entities-459123). Key players are entities – structured information from the Knowledge Graph, which connects services like Search, Discover, and YouTube. Google also uses specialized AI agents (e.g., MedExplainer, NeuralChef) and tracks users via systems like Nephesh or VanGogh, which create vector behavior profiles. SEO strategies must adapt: it's not content quantity but semantic consistency and "validation" as an entity that matters.

**How to write and design for people with limited English knowledge.** Irina Morozova recommends writing simply, using meaningful headings, avoiding idioms and complex language, and considering multimedia accessibility. The key is to plan accessibility from the start and focus on intuitive design that [reduces cognitive load and enhances user autonomy](https://equalentry.com/accessible-content-limited-english-proficiency/).

**Claude Imagine generates software at runtime.** [Visionaries told us](https://www.ohad.com/2025/07/10/voidware/) not so long ago that AI would generate interfaces contextually at runtime, but that it would come this soon, we probably didn't expect. Anthropic released the *Claude Imagine* experiment to subscribers, which now demonstrates [generating interfaces at runtime](https://www.anthropic.com/news/claude-sonnet-4-5#:~:text=Bonus%20research%20preview).

**Indi Young introduces the method of emergent data synthesis.** She rejects predefined categories and interpretations, instead capturing the true meaning of what people convey. It's particularly useful when we want to understand deeper motivations and mental models of people we aim to serve. In each conversation, one can capture dozens of concepts expressing inner thoughts, emotions, or personal rules. The key is to summarize their true meaning – not what we think they mean. The method thus allows us to learn [how people actually think and how their mental worlds are similar or different](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/indiyoung_userresearch-activity-7366560339112443904-V3JM/).

**The new version of the WCAG 3.0 standard** brings a fundamental shift in the approach to digital accessibility. Instead of a checklist, real‑world product usability will be assessed – across websites, apps, wearables, and virtual reality. The new scoring in [bronze–silver–gold tiers will replace the existing A to AAA levels](https://www.w3.org/TR/wcag-3.0/). Greater emphasis is placed on user experience and the needs of people with cognitive disabilities. Companies should prepare now, as WCAG 3.0 redefines what it means to be inclusive. Accessibility is shifting from a compliance requirement to a performance indicator. [Chris White summarized the updates](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7372268697685774337/) on Linkedin.

**The seven principles of universal design** serve as [a guide for creating products](https://universaldesign.ie/about-universal-design/the-7-principles), environments, and communications accessible to the widest possible range of users. They include equitable use, flexibility, simplicity, perceptibility, tolerance for error, low physical effort, and size and space for approach and use. The principles are applied both when designing new solutions and evaluating existing ones, aiming not to disadvantage anyone based on physical, sensory, or mental abilities.

## Jesse James Garrett created a framework for UX

![Jesse James Garrett (AI-generated drawing based on photo)](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/ee78873b-e48f-4d8f-aa4a-750fb9f714cd_1536x1024.webp "1456x971")

Jesse James Garrett divides UX into five layers

[Jesse James Garrett](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/jesse-james-garrett) became an icon of UX design thanks to his ability to name, structure, and share key principles of the field. In 2000, he introduced the [diagram of the five planes of UX](http://jjg.net/elements/), later expanded in the book [The Elements of User Experience](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/1867.The_Elements_of_User_Experience), where he connects business goals with specific design layers. In 2005, he introduced the term [Ajax](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Glossary/AJAX) – an approach to web applications that enabled dynamic data loading without page refreshes. Garrett also created a [visual vocabulary](http://jjg.net/ia/visvocab/) for documenting interactions. His tools helped define UX as a profession – and their influence persists today.

## Toolbox

The [60fps.design](https://60fps.design) website is a carefully maintained gallery of microinteractions and motion details from digital design. It was born from frustration with the ephemerality of product videos, which often disappear after launch. It has gradually grown into an inspirational library of gestures, animations, and effects that typically go unnoticed. 60fps celebrates craft precision and reminds us that design is not just visual but also a motion experience. It targets developers and designers, offering a space to discover and preserve the beauty of digital details.

## So we understand each other

An [Application Programming Interface](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/api) (API) enables communication between applications. It's used to share data, perform actions across platforms, and streamline development. A well‑designed API adheres to standards, is versioned, has clear documentation, and ensures security. It facilitates service integration and supports automation. Conversely, a poor API can pose risks to both functionality and security. Do you have a public API? And can we see it?

The [Law of proximity](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/proximity) states that elements placed close together are perceived as related. By arranging elements, you can influence how easily users orient themselves in an interface. Properly applied proximity rules enhance usability and product credibility, even without prominent visual elements. Ignoring it leads to confusion and errors – especially on small screens.

The [peek and pop](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/peek-and-pop) design pattern allows quickly previewing content and opening a context menu without the user leaving the current screen. Originally associated with Apple's *3D‑touch*, it now also works based on the *touch and hold* gesture on Android, or *long press* on touch devices in general. The advantage is fluid app movement and clean interfaces; the downside is the difficulty of discoverable gestures.

[User testing](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/user-testing) is a simple and inexpensive way to uncover design flaws before launching a product. Even briefly observing five people often reveals critical usability issues. Without testing, feedback arrives too late and in much more expensive forms. Tests remind us that the error is not on the user's side but the system's. Therefore, it pays to test early and repeatedly.
