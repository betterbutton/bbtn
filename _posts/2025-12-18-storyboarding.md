# Storyboarding
> Displays the user scenario over time and helps understand context, motivation, and consequences before the interface or code is created.

[Storyboarding](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/article/ux-storyboards) in UX design is a quick and inexpensive way to understand how a product will be used in real life. It shows a specific user, a specific goal, and a specific situation step by step. It focuses not on screens, but on situations.

The foundation of a storyboard is a short sequence of simple panels with very concise descriptions. Each panel captures one moment. What is happening, where it is happening, and what the user is thinking or feeling. The drawings are intentionally rough. Their purpose is not appearance, but comprehensibility.

Storyboards are useful because many usability problems are not caused by the interface, but by the context. Websites and apps are used in a hurry, with distractions, on small screens, or in unfamiliar environments. Wireframes and user flows often hide these circumstances. Storyboards, on the other hand, reveal them.

For people responsible for websites and digital services, storyboards are also a powerful tool for team alignment. Designers, developers, and managers all work with the same story. Discussions then focus not on personal preferences, but on specific usage, pain points, and risks.

In the UX process, storyboards have the greatest benefit after research, during idea exploration, and before prototyping. They translate research findings into believable situations, allow for comparing different approaches, and timely reveal weak assumptions.

A good storyboard focuses on one scenario. It uses a minimum of details, a clear sequence, and short descriptions tied to design decisions. Both excessive ornamentation and an overly broad scope reduce its value.

A storyboard is not a solution for every problem. For complex, dynamic systems, it must be combined with other tools. But if you need to think about real-world usage, few methods are as effective and accessible.
