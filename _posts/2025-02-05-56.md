# Linkodrome

**Returning to craft usually signifies an inability to deliver work with impact.** Peter Merholz returned from the [Design Leadership Summit](https://designleadership.io/) with the conviction that designers who are fixated on returning to honest craft are merely masking their inability to design with real impact. [He writes about it in Tuesday's newsletter.](https://buttondown.com/petermerholz/archive/tma-quality-craft-reframe-your-mandate/) Design in many companies has been reduced to a production function, and craft has become an escape mechanism for teams that have no influence on key decisions. A panel discussion on craft revealed that designers refuse to define quality specifically, which undermines their credibility.

**Not all SEO advice is equally reliable.** Marek Prokop on Linkedin is pondering [the reliability of advice given by SEO consultants](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7292494621253722113/) to their clients. According to Marek, recommendations vary based on the certainty of their success. The most reliable are fixes of clear technical and content errors – these always improve the site. The second level consists of best practices, which usually lead to improvement but can have short-term fluctuations. The least certain are experiments. They can bring great results but also failure. – We designers have two favorite proverbs: *"It depends."* and *"Trust me."* In SEO, it will probably be similar.

**The right to user-friendliness.** Lawyer Petra Dolejšová and designer Ondřej Ilinčev jointly tackled [the biggest UX pains](https://www.petradolejsova.cz/blog/checkbox-kam-se-podivas) in design caused by legal requirements. Checkboxes on websites are often unnecessary, but legal departments push them everywhere – even where they harm UX and performance. A legally valid opt-in does not mean endless checkboxes – a clearly formulated active step by the user is enough. Examples of good practice show that consents for newsletters, registrations, and cookies can be handled more elegantly. The key is to seek a balance between legal certainty and user-friendliness.

**The future that wasn't.** Do you still remember the time when it wasn't clear that a mobile phone is a rectangle filled with a touchscreen with a cutout for the camera lens? [The Nokia Design Archive](https://nokiadesignarchive.aalto.fi/) brings contemporary documents from the years 1992 to 2013. Besides photos of actual phones, you can rummage in the archive through sketches and rendered images of visions of mobile devices that will never see the light of day.

# To Understand Each Other

Article titles from the web design glossary could be read as a sentence: [A Letter from the Future](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/dopis-z-budoucnosti), [People Don't Read on the Web](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/lide-na-webu-nectou).

People do read on the web. After all, you are proof of that right now. But we approach reading a bit differently than content creators might imagine. This article discusses three-pass reading and offers recommendations on how to accommodate it.

A Letter from the Future is a variant of a brief that, unlike more formal methods, has a chance for the team to truly engage with it, remember it, and work with it throughout the product development. Ultimately, it will also serve well as a metric for verifying the result.
