# Law of Similarity
> A Gestalt principle whereby people perceive similar elements as related, making interface navigation and use easier.

The Law of Similarity ([law of similarity](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/law-of-similarity)), one of the fundamental [Gestalt psychology principles](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/gestalt), states that people naturally group elements that look alike—for example, those with the same color, shape, or size.

In user interface design, this principle helps us show [what belongs together](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principles_of_grouping). When buttons have the same appearance, people assume they perform similar functions. Likewise, form fields with identical borders are perceived as part of a single group.

Properly applied similarity increases clarity and reduces the demand on the user's attention. This makes it easier to navigate the interface and complete tasks faster. For instance, if all links look the same, users easily recognize them as links without having to think about it.

Similarity also serves to create a natural visual hierarchy. When headings of the same level share a consistent style, users instantly recognize them as equivalent. Conversely, deliberately breaking similarity—for example, by using a different color for a primary button—helps draw attention where it's needed.

However, it's important not to overdo it. If different functions look the same, users might confuse them. And too much uniformity can blur the structure and make navigation harder.

The Law of Similarity is a powerful tool. When visual similarity matches actual function, the interface feels natural and is easy to use.
