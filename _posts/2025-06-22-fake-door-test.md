# Fake Door Test
> A quick and cheap way to validate interest: offer a feature that doesn't exist yet and collect contact info from users who want to try it.

[Fake Door Test](https://medium.com/design-kisk/fake-door-testy-v%C3%BDhody-a-%C3%BAskal%C3%AD-13d8a2303618) ([fake-door test](https://www.command.ai/blog/painted-door-testing/)) is a simple way to find out if users are genuinely interested in a new feature. You add a button to your app or website that promises something—like *"Try our new AI assistant"*. But instead of the actual feature, clicking it shows a message like: *"We're still fine-tuning it. Want to know when it's ready?"*

This method lets you verify whether people would actually use the feature without much effort. If many people click the button, it's a good signal. If they also leave an email or feedback, you can proceed with more confidence.

However, it's important to be fair. If users discover you promised something that doesn't actually exist, it can undermine trust. That's why it's always better to add an explanation—perhaps a short message or a form where they can sign up for a waitlist.

The fake-door test method is popular with product teams that want to follow [evidence-based design](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/evidence-based-design). It helps validate interest before investing weeks or months of development. But it must be used thoughtfully—and especially, if interest is confirmed, it can't stop at the test. Then it's time to move from promises to results.
