## Will We All Create Our Own Software with AI?

Today, supposedly, everyone can create their own application. Just say what you need, and AI tools for [vibe coding](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/vibe-coding) will generate the code themselves. You don't need to know how to program. You don't need technical education. Just an idea and a prompt.

A moment ago, we were sitting in a café near Anděl with a blind respondent after accessibility testing for a bank, who enthusiastically showed us a text-based game from a medieval setting. With the help of Github Copilot in VS Code, he has been creating it since mid-July completely without programming knowledge.

> “With AI, everybody will make their own software\
> just like how we all 3D print our own furniture.”\
> **— [Eren Bali](https://x.com/erenbali/status/1957488321099399296)**

It sounds like a revolution. But we had similar hopes with 3D printing. We also thought we would manufacture furniture or spare parts at home. The result is not mass production in garages. Just a few enthusiasts, the rest keep buying from IKEA.

And it could be the same with software. Yes, AI today can create a blog, a simple app, or a chatbot. But most people won't even start. Others will give up after a few attempts. They don't want to deal with bugs, dependencies, or security. They don't want to think through the feature set. They want something that just works – without programming, setup, and tuning.

![Sam Altman: Entering the fast fashion era of SaaS very soon](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/0593c7b5-4cf7-4afe-95af-2022cc899821_1100x280.png "564x143")

Sam Altman [expects the imminent arrival of fast fashion](https://x.com/sama/status/1952084574366032354) in SaaS.

Some visionaries predict the end of an entire category known as enterprise SaaS. Why should companies subordinate themselves to processes rigidly embedded in software when internal processes are often part of the know-how? Large companies have customized their ERP to their needs before. But will small and medium-sized entrepreneurs have the energy for it?

It's similar with our blind respondent who is writing his own computer game: *“The hardest part isn't programming it. The hard part is keeping it meaningfully together. I've already written the sixth version from scratch in a month.”*

> “Most people just have no idea how hard it is to take a product from zero to one.”\
> **— [David Finnegan](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7363502508951900162/)**

So what awaits us: Rather than custom development, there will be greater adaptability. Applications will transform into tools that understand voice, gestures, or simple requests. We won't create software, but modify it to suit ourselves.

AI will thus lower not only technical barriers. It will mainly change the way software is controlled. Instead of creating it, we will shape it. However, it will require something from us users. If [opinionated software](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/opinionated-software) is replaced by AI, we will have to tell it how we want the processes inside the software to work. And that's not exactly easy.

The future does not belong to the people's programmers. It belongs to users to whom technology will adapt.

**Marek Prokop: How to Survive the SEO Revolution.** The legendary SEO specialist calls the advent of AI the [greatest turning point in SEO history](https://www.cesky-hosting.cz/clanky/novinky/seo-od-roku-2025-nejvetsi-revoluce-za-ctvrt-stoleti-1257/). > “The most valuable skill in 2026 will be knowing when not to use AI.”\

**Checklist for Navigation Menu:** Page Laubheimer offers [17 principles for designing navigation that is understandable and functional](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/menu-design/). The main principles: do not hide the menu on large screens, adhere to expected placements, ensure readability and contrast, always clearly show where the user currently is, and use familiar and specific item names. Avoid multi-level cascading menus, small clickable areas, and innovative flashy solutions that confuse rather than help. The goal is simplicity, consistency, and easy orientation across devices.

**Three Levels of Design According to Don Norman,** which [shape our emotional experience with products](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/article/norman-s-three-levels-of-design?srsltid=AfmBOoonAJrEFq-yVhnyJSSgzwVl_Lszgsg-cY70EHmhTKN18q0RdQDc). *Visceral design* focuses on appearance and immediate emotional impression – hence the role of colors, shapes, or style. *Behavioral design* relates to functionality and ease of use; a good user experience brings joy, while complex control evokes frustration. *Reflective design* represents the conscious plane where we consider how the product fits into our life, identity, and social image.

**How to Measure Design System Usage:** Tony Ward describes [a new way to audit design system usage](https://www.tonyward.dev/articles/a-new-way-to-audit-usage). Instead of counting components, he recommends visually highlighting them directly in the interface. According to Tony, this approach better shows the real impact on users and return on investment. He practically proposes a tool that colors components on the page, ideally as a browser extension or connected to end-to-end tests. Thanks to screenshots, reports can then be easily created, savings tracked, and unused opportunities uncovered. Design system adoption will thus become more visible and understandable to managers.

**Validate Business Ideas in Three Days.** Erika Hall debunks entrepreneurs' excuses for not doing research: On the first day, use the Ethnio tool to recruit respondents. On the second day, ask them a single question – *“Walk me through your yesterday.”* And on the third day, analyze what you discovered. The whole process costs less than ten thousand crowns and requires no special expertise – just willingness to listen. [Result? Quick insight into customers' lives](https://medium.com/mule-design/minimum-viable-ethnography-a047e9358df0), which reliably tests business assumptions and often brings new ideas on how to better ground a product or service in reality.

**Research Through Design.** Roman Sellner Novotný proposes [a new framework for documentation and theory building](https://medium.com/design-kisk/z%C3%A1znam-nevysloven%C3%A9ho-framework-pro-teorii-v-research-through-design-f18170d0d506) within research through design. It is often perceived as methodologically ungrounded. Roman connects practice with theory. [The result is a canvas](https://miro.com/app/board/uXjVJcWnuYM=/?share_link_id=274409217628) that, using types of mediation and reflective questions, allows recording new ways of knowing generated by design artifacts.

> “Your old site is the best prototype of your new site.”\
> **— [Hoa Loranger](https://www.nngroup.com/videos/old-website-best-prototype-new-website/)**

**Stéphanie Walter explains the process for creating accessible color palettes.** She always starts from her base color and uses the Figma plugin [Color ramp generator](https://www.figma.com/community/plugin/1482371446196684379/color-ramp-generator) to generate dark and light variants. She then checks if they meet the recommended contrast ratio of 4.5:1, even between the primary color and shades of gray. For verification, she uses [a contrast check plugin](https://www.figma.com/community/plugin/937465522075454889/color-contrast) and tests the final colors directly on components. She [shares her process as inspiration](https://bsky.app/profile/stephaniewalter.bsky.social/post/3lp7mqkodqv2s) for designers who want to align aesthetics with accessibility.

**A misconception arises that meeting WCAG 2.2 guarantees compliance with the European Accessibility Act (EAA).** In reality, WCAG is only part of the puzzle – [EAA goes much further.](https://www.getstark.co/blog/wcag-vs-eaa/) It does not address only UI elements but the entire lifecycle of digital products: from marketing through interoperability to customer support. Key standards are [EN 301 549](https://www.etsi.org/deliver/etsi_en/301500_301599/301549/03.02.01_60/en_301549v030201p.pdf) and [EN 17161](https://accessible-eu-centre.ec.europa.eu/content-corner/digital-library/en-171612019-design-all-accessibility-following-design-all-approach-products-goods-and-services_en), which establish accessibility as an organizational process similar to security or privacy management. It's time to abandon the checklist approach and build a permanent infrastructure for accessibility.

## Bert Bos – Unassuming Co-Creator of CSS

![A drawing shows Bert Bos sitting on the ground leaning against a wall. On his lap is a laptop plugged into an outlet. Next to him lies a canvas bag and a PET water bottle stands.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/ba78de0b-4a16-4491-ba1a-c8dba29db52b_1536x1024.webp "586x390")

[Bert Bos](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/bert-bos) is a co-creator of CSS.

[Bert Bos](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/bert-bos) (*1963) became famous as a co-creator of CSS. Together with *Håkon Wium Lie* he promoted the idea of separating web content from its visual appearance, which resulted in the W3C specification in 1996. Previously, Bos developed the [Argo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argo_(web_browser) browser with a built-in styling system that foreshadowed today's browser extensions. At W3C, he advocates not only for the development of further CSS versions but also for web accessibility and multilingualism. Thanks to his vision, CSS became the universal language of web appearance used by all modern websites.

## To Understand Each Other

[Law of Prägnanz](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/pragnanz) is a principle of Gestalt psychology that explains why we prefer simple and stable shapes over complex and chaotic ones. The brain automatically fills in missing patterns and groups elements to save energy. In design, this means that clean and consistent interfaces feel natural, while unbalanced spacing or poor alignment confuse and tire. Simplicity is not just an aesthetic value but a practical way to reduce users' mental load and create interfaces that are used intuitively.

The term [look and feel](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/look-and-feel) combines a product's visual appearance with its interactive behavior. Colors, typography, or layout create the look, while responses to user actions shape the feel. Together they influence both usability and brand identity. Consistent look and feel simplify orientation and build trust.

[Wizard of Oz](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/wizard-of-oz) allows testing product features even before development – the user has the impression of interacting with a finished system, but responses are actually prepared by a person. This illusion provides a quick and cheap path to valuable feedback, uncovering weaknesses and validating assumptions. The advantage is easy variability and the possibility of immediate adjustments.

[Empty state](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/empty-state) occurs when an app has nothing to display – upon first use, after deleting content, or during a search with no results. Instead of a blank screen, offer a clear explanation and guidance on what to do next. A well-handled empty state is an opportunity to build a relationship with the user and strengthen their trust.

[Baader-Meinhof effect](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/frequency-illusion) is a cognitive bias where something newly noticed seems to appear at every turn. It is a combination of selective attention and confirmation bias. The brain focuses on what we are currently perceiving and overestimates its frequency.
