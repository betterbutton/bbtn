# KISS – Keep It Simple and Stupid
> KISS is a principle that says simplicity is not about poverty but about natural usability.

When something doesn't work, it's often not because something is missing, but because a person gets lost in it. That is precisely why the [KISS design principle](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/article/kiss-keep-it-simple-stupid-a-design-principle?srsltid=AfmBOorBo5htNfsc4z0Lx5u-vdJX515NksR00HnUuG0xkEf8nc_CpEMS) was created. It means: [keep it simple](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/KISS) ([Keep It Simple and Stupid](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/keep-it-simple-stupid?srsltid=AfmBOopbOraLD0cDYDgSx67RNjx_N9qeInCrEhiRTAOKPPAy4sprlUxr)). Don't make things more complicated than they need to be. It's not about cutting everything down to the bone. It's about making sure a person understands what to do – without a manual and without searching.

> The KISS principle originated in the late 1950s in the United States, specifically within the American Air Force. Authorship is attributed to engineer [Kelly Johnson](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clarence_Johnson), who worked at the legendary development division [Lockheed Skunk Works](https://www.armadninoviny.cz/dukazy-pribyvaji-americane-stavi-hypersonicky-bombarder-sr-72.html). It was there that a series of technologically groundbreaking yet reliable aircraft were created. Johnson coined the idea that systems must be simple enough that an average mechanic could repair them under combat conditions – without the need for complex training or tools.

When a user interface designer adheres to simplicity, they ask: Does this make sense? Is it clear? Is it overloading the person with unnecessary choices? Does it force decisions where it shouldn't? Great products aren't stupid – they just don't make the user feel stupid.

Keeping things simple is hard. It's a question of self-discipline. You must resist the temptation to add another option or step just because you can. You must have the courage to say no. And above all – you must think with the eyes of the person seeing your application for the first time.

Simplicity is not a trend. It's a sign of respect. For the time, attention, and nerves of those who will use your product. And when you're not sure if you've over-engineered it, try asking yourself a simple question: Couldn't this be said better – and more simply?
