# Walled Garden
> A carefully managed digital ecosystem where platforms set the rules, access, and content, thereby fundamentally shaping the experience for both users and developers.

[Walled Garden](https://365tipu.cz/2016/02/01/tip397-co-je-to-walled-garden-a-proc-je-to-spatne/) ([walled garden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_platform)) is a digital environment in which one company has full control over what users see, how they access content, and how they can use it. The term is sometimes also translated as *closed ecosystem* or *closed platform* in contrast to open systems on the World Wide Web.

Typical examples of walled gardens are Apple's App Store, Meta's platforms, or Amazon's marketplace. Such systems limit external influences and set clear rules for everyone who wants to be inside. Characteristic of walled gardens is the requirement of membership for both accessing information and publishing.

For developers and creators, it is a convenient environment. They gain access to a massive audience, a secure payment system, and a unified interface. But with that come limitations. Nothing can be published freely, earnings are subject to fees and approval, and visibility depends on algorithms that cannot be influenced.

However, the walled garden model is not just about security or convenience. It is primarily about power. Whoever controls access can set fees, track user behavior, and build dependency on their environment. The higher the wall around the garden, the harder it is to leave.

All this represents a key question for anyone designing digital products today. Is it worth trading freedom, independence, and control for comfort and reach? Some accept this trade-off. Others look for a way out—for example, through the open web.

Understanding how walled gardens work and what drives them is crucial. That is because it stands on the very ground we walk on—or the wall we may one day have to climb over.
