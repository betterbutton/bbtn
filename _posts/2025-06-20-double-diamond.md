# Double diamond
> The double diamond is a framework for designing products and services that visually separates the problem description in the brief from the creative solution.

The design framework [double diamond](https://www.designcouncil.org.uk/our-resources/the-double-diamond/) was introduced by the British Design Council in 2005. It offers a clear and understandable framework for solving complex problems with an emphasis on the user's perspective. Two consecutive phases alternate between expanding and narrowing approaches. The divergent and convergent approach was inspired by Béla Banáthy and his [systemic design](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/3372745). The first phase focuses on understanding the problem, the second on designing and delivering the solution.

![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/491529a5-1d4a-4eab-a16c-e7936a4aac15_5638x3800.webp "5638x3800")

Double diamond (illustration © [Kateřina Čápová](https://katerinacapova.cz/75-tipu/))

The framework consists of four stages: *[discover]*, *define*, *develop*, and *deliver*. In the first stage, designers try to understand the people for whom the design is being created, using user research. Then they move to defining the problem: the team summarizes the findings together and creates a clear brief.

In the third stage, a geyser of ideas with solution proposals emerges. Idea generation, prototyping, and testing take place. In the final stage, the solution is verified, refined, and prepared for implementation. This rhythm—from open exploration to targeted decision-making—supports both creativity and efficiency.

The main advantage of the double diamond is that it helps design products that truly solve the right problems. It ensures that teams do not skip crucial steps of understanding and verification. The model is also flexible: phases can be adapted or repeated according to the project's needs.

The double diamond is ideal where thoughtful work and collaboration with various professions are required. It works as both a map and a way of thinking: first understand, then design. Verify before you launch.
