# Moravec's Paradox
> An insight from robotics explains why AI can write medical reports but stumbles at walking – and what it means for designers.

Today, artificial intelligence can write an essay, create a program, or pass a medical exam – yet it still struggles to pour a cup of coffee or understand speech in a noisy room. This surprising imbalance is known as [Moravec's paradox](https://www.zive.cz/clanky/ctyricet-let-stary-moravcuv-paradox-vysvetluje-proc-ai-umi-hrat-sachy-ale-nedokaze-slozit-pradlo-ci-namazat-toast/sc-3-a-238006/default.aspx) and offers crucial lessons for designers creating interfaces for AI-driven systems.

The term was coined by roboticist and native of Choucna, Austria, *[Hans Peter Moravec](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Moravec)*. He describes that *abstract reasoning is easier for machines than basic perception or movement*. The reason is evolutionary: nature honed sensory perception and motor skills over hundreds of millions of years, while conscious thinking is a recent addition to our brain.

In practice, this means large language models can analyze texts or plan steps, but humanoid robots still grapple with walking, grasping objects, or navigating their surroundings. Even in 2025, physical interaction remains the main bottleneck for automation.

This is where user experience designers come in. Moravec's paradox highlights what humans are irreplaceable for – adaptation, intuition, working in context – and where machines need guidance. Designing interfaces that combine AI's computational power with human intuition is a new challenge.

Whether it's interacting with robots, spatial interfaces, or physical controls, UX is moving from the world of screens into the realm of movement, space, and collaboration. Moravec's paradox won't disappear – but by understanding it, we can design technologies that truly take people into account.
