# Human-centered design
> Human-centered design – an approach to design that emphasizes human needs and experiences and places humans at the center of every decision.

Human-centered design ([human-centered design](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/human-centered-design)) is a design approach that puts people first. It's not about people learning to control complex technologies. On the contrary. Technologies should be designed to meet human needs, emotions, and abilities. This approach was significantly developed by Donald Norman. The firm IDEO brought it into broader practice, showing how to design with a human dimension from the first idea to the final solution.

The main principle of human-centered design is simple. First, find out what people really need. Then quickly create prototypes and test them as early as possible in real life. The [IDEO process](https://www.designkit.org/) divides it into three steps: first we seek inspiration, then we generate ideas, and finally we implement them.

If you like standards, you'll also find human-centered design in [ISO 9241 Ergonomics of human-system interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_9241) as part of [9241-210 Human-centred design for interactive systems](https://www.nist.gov/itl/iad/visualization-and-usability-group/human-factors-human-centered-design). According to this standard, system design should always be based on an explicit understanding of users, their tasks, and the environment. Users must be involved in the design process from the very beginning, not only when the product is almost complete. Designs must evolve iteratively, in cycles where each round of testing and feedback improves the final outcome. The standard also emphasizes that design must focus not only on functionality but on the overall user experience, i.e., the feeling that using the system evokes.

The ideas of human-centered design emerged as early as the 1970s and 1980s. At that time, researchers began to notice more that people don't use technology as the instructions say they should. [Lucy Suchman](https://www.lancaster.ac.uk/sociology/people/lucy-suchman) showed that human behavior is not precisely planned. We respond to situations as they arise. [Bill Moggridge](https://billmoggridge.com/) and [Bill Verplank](https://www.linkedin.com/in/bill-verplank-0b2b43123/) then pushed design even further. They focused on how we naturally communicate with technology.

Together they laid the foundation for the idea that technology must be understandable and accessible. Today, other approaches such as *user-centered design* or *design thinking* are also associated with human-centered design. And although they differ in details, all have the same goal: to create things that serve people, not the other way around.
