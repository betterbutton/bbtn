**Write understandable error messages.** The error should help, not just scare.\
\
❌ "Form error. Try again."\
✅ "The password is too short. Use at least 8 characters."\
\
👉 The user understands exactly what is wrong.\
👉 They get to a successful submission faster.\
\
A good error message is a concise guide, not a cry into the void.

## What will happen to open-source libraries for the web

![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/533223c0-5979-4f19-b8df-4845df26f565_800x533.webp "506x337")

What will happen to open-source development in the era of coding AI agents?

If you didn't miss the mention in the *[In a Nutshell](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/i/182974152/telegraficky)* section about [75% of developers that Tailwind Labs just laid off](https://devclass.com/2026/01/08/tailwind-labs-lays-off-75-percent-of-its-engineers-thanks-to-brutal-impact-of-ai/), you suspect something is happening around open-source software. The Tailwind crisis shows the fragility of open-source in the AI age. AI coding agents love using open-source. But they bypass [premium paid services](https://www.smalltechbusiness.com/monetizing-open-source-tailwind/), thanks to which open-source development is sustainable. The Tailwind case shows how problematic such a model can be.

Tailwind Labs laid off three-quarters of its developers after its revenue plummeted by eighty percent—despite record usage of its popular CSS framework. Why? The main users of Tailwind are no longer people, but coding AI agents. They can generate front-end code at scale, but pay nothing. They don't need support, [don't buy UI components or order consultations](https://tailwindcss.com/plus). This collapses the business model based on paid services.

Tailwind CSS is a free open-source tool. The company sustained itself with add-ons and premium products. However, Copilot, Replit, Claude Code, Cursor, and other AI-driven tools use it without any feedback or revenue.

Vercel's CEO *Guillermo Rauch* promised that [further Tailwind development will be financially supported](https://x.com/rauchg/status/2009336725043335338). But the question remains: If most open-source is used by machines today, who will pay for it all?

Proposals are emerging for paid APIs or access restrictions for bots. But this could undermine the very principle of open-source. [The developer community thus faces a fundamental challenge](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7415115103953477632/): adapt to an era where users are not people, or gradually disappear from the scene. And that would be a shame for all of us.

## Linkodrome

**Ideas are cheap, execution is ~~everything~~ also cheap.** For a long time, it held that ideas are cheap and execution is everything. The advent of LLM tools completely dismantled this old truth. The decisive [skill is no longer knowing how to code, but iterating quickly](https://davekiss.com/blog/ideas-are-cheap-execution-is-cheaper/), having taste, knowing how to choose the right problem and reach the right audience. The most valuable becomes judgment and the ability to decide what is even worth building, says *Dave Kiss*. So not much changes after all.

**You cannot design software you don't use yourself.** We don't entirely disagree with this: You don't need to have cancer to find a cure for it. Fortunately, *Sean Goedecke* meant it a bit differently: Meaningful software design is only possible when [the designer actively participates in developing](https://www.seangoedecke.com/you-cant-design-software-you-dont-work-on/) the given system. Without direct responsibility for implementation, the design loses meaning and impact. And that makes sense to us too.

**How to grasp your career in 2026?** *Vitaly Friedman* offers designers an annual retrospective. He emphasizes the importance of a lateral move instead of blindly climbing the ladder and [urges designers to actively reshape their roles](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/product-designers-career-paths-vitaly-friedman-ahedf/). He sees AI as an opportunity to deepen the human approach, not as a replacement. According to him, the key to success is clarity about where we want to move.

**How design is changing in the AI era,** describes one of the world's first model designers *Barron Webster* from Figma. He reveals [why designing AI-first products is fundamentally different](https://www.aidesignfieldguide.com/articles/barron-webster)—instead of traditional UI, designers must delve into model behavior, evaluations, and prompts. On the website *[AI Design Field Guide](https://www.aidesignfieldguide.com/)*, articles from *Nate Parotta* from *Anthropic*, *Amelie Wattenberger* from *SHV*, or *Maddie Simens* from *OpenAI* are already waiting in stock.
