# Participatory Design
> Participatory design is a design approach where people directly participate in the creation of a product or service. This results in solutions that work better in the real world.

**Participatory design** or also **co-creation** ([participatory design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Participatory_design)) means that people are involved in the design from the very beginning. Not just as test subjects, but as equal partners. Together they sketch designs, try out ideas, or test prototypes in everyday situations.

This approach originated in Scandinavia in the 1970s. Today it is used in designing websites, applications, public services, but also buildings or educational programs. The basic idea is simple: The people who will use the result have experiences that designers themselves cannot gain.

It's not just about being fair. What's important is that it actually works. When you design something together with users, you uncover errors earlier. You find out what is understandable for people. The result is a product that better meets their needs and expectations.

But it also has its demands. Collaborative work takes more time. Sometimes it turns out that people want something that isn't easily feasible. And the designer must learn to listen more than to decide.

But when it succeeds, the result is worth it. Better usability, more satisfied users, and fewer blind alleys. [Participatory design](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/participatory-design) is not just a method. It is a mindset. It means inviting people to the table even before the first design is ever created.
