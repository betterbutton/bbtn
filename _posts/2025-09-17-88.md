**Generative AI is dramatically transforming how people search for information.** A study by the Nielsen Norman Group shows that even though search habits are deeply ingrained, AI tools—from overviews in Google to chatbots like ChatGPT or Gemini—save users time and energy on more complex tasks. So far, AI isn't replacing traditional search, but it acts as a first point of contact with information and is increasingly changing established routines. Meanwhile, popularity and trust in specific [tools often hinge on simple familiarity and habit](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ai-changing-search-behaviors/).

**Microcopy must inform, influence, and navigate.** According to the Nielsen Norman Group, [microcopy has three main goals](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/3-is-of-microcopy/): to inform, influence, and support interaction. Microcopy simplifies decision-making, increases conversions, and helps with orientation in the interface. Its effectiveness doesn't lie in where it appears (e.g., tooltip, CTA, or headline), but in its intent. Successful microcopy is always targeted—rather than saying everything, it says what's essential clearly and understandably.

**When animations improve the user experience and when they harm it.** Emil Kowalski explains that animations can make the user interface more pleasant, clearer, and faster—but only if they have a clear purpose and proper timing. They are meaningful when they explain something, support spatial orientation, or add a subtle sense of liveliness. Conversely, for frequently repeated actions or keyboard shortcuts, it's better to omit them because they slow down and fatigue. Key factors: purpose, frequency, and animation speed. Properly set [animations enhance trust and perceived speed](https://emilkowal.ski/ui/you-dont-need-animations), inappropriate ones deter.

## To Understand Each Other

[Point-and-click](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/point-and-click) interfaces revolutionized computer control. They replaced text commands with visual elements: icons, buttons, or windows. They first appeared in the 1970s at Xerox PARC, but achieved mass adoption with the Macintosh in 1984.

[Survivorship bias](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/survivorship-bias) distorts our decision-making by showing us only successful cases and ignoring those that failed or disappeared without a trace. It's not enough to monitor satisfied users; key insights are often hidden in incomplete registrations and abandoned apps. True understanding comes only when we ask about the causes of failure, not just the stories of winners.

[Voice and tone](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/voice-tone) are crucial for how people perceive a digital product and whether they trust it. Voice is the consistent brand personality, while tone changes depending on the situation. Together they create an impression of humanity and safety—especially when something goes wrong.

[A walled garden](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/walled-garden) is a digital space where the platform fully controls access, content, and rules—examples include the App Store, Meta, or Amazon. Users and creators find comfort and security here, but at the cost of dependency, fees, and limited freedom.
