# Assistive Technology
> Tools like screen readers, captions, and keyboard controls help people with disabilities access digital products – if developers meet them halfway.

[Assistive Technology](https://speechify.com/cs/blog/what-is-assistive-technology-and-how-can-it-help-me/) ([assistive technology](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/assistive-technology-definition-and-safe-use/assistive-technology-definition-and-safe-use)), such as screen readers, voice recognition, or captioning tools, enable people with health impairments to use websites and mobile applications. These tools are essential for users with visual, hearing, motor, or cognitive impairments – but they only work correctly when developers and designers prepare a suitable environment for them.

For example, screen readers require well-structured HTML code, proper labels for forms, and understandable headings. Without these, navigating the content is confusing or impossible. People who use only a keyboard rely on a logical key sequence, visible element highlighting, and the ability to skip repetitive page sections. Captions and text-to-speech help people with hearing or reading impairments, while voice control is important for users with limited mobility.

Yet, accessibility is often pushed to the end of a project or considered merely a legal necessity. As a result, digital services can be non-functional or very difficult to use for some users.

Availability, however, should not be seen just as a problem-solving measure. It is an opportunity to improve usability for everyone – whether it's larger fonts, higher contrast, or easier navigation.

Developers and product teams should think inclusively from the very beginning. When accessibility becomes a natural part of design and development, better, more user-friendly products are created for all users.
