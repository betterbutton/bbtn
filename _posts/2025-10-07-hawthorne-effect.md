# Hawthorne Effect
> When people know they are being observed, they behave unnaturally, and UX researchers have to account for this.

When people know they are being observed, they behave differently. This is known as the [Hawthorne effect](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawthornsk%C3%BD_efekt) ([Hawthorne effect](https://catalogofbias.org/biases/hawthorne-effect/)). The term originated in the 1920s during research at the Western Electric Hawthorne Works in Chicago. Researchers were studying whether changes in the environment, such as adjustments to lighting, affected workers' productivity. It turned out that workers increased their output with any change in conditions—whether the light was brighter or dimmer. The reason was not the environmental change, but the feeling that the researchers were interested in them.

In UX research, this phenomenon still appears today. People in usability tests try to do things correctly, speak carefully, and sometimes even give overly positive feedback. It's not insincerity, but a natural human reaction to attention.

Related to this phenomenon are other terms. The [observer's paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observer%27s_paradox) refers to any change in behavior caused by the act of being observed itself. The [observer-expectancy effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observer-expectancy_effect), sometimes also called the [experimenter effect](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Efekt_experiment%C3%A1tora), occurs when a researcher's expectations influence participants' behavior or the evaluation of results. The experimenter effect is a broader term—it encompasses all the ways a researcher can unconsciously influence outcomes through their approach.

The difference lies in where the influence comes from: The Hawthorne and observer's paradox relate to the participants, while the observer-expectancy and experimenter effects relate to the researcher. Together, they remind us that UX research is not an objective measurement, but a social situation where people influence each other.

An experienced researcher can mitigate these influences. They speak calmly, avoid judgment, test in natural settings, and combine various methods. The awareness that observation changes behavior is not a weakness of UX research, but its strength—because it leads to a deeper understanding of how people actually behave.
