# Statistical Significance Does Not Belong in Qualitative Research
> 

James Weaver's article points out that [the pursuit of statistical significance in qualitative research](https://medium.com/@uxsprintlab/the-misguided-pursuit-of-statistical-significance-in-qualitative-research-a-call-for-rethinking-e8e565b196d6) is a mistake. James emphasizes that qualitative research is meant to uncover nuances in user behavior, not to find general trends.

> “Qualitative research is for discovering and understanding the ‘why’ and ‘how’ of user behavior.”\
> **— [James Weaver](https://medium.com/@uxsprintlab/the-misguided-pursuit-of-statistical-significance-in-qualitative-research-a-call-for-rethinking-e8e565b196d6)**

An excessive number of interviews not only wastes time and resources but also distracts attention from real insights. He similarly warns against long schedules that slow down product development and weaken the ability to respond quickly to market changes. Qualitative research should provide efficient and valuable insights that product teams can apply quickly.

The number of respondents is also addressed in the popular article by Jakob Nielsen, [Why You Only Need to Test with 5 Users](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/why-you-only-need-to-test-with-5-users/), which shows through meta-studies that in qualitative research, insights begin to repeat quickly, and therefore a smaller number of sessions with respondents is sufficient. Or as [Běla Beránková](https://cz.linkedin.com/in/belaberankova) says: *“How many cars have to hit a pothole on the road for it to be conclusive that the pothole is really there?”*
