# Perceived Affordance
> Perceived affordance refers to the possible uses of an object that stem from its properties. The concept is key to intuitive design.

Perceived affordance (English [affordances](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UtulTXJLGOI)) represents the possibilities for use that arise from the essence or properties of an object. You look at an object, and you immediately know what to do with it. Perceived affordance is one of the prerequisites for an interface to be intuitive.

Size, lightness, and roundness predispose a ball to be kicked rather than a cannonball. The flatness of the head and the pointed tip of a hammer indicate where to strike a nail. An elevator button invites pressing more than a backlit number on milky white glass.

Perceived affordance is culturally and historically conditioned. Before 1990, it would not have occurred to anyone that underlined text on a screen is a hyperlink.

The term affordance was defined in 1979 by psychologist James Jerome Gibson in the book [The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception](https://archive.org/details/pdfy-u5hmFOvOM2Civ4Gz). A decade later, Don Norman brought it into the realm of human-centered design. It entered pop culture through [Norman's mème about doors](https://www.google.com/search?q=norman+doors&udm=7). A vertical handle suggests the door should be pulled, a recessed one that it slides, a kickplate signals the door should be opened with a foot. Perceived affordance helps overcome the [gulf of execution](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/gulf-of-execution-and-evaluation).

**Translation Note:** Whenever we use the term *perceived affordance*, we partly smirk at the clumsy translation of the English term *affordances*, and partly laugh nervously because we have no better translation. It appears in the text [Intention, Affordance, and the Meaning of Cultural Objects](https://digilib.phil.muni.cz/sites/default/files/pdf/123942.pdf), published in 2009 by Ladislav Kesner. Kesner offers a second translation variant: *perceived possibility*. In the text, he freely alternates between perceived affordance and the Czech-spelled *affordance*.

At the same time (2010), Ondřej Hrabinec grappled with the need for translation in his bachelor's thesis [The Motivational Structure of the Game World of Warcraft](https://dspace.cuni.cz/handle/20.500.11956/29575?show=full). He chooses *offers for action*, only to never use it again in the text. The word *affordance* has become naturalized in Czech professional literature with Czech spelling. For better clarity, we recommend forgetting attempts at translation.
