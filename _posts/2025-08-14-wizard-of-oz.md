# Wizard of Oz
> Simulating product functions with a hidden human operator to gather feedback before development.

The Wizard of Oz method ([wizard of Oz](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/wizard-of-oz-prototypes)) allows you to test an idea without having to build the product right away. The user feels they are working with a finished system, but in reality, a person is preparing the responses instead of a server. This makes it possible to gain important insights early on—without high costs and lengthy development.

The main advantage is speed. You can observe real user behavior, validate assumptions, and uncover usability weaknesses before writing a single line of code. Because the simulated system can be adjusted instantly, designers can easily test different versions and quickly discard those that don't work.

Wizard of Oz pairs perfectly with paper prototypes, which the wizard can prepare in real time right in front of the experiment's respondents.

The method is particularly useful where automation is expensive or complex—for example, with voice assistants, chatbots, or smart devices. Instead of building a fully functional AI-driven healthcare assistant, a "wizard" in another room could write responses to user queries in real time. The user sees the system working smoothly, while the team notes how people ask questions and which features they try.

Sometimes the Wizard of Oz method is confused with [concierge MVP](https://codup.co/blog/what-is-concierge-mvp/). While Wizard of Oz is an experiment with a prototype, concierge MVP is a business validation of an entrepreneurial idea. Wizard works with user research participants; concierge MVP serves real customers.

Ideally, it's best to maintain the illusion. If users start to suspect a person is behind the system, their behavior may change and the results won't be credible. The wizard must therefore adhere to the constraints a real product would have.

The Wizard of Oz method doesn't replace development itself, but it's a safe way to bridge the gap from idea to reality. It helps uncover design flaws before time and money are spent, and ensures the final product is not only functional but also understandable and user-friendly.
