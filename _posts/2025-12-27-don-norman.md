# Don Norman – designing with responsibility in everyday life
> He shifted responsibility for errors, usability, and ethics from users to design itself. He defined the intellectual foundations of user experience and opened the question of moral responsibility in design.

![Don Norman (illustration generated from a photograph)](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/daea6833-91a7-49a9-8326-bb5453b2d024_800x533.webp "550x366")

*[Donald Arthur Norman](https://jnd.org/)* (*December 25, 1935) is most commonly associated with one simple, yet radical idea: when people fail, the design failed first. The principle, formulated most clearly in the book *[The Design of Everyday Things](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/840.The_Design_of_Everyday_Things)*, changed how we view design. Doors that confuse, interfaces that deceive, or systems that punish errors are not accidents. They are the predictable result of poor design decisions.

> “Good design is actually a lot harder to notice than poor design, in part because good designs fit our needs so well that the design is invisible,”\
> **― [Donald A. Norman](https://www.goodreads.com/work/quotes/18518-the-psychology-of-everyday-things), The Design of Everyday Things**

From this premise grew a lasting intellectual framework. Norman gave designers precise language – [affordances](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/affordances), signifiers, feedback, constraints, mapping, and conceptual models – enabling the systematic analysis of interaction failures, rather than post-hoc excuses. These concepts didn't just improve interfaces; they legitimized design as a discipline grounded in cognitive science, not taste or intuition.

In the 1990s, during his time at *Apple*, Norman coined the term user experience. He didn't mean screens. He meant the entire arc of experience: expectations, learning, errors, recovery, and memory. User experience, in this conception, is the cumulative judgment formed over time – and it is often shaped most profoundly by moments of failure.

In his later work, Norman pushes boundaries further, from human‑centered design to so-called humanity‑centered design. Attention here expands from the individual to systemic impacts: the environment, social justice, and long-term sustainability. Design, according to Norman, is never neutral. It always carries values – and designers bear responsibility for the worlds they help shape through their decisions.

Norman's legacy, therefore, is not a set of rules, but an attitude: rigorous, human, and ethically demanding.
