# Adele Goldberg as the Godmother of the Graphical Interface
> She co-created the modern graphical user interface with windows, icons, menus, and a mouse cursor.

![Adele Goldberg](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/f1856b44-da85-4705-83ab-cc7c6be01282_1536x1024.webp "1456x971")

Adele Goldberg popularized the graphical interface

**Adele Goldberg** (*July 22, 1945) as a member of the three-person team at Xerox PARC in the 1970s played a key role in the creation of the graphical user interface. Today's computer interfaces are based on the concepts from that era. She contributed to the design of the [Smalltalk](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smalltalk) environment, which introduced windows, icons, menus, and a mouse. This model, referred to as [WIMP](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/WIMP), remains the core of operating systems to this day.

Her work transformed abstract operations into easily understandable actions. Overlapping windows allowed users to work on multiple tasks at once. Icons represented objects, dragging files enabled simple manipulation, and menus presented choices directly on the screen. This made computers understandable even for ordinary people.

To her team, Adele brought [Alan Kay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alan_Kay), who was impressed by her work at Stanford University in the field of computer-supported learning. Kay was looking for someone to help him realize his vision of a [Dynabook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynabook) – a portable computer for children of all ages, which later inspired the creation of [PDAs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Newton) and tablets.

In 1979, [Steve Jobs](https://www.apple.com/stevejobs/) visited PARC. Adele, initially reluctant, had to show him Smalltalk. The [Macintosh](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macintosh_128K) interface, created shortly thereafter, adopted many of her ideas and spread them worldwide.

> “The satisfaction comes from being part of a really creative group of people who not only invented an idea, but made it real enough that other people could take advantage of that idea in a way that was enormously empowering.”\
> **— [Adele Goldberg](https://ethw.org/Oral-History:Adele_Goldberg#:~:text=The%20satisfaction%20comes%20from%20being%20part%20of%20a%20really%20creative%20group%20of%20people%20who%20not%20only%20invented%20an%20idea%2C%20but%20made%20it%20real%20enough%20that%20other%20people%20could%20take%20advantage%20of%20that%20idea%20in%20a%20way%20that%20was%20enormously%20empowering.)**

Adele was not only a co-creator but also an advocate for a new approach. Together with David Robson, she wrote the book [Smalltalk-80: The Language and Its Implementation](http://stephane.ducasse.free.fr/FreeBooks/BlueBook/Bluebook.pdf) and other works that documented not just the language but also the environment. Thanks to them, developers around the world learned to understand not only programming but also a new way of working with computers.

Her focus on usability stemmed from the goal of making computers accessible *to children of all ages*. By designing software as an easily understandable and visual environment, she preceded the field of user experience design by decades.

Today, when we move a window across the screen or select from a menu, we carry Adele Goldberg's legacy within us. Her vision transformed the computer into a tool for learning and creativity, not just for computation. And for everyone designing new interfaces today, her work remains proof that the right interaction can change the world.
