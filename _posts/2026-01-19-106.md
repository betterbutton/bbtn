## Everyone Builds Their Own Software

![Generated drawing shows a young woman at a bar. An androgynous robot is serving her. The woman says: I'm imagining something like my personal dashboard.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/00ae18e2-016f-438b-b08d-be5e4c249405_724x478.webp "724x478")

Last week, another twenty multimedia students in Martin's course learned [to create interactive websites using only prompts](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7415163006310932480/) given to *Github Copilot* in *VS Code*. The results were web applications using a database and published on platforms like *Vercel* and similar.

The goal of the vibecoding exercise was to teach future multimedia creators that they can prototype their ideas directly in code instead of *Figma*. However, a side-effect occurred: the students realized that using programming AI agents can solve some of their needs for which they couldn't find suitable ready-made products. They embraced the new trend of micro-apps.

This is no coincidence. [Vibecoding](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/vibe-coding) and AI-supported micro-apps are changing software from something we download into something we assemble ourselves. That shift affects how tools are created, distributed, and maintained. It also transforms the role of web professionals. Tools like *Cursor* or *Claude Code* allow describing intent in natural language and getting a functional application. Vibecoding leads to a boom in micro-apps: small, highly personal tools designed for one person, one situation, one problem.

It's not primarily about a technological novelty, but a change in behavior. Instead of the question *"What app should I download?"* comes the question *"What if I create it myself?"* Tools created this way usually don't target the mass market or monetization. They are created because a spreadsheet is no longer sufficient and a commercial product is unnecessarily cumbersome. They fill the gap between a document and software. A typical example is the *[GSC Opportunity Mapper](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/gsc-opportunity-mapper)* application we wrote about last week.

Currently, the bottleneck preventing wider adoption of micro-apps is the deployment hurdle. Platforms that combine generation, hosting, and publishing allow turning an idea into a web app in minutes, and an AI agent can handle deployment for the user. The user often has no idea how the app got on the web. Thanks to easy sharing, the web is once again becoming a natural distribution layer. In contrast, app stores with fees, approvals, and restrictions are poorly suited for personal and ad-hoc creation styles.

But there are, of course, risks. Code generated by AI commonly contains security bugs and structural weaknesses. People without formal training cannot assess what they are actually publishing, especially if the application handles sensitive data. Another problem is maintenance and development.

A new role for specialists is opening up. People come with ready-made prototypes created using AI. The real value shifts to cultivating these tools for understandability, accessibility, security, and long-term sustainability. Less building from scratch, more expert judgment.

Software is becoming a malleable material. Once people get used to tools adapting to them, their expectations and approach to software will completely change, and there will be no going back.

## Linkodrome

**How to effectively improve visual design** through practical training, repetition, and mindful reflection is broken down by *Filippos Protogeridis*. [Start by mastering the basics](https://blog.designary.com/p/the-practical-guide-to-improving) (typography, color, hierarchy), studying design languages (e.g., Apple HIG, Material 3), and practical creation: imitating existing interfaces, remixing your own work, or designing concepts outside standard assignments. Filippos emphasizes that visual skills are not gained by studying theory, but by repetition and observing differences.

**Design tokens are portable units of design decisions** that connect designers and developers across platforms. *Francesco Improata* explains [the difference between code and tokens](https://designtokens.substack.com/p/using-design-tokens-as-variables) and describes best practices for using them in Figma, CSS, and Sass. Focus on clear semantics, version control, structured naming, and automation.

![A mobile phone alarm offers the option to snooze for $10.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/ccee7b95-139c-4e93-9be5-9d795071d539_949x2048.jpeg "240x517")

[Pay to Snooze](https://x.com/Karim_RC/status/2011069617855479888/) could be the new business hook.

**Figma isn't dead, but the designer's role is changing.** *Thomas Budiman* responds to the provocative claim that Figma has lost its purpose and develops a deeper reflection on AI's impact on creative work. He points out that [tools change, but design as a process remains](https://designbuddy.substack.com/p/is-figma-dead). AI pushes boundaries and lowers barriers, which benefits juniors, but leaves room for seniors where intuition and understanding are needed.

**The job market for designers in 2026 has fundamentally changed the rules.** Companies are looking for specialists skilled in AI with a clear business impact, not generalists, writes *Tom Scott*. [Show results, strategic thinking, and adaptability.](https://verifiedinsider.substack.com/p/the-playbook-to-finding-a-design) Portfolios must be targeted and measurable, supported by prepared videos, presentations, and concise summaries. Personal branding and visibility on networks are the path to offers.

> “If you want to stand out as a designer, please get yourself a custom domain and email address. out of the 575 applicants, only 28 had both.”\
> — [Jesse Vermeulen](https://x.com/jesse_vermeulen/status/2011736102210806130?s=12)

**The shift in design system design from static components to adaptive interfaces** is a trend described by *Yesenia Perez‑Cruz*. The new generation of [UI must focus on product primitives](https://yeseniaperezcruz.substack.com/p/when-the-output-becomes-the-material) – objects that users create (e.g., a task, a discount) – and adapt the visual language and behavior to them across different contexts. AI can use context (intent, work phase, permissions) and dynamically change the interface based on the user's experiences and needs.

**Just‑in‑Time UI** represents a new design approach that, instead of static screens, offers users dynamic, context-sensitive interfaces, writes *Andrew Sims*. With AI's help, the interface becomes flexible, proactive, and capable of leading a conversation, not just reacting. [The user experience is shifting from predetermined paths to living interactions](https://signalpath.substack.com/p/just-in-time-interfaces) that adapt to the user's needs, intentions, and situation in real time.

[Tu du du dum.](https://x.com/pmddomingos/status/2010833505207415075/photo/1)

**It's time to rethink UX metrics.** Traditional UX metrics like task success or user satisfaction are no longer sufficient. Important are [outcome-focused metrics](https://uxuniversity.io/p/how-to-set-ux-metrics-that-matter), connected to the business, shared across teams, and tracked over time. UX doesn't stand on the number of tests, but on what changed because of them.

**Card decks help in writing, research, and leadership.** Three popular decks: *[Storyteller Tactics](https://pipdecks.com/pages/storyteller-tactics)*, *[Laws of UX Cards](https://lawsofux.com/cards/)*, and *[The Leader’s Toolkit](https://leadertools.co/products/the-leaders-toolkit)* help *Andree Chiarelli* overcome the blank page effect, focus, and act more effectively. [Each deck provides concrete tools](https://andreachiarelli.substack.com/p/thinking-with-decks): from creating strong stories to improving user experience to everyday managerial situations.

*[Jeffrey Zeldman](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/jeffrey-zeldman)* was seventy-one. At a time when the web relied on proprietary solutions, he advocated for web standards. Through initiatives like *A List Apart*, *Web Standards Project*, or the *An Event Apart* conference, he connected the community and promoted knowledge sharing.

*[GSC Opportunity Mapper](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/gsc-opportunity-mapper)* converts exports from *Google Search Console* into an actionable SEO plan. It helps prioritize existing content by potential and highlights weak spots, such as competing pages for the same query, low CTR, or missing internal links.

[HTML](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/html) is the language that defines the meaning and structure of web content. It doesn't deal with appearance or behavior but describes what the content is. Semantics serves both accessibility and SEO. HTML was created as part of *[Tim Berners-Lee](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/timbl)*'s vision and its development is today managed by the W3C.

[Conversational interfaces](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/conversational-ui) allow controlling a website or service with natural language instead of classic menus. Language models increase its flexibility but don't make it magic. It must work with context, be transparent, and handle errors. A good interface combines free text with controlled elements, thereby reducing error rates and supporting orientation.

[Content delivery network](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/cdn) (CDN) is a distributed network of servers that ensures the delivery of web content as close to users as possible. It reduces loading time, decreases the load on the origin server, and increases stability even under demanding conditions. It acts as a cache and primarily serves static content.
