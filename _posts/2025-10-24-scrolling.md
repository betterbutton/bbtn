# Scrolling
> Scrolling is the fundamental method of page navigation that allows access to content beyond the visible screen – it originated from the limitations of early computers and evolved with touch and the web.

[Scrolling](https://jecas.cz/znazorneni-scrollovani) (in Microsoft's localization [posouvání](https://learn.microsoft.com/cs-cz/cpp/mfc/scrolling-and-scaling-views?view=msvc-170), in English [scrolling](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/saving-scroll-position/)) is derived in English from ancient scrolls ([scroll](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scroll)). If we wanted to create a Czech term by calque, it would be "převíjení" or "rolování", but if you like jokes, feel free to call it [svitkování](https://x.com/tomaskafka/status/1977731799066264010). In earlier English-written literature, you will also encounter the term [panning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panning_(camera)) ([švenkování](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panning#:~:text=Term%C3%ADn%20panning%20je%20odvozen%20od,je%20pot%C5%99eba%20krat%C5%A1%C3%AD%20expozi%C4%8Dn%C3%AD%20%C4%8Das.)), which is borrowed from the horizontal (panoramic) movement of a camera in film.

Scrolling is a key mechanism in user interfaces that allows movement through content that does not fit on a single screen.

Scrolling first appeared in computer graphical environments in the 1970s at the *Xerox PARC* research center. It was also common on terminal screens, such as the *[DEC VT100](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/VT100)* from 1978.

Later, it became the default method of control on the web and in mobile applications. The transition from visible scrollbars to gesture control transformed not only how content is accessed but also how designers indicate that content is longer than what is displayed.

![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/baf68651-9dd3-4a38-befb-1298a7a7ce1a_1475x1230.webp "506x421")

[Explanation of scrolling](https://twitter.com/superbetsy/status/1977515783631835210/photo/1) in the user manual for the Macintosh computer in 1984.

Modern scrolling began with the scrollbar in the [WYSIWYG](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/wysiwyg) editor *[Bravo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bravo_(editor))* in 1974 and in the *Smalltalk* system at the turn of the 1970s and 1980s. *Apple Lisa* and *Macintosh* further developed it. In the 1990s, [Eric Michelman](https://www.linkedin.com/in/eric-michelman-93751b10/) created the [mouse wheel](http://www.ericmic.com/history%20of%20the%20scroll%20wheel.htm) for better movement in Excel, which accelerated and refined scrolling on the desktop.

With the arrival of the *iPhone* in 2007, scrolling became a smooth touch gesture accompanied by inertia and animations. Visible scrollbars almost disappeared.

The web was initially divided into short pages. From the mid-2000s, long content with infinite scrolling began to appear – this was invented in 2006 by [Aza Raskin](https://web.archive.org/web/20060507143945/http://humanized.com/weblog/2006/04/25/no_more_more_pages/), after he was frustrated with clicking through paginated search results on Google. It increases user engagement but makes orientation, returning, and a sense of closure more difficult.
