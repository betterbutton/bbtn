# Linkodrome

**The color selection tool in the form of a color wheel** is indispensable for designers. It facilitates the selection of harmonious color palettes. You'll find it in every modern design tool. Figma, however, additionally [devotes an entire section to the color wheel](https://www.figma.com/color-wheel/) on its educational website.

![A black and white illustration of the color wheel Newton invented with seven colors labeled. ](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/fd7208f2-8a7d-4343-b804-3a3179d705d1_438x450.png "438x450")

Sketch of the color wheel from Isaac Newton's book *Opticks, or, A treatise of the reflections, refractions, inflections and colours of light* published in London in 1704.

The [color wheel was first proposed by Isaac Newton](https://library.si.edu/exhibition/color-in-a-new-light/science) [in 1666](https://study.com/academy/lesson/who-invented-the-color-wheel.html#:~:text=How%20did%20Isaac%20Newton%20discover,color%20wheel%20he%20first%20devised.). Two centuries later, colors were also addressed by Johan Wolfgang Goethe in the book [Theory of Colours](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_Colours). The book contains several sketches that are [colored in surviving copies](https://archive.org/details/ZurFarbenlehre00GoetA/page/n35/mode/2up), which was quite unusual for that time.

![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/69090e52-138b-4a26-9f4a-dbbaa2eebef6_600x913.jpeg "336x511")

Symmetrical color wheel by J. W. Goethe

To this day, the [colour picker](https://www.google.com/search?q=colour+picker) helps in creating visually appealing designs. The standard color wheel contains 12 colors divided into primary, secondary, and tertiary. Modern wheels often include a broader spectrum of shades, tones, and color variations, which is particularly useful in digital and UI design. The use of color in design significantly influences user emotions and helps tell a visual story.

**There's a nice typographic easter egg on Google.** [Veronika Burian](https://fonts.adobe.com/designers/veronika-burian) is a great typeface designer. She founded the type foundry [Type Together](https://www.type-together.com/) with [José Scaglione](https://fonts.adobe.com/designers/jose-scaglione), and their fonts like Adelle, Athelas, or Iskra are among the best that digital typography has brought to the world. Some Type Together fonts are also available on [Google Fonts](https://www.google.com/search?q=site%3Afonts.google.com+%28%22type+together%22+OR+%22typetogether%22%29), so you can use them to elevate the quality of your presentations and applications.

And where is the joke? When you [search for Veronika's name](https://www.google.com/search?q=veronika+burian) or [José's](https://www.google.com/search?q=jos%C3%A9+scaglione), the search results are displayed in the font [Bree Serif](https://www.type-together.com/bree-serif-font) from their workshop. A similar monument was also given to the author of the Verdana font, [Matthew Carter](https://www.google.com/search?q=Matthew+Carter), and according to online reactions, also to other typeface designers, but we haven't managed to trigger the easter egg for them. If you [like](https://www.google.com/search?q=times+new+roman) [fonts](https://www.google.com/search?q=Amatic+SC+font) [in general](https://www.google.com/search?q=courier+font), you'll find more similar jokes on Google.

# Life with Technologies

In our field, we often use the term [technical debt](https://www.strafelda.cz/technicky-dluh). It refers to potential future costs for reworking completed work. It arises from prioritizing quick project completion over a well-thought-out long-term design. However, this assumes that the project will function and evolve long enough to require rework. Many projects simply end. They die. Efficiency and a pragmatic approach are more worthwhile for them.

![Comic panel shows a dilapidated house in the background and two bricklayers talking in the foreground: "I don't understand why it takes so long to install a new window?!"](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/aad09ff4-1772-494b-86c5-49d29cd12969_968x855.jpeg "376x332")

Sometimes, the course of events renders even a well-designed architecture obsolete. I always remember this when we travel by car with the whole family on European highways in the summer. Somewhere you pay with a sticker stuck to the windshield, and customs officers check it with binoculars. Elsewhere, meanwhile, they have introduced costly toll gates because each highway is operated by someone different and it's fair to pay based on actual usage.

Instead of customs officers with binoculars, attendants sit at the gates. They no longer need to give change to drivers because card payment was introduced, but they still deal with some technical issues. Card payment was replaced somewhere by boxes with a radio signal. But imperfectly. And so we have a gate with a terminal and a queue of cars, around which owners of wireless [gadgets](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gadget) whiz.

And then there's the license plate recognition with online payment. To the chagrin of vignette sellers and toll gate builders, it's not only the most advanced technology but surprisingly also the cheapest. This can also happen with digital technical debt. Simply, the new solution is so cheap that the old one can still be pragmatic.

And how do they view all this in countries where highway tolls are not collected? We drove through Poland from Czech Královec in the south to Russian Královec in the north without encountering those two toll highways, and on the way home we returned via Germany. While somewhere developers are considering the best options, elsewhere they have built a dense highway network in the meantime.

# Tip from Our Book

**Hire a guide for website creation**

![A tour guide with a wireless microphone in hand, a speaker hanging around her neck, and an umbrella over her head stands on one foot on a box labeled "UI Tours".](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/ab363c4b-5d60-47a6-853b-16519d6df73c_3800x3800.jpeg "440x440")

Illustration: [Kateřina Čápová](https://www.katerinacapova.cz/) ([75 tips on how to ask for a website](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/kniha))

Whether you're diving into website development yourself, have your own development team, or are collaborating with an external agency, it's certainly worth considering hiring a professional consultant with whom you can continuously go through the individual project steps.

You won't have to rely solely on your own judgment, and when you encounter a problem, you'll have an experienced person to turn to. A quality guide will help you prevent complications thanks to their experience from various areas of website creation. They will advise you on choosing the most suitable practices that will lead to achieving the expected results.

The guide, thanks to their expertise, will also ensure greater respect from suppliers and can increase pressure to maintain quality. You'll appreciate this especially in situations where you encounter unexpected problems or need to challenge the level of deliveries.
