# Paul Fitts – the psychologist who defined the foundations of human-computer interaction
> Paul Fitts' research changed the understanding of movement, response, and control in user interfaces – principles that still form the basis of digital design today.

![Paul Fitts (drawing based on a photograph) and his list of properties where humans are better than machines.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/d07b683c-9491-4655-9aee-1309254edbda_1536x1024.webp "630x420")

[Paul Fitts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Fitts) defined a list of properties where machines excel over humans and vice versa.

[Paul Morris Fitts](https://research.osu.edu/paul-m-fitts) (May 6, 1912 – May 2, 1965) was an American psychologist who laid the foundations for the field of Human-Computer Interaction. His most famous contribution? The rule stating that important controls should be as close as possible and sufficiently large. *[Fitts' Law](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/fitts-law)* mathematically describes how much time a person needs to point at a target. This forever changed the face of interfaces – from menu layouts to the size of buttons on touchscreens.

But Fitts didn't just study movement. He also focused on how well a system's response matches the user's mode of control. His concept of *[stimulus-response compatibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stimulus%E2%80%93response_compatibility)* still explains why interfaces must be understandable – from switches to light and vibration signals.

> “It should be possible, therefore, to eliminate a large proportion of aircraft accidents by designing instruments in accordance with human requirements.”\
> **— [Paul Morris Fitts & Richard E. Jones](https://scispace.com/pdf/psychological-aspects-of-instrument-display-1-analysis-of-3x18sqyuaq.pdf)**\

In 1951, he created *[Fitts' List](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/The-original-Fitts-List-Reprinted-with-permission-from-Human-Engineering-for-an_fig2_3454029)*, which compares human and machine capabilities. Machines excel at repetition and precision; humans are strong in judgment and pattern recognition. This influenced cockpit design and today's questions about artificial intelligence and automation.

Fitts showed that design isn't just about appearance – it's about adapting systems to human abilities. Where others blamed the pilot for error, Fitts looked for the cause in control layout. And in doing so, he laid the foundations of ergonomics before the term UX even existed.

His legacy lives on. Every designer who makes a button larger, simplifies a form, or ensures an automatic feature remains under human control is working – willingly or not – in the spirit of Paul Fitts.
