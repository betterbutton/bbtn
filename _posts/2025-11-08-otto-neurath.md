# Otto Neurath Transformed Data into Empathy Half a Century Before UX Was Born
> Austrian philosopher, sociologist, and reformer of visual communication. Creator of the Isotype system and a forerunner of human-centered design and user experience.

![Otto Neurath in later age standing before an information panel using Isotype](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/ef2d2a33-13c6-4bd8-89d4-b985339926aa_1536x1024.webp "534x356")

To remember simplified pictures is better than to forget accurate figures. ([Isotype representing social facts pictorially](https://isotyperevisited.org/Isotype_representing_social_facts_pictorially.pdf))

When we talk about *human-centered design* today, we imagine digital teams testing prototypes and refining interface usability. Long before the term was even coined, *[Otto Neurath](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_Neurath)* (December 10, 1882 — December 22, 1945) built a thoughtful system for conveying complex knowledge to ordinary people. His project *Isotype – International System of Typographic Picture Education* translated social and economic data into a visual language and [became a forerunner](https://www.theguardian.com/artanddesign/2019/aug/27/pictures-unite-graphic-design-vision-marie-otto-neurath) of today's information design, standardized information symbols, and emojis.

![Isotype used repetition of pictograms to express quantity. In a statistics chart about marriage rates over time, instead of a bar chart, it uses a repeating pictogram of a bride and groom, forming a graph.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/e71b7307-a845-49c8-ad86-7cc74fedee83_693x562.webp "693x562")

Isotype uses repetition of pictograms to express quantity.

The foundation of Neurath's vision was a radical idea: *information is a civil right*. In 1920s Vienna, he founded the *Museum of Society and Economy*, where he created pictograms from numbers that were understandable to anyone. “*Words divide, images unite,*” he would say. He saw the museum as a public service, not an academic curiosity.

Behind every image were principles we now consider fundamental to UX. The Isotype team – Neurath as theorist, artist *[Gerd Arntz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerd_Arntz)*, and *[Marie Neurath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marie_Neurath)* as *transformer* – worked in iterations, sketching, testing, and revising. Marie described herself as a *“curator of public understanding,”* which perfectly captures today's content design. It wasn't about beauty, but about comprehension: Can a person understand it and act accordingly?

> “What you can present by means of pictures do not express by means of words.”\
> **— [Otto Neurath](https://centaur.reading.ac.uk/88323/1/9789004680203-BP000015.pdf)**

Every chart adhered to strict design system rules: repeat symbols to express quantity, do not distort scale, use limited colors, and maintain a unified visual grammar. Neurath's *minimum necessary* principle echoes today's minimalism in UX: The best teacher is one who knows what to leave out best. He saw learning as a process of collective improvement: *“We are like sailors who must rebuild their ship on the open sea.”*

After fleeing Austria from Nazism, he brought Isotype to The Hague and then to Britain, where it became part of postwar education and civic planning. These principles later influenced Olympic pictograms ([Tokyo 1964](https://www.theolympicdesign.com/olympic-games/pictograms/tokyo-1964/), [Munich 1972](https://www.theolympicdesign.com/olympic-games/pictograms/munich-1972/)), [transport symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DOT_pictograms) by AIGA/DOT, and international symbols [ISO 7001](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_7001) – the institutionalized form of his belief that design should serve understanding.

Today's UX approach – clarity, accessibility, and empathy – has its roots in Neurath. *[Edward Tufte](https://www.edwardtufte.com/)* praised his discipline in visual data, *[Ellen Lupton](https://ellenlupton.com/)* wrote that Isotype *“transmuted logical positivism into humanism,”* and *[Richard Saul Wurman](https://www.wurman.com/)* built on his ideas with the term *information architecture*. Neurath proved that visual design is not decoration but social technology – a tool that empowers people to see, understand, and co-decide.
