# Less is More
> A design principle stating that removing non-essential elements increases clarity, usability flow, and user focus by reducing cognitive and visual load.

> *You are opening the last, twenty-fourth window of the BBtn Advent Calendar for 2025. In this year's advent series, we'll look at popular UX designer proverbs. Today's is: Less is more.*

*[Less is More](https://www.ilincev.com/mene-je-lepe)* is among the most frequently quoted UX sayings. Users have limited attention, little patience, and arrive with a specific goal. If an interface forces them to first decide what to ignore before what to do, unnecessary friction arises.

In practice, therefore, removing non-essential elements often leads to content that is more understandable, quicker to read, and easier to use. In UX, this saying does not denote a visual style. It denotes a result.

The goal is not emptiness or aesthetic minimalism, but clear page purpose. When there are fewer competing messages, controls, and visual signals, the user more easily recognizes the main task and completes it with confidence. Once the non-essential disappears, the signal-to-noise ratio improves and the interface feels simpler.

The problem arises when the saying is taken literally. Removing content, controls, or explanations can reduce comprehension, hide important options, or increase uncertainty. An interface may look clean, but the user isn't sure where to click, what happens next, or how to fix an error.

In such cases, the work doesn't move away—it shifts onto the user. Used correctly, "less is more" means reducing unnecessary complexity when it doesn't help. Clear hierarchy, strong visual signals, sensible default choices, and progressive disclosure of information usually work better than radical reduction.

The question isn't what can be removed, but what can be removed without making the user think more. Ultimately, it's not about having less interface. It's about wanting less from the user.
