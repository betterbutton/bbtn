# Hero section
> The hero section dominates the website's first impression. It attracts attention with a visual, headline, and call to action. It strengthens branding and conversions, but can slow down loading and reduce engagement below the fold.

The **hero section** ([hero section](https://uxplanet.org/unsung-heros-of-ui-look-at-hero-images-in-web-design-881e04fe7bd2)) is a prominent area at the top of a webpage [above the fold](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/above-the-fold). Its goal is to grab the user's attention, convey key information, and prompt action. It typically features a striking headline, one or two lines of text, a call to action, and large visual elements like an image, video, or illustration. The [hero section](https://uxknowledgebase.com/hero-section-best-practices-inspiration-part-1-6944f473c505) originates from print media, where large images dominated advertisements and magazine covers.

A well-designed hero section improves brand recall and increases conversion. On the other hand, some users instinctively skip the hero section due to banner blindness. Studies show that users perceive visual elements more than text. However, text is important for SEO. Another disadvantage of large images on the first screen is slowed loading. The hero section can also increase the bounce rate for users who don't notice that the page continues below the fold. Last but not least, it complicates responsive design.

The term was allegedly first used by [Marcel Moerkens](https://dribbble.com/unitedthemes) in 2013 while developing the [Brooklyn](https://themeforest.net/item/brooklyn-responsive-multipurpose-wordpress-theme/6221179) template for WordPress. According to him, the term emerged as a variation on the [jumbotron from Bootstrap](https://getbootstrap.com/docs/4.0/components/jumbotron/). However, Google Trends recorded searches for the phrase "hero section" five years before that.
