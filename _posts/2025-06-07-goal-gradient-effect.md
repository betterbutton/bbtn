# Goal-gradient effect
> The closer people are to completing a task, the more effort they exert. A key insight for UX design.

The goal-gradient effect ([Goal-gradient effect](https://learningloop.io/plays/psychology/goal-gradient-effect)) describes how people speed up when they are close to completing a task. This phenomenon was [first observed in animals](https://psycnet.apa.org/doiLanding?doi=10.1037%2Fh0072640). Rats ran faster once they felt they were approaching the reward. [It manifests similarly in humans.](https://home.uchicago.edu/ourminsky/Goal-Gradient_Illusionary_Goal_Progress.pdf) When someone fills out a form, collects points, or completes registration, they often start working faster once they see that only a little remains.

This can be well utilized in designing digital products. Users who see that only a small step remains are more inclined to continue. Simple sentences like 'Just one last step' or 'Almost done' help with this. Such messages increase the chance that a person will actually complete the task.

Conversely, when no progress is visible, motivation quickly drops. The user doesn't know how much remains and may give up. Therefore, it's important to break down complex tasks into smaller parts and clearly show how one is doing. Short feedback that confirms correct progress also helps a lot.

Progress indicators have a big impact. A simple bar or numerical step is enough, and people are more inclined to continue. In some cases, the completion rate nearly doubled. The goal-gradient effect isn't about pushing users forward. It's about showing them how close they already are to the goal. And that often suffices.
