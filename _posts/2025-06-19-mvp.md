# Minimum Viable Product
> Minimum Viable Product is the simplest version of a product used to validate an idea in the real world and gather feedback as soon as possible.

[Minimum Viable Product](https://www.strafelda.cz/mvp), also known as [minimum viable product](https://www.atlassian.com/agile/product-management/minimum-viable-product) ([MVP](https://www.productboard.com/glossary/minimum-viable-product-mvp/)), is a basic version of a product designed to verify a specific assumption directly with real users in a live environment. The goal is to discover how people actually use the product, without unnecessary investment of time and money into full development. For developers and digital teams, an MVP is the fastest path to useful feedback.

Such a product must solve a real user problem with the fewest possible [features](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/feature). It is not a half-finished prototype, but a version that delivers clear value to the user—for example, a simple to-do list with checkboxes, or a delivery service managed via a form and a spreadsheet.

The purpose of an MVP is to get the product into real operation as quickly as possible. Testing within the team, colleagues' opinions, or even user testing cannot replace the insights gained from observing how real users behave. The goal is not to guess, but to know—[based on concrete data](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/evidence-based-design). This means launching early, closely monitoring reactions, and flexibly adjusting further development.

However, the same principle of moderation applies here. If an MVP seems too unfinished, it can damage trust. If it's too polished, it can become a trap, tempting further development without a validated foundation. The art lies in choosing what is truly essential.
