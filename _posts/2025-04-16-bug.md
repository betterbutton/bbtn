# Bug
> Bug (error) as an invisible consequence of a developer's mistaken assumption, which only manifests in real operation and shows that reality is always more complex than tests.

Bugs (errors) in software often don't arise from bad code, but from incorrect assumptions. Everything looks correct. Tests pass. Nothing crashes. And then the first real user arrives. They click differently. They do something unexpected. And a bug is born. In Czech, we say software has *mouchy* (flies/bugs).

These aren't technical errors. They are hidden problems that appear only in a real environment. They arise the moment a developer thinks the user will act like them. But they don't. The user might have a different device, different habits, a different language. And the system isn't prepared for that.

Fixing an error in the code is often easy. Harder is changing the way of thinking. Admitting that our view of things isn't the only one. And that we must account for the fact that the world is more complex than our test scenarios.

Preventing such errors means looking at things through someone else's eyes. Asking what we simply *think*. What we take for granted. And whether that truly applies to others.

It helps to involve people from outside in design and testing. Colleagues who didn't write the code. And especially users. Their behavior often reveals what we overlooked.

Then a bug isn't a disgrace. It's an opportunity. It shows where we were wrong. And gives us a chance to do better next time.

Flawless software isn't possible. But we can create software that knows how to learn from its mistakes.
