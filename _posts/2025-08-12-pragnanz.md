# Law of Prägnanz
> The mind's natural tendency to perceive the simplest and most stable shape is a fundamental element of Gestalt psychology.

The Law of Prägnanz ([law of prägnanz](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/law-of-praegnanz)), a principle from [Gestalt psychology](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/gestalt), states that people tend to interpret complex visual stimuli in the simplest and most stable way possible. The English term borrowed the word *pregnancy* from German, where it has the same meaning as in Czech—conciseness, substance, and of course the original Latin meaning of pregnancy.

This principle explains why clean, balanced design feels natural and chaotic design feels tiring. The brain tries to save energy and prefers the interpretation that requires the least effort—even if it isn't completely accurate.

When the founder of shape psychology [Max Wertheimer](https://leonardo.info/isast/articles/behrens.html) observed flashing warning lights at a railway crossing, it seemed to him as if the light was jumping smoothly from side to side. His brain filled in the missing trajectory between one position and the other.

For digital products, this means users automatically group elements, align shapes, and infer patterns, even when none were intended. A well-designed website menu with consistent spacing reveals its hierarchy instantly. An inconsistent menu forces users to guess—and often guess wrong. Poor alignment, uneven spacing, or random grouping can create a false sense of structure or hierarchy and mislead.

The lesson is clear: design should minimize visual ambiguity. Simplicity isn't just an aesthetic question. It reduces mental load and facilitates interaction. When a designer works in accordance with the [Law of Prägnanz](https://lawsofux.com/law-of-prägnanz/), they achieve a match between what is displayed and what is understood, and the brain doesn't have to infer meaning on its own.

The inclination toward clarity works automatically and without conscious effort. That's why we can read a torn poster as a whole or mistake an abstract icon for a familiar symbol. Understanding this shortcut allows creators to design interfaces that feel obvious—and don't require the user to think about why they work.
