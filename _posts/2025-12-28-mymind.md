# MyMind: the designer's second brain

> MyMind is a tool for storing inspiration, notes, and references. It doesn't try to teach you a new work system – it's designed to respect the way people think.

![Screenshot from MyMind.com service](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/6d9404e4-b1cf-4790-b81e-8bd8cb3151ba_1365x767.png "608x341")

[MyMind](https://mymind.com/) promises automatic information management

*[MyMind](https://mymind.com/)* is based on a simple idea: you should be able to save things immediately, without deciding where they belong. No folders, no complex structure, no system maintenance. You simply save what's important to you and return to it when you need it.

The design is by *[Tobias van Schneider](https://www.linkedin.com/in/tobiasvanschneider/)* – a designer long known for his clear opinions, simplicity, and products without unnecessary noise. And it shows a lot in MyMind. The app isn't neutral. It has clearly defined boundaries, and they aren't random.

For UX designers, MyMind is naturally close. Screenshots of interfaces, UI patterns, articles, short notes, or color palettes all have the same weight. You don't deal with whether something is a note or inspiration. It's all material for thinking. You then access content through search, context, or visual similarity – by color, mood, or topic.

Also important is what you *won't* find in MyMind. There are no social feeds, sharing, collaboration, or metrics that force you to use the app. It's a quiet, private place. More like a personal archive or digital sketchbook than another productivity tool that wants your attention.

That's precisely what makes MyMind interesting. Not just as a service, but also as an example that a good product doesn't have to do everything. It's enough if it does a few things well – and with respect for the people who use it.
