# It's the Value That Belongs in a Portfolio, Not the Process
> 

Common advice for designers creating their portfolios is: show your process. But why? The process of most designers is more or less the same: Understand the brief, come up with ideas, validate the prototype. Focusing on the design process in a portfolio can be a mistake. Most clients and employers aren't interested in the procedure itself, but in the results and the value it brings to the company.

[This is how Christopher K. Wong looks at portfolios](https://dataanddesign.substack.com/p/focusing-on-your-design-process-is) and Aneta Kmiecik holds a similar view in the article [Showing your project impact](https://uxdesignexpress.beehiiv.com/p/ux-portfolio-showing-project-impact): *„Highlighting your project outcomes and impact is what hiring managers are looking for.“*

Companies look for the least risky candidate who can demonstrate the value and impact of their work. The process becomes risky if it shows that a designer blindly follows standard procedures without understanding business goals. According to Christopher, the key is to present results using the Why—What—How model, which emphasizes the project's reason, the final outcome, and the way goals were achieved.

And Aneta adds – If measurable results are missing, focus on hypothetical metrics and your learnings from the projects. Describe how you improved team processes or increased product value. Even without specific numbers, you can demonstrate your abilities and value through concrete examples and by choosing the right way to present them.
