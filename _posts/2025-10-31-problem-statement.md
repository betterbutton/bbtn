# Problem Statement
> A concise and evidence-based determination of the user's true need that guides the design from research through to implementation.

A problem statement ([problem statement](https://www.figma.com/resource-library/problem-statement/)) is the core of every project in UX and HCI. It is a short, understandable description of the gap between the current and the desired state from a genuine user's perspective, and an explanation of why it matters. It helps to understand the difference between how people do something today and how they would like to do it. This keeps the project team focused on solving the right thing – not on a beautiful but unnecessary interface.

In the [human-centered design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human-centered_design) process (ISO 9241‑210), the problem statement stands at the very beginning and we return to it in iterations. It translates research findings into a clearly named need before a single screen design is created. A good problem statement is user-focused, describes an activity, not an interface, and does not limit the team to a single solution. It helps decide what to explore, what to prototype, and how to evaluate the benefit.

> **For example:** A shop owner just starting their business needs to quickly compare fees for payment terminals because technical terms prevent them from making a decision.

A problem statement first grounds the reality: who, what needs, and why. Only then do we derive a design challenge – a deliberately open question for ideation. The challenge is the bridge between the *[Define](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/reframing)* → *[Ideate](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/geyser-of-ideas)* phases; it must be narrow enough to hold focus and broad enough to allow for multiple solutions.

Designers use various forms of challenges: [defining user needs](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/user-need-statements/), a "How might we" [question](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/how-might-we), brief [user scenarios](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/user-story), or use cases, i.e., descriptions of a specific task flow. In agile development, they are followed by [user stories](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/user-story) – but these already represent small, concrete parts of the solution.

A strong problem statement is specific, research-validated, and verifiable in practice. It focuses on verbs – like compare, decide, find – not on buttons or pages. Instead of vague phrases like *"a better experience"*, it measures success by whether the user achieves their goal faster and easier.

When a problem is well-defined, it becomes the design's compass. It sets the direction, unifies the team, and reminds us that design doesn't start with a screen, but with understanding a human.
