# Feedback is a Gift
> Feedback is a Gift – freely available data that shows us the difference between what we intended and what people actually experience.

> *You are opening the second window of the BBtn Advent Calendar for 2025. This year's advent series looks at popular UX designer proverbs. Today's is: Feedback is a Gift.*

Feedback is the most direct and sincere signal you can receive. Every comment, bug report, or negative review is proof of how your website performs in the real world. Statistics show what people do, but feedback tells you why they do it. Ignoring it is like covering up a warning light on the dashboard.

Those who treat feedback as a gift improve faster and waste less time. Users who complain give us something valuable – most others simply leave. When we listen, we can turn frustration into useful insights: a confusing shopping cart, an inaccessible form, unclear navigation. When we fix these, conversions and satisfaction grow, and support queries decrease. Constant feedback keeps a website current and trustworthy.

Giving feedback is also a skill. Effective criticism is specific and kind – it describes behavior and impact, not the person. The sentence *„the heading structure is hard to read“* helps, but *„the design is bad“* does not. Receiving feedback requires calm and curiosity – the ability to separate ego from evidence. In healthy teams, criticism targets the interface, not the individual.

Not all feedback fits. Some comments reflect only personal taste or edge cases. The designer's task is to distinguish patterns from noise and decide based on goals and priorities. Openness is key – acknowledge comments, act where it makes sense, and show what changed because of them.

When we accept feedback as a gift, it becomes an advantage. It helps create better design, strengthens trust and team collaboration – transforming criticism into an engine for continuous improvement. Remember, however, that even a gift can be refused. Sometimes feedback is loud and insistent, but if it doesn't concern the relevant target group, following it would be a mistake rather than a path to a better outcome.
