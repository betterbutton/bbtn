❌ Icon without text only\

## New Books About the Web from the First Half of 2025

Packing books for vacation? We try to keep up with new additions, and here is a list of ten books we've caught on our radar this year. If you've already read any of the following books, share your insights with us.

> CARPENTER, Jeff: *[Hey, UX! Hey, PM!](https://www.heyuxheypm.com/)* Released February 9, 2025. (ISBN 979-8218598778. 242 pages)

This book is about the work of a UX designer on one side and about product managers on the other. Two in one. Well, don't buy it!

> DASHINSKY, Artiom: *[The Path to Staff Product Designer: How to Progress to the Next IC Level Beyond Senior (Staff/Lead/Principal) in UX Design.](https://productdesigninterview.com/the-path-to-staff-product-designer)* Released March 21, 2025. (ISBN 979-8312944594. 249 pages)

Artiom writes one book after another, and with each one, he raises the career level of the UX professional he's writing for. What will probably follow after this book?

> SETH, Utkarsh: *[Applied UX Research: Bridging the gap between academia and industry: Written for early UX Research career professionals.](https://uxcoaching.wordpress.com/)* Released March 25, 2025. (ISBN 979-8315422914. 242 pages)

Utkarsh promises to show us how to maintain the principles of UX research without staying only with the theory learned at university.

> BECKER, Christopher Reid: *[Effective UX Design Strategies: A practical guide to human-centered design and agile UX implementation.](https://bpbonline.com/products/effective-ux-design-strategies)* Published by BPB Publications March 29, 2025. (ISBN 978-9365893731, 236 pages)

Although the book's title mentions strategies, according to the content description, it's simply an introduction to UX.

> TAFFE, Simone. KELLY, Meghan: *[CoDesign: People, Participation, Practice.](https://www.routledge.com/CoDesign-People-Participation-Practice/Taffe-Kelly/p/book/9781032064956)* Published by Routledge April 29, 2025. (ISBN 978-1040352151. 211 pages)

This is a book we will probably get because involving users in design is something we would very much like to practice.

> SCHIRMEIER, Monika: *[Nachhaltige User Experience: Ein Handbuch für die praktische (Umsetzung und erfolgreiche Unternehmensintegration.](https://shop.haufe.de/prod/nachhaltige-user-experience)* Published by Haufe Fachbuch May 7, 2025. (ISBN 978-3648181621. 224 pages)

Sustainability in UX sounds interesting, but we'll probably wait for the English edition of the book, which Monika promises still this year.

> NUDELMAN, Greg: *[UX for AI: A Framework for Designing AI-Driven Products.](https://www.uxforai.com/c/ux-for-ai-book)* Published by Wiley May 13, 2025. (ISBN 978-1394345922. 352 pages)

AI in the book's title looks like an attempt to carve out a large share of the book market. The author promises 35 case studies from real products.

> MALCHANAU, Stanislau: *[You - Designer: A fast-track method to enter UX/UI for beginners.](https://www.stanislaumalchanau.com/ux-ui-books-you-designer)* Published by PublishDrive May 30, 2025. (192 pages)

Stanislau has a similar approach to Artiom Dashinsky. Only in this case, the book is focused on beginners.

> VALLAURE, Christine: *[Solo. Build, Launch, and Grow Your Own Digital Products. A practical guide to solopreneurship.](https://thesolo.io/)* (250 pages. PDF)

We hope to meet Christine Vallaure at one of this year's conferences. Her [talk from last year's Berlin BT conference](https://www.youtube.com/watch?time_continue=2&v=eC6sGhqaQHI&embeds_referring_euri=https%3A%2F%2Fbeyondtellerrand.com%2F&source_ve_path=Mjg2NjY), looks promising.

> HALLAND, Are. HALLAND, Mona: *[The Core Model. A Common Sense Approach to Digital Strategy and Design.](https://www.thecoremodel.com/book)* Published by Kraft Publishing. (ISBN 978-82-693269-0-1. PDF)

Are and Mona wrote a book about creating digital content. It should be useful mainly for content strategists from corporations.

## Linkodrome

**Grid replaces Auto Layout in Figma.** In May, Figma introduced [a new grid inspired by the CSS grid](https://uxdesign.cc/figmas-new-grid-you-must-understand-css-grid-as-a-designer-fbb00416e1cc). Unlike the previous Auto Layout, it allows working in both rows and columns, similar to CSS. Understanding CSS grid will help you understand the new tool. Designers should understand the difference between explicit and implicit grids and between one-dimensional ([flexbox](https://www.vzhurudolu.cz/prirucka/css-flexbox)) and two-dimensional ([grid](https://www.vzhurudolu.cz/prirucka/css-grid)) layouts. Only then can they create designs that are easy to translate into code.

**What should design specifications for developers contain:** [Two inputs are passed to development.](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/creating-design-specs-for-development/) The first – the brief – usually comes from the product owner. The brief contains: Project goal, project scope, functional and non-functional requirements, usage scenarios, risks and their mitigation, design preview, and a link to the design (now mostly in Figma). The second input is the design. The designer prepares it and it should consist of these parts: **user journeys** (what happens after a click or tap), **visual design** (named styles for colors, typography, icons), **layout and responsiveness** (grid, responsive breakpoints), **interface components** (buttons, fields, states, micro-interactions), **real content** (actual text and correct image sizes), **accessibility guidelines** (e.g., tab order, alternative text).

**Visual illusions in typography and architecture.** Niko Kitsakis [writes about optical corrections](https://www.nubero.ch/blog/015/) that take human perception into account in visual design and architecture. He explains why geometric precision is often insufficient: what is *correct* according to the eye may not match measurement. Using the example of the Futura font and the Parthenon temple, he shows that visual harmony requires artistic sensitivity and experience – not just technical precision. He criticizes the current superficiality in design approach and highlights the value of beauty and craft care as an expression of culture.
