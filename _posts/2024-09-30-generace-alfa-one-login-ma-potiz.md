# British Government's One Login Faces Issues with Generation Alpha
> 

After Brexit, Britain also separated from [eIDAS](https://www.mvcr.cz/clanek/eidas-sluzby-vytvarejici-duveru-a-elektronicka-identifikace.aspx) – the European system for electronic identification. Currently, it is transitioning to login via [One Login identity](https://home.account.gov.uk/contact-gov-uk-one-login). One Login is by design a simple system, not particularly different from commercial services: the user logs in with a username, which is an email address, a password, and a second factor.

However, the system surprisingly encounters issues with users from the just-maturing [Generation Alpha](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generace_alfa) and the adolescent [Generation Z](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generace_Z). These people are indeed [digital natives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_native), but unlike the previous [Millennial](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generace_Y) generation, they use the internet in a different way. They are people aged from 13 up to perhaps 24 years. An article on the British Government Digital Service blog addresses this: [GOV.UK One Login: Designing for inclusion at scale](https://gds.blog.gov.uk/2024/09/19/gov-uk-one-login-designing-for-inclusion-at-scale/).

The One Login system was designed by the parents of these generations according to their own user habits. For the younger generation, even choosing an email address for their identity is problematic. Younger people barely use email and usually do not have an email address they would want to permanently associate with their identity.

Another stumbling block is identity verification via financial transaction records. Very young people often do not have their own bank accounts, and they frequently do not use traditional banking services at all. The problem is also that young people typically do not yet pay their own bills for water, electricity, gas, and mobile phones, which the system requires for identity verification.
