# Key Performance Indicators
> An indicator used to assess progress toward a goal and to decide what to change, prioritize, or discontinue.

Key Performance Indicators ([key performance indicators](<<<URL_0_5689e>>>), KPI) are meant to aid decision-making. When they work, they clarify progress and reveal necessary trade-offs. When they fail, they become backstage. Numbers are collected and reported regularly but do not influence the next steps. The difference is not in the tools, but in whether the indicator is truly tied to a meaningful goal.

A KPI is not the same as a common metric. Metrics describe what is happening. KPIs express whether it is important. Traffic, load time, or error count remain mere descriptions of reality until they are explicitly linked to a goal. This could be, for example, increasing quality demand, improving task success rates, or reducing operational risks. Only then do they become performance indicators, not just activity.

A functional KPI system is intentionally lean. A small number of indicators forces prioritization and clarifies accountability. The attempt to measure everything usually leads to the opposite. Meaning blurs and accountability dissolves. Therefore, mature approaches combine a few outcome KPIs with supplementary indicators that prevent optimizing one area at the expense of another.

In the web environment, KPIs typically relate to outcomes, user experience, and operations. Each of these areas is important, but none is sufficient on its own.

Poorly designed KPIs are easy to game. Once a number becomes a target, behavior adapts to the number, not the original intent. Every KPI therefore must have a clear definition and accountability. Above all, it should answer the simple question of what decision will change if the value moves significantly.

Used correctly, KPIs are not a control tool. They are an orientation tool. They help decide where to act further and where, conversely, not to intervene.
