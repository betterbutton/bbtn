# Jon Postel – architect of fundamental internet protocols
> RFC Editor, founder of IANA and the quiet force behind the protocols that power the internet.

![Jonathan Postel, drawing generated from a photograph](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/fa2509b2-cdda-419e-8e6d-3e401b4d49c4_1536x1024.webp "1456x971")

[Jon Postel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jon_Postel), The God of The Internet

[Jonathan Bruce Postel](https://icannwiki.org/Jon_Postel) (August 6, 1943 — October 16, 1998) was one of the key figures in creating the technical foundation of the internet. As editor of the [Request for Comments](https://www.ietf.org/process/rfcs/) documents, abbreviated RFC, and founder of the [IANA](https://www.iana.org/) organization, he had a fundamental influence on the development of the [TCP/IP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_protocol_suite), [SMTP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_Mail_Transfer_Protocol), and [DNS](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_Name_System). protocols. It is precisely these technologies that allow the internet to function reliably across different networks and devices.

His most famous principle, known today as [Postel's Law](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/dont-break-the-web), is a simple rule:

> *“Be conservative in what you send, and liberal in what you accept.”**
> — Jonathan Postel*

This idea continues to help build stable and mutually compatible systems.

Postel served as RFC editor for nearly thirty years. He carefully and quietly led the process of creating internet standards. He always preferred openness and simplicity over unnecessary bureaucracy. When the [ICANN](https://www.icann.org/) organization was formed in the 1990s to take over some of IANA's functions, he expressed concerns about the loss of community control over the internet.

In January 1998, just a few months before his death, he [hijacked the internet](https://www.wired.com/2012/10/joe-postel/). He took control of the internet away from the US government and handed it over to the international community. He wrote to eight of the twelve institutions that managed the root records for internet domains, instructing them not to use the list provided by the US government agency as authoritative, but to use the name server of the University of Southern California. All the contacted institutions complied with the instruction without contradiction or questions.

The next twenty years [saw a formal process](https://www.bbc.com/news/technology-37114313) for the transfer of the name servers from the US government. As [later emerged](http://songbird.com/pab/mail/0472.html), Postel did not consult anyone about his move, which is thus evidence of his personal authority and power over the internet. Probably for this reason, the BBC wrote about him as the [God of the Internet](http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/science/nature/196487.stm).

Although he was one of the most influential people in the history of the internet, he avoided attention. He acted modestly and stayed in the background. His sudden death in 1998 closed one era—the time when internet governance was built on trust, simple tools, and personal responsibility.

Jon Postel's legacy lives on. He is present in every DNS query, every sent email, and every request for a web page. For everyone who today creates or manages digital systems, he remains a model of technical integrity and quiet leadership.
