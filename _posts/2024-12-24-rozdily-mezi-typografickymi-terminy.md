# Differences between typographic terms typeface and font
> 

In design and typography, the [terms](https://medium.com/@maheshikam/typeface-vs-font-a6bc812dba0c) *[typeface](https://medium.com/@maheshikam/typeface-vs-font-a6bc812dba0c)* [and](https://medium.com/@maheshikam/typeface-vs-font-a6bc812dba0c) *[font](https://medium.com/@maheshikam/typeface-vs-font-a6bc812dba0c)* are often confused, but they refer to different things. A typeface represents the design style of a character family, such as Helvetica or Arial, encompassing all variants like bold, italic, or different sizes. In Czech, we might say it's a *písmo* (typeface). Sometimes "typeface" is translated as *písmová rodina* (type family), but these are not entirely interchangeable terms. A type family is rather a set of variants, cuts, and sizes of one typeface (*font family*).

A font is a specific variant of a typeface, for example, Helvetica Bold 12 point. The correct use of these terms is crucial for work in branding and digital content. In Czech, we don't have such a precise term. We could say it's a combination of type variant, cut, and size of a typeface. In Gutenberg's metal movable type printing, a font would represent the contents of a type case (a drawer with cones divided into compartments by character – [in the image](https://unsplash.com/photos/brown-wooden-cube-blocks-on-brown-wooden-shelf-31ZaaX6dHvM))
