**Better not even ask these questions.** People cannot reliably explain their thought processes, motivations, or future behavior. We should ask for specific examples from the past, because [the past is a better predictor than hypotheses](https://medium.com/design-bootcamp/evidence-based-interviewing-deep-dive-the-case-against-some-common-interview-questions-67a30848884a). *Riikka Iivanainen* draws from the famous study *[Telling More Than We Can Know](https://home.csulb.edu/~cwallis/382/readings/482/nisbett%20saying%20more.pdf)* and offers a practical guide on how to formulate questions to lead to useful insights from user research.

**Participatory roadmaps allow you to involve customers in feature prioritization** and thereby reveal what is truly important to them. *Christina Wodtke* recommends using visual mockups and physical tools, like a board with post-its, during conversations with target users, which forces customers to make decisions. [Participatory roadmaps](https://medium.com/the-creative-founder/customer-development-with-participatory-roadmaps-e10491fe154d) help reveal user language and their mental models. Early customer involvement thus increases the chance that the MVP will hit the mark.

The study [News Integrity in AI Assistants](https://www.ebu.ch/Report/MIS-BBC/NI_AI_2025.pdf) (PDF, 1.3 MB) examined how four major AI assistants (ChatGPT, Copilot, Gemini, and Perplexity) work with news content. **Product teams don't know specific users or their real experiences.** *Jared Spool* points out that common phrases about users hide the fact that few on the team can name a real person the product is meant to help. There's a lack of deeper insight into differences between users and their daily reality. Anonymity leads to assumptions, poor decisions, and weak results. Jared appeals for UX teams to become [experts on specific people](https://articles.centercentre.com/its-time-we-seriously-talk-about-users-and-experiences/) for whom they are designing.

**The web is dead again.** *Jeffrey Zeldman* ironically [recaps thirty years of predictions about the end of the web](https://zeldman.com/2025/10/25/receipts-a-brief-list-of-prominent-articles-proclaiming-the-death-of-the-web/), mainly brought by the Wired editorial staff. As early as 1997 they prophesied the end of web browsers due to the PointCast application. In 2004, the web was to be absorbed by RSS, and in 2010 again by applications like Skype or Netflix. Most recently, AI is the threat. But Jeffrey dismissively shows that the web not only survived but absorbed most of the technologies meant to destroy it. According to him, the web is a viable ecosystem and is far from dying.

[John Resig](https://johnresig.com/), author of [jQuery](https://jquery.com/), revolutionized web development by making it easier for developers to work across browsers. His library helped standardize access to browser properties via JavaScript and launched an era of extensible and accessible web. From the original idea of *"write less, do more"* emerged a tool that powered most of the world's websites.

Resig didn't stop with jQuery – he ported [Processing](https://github.com/processing-js/processing-js) to JavaScript, creates interactive educational tools, and digitizes cultural heritage. His work connects technology with humanity and shows that empathy is key to lasting impact.

## So we understand each other

[Law of similarity](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/similarity), one of the principles of Gestalt psychology, states that people automatically perceive similar elements as related. It improves comprehensibility and orientation – for example, buttons of the same appearance are associated by the user with a similar function. It also helps create visual hierarchy, where a uniform style for headings signifies their equivalence.

[Single-page applications](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/spa) (SPA) work by loading only one HTML page and then dynamically updating content using JavaScript, without reloading from the server. They offer a fast, smooth experience similar to native apps. The disadvantage is higher complexity on first load, problematic optimization for search engines, and potential accessibility barriers.

[Linkbuilding](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/linkbuilding) doesn't just belong to SEO. Links also play a crucial role in visibility in AI-powered search. Instead of outdated practices like link exchanges, it's important to build relationships, respond to journalists' requests, or publish on respected platforms.

[Scrolling](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/scrolling), the basic way to move through content in digital interfaces, has its roots in the 1970s at *Xerox PARC* labs. In the 1990s it was enhanced by the mouse wheel, and fifteen years later the iPhone brought smooth, touch-based scrolling with animations.
