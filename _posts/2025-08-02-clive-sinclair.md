# Clive Sinclair Brought the Computer Home
> The British inventor who changed the world of technology by making computers accessible to the general public.

![Clive Sinclair and the ZX81 Computer](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/e5c84189-7b12-4409-9639-92a4f679df1d_1536x1024.webp "660x440")

[Clive Sinclair](https://rk.nvg.ntnu.no/sinclair/sinclair/sinclair.htm) – pioneer of the eight-bit computer revolution for households

[Sir Clive Sinclair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clive_Sinclair) (30 July 1940—16 September 2021) was a pioneering British inventor. He transformed the world of computing by bringing computers into homes. Through computers like the [ZX Spectrum](https://www.zx-spectrum.cz/) and earlier ZX models, he enabled millions of people to work with digital technology for the first time and laid the foundation for the development of the British software and gaming industry. His influence extended beyond Britain's borders and inspired a generation of programmers worldwide.

The [ZX80](https://www.8bity.cz/zx80-replika/) computer, which Sinclair introduced in 1980, was the first home computer in the United Kingdom whose price did not exceed one hundred pounds. It was followed by the [ZX81](https://rk.nvg.ntnu.no/sinclair/computers/zx81/zx81.htm) model and in 1982 the legendary ZX Spectrum with color graphics and sound. These devices were compact, affordable, and programmable – and it was this that transformed computers from an exclusive tool for businesses and universities into personal devices.

Not all of Sinclair's inventions, however, were commercially successful. His electric tricycle, the [Sinclair C5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinclair_C5) from 1985, was ahead of its time but was ridiculed by the public. Pocket televisions, although technologically advanced, failed to catch on. Nonetheless, these projects reflected his belief in the future of miniature technology.

Clive Sinclair was knighted in 1983. He was eccentric, idiosyncratic, and unwavering in his focus on innovation. Paradoxically, he barely used computers himself and rejected the internet – yet thanks to him, we have technology all around us today.
