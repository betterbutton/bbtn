# Solve Your Own Problem First
> A product design approach where the designer starts from a personally experienced problem. It serves as an initial signal of need, not as proof that everyone has the same problem.

> *You are opening the twentieth window of the BBtn Advent Calendar for 2025. In this year's advent series, we'll look at popular UX designer proverbs. Today's is: Scratch your own itch.*

The saying *“solve your own problem first”* ([scratch your own itch](https://37signals.com/podcast/scratch-your-own-itch/)) is used in UX and product design as practical advice. It stems from a simple consideration. Build something you need yourself, because you know your own problem best. For webmasters, designers, and site owners, this is very relatable. You know exactly where publishing slows down, where settings are fragile, and where tools get in the way of actual work.

The origin of this saying is most often linked to open‑source culture and to texts by *[Eric S. Raymond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eric_S._Raymond)* from the late 1990s, particularly the essay *[The Cathedral and the Bazaar](https://monoskop.org/images/e/e0/Raymond_Eric_S_The_Cathedral_and_the_Bazaar_rev_ed.pdf)*. Raymond described the experience that quality software often arises when a developer first solves their own, personally felt problem. In that context, it made sense. The author was also the user, feedback was immediate, and the solution spread because it turned out the problem wasn't unique.

From a UX perspective, solving your own problem is therefore a valuable initial signal. Personal frustration provides rich context and strong motivation. It allows for rapid iterations and daily use of your own solution, making it possible to uncover small but recurring issues in a timely manner.

The risk arises when this idea becomes a complete design method. You are rarely a typical user. You have more knowledge, higher tolerance for complexity, and different assumptions than editors, clients, or administrators. What is obvious to you may be confusing, inaccessible, or unnecessary to others.

Used correctly, “solve your own problem first” shows where to start looking. Human‑centered design then decides whether the problem is shared, how it manifests in different contexts, and what the solution should actually look like. Start with your own pain, but end with the users.
