# Handoff
> Handoff is an English term for handing over a design as a basis for conversion into functional code – a process for the smooth and precise launch of a digital product.

**Handing over the design for production** ([handoff](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/design-handoffs)) is an important process where digital teams move from design to actual development. It is the transition from a static design to a functional interface. When everything goes smoothly, the result matches the original intent, the risk of errors is reduced, and the entire development is faster.

It's not just about sharing files or images. It is necessary to clearly explain how the user interface should function, what should happen in various situations, and how it should look on different devices. Teams often use tools that help align communication between designers and developers. These include design tools with source files and documentation for a component library with examples. Designers should think like developers – considering consistency, clarity, and supplementary notes.

If the handoff phase is neglected, it quickly becomes apparent: the interface doesn't match the design, some elements behave differently than they should, and the entire team has to fix things unnecessarily. Most often, this is caused by designers and developers not understanding each other or starting to communicate too late.

Handoff is not a form of output, nor is it a meeting. Instead of a one-way handover, there should be a dialogue between designers and developers. When both designers and developers are looking in the same direction and communicate in a timely manner, the result matches both the visual design and the technical possibilities. The right handoff begins by involving developers already during the project definition phase and ends with the website launch. Only then are digital products created that feel thoughtful and natural.
