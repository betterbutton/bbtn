# The Historical Development of HTML Elements at a Glance
> 

When I learned HTML 3.2, it had 70 elements compared to the current 111 – says Martin. Jens Oliver Meiert offers an [overview of HTML history and optimization](https://meiert.com/en/blog/html-cheat-sheet/). [Cheat sheet](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/10Lko1HHsRT6GVfvNY2KbUexybNTAnk0S86OIl0MZjTs/edit?gid=0#gid=0), a supplement to the HTML element index and an ebook on HTML and CSS, provides an overview of all important HTML elements across specifications.

Jens, a technical leader with experience at companies like Google, has maintained this content for 17 years and encourages support for his publications. His work also includes active contributions to standards and the creation of specialist books.

And what is it good for? Probably nothing, since we've had the HTML 5.2 standard for seven years now and browsers have no issues with it. But it might come in handy for quizzes when you go teambuilding with colleagues. And you might discover some new element, such as [slot](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/HTML/Element/slot), [meter](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/HTML/Element/meter), or [mark](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/HTML/Element/mark), which we don't commonly encounter.
