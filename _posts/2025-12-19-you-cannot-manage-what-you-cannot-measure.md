# You Can't Manage What You Can't Measure
> Improvement is only possible through measurement – and in user experience, it reminds us that metrics only make sense when they truly reveal user behavior and experience.

> *You are opening the nineteenth window of the BBtn Advent Calendar for 2025. In this year's advent series, we'll look at popular UX designer proverbs. Today's is: You can’t manage what you can’t measure.*

The statement *“You can’t manage what you can’t measure.”* is one of the most frequently quoted phrases in UX, product management, and analytics. It is often supplemented by the claim that *what gets measured improves*, and is usually attributed to *[Peter Drucker](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Drucker)*. Together, they create the impression that systematic measurement is a fundamental prerequisite for better user experience. In practice, however, this notion is only partially true.

Measurement is important because without it, discussions about UX easily turn into battles of opinion. Data helps set priorities, track changes over time, and defend decisions. The international standard *[ISO 9241‑11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_9241)* gives this effort a clear framework by defining usability through effectiveness, efficiency, and satisfaction in a specific context of use. Metrics like task completion success or usability scores create a common language for teams and leadership.

At the same time, risks must be considered. Drucker himself warned against blind faith in numbers and as early as the 1950s showed *V. F. Ridgway* that [metrics can lead to undesirable behavior](https://www.jstor.org/stable/2390989). Once a metric becomes a target, people optimize the number, not the real goal. In UX, this manifests as, for example, conversion growth bought with user frustration, or longer visits caused by confusion rather than engagement.

Modern frameworks, such as *[HEART](https://www.heartframework.com/)*, try to address this imbalance by combining user behavior with subjective assessment and business goals. The point is not to reduce experience to an overview of metrics, but to measure what truly matters for both users and the organization.

In UX, you cannot manage without observation. However, measurement only works when it supports understanding. What improves is not what is merely recorded, but what is correctly understood and thoughtfully reworked.
