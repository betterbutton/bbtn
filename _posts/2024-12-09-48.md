# Linkodrome

**How to calculate the benefit of user research?** By democratizing research, we have gradually reached a point where [it has lost value in the eyes of colleagues](https://medium.com/design-bootcamp/why-the-idea-that-anyone-can-do-user-research-is-a-bad-one-8b259d4d67bb). Besides in-depth interviews and user testing, almost nothing else is researched. Thomas Stokes shows [three scenarios for calculating return on investment](https://depth.drillbitlabs.com/p/how-to-calculate-roi-for-ux-research): evaluating past projects, estimating the potential of future projects, and justifying the purchase of tools. Using ROI can, in turn, restore the value of user research in the eyes of stakeholders.

![Meme: Why do you need three displays? (Three displays, each showing one head of a three-headed sled)](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/84e07b6d-5444-4919-9a2c-c849179942dc_1080x793.jpeg "454x333")

**Task Success Rate** is one of the metrics for measuring usability. It indicates [the percentage of users who successfully complete a task](https://hamedayeung.substack.com/p/ux-deep-dive-mini-series-part-2-task). It's good to implement it, for example, for a shopping cart and monitor its development over the long term. Unlike the [conversion rate](https://hamedayeung.substack.com/p/ux-deep-dive-mini-series-part-1-conversion), which is more of a marketing metric, task success rate makes it easier to identify problems such as unclear navigation, complex processes, or inappropriate error messages.

**How to approach user stories:** Using user stories in product development, especially in agile, we describe the requirements for developing new features. They should stem from validated product ideas and focus on user goals, not just stakeholder requirements. [The key to success are the 3Cs:](https://substack.com/@huryn/note/c-80616931) a card with a description, a conversation with the product team to understand the benefits, and criteria to confirm that the requirement has been met.

Good User Stories fulfill the INVEST principles – they are Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, and Testable. When splitting large stories into smaller ones, it's important to focus on simplicity and quick value delivery. The entire team should feel ownership of the product and contribute to creating user stories.

Illustration: [Kateřina Čápová](https://www.katerinacapova.cz/) ([75 tips on how to ask for a website](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/kniha))

Before diving into full development, validate the concept with future users of your website. Sketches allow you to quickly determine whether the website's structure and navigation align with your users' ideas and expectations. Thanks to testing at this early stage, you can easily uncover shortcomings in the user interface or the sequence of steps in individual processes.

If you enjoy a creative approach, try, for example, the [Wizard of Oz](https://kisk.phil.muni.cz/100metod/carodej-ze-zeme-oz) method. It involves using hand-drawn designs instead of an interactive prototype. In real-time, you flesh them out based on user interactions. The respondent simulates working with the website, while the facilitator adjusts the sketch and displays further steps or system reactions.

For easier testing, [photograph the sketches and link them together](https://marvelapp.com/) to form a functional prototype. This allows you to conduct tests not only on a computer but also on a mobile phone or tablet, either directly in the field or remotely via screen sharing. Testing at this stage helps you save time, costs, and most importantly, prevent bigger problems in the future.
