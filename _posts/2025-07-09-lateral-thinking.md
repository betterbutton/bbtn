# Lateral thinking
> Lateral thinking – a creative approach to problem-solving that bypasses logic and finds unexpected solutions.

[Lateral thinking](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Later%C3%A1ln%C3%AD_my%C5%A1len%C3%AD) ([lateral thinking](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/lateral-thinking)) is a problem-solving method that avoids conventional procedures and instead seeks new, often surprising paths. This term was coined by [Edward de Bono](https://www.debono.com/) and can make a fundamental difference in digital design. It helps where conventional logic fails or where a problem is unclear, complex, or overly bound by rules.

Unlike classical, so-called vertical thinking, which proceeds step-by-step and adheres to proven patterns, lateral thinking dares to step aside. It draws from other fields, asks unusual questions, and disrupts what we take for granted. In areas like UX, app development, or digital services—where there is often no single correct solution—this can be a crucial advantage.

An example is the development of CAPTCHA tests. Initially, they used distorted text. But once machines learned to read them, that was no longer sufficient. The lateral solution was to use image recognition—and eventually, a system that also helps train artificial intelligence. It wasn't just an improvement. The very nature of the problem changed.

Lateral thinking isn't just random creativity. It can be trained. Questions like *"What if the user never touched the screen?"* or *"What if success meant failure?"* can help. Such questions can open up entirely new possibilities.

For designers and developers who feel they're spinning their wheels, this way of thinking can provide a fresh impulse. The path may not be direct, but it can lead to truly novel solutions.
