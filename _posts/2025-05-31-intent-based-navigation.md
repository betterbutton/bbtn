# Intent-Based Navigation
> A design approach where the interface adapts to the user's goal - it guesses what they want to do instead of guiding them through menus.

Intent-based navigation ([intent-based navigation](https://medium.com/@leobothmer/ios-y-an-intent-based-os-part-2-261a8e8f8f4f#:~:text=Intent%2Dbased%20Navigation)) is a way of designing interfaces that focuses on what a person wants to achieve. It's less about where to click or how to navigate, and more about what the outcome should be. Instead of having a person dig through complex menus, the system itself offers what they likely need.

For example: You're searching for a flight from Vienna to Berlin. When you move from the search results to a ticket website, instead of having to enter the entire flight details again, the page immediately offers the one you were just looking for. A music app will start playing your favorite playlist before you even get to the search function. This behavior is based on the assumption that the system can recognize your intent.

Commonly, [intent-based navigation](https://developer.android.com/develop/devices/assistant/intents-assistant-nav-app) is used when a user moves from a website to an app or between apps. In such cases, the intent is passed to the target app via a URL or by sending a message through a programming interface.

When it works, it's pleasant. Things move faster and more simply. But when the system misjudges the intent, it can become more of a hindrance.

This is a different approach than how websites and apps were designed before. Previously, everything was divided into categories and subcategories. Today, designers try to understand human behavior—what people most likely want to do, what they're looking for, what they've done before.

For the designer, this means less planning based on content and more thinking about how the system responds. They need to be smarter, but also know when to step back if their guess is off. Building an intent-based application is now easier thanks to the AI capabilities of large language models.

In the end, it's always about the same thing: for the interface to not get in the way, but to help. For the person not to have to understand the system—but for the system to understand them.
