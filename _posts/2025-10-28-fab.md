# Floating Action Button
> A highlighted, hovering button typically in the bottom-right corner of the screen that triggers the primary non-destructive action.

The Floating Action Button ([floating action button](https://m3.material.io/components/floating-action-button/overview), FAB) is a UI element that hovers above the content and indicates what the most important action to take on a given screen is. It first appeared in Google's [Material Design](https://m1.material.io/components/buttons-floating-action-button.html) system in 2014 and quickly became a symbol of modern digital simplicity—an answer to cluttered menus and hidden features.

It is most commonly found in the bottom-right, within thumb's reach, and provides instant access to a primary action, such as creating a message or a new record. Originally circular, it changed to a square with rounded corners in [Material Design version 3](https://m3.material.io/components/floating-action-button/overview).

Designers use FABs to highlight one key activity. Its elevation above content and shadow distinguish it from other elements, and its simplicity—often just an icon—clearly indicates its purpose. There are small, large, and extended variants; the extended version combines an icon with text for greater clarity. More advanced versions, known as speed dial ([speed dial](https://m2.material.io/components/buttons-floating-action-button#types-of-transitions) or [FAB menu](https://m3.material.io/components/fab-menu/overview)), allow several related actions to expand upon press without cluttering the interface.

Research shows that FABs initially do not increase usage speed but improve perceived experience quality—they feel friendly, visually appealing, and boost control confidence. It's important to follow accessibility principles: a sufficiently large touch target, understandable icons, and proper labels for screen readers.

However, the power of the FAB requires moderation. It should represent a single, constructive action. Multiple FABs on one screen, unclear icons, or destructive functions disrupt its meaning and clarity. In the current Material Design 3, FABs naturally integrate into bottom navigation bars, combining aesthetics with ergonomics.

Its enduring charm lies in its simplicity: an unobtrusive shape, a clear meaning. When used correctly, it turns intent into instant action—and literally puts it at your fingertips.
