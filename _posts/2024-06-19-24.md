# Linkodrome

**Present research as a story.** Gerry Duffy wrote an article for A List Apart called [User Research Is Storytelling.](https://alistapart.com/article/user-research-is-storytelling/) Gerry argues that user research is effective when presented as a story. This achieves not only a better understanding of the problems but also greater motivation among stakeholders to solve them. He suggests structuring the narrative presentation of research into three acts: introduction, conflict, and resolution.

**Overly restrictive forms.** Would you believe that there are still forms that don't accept characters outside of ASCII? Product designer Noémie Peter complained on LinkedIn that many British [websites force her to write her name without diacritics](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7205939047159005185/). Like Jakub and Martin, we haven't experienced this with names, but we regularly receive shipments with question marks and underscores instead of letters in the address. And you haven't even heard about our Vietnamese students with two-character names, who get error messages stating that a name must have at least three letters.

Persona Spectrum from the [Microsoft Inclusive Design Toolkitu](https://download.microsoft.com/download/B/0/D/B0D4BF87-09CE-4417-8F28-D60703D672ED/INCLUSIVE_TOOLKIT_MANUAL_FINAL.pdf) (PDF 21 MB)

We can go even further. You don't need bright sunlight glaring on your display, noise around you, or full hands to benefit from accessible content. For example, just wanting to interact with content using copilots and AI. We sometimes have a website read aloud to us, have complex text rephrased, or displayed in reader mode.

In a year, the [European accessibility law](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/21?utm_source=publication-search#:~:text=Evropsk%C3%BD%20akt%20o%20p%C5%99%C3%ADstupnosti%20produkt%C5%AF%20a%20slu%C5%BEeb) will also apply to websites, applications, and even physical objects and services outside the public sector. Many additional costs will be associated with this for already existing projects where accessibility wasn't considered from the start. Nevertheless, we will all appreciate accessibility adjustments, whether we have a disability or not.

Besides accessibility for users with visual impairments, who have it the longest on the web, legislative pressure is also bringing new approaches that include, for example, neurodivergent users. If you want to learn more about [designing for neurodivergent users](https://stephaniewalter.design/blog/neurodiversity-and-ux-essential-resources-for-cognitive-accessibility/), listen to an episode of the [Informed Life podcast with Stéphanie Walter](https://theinformed.life/2024/06/16/episode-142-stephanie-walter/).

> "When I talk about cognitive accessibility, it's about designing for people with learning disorders, but also for people who have different perceptions."
> **— Stéphanie Walter, [Informed Life](https://theinformed.life/2024/06/16/episode-142-stephanie-walter/).**

# Tip from our book

![Illustration from the book: From the doors of the 80s hair salon Mullets, a man and a woman with mullets emerge.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/88a227c9-9bcd-494d-a7d9-d0d928ad2dea_480x480.avif "480x480")

Illustration: [Kateřina Čápová](https://katerinacapova.cz/) ([75 tips on how to Better Button](https://reknisioweb.cz/p/kniha))

**Clarify who the website is for**

Marketing loves the so-called target user group. The idea that your website visitors will be precisely segmented groups of people might look good in a presentation, but reality is different.

Don't define your target based on age, gender, income, and other socioeconomic criteria. Focus on needs and life situations. When do visitors come to your site? What do they already know from elsewhere? What do they do most often? Where do they most often make mistakes? Which site did they come from and where do they head in case of failure or dissatisfaction?

Project model users into website goals and don't exhaust yourself describing them.
