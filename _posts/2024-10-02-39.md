# Linkodrome

**Content first** – this is a design approach based on the assumption that users come to a website primarily for content, so the design should subordinate to content needs. It's clear that the [content-first approach](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/content-first) applies to content websites. For interactive applications, [task-oriented design](https://hcibib.org/tcuid/) is more appropriate, but that's a topic for another time.

![Content first: 1. Copy, 2. UI, 3. Interaction, 4. Media](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/1dccafc5-cdf1-4852-a44b-3e0b911c3cfe_1920x1210.png "1456x918")

Checklist from the article [What comes first](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/article/mobile-first-and-the-power-of-mobile-computing) on IDF.

[Designing around content](https://medium.com/@francesthurston12456/why-is-content-first-important-111b03310292) assumes content is available from the start, which might surprise you. How does such content-first design work? Nine years ago, [Machal](https://www.vzhurudolu.cz/blog/38-design-v-prohlizeci#:~:text=M%C5%AFj%20proces%20n%C3%A1vrhu%20rozhran%C3%AD%20vypad%C3%A1%20takto%3A) described it brilliantly at WebExp:

1.  Create understandable text with a defined heading structure (important for accessibility).

2.  Add UI elements like buttons and icons to support the content.

3.  Implement interactive elements, such as animations, that enhance the user experience.

As designers with a [content-first mindset](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/article/mobile-first-and-the-power-of-mobile-computing), we choose slightly different methods during design. We work, for example, with a [content priority guide](https://alistapart.com/article/priority-guides-a-content-first-alternative-to-wireframes/) and a [content inventory](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/content-audits/). Over time, designers have gradually started calling themselves UI designers, leading to a shift from creating content websites to applications. Competencies in areas like information design and architecture, gradually mastered by SEO specialists, are thus fading away.

**Verify that your initial assumptions are correct**

The first love for an idea is exciting but often fleeting. Don't get too carried away by how great your idea looks at first glance. It's important to subject it to scrutiny and verify if it truly stands on solid ground. It pays to invest time and energy into critically evaluating your initial assumptions. Make sure your hypothesis actually solves the problem for which you're creating the website.

Be careful what you wish for – it might just come true. For example, if you wish for your customers to be able to change their booking date themselves, it could lead to a complex and expensive booking system. Often, it's enough to allow them to contact you easily without having to pick up the phone and call.
