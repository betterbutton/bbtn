# Selection Bias
> A systematic error that occurs when the people or data included in a study differ from those who should be represented, thereby misleading the results.

[Selection bias](https://www.umimeinformatiku.cz/cviceni-data-zkresleni#:~:text=typy%20zkreslen%C3%AD%20zn%C3%A1t.-,V%C3%BDb%C4%9Brov%C3%A9%20zkreslen%C3%AD,-P%C5%99i%20sb%C4%9Bru%20dat) ([selection bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selection_bias)) distorts research results by creating a gap between the population we want to examine and the group we actually observe. The error occurs when not everyone gets into the data randomly, but according to certain characteristics that are related to the outcomes. The result then seems convincing, even though it misrepresents reality.

In practice, selection bias appears at various stages of research and product testing. [Self-selection](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samov%C3%BDb%C4%9Br) happens when only some users—usually the more active or satisfied ones—sign up for a survey or experiment. Bias caused by participant attrition occurs when people drop out over time and their reasons for leaving are not random. And, for example, [Berkson's bias](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berkson%C5%AFv_paradox) occurs when we only evaluate the users who stayed with the product and forget about those who left.

Data on active users is often incomplete. When we measure satisfaction or success only among those who persisted, we overlook precisely those who had difficulties and left. Unaware of this bias, we might unintentionally focus on the successful cases instead of understanding why others failed.

The best defense is careful selection and transparency. Clearly define who your findings represent, track who drops out of the research, and remember to describe this in the report. Statistical weights or interviews may mitigate the bias, but they never eliminate it completely. The key is to know that the numbers we have only tell the story of those who stayed.
