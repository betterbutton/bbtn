## From The Designer

![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/a01a2c7c-d6e4-4212-abcb-2891c66e33db_2160x1080.png "1456x728")

**Don't turn paragraphs into a solid wall of text.** Large blocks of text = off-putting wall. Long text paragraphs don't benefit [text scanning](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/lide-na-webu-nectou). You lose user attention and increase the risk of immediate website abandonment.\
\
❌ 10 lines without a paragraph\
✅ Short paragraphs, ideally 3 — 7 lines\
✅ Use bullet points or subheadings\
\
👉 Structured text is easier to read\
👉 Maintains attention and people get absorbed\
\
✂️ Chop text into pieces. It works for both websites and newsletters.

## Linkodrome

**Switch to nice text alignment.** Cascading Style Sheets allow switching between [text alignment algorithms](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/CSS/text-wrap). The default algorithm wraps text at the end of a line when there's no more space for the next word (or splits the word if you allow hyphenation). Among other options, note the values `text‑wrap: balance` and `text‑wrap: pretty`. The *balance* value finds use in centered headings – it tries to make lines approximately the same length. The *pretty* value [improves the appearance of longer paragraphs](https://daringfireball.net/2025/04/webkit_textwrap_pretty), applying typographic rules to them ([single characters](https://jednoznakovky.cz/), [widows and orphans](https://www.nabla.cz/obsah/cestina/clanky/parchanty-vdova-sirotek.php), [rivers](https://www.strafelda.cz/typograficka-reka), multiple hyphens…).

**UX can simplify cybersecurity.** [Thanks to intuitive UX design](https://medium.com/design-bootcamp/the-power-of-intuitive-design-1c539b8221b5), security elements can be almost invisible yet effective. The author argues that most security systems today don't account for user behavior, leading to rule circumvention. The solution is design that adapts to users, not the other way around. For example, passwordless login can significantly improve both user experience and security simultaneously.

[The Art of Imperfection in Comic Sans](https://microsoft.design/articles/comic-sans-and-the-art-of-imperfection/)

**Comic Sans brought humanity into the digital world.** Created in 1994 as a friendly alternative to Times New Roman, it quickly gained popularity due to readability and informal style. In the era of cold digital interfaces, it brought warmth and understandability, especially for children and tech newcomers. Today, in the age of perfect algorithms and minimalist design, its irregularity is trendy again. Comic Sans reminds us that [durability lies in authenticity, not perfection](https://microsoft.design/articles/comic-sans-and-the-art-of-imperfection/).

**Quality typography as proof!** The occurrence of a long dash in text is considered by people a sign of AI-generated content, [writes Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2025/04/09/ai-em-dash-writing-punctuation-chatgpt/). Critics argue AI overuses it – proponents defend its aesthetic and semantic power. Journalist Aileen Gallagher calls the long American em dash a powerful tool, and other authors defend the dash as part of their style. AI experts admit models may prefer the square em dash, but writing style adapts to user prompts. The discussion about punctuation thus unexpectedly gained an ethical and aesthetic dimension.

**AI is suffocating Stack Overflow. Again!** Tomaž Weiss tracks [which topics are declining on Stack Overflow](https://tomazweiss.github.io/blog/stackoverflow_decline/) since AI's arrival. Most affected were general programming topics and data analysis tools like "lists", "pandas", or "SQL". Conversely, topics requiring visual context – such as operating systems and development tools like Android Studio or Azure – fared better. One can infer that where AI falls short on context and visualization, humans still have the upper hand.
