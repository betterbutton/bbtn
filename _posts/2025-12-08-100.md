## From The Designer

**Limit text line length.** Text readability also depends on paragraph width.

❌ Long lines across the entire screen\
✅ Lines approximately 60 — 80 characters long

👉 Text is easier for the eyes to scan.\
👉 Users get lost in lines less.

Reasonable paragraph width → more comfortable reading.

## Linkodrome

**One-time codes are ending, replaced by passkeys.** Traditional one-time verification codes can no longer sufficiently protect against phishing – [security experts and major companies are turning to passkeys](https://www.theregister.com/2025/12/06/multifactor_authentication_passkeys/). They work on the principle of cryptographic keys and are resistant to common attacks. They are supported by Apple, Google, and Microsoft, and their adoption is growing rapidly. Companies report significant reductions in login time and fewer helpdesk incidents.

**UX design for crisis situations.** *Vitaly Friedman* provides a practical guide on designing digital products that can withstand crisis and stressful situations. He warns that [stress reduces users' ability to think](https://www.smashingmagazine.com/2025/11/designing-for-stress-emergency/), make decisions, and prioritize – and design must adapt to this. He suggests reducing cognitive load, supporting clear steps and planning. He recommends testing products under real stressful conditions and preparing emergency modes.

**Precise prompts improve AI tool designs.** With the advent of AI prototyping tools, designers are learning to provide clear prompts instead of manual work. According to *Huei-Hsin Wang*, vague prompts fail: they lead to chaotic, illogical, and cluttered interfaces without hierarchy. AI lacks context and cannot decide like a human designer. For better results, Huei-Hsin [recommends using visual references, specific keywords, examples of data or code, and feedback from AI](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/vague-prototyping/).

**Usability principles in the era of generative AI.** Classic [Nielsen's usability heuristics](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/usability-heuristics) are no longer enough. Generative AI changes the way we interact. *Matt Soriano* proposes [10 new UX principles for AI](https://emesstyle.com/usability/). (But the website was probably generated by AI, so you first have to click on Gen AI and then on Start Exploring – the links probably aren't sexy enough.) Matt draws from research by *[Nielsen Norman Group](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ai-paradigm/)* and *[Microsoft HAX Toolkit](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/project/hax-toolkit/)*.

> “The best tech innovation in the last couple of years is not AI, it’s the ‘undo’ button when you just sent an email and noticed you forgot the link/attachment.”\
> **— [Stéphanie Walter](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7403445192143572992/)**

**Feedback doesn't scale (and what to do about it).** Feedback ceases to be effective once a team exceeds a certain size – usually around a few hundred people. Carter Baxter describes how a private conversation among acquaintances turns into an impersonal avalanche of opinions without context, which acts like an attack. As a company grows, personal relationships fade, trust and motivation are filtered out, and without a structured system for processing feedback, leaders drown in noise. The solution is delegated trust, structured channels, and the ability to separate meaningful criticism from noise. [Honesty, transparency, and humanity.](https://another.rodeo/feedback/)

**A recipe for a design review** that everyone will love. Designers at *Blinkist* faced a problem: their regular design review meetings were being canceled because no one wanted to present. Why? Stress, the need for perfect preparation, and unhelpful feedback. The solution was a format change – from *critique* to *Design Open Hour*, where the focus is not on evaluation but on collaboration. Every participant actively engages and contributes ideas in Figma. [The new approach improved output quality and the desire to meet.](https://uxdesign.cc/a-recipe-for-a-design-review-that-everyone-will-love-4991ab465772)

**GitHub Design System banned toast** messages due to inaccessibility. *Adam Silver* [agrees and analyzes four common objections](https://adamsilver.io/blog/can-you-make-toast-messages-accessible/) to the ban. Even though some experts claim toasts can be improved, according to Adam, this denies the very nature of the UI element. Toasts disappear, distract users, and are easily overlooked – even with technologies like screen readers. GitHub backed its [decision](https://primer.style/accessibility/toasts/) with research involving users with disabilities.

**Line length changes your view of layout.** *Andy Clarke* reflects on the traditional typographic rule called *measure* – line length – and shows how it fundamentally affects readability and the overall composition of a web layout. Instead of adapting content to screen width, he suggests the opposite approach: [let text dictate layout width](https://css-tricks.com/getting-creative-with-the-measure/). Using CSS variables and logical properties like `max-inline-size` or `column-width`, he proposes a system where measure becomes the foundation of design. When layout stems from text, it creates a more harmonious, natural, and readable web. Andy thus builds on the tradition of manual typesetting into the [sázítko](https://encyklopedieknihy.cz/index.php/S%C3%A1z%C3%ADtko).

*[Jared Spool](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/jared-spool)* fundamentally changed thinking about web design. Since the 90s, he has promoted the idea that the web should not be primarily an expression of technological capabilities or the creator's artistic vision, but should stem from user behavior and needs.

> “Good design, when it’s done well, becomes invisible.”\
> **— [Jared Spool](https://talks.ui-patterns.com/videos/the-curious-properties-of-intuitive-web-pages-jared-spool)**

With his research organization [UIE](https://aycl.uie.com/), he systematically proved that simple usability testing with real users uncovers fundamental problems that internal teams miss.

Jared helped establish data-driven design as a standard followed by leading web projects worldwide. His later work on strategic UX and [outcome‑driven design](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kWNzDES1zk4) moved the field forward – from the question *“Is the web usable?”* to *“Are we achieving business goals?”*

Jared's influence thus extends not only to the quality of individual websites but to the way web teams organize, make decisions, and measure their success today.

## Toolbox

[DownDetector](https://downdetector.com/for-business/) doesn't monitor servers but collects outage reports from users. It then compiles an overview of online service outages. The business version allows connection to internal systems and provides real-time alerts.

## To Get on the Same Page

[Plain Old Semantic HTML](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/posh) is a return to roots: writing HTML source code so that it carries meaning itself. Developers prefer semantic tags over visual classes because they ensure accessibility, search engine understanding, and long-term code maintainability.

[SARAH](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/sarah) (Shock, Anger, Denial, Acceptance, Help) describes the emotional phases users go through when digital products change. It's based on Elisabeth Kübler-Ross's grief theory and helps designers better understand why people often react negatively to new interfaces. Designers who apply this framework can turn change into a smooth transition and improve adoption.

[Vyfikundace](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/vyfikundace) is a playful Czech term for a seemingly smart improvement that easily turns into unnecessary complication. In digital design, it names modifications that start as innovations but end in frustration – they slow loading, confuse users, and destroy the interface's purpose.

[Conformity bias](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/conformity-bias) is the tendency to uncritically follow the majority. Designers use conformity with elements like ratings, leaderboards, or *“X people are currently watching”*. If used honestly, it helps people decide and builds trust. When misused – for example, with fake numbers or made-up reviews – it risks loss of trust and legal consequences.
