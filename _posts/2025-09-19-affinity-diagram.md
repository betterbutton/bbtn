# Affinity Diagram
> A practical method for sorting unordered data, uncovering patterns, and finding connections.

An affinity diagram ([affinity diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affinity_diagram), [K.J. method](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/243785588_The_KJ_Method_A_Technique_for_Analyzing_Data_Derived_from_Japanese_Ethnology)) is a simple tool that helps organize large amounts of unordered information by grouping related ideas together. It is mainly used in qualitative user research, product design, or workshop facilitation. It is essentially synonymous with [thematic analysis](https://kisk.phil.muni.cz/100metod/tematicka-analyza). It helps quickly find patterns and gain meaningful insights from data that at first glance makes no sense.

The process is simple. Everything starts with individual notes—such as insights from interviews, feature ideas, problems, or observations. These notes are spread out on a wall or digital canvas and then sorted into groups based on similarity. Once natural clusters appear, you assign them names that capture the common theme.

An affinity diagram is useful wherever there is too much information or it doesn't make clear sense. It allows you to focus on the relationships between individual points and collectively reach understanding. It works best in the early phases of product design, when there is a need to align different perspectives and find a way forward.

When used correctly, an affinity diagram helps find structure in chaos. No complicated technique is needed—just paper, walls, and a willingness to collaborate. It reveals not only recurring themes but often also overlooked or contradictory opinions. For web and mobile app creators, it's a great way to manage complexity, make data-driven decisions, and find alignment without unnecessary documentation.

The method was developed by the Japanese ethnographer [Jiro Kawakita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jiro_Kawakita), which is why this approach is also called the K.J. method. It is not just a method. It is a way of thinking: first sort, then name—and let the meaning emerge on its own.
