# Visual hierarchy
> Visual hierarchy – a design principle that uses size, color, and layout to determine what the user pays attention to and in what order.

Visual hierarchy ([visual hierarchy](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/visual-hierarchy#:~:text=Visual%20hierarchy%20is%20the%20principle,users%20can%20understand%20information%20easily.)) is a silent guide that suggests where a person will look first. It helps ensure that important elements, like headings or buttons, capture attention before less important ones. When it's missing, users feel lost, get confused, and often abandon the interface completely.

[Visual hierarchy is created using several basic elements](https://visme.co/blog/visual-hierarchy/): size, contrast, spacing, alignment, and placement. Larger and more prominent elements are noticed first. Color and contrast guide the eyes. Proximity of elements shows they belong together. Alignment and order help better understand what belongs together and what is important.

When all elements have the same weight, the interface feels chaotic. [A clear hierarchy, on the other hand, creates order.](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/visual-hierarchy-ux-definition/) It helps eyes scan content naturally and without thinking. It also contributes to clarity and improves results because users can more easily find what they need.

On a login screen, the login fields should be prominent. Links like "forgot password" or "terms of service" should be visible but unobtrusive. Ample whitespace and a prominent confirmation button also help.

[Good visual hierarchy doesn't feel intrusive.](https://www.figma.com/resource-library/what-is-visual-hierarchy/) It just quietly does its job and helps people use the app without unnecessary thinking. When users can orient themselves at a glance, it's a sign of a job well done.
