## Did Steve Jobs do user research?

TL;DR – [He did.](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TzimyboADaw&list=PLP3wVq5za6jj0xg83lV9TBX1BemK3qi_C&index=2)

Every now and then, someone emerges who starts questioning the purpose of user research by citing that *Steve Jobs* didn't do any user research and yet led one of the most innovative product companies. The Jobs argument.

Actually, we know where this came from. Steve Jobs once said in an interview with BusinessWeek: *"It’s really hard to design products by focus groups. A lot of times, people don’t know what they want until you show it to them."* And from that, we were left with the idea that Steve Jobs didn't acknowledge user research.

![There's Sanity Returning. BusinessWeek (May 25, 1998). Steve Jobs: „It’s really hard to design products by focus groups. A lot of times, people don’t know what they want until you show it to them.“](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/ad6cebc1-f14b-4059-95a2-9e28acfc8667_665x569.png "449x384")

[There's Sanity Returning.](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/1998-05-25/steve-jobs-theres-sanity-returning) BusinessWeek (May 25, 1998)

But that's not quite true. Steve Jobs simply knew that user research isn't there for designers to go around asking users what to design. On YouTube, you can watch his [1991 lecture](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TzimyboADaw&list=PLP3wVq5za6jj0xg83lV9TBX1BemK3qi_C&index=2), where he mentions several times that he works with user data, that he meets with users to see how they work with a computer.

Ten years ago, *[Egan Schulz](https://www.linkedin.com/in/eganschulz)*, among other things a former designer of Apple's [Aperture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aperture_(software) app, gave a lecture in Plzeň. When asked about user research, he replied something to the effect that they didn't do any user research. Instead, Apple arranged for them to spend a year observing the best photographers of their time at work and talking with them about how they sort and archive their images.

What else is user research if not the ability to observe potential users of a proposed product while they work?

Also *[Joy Mountford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joy_Mountford)*, former Head of the Human Interface Group at Apple, mentions in an [interview for the ACM oral history](https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3505180) that her interdisciplinary team conducted experiments with users. It is true, however, that Joy joined Apple after Jobs left and left around the same time as Don Norman before Jobs's return.

Maybe we just have a too distorted idea of what user research is and what it's good for.

## Linkodrome

**You don't have a better position in search, it's a measurement error.** Google turned off the `num=100` parameter in the URL of the results page. Simply put: you or scraping tools for tracking search positions can no longer display a page with more than the default ten search results. Measurement tools thus no longer record lower positions in search results, and as a result, the average position comes out jumpily better. Some tools like Semrush or Ahrefs already indicate a limit on the number of tracked positions in search results. If Google doesn't reverse the change, position tracking will become significantly more expensive, and the SEO community [will have to reconsider its data interpretations](https://brodieclark.com/the-great-decoupling-num100/).

**Design patterns for a successful product.** Product design isn't magic, but a craft that can be based on proven patterns. [Learning Loop](https://learningloop.io/playbooks/) offers an overview of four types of product design patterns: validation patterns help test hypotheses before investing, business models show paths to sustainable revenue, persuasive patterns use psychology to influence user behavior, and workshop patterns support team collaboration. By combining these approaches, products can be designed more effectively, with less risk and a greater chance of market success.

> “‘My toddler could do that.’ Maybe I could do better, maybe I couldn’t but I didn’t. Respect to the executioners.”\
> **— [Christina Wodtke](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7375607765853118464/)**

**When to reach for structure and when for ideas?** Decision-making is often slow and cumbersome, so [the best leaders combine two opposite approaches](https://plan.io/blog/convergent-vs-divergent-thinking/): convergent and divergent thinking. Convergent thinking relies on logic, data, and detailed analysis, reducing risks and providing certainty. Divergent thinking, on the other hand, opens the path to ideas, innovations, and unexpected solutions. *Jory MacKay* shows a four-step process for combining them in projects: first explore causes (divergent), then narrow down options (convergent), generate ideas again (divergent), and finally plan and deliver the solution in detail (convergent).

**The support team is often the most affected by every UX decision,** yet it is frequently an overlooked partner in product development. Yet they have the most direct contact with users and can offer valuable insights and concrete suggestions for improvement. We should [actively collaborate with support during design](https://medium.com/design-bootcamp/how-to-unlock-your-hidden-ux-partner-the-support-team-fff32f5b35ec) – listen to them, observe their work in practice, invite them to share ideas, and publicly acknowledge their contributions. Even minor UX changes can significantly impact their daily work and, in turn, the perceived quality of the product.

**When do companies start taking design seriously?** *Jared Spool* explains in an interview what the so-called [UX tipping point](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CBU6y2av3sI&list=PLwi5x0jZPGVd9trTkS-QyC8_YiTE9gYiW&index=53) means – the moment when a company starts placing equal or greater emphasis on design than on technical functionality and business requirements. Until then, it often holds that *if it technically works, it's enough*, even if the user experience is terrible. Crossing this threshold changes the game: the company realizes that a poorly designed product is against its brand and values. The path to this is long, requiring skill improvement, cultural and hiring changes, and years of patient work.

> “The better your organization understands its customers and the value you provide to them, the less susceptible it will be to FOMO over hyped-up trends.”\
> **— [Erika Hall](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7374898605293420544/)**

**The current state of support for alternative text for CSS-generated content** is summarized by *Sara Soueidan*. The new syntax `content: ... / "alt text"` allows specifying how such content should be interpreted by screen readers. Although browser support for this property has improved, screen readers process it inconsistently, and CSS content remains unsearchable, untranslatable, and invisible in modes like Reader Mode. Sara recommends [always putting meaningful content into HTML](https://www.sarasoueidan.com/blog/alt-text-for-css-generated-content/) and using CSS more for decorative purposes – with an empty alt text.

**Case study: NotebookLM.** *Jason Spielman*, the project's lead designer, describes the journey [from early sketches to an adaptive three-panel interface](https://jasonspielman.com/notebooklm), which combines reading, chat, and creation into one fluid experience. Special emphasis was placed on solving *“tab overwhelm”* and on rapid iteration in close collaboration with users. The result is an AI-native tool that brings not only chat but also innovations like Audio Overviews, flashcards, or quizzes – and shows that small teams at Google can move entire industries.

![HOW MIGHT WE vs. AT WHAT COST OUTCOME: What's the intended benefit? What might go wrong? What risks or harms might emerge? CONSEQUENCES: What are the expected short- or medium-term benefits? What are potential short- or medium-term risks or downsides? RIPPLE EFFECTS: What are long-term or wider impacts (e.g. social, environmental, ethical)? Who might be harmed in the long run? As designers, we are responsible for the intentional and unintentional consequences of our work.

**Design must bear responsibility too.** *Ida Persson* issues an urgent call to rethink the *“move fast and break things”* ethos in design and technology, especially in light of the tragedy of a fourteen-year-old boy who took his own life after interacting with a chatbot. Ida emphasizes that design must be a balance between curiosity (How might we) and critical consideration of impacts (At what cost). She points out the necessity of [considering impacts and asking ethical questions](https://uxdesign.cc/from-how-might-we-to-at-what-cost-b635a595afd2) before we act.

**I'm not a robot** – easy to say, harder to prove. *Neal Agarwal* collected pretty much all kinds of CAPTCHA examples and [turned them into a game](https://neal.fun/not-a-robot/), where solving each task advances you to a higher game level. ## Adele Goldberg is the godmother of graphical interfaces

![Adele Goldberg](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/f9c24872-9d6c-43c5-8f73-19189db40b1e_1536x1024.webp "598x398")

[Adele Goldberg](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/adele-goldberg) developed and popularized WIMP.

[Adele Goldberg](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/adele-goldberg) designed the first graphical user interface at the *Xerox PARC* labs. She collaborated on the development of the object-oriented programming language *Smalltalk* and was instrumental in creating the [WIMP – windows, icons, menus, pointer](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/WIMP) model. Her work became the foundation for today's operating systems and web technologies. Steve Jobs adopted the concept for the [Macintosh](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macintosh_128K) operating system and the [Apple Newton](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Newton) PDA. She advocated for the [model–view–controller](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model-view-controller) architecture and emphasized accessibility. In the famous [Smalltalk­‑80](https://www.goodreads.com/author/show/427672.Adele_Goldberg) book series, she described not only the language but also the philosophy of working with a computer. Thanks to her, the computer became a [tool for learning](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynabook), creativity, and daily work – and she continues to influence the world of computing.

## Toolbox

[Poline](https://meodai.github.io/poline/) is a tool for generating color palettes that uses arcs in polar coordinates instead of classic interpolation. This creates unexpected and smooth transitions, suitable for generative art, UI themes, or experimental design.

## To understand each other

[Keyboard shortcuts](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/keyboard-shortcut) are a way to increase speed, accessibility, and efficiency in controlling digital tools. They help users save time, reduce the need to use a mouse, and support a smooth transition from beginners to advanced users. Well-designed shortcuts won't fix bad design, but they will significantly enhance good design.

[Affinity diagram](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/affinity-diagram) is a technique for sorting unordered data into thematic groups. It helps reveal patterns, connections, and meaningful insights – ideally in the early phases of product design or during workshops. The method involves visually grouping notes by similarity and naming the resulting clusters.

[Feature creep](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/feature-creep) describes a common problem in digital products: the continuous addition of new features, which ultimately weakens the original purpose of the tool. Each new feature seems harmless, but without a clear vision and the ability to prioritize, the product becomes cluttered, slow, and discourages new users.
