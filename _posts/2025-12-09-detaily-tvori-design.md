# Details Make Design
> In UX, even the smallest details—in text, visuals, and interaction—decide on trust, satisfaction, and perceived quality.

> *You are opening the ninth window of the 2025 BBtn Advent Calendar. In this year's advent series, we'll look at popular proverbs from UX designers. Today's is: Details make design.*

A good design stands on details. Research shows that users form an impression of a website [in just fifty milliseconds](https://static.googleusercontent.com/media/research.google.com/en//pubs/archive/38315.pdf). From layout, colors, spacing, and typography, they judge whether they trust the page and whether they will stay. Aesthetically appealing interfaces are also perceived as more usable—this is the [phenomenon of aesthetic usability](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/aesthetic-usability) confirmed by research by *[Kurosu](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/221517663_Apparent_usability_vs_inherent_usabilityhttps://www.researchgate.net/publication/221517663_Apparent_usability_vs_inherent_usability)*[,](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/221517663_Apparent_usability_vs_inherent_usabilityhttps://www.researchgate.net/publication/221517663_Apparent_usability_vs_inherent_usability) *[Kashimura](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/221517663_Apparent_usability_vs_inherent_usabilityhttps://www.researchgate.net/publication/221517663_Apparent_usability_vs_inherent_usability)*, and *[Tractinsky](https://www.ise.bgu.ac.il/faculty/noam/papers/00_nt_ask_di_iwc.pdf)*.

But it's not just about looks. Button states, animation rhythm, or progress indicators—these are small [microinteractions](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/microinteractions), as *[Dan Saffer](https://www.odannyboy.com/)* calls them. They provide feedback and give the system a sense of responsiveness. Short texts are equally important: for example, the phrase *“No payment card required”* can alleviate uncertainty and encourage action. Even the perception of speed improves when loading is accompanied by subtle visual cues.

Ignored details, on the other hand, destroy trust. Broken links, misaligned elements, or outdated copyright give the impression that no one cares about the website. This is the digital equivalent of *[“broken windows”](https://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/archive/1982/03/broken-windows/304465/)*. A consistent design system prevents these errors and allows quality to be maintained even as the project grows.

The idea isn't new. *[Charles Eames](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_and_Ray_Eames)* said that *“details are not details—they make the product itself.”* *[Dieter Rams](https://www.vitsoe.com/eu/about/good-design?utm_source=chatgpt.com#ten-principles-for-good-design)*, meanwhile, insisted that good design is meticulous down to the last detail. The same holds true in the digital world: attention to minutiae expresses respect for the user and for the craft.

Every pixel, every word, and every delay influences how people will feel. Details won't save a weak concept, but they can turn a good design into a trustworthy, smooth, and lively experience. Ultimately, it's these invisible minutiae that give design and user experience genuine life.
