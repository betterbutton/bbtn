❌ Cramped text, small gaps\
✅ More space between headings, paragraphs, and blocks

👉 Content is clearer and stands out better.\
👉 Readers' eyes scan faster.

## Linkodrome

**Discard scales in surveys,** recommends Mohsen Rafiei. People don't rate experiences with numbers, but by comparison – and UX research should reflect that. Mohsen describes his journey [from rating questions to comparison-based questioning](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7320955956039741440/). People rate relatively, not absolutely. Although quantitative data has its place, asking for comparisons is key to deeper understanding.

![Selection bias: a presenter proudly shows the conclusion of a bar graph of responses from people asked if they like answering surveys. A clear "yes".](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/b8f64d56-bd8b-4d70-8f54-2dbf8f6ee824_3458x2931.jpeg "476x403")

[Sampling bias from Sketchplanations](https://sketchplanations.com/sampling-bias) ([CC BY-NC](https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-nc/4.0/))

**A glossary of terms for design tokens.** Are you confused by the terms frontend developers use around design tokens? The [DesignTokens.FYI](https://designtokens.fyi/#/terms/dtcg/) project should clarify things for you. You'll find an overview of terms used in the [Design Token Community Group](https://www.designtokens.org/) along with examples.

**A/B testing at Google and Meta is misleading.** Adam Šilhan warns that Google and Meta reinforce a false sense of data-driven decision-making among marketers, while their own A/B testing tools have fundamental limitations. Platforms deliberately show different ad versions to different user groups based on expected interest during tests. This disrupts randomization and [A/B test results cannot be generalized beyond the platforms](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7320754565023162369/). To effectively scale campaigns and gain valuable insights, one must be very careful when interpreting results.

**The future belongs to ~~aluminum~~ artificial intelligence.** At least when it comes to accessibility. [Karl Groves will try to convince you](https://karlgroves.com/ai-is-the-future-of-accessibility/) of this. Even today, AI significantly improves the lives of people with disabilities – from automatic captions to assistive apps and navigation aids. Accessibility now has a chance to technologically leapfrog its limitations, if its advocates embrace and help shape AI. Karl calls for collaboration and innovation to make the future inclusive.

**Vibe coding with the UML language** isn't about writing complete specification documents. It's about capturing intent enough to share it effectively. Jan Kodera suggests using [UML as a supporting tool for vibe coding](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/vibe-coding-meets-uml-structured-approach-creative-software-kodera-lgjye/?trackingId=Krs3%2Fu1CQqyd4VVkHNacDg%3D%3D). Not to limit creativity, but to improve its sharing and maintenance in growing teams. UML serves him as a visual meta-language that facilitates contextual anchoring, faster colleague onboarding, and increased code testability. However, this approach is mainly suitable for advanced developers – you must first [understand UML](https://www.itnetwork.cz/navrh/uml/uml-uvod-historie-vyznam-a-diagramy).
