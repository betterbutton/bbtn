# OOUX: Object-Oriented UX
> Object-Oriented UX is a design approach that builds design on objects, their properties, and relationships. Not on user tasks.

[Object-Oriented UX](https://www.ooux.com/), known as [OOUX](https://uxdesign.cc/what-is-object-oriented-ux-19e14c435d18), designs interfaces based on what actually exists in a system—namely, objects. Not based on what the user needs to do. Instead of tasks, the design centers on things like a course, product, or profile. When well-named and connected, they help users orient themselves quickly.

Classic UX methods often start with what goals the user has or what they need to do. OOUX starts with a different question: what objects does our system contain? What are their properties? And how are they related? The ORCA framework—objects, relationships, actions, and attributes—describes this in detail [by Sophia Prater in an article on A List Apart](https://alistapart.com/article/ooux-a-foundation-for-interaction-design/). The first presentation of this approach was published [on the same site as early as 2015](https://alistapart.com/article/object-oriented-ux/), where she showed how OOUX can be used as a foundation for interaction design.

OOUX is particularly useful where content is complex. This applies to university websites, large editorial systems, or tools with rich data structures. In such an environment, a simple "search" button or "submit form" often isn't enough. Users are helped when they can navigate between understandable objects.

The principles of OOUX are also summarized in [a short video from the Nielsen Norman Group](https://www.nngroup.com/videos/object-oriented-ux-ooux/), which shows how object-oriented thinking makes it easier to navigate an interface. [The LogRocket blog](https://blog.logrocket.com/ux-design/object-oriented-ux-ooux/) then adds further practical examples. OOUX is not a trend. It is a design approach that reminds us that before actions, comes understanding of what we even have in front of us.
