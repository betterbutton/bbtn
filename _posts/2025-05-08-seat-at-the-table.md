# Seat at the Table
> Having a seat at the table means being part of the decision-making, especially at the company leadership level.

Design is becoming an integral part of successful business. Companies are hiring more designers, entire design teams are being formed, managers are being brought on board, and design thinking is becoming part of the corporate culture. Yet, designer participation at the top level of corporate governance remains an exception.

Some examples are well-known. Pinterest founder [Ben Silbermann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ben_Silbermann) is a designer. [Brian Chesky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brian_Chesky) from Airbnb is one of the few designers who leads a large company. But that's almost where the list ends. Even where design plays a key role—like at Apple, Google, or Intuit—leadership often remains in the hands of people with business or technical backgrounds.

Why is that? Many designers don't speak the language of business. They don't work with financial statements, don't read balance sheets, don't talk about strategies, and don't understand legal contexts. How many designers have an economic education? How many of them know what mergers or acquisitions entail? If we don't understand the basic concepts of company leadership, it's hard to claim a seat among the decision-makers.

Yet, it is precisely design that brings what corporate leadership needs—the ability to understand people, navigate uncertainty, and create meaningful solutions. These are skills often missing in boardrooms. But it's not enough to just want to be heard. You need to understand the context and speak the same language as the others at the table ([seat at the table](https://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/english/seat-at-the-table)).

If designers want to influence the direction of the company, it's not enough to just draw interfaces. They must understand the numbers, the decisions, and the risks. And then it doesn't matter who invited you to the table. What matters is that you know why you are there.
