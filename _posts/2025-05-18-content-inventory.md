# Content Inventory
> A structured overview of content that facilitates redesign, auditing, and the creation of user-friendly solutions – content inventory.

Content Inventory ([content inventory](https://theuxcookbook.com/content-inventories)) is the foundation for any larger digital project, especially during redesign, migration, or evaluation of existing content. It is a clearly structured list of all elements contained on a website or in an application – from pages, PDF documents, and videos to product cards or meta-information.

Its true value lies in what it reveals: outdated content, structural deficiencies, duplicates, or gaps in information coverage. For designers and architects, it's not just the scope that matters, but primarily the quality and coherence.

Unlike a [content audit](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/content-audits/), which evaluates content quality and effectiveness, an inventory focuses on describing the current state. It answers the question: what do we have. It often serves as the basis for further analyses.

A typical content inventory includes page addresses, titles, content types, location within the site, responsible persons, last modification date, and often also data on traffic or occurrence in navigation. Thanks to added tags or metadata, it creates a map-like foundation according to which the entire content ecosystem can be understood and managed.

Thanks to the inventory, an information architect can easily recognize how content is distributed, what the relationships between individual elements are, and where there might be weak spots in orientation. For a content designer, the inventory is a valuable resource for identifying unsuitable communication tones, inappropriate formats, or missing content parts. The document also highlights broken paths, interrupted scenarios, or missing supporting information.

An inventory can be created manually or using automated tools. A manually compiled document allows for higher precision, while an automated one provides a quick overview. Best practice is often a combination of both.

It is important that the inventory does not remain a static document – it should be shared, regularly updated, and used across teams. A well-maintained content inventory serves not only UX and content design but also development, SEO, the legal team, or the editorial staff.

A content inventory is not just a list – it is a strategic tool for creating meaningful, accessible, and user-friendly digital spaces.
