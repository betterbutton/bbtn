## Linkodrome

**Seznam Email is introducing the Orders tab.** It will display an overview of orders and their current status based on structured data from emails. This is great news for users, but we website creators need to do something about it. Seznam supports [structured data for Order](https://schema.seznam.cz/objednavky/dokumentace/) in JSON-LD and microdata formats according to the [schema.org](https://schema.org/Order) standard. Order updates occur based on new emails with the same order number. The feature will not affect email delivery or sorting. Launch is expected by the end of March, and testing is already underway.

![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/168b8732-d088-49cd-87e4-3166003c7ecb_738x738.png "410x410")

**Search engines are turning into responders.** Perplexity.ai admits this openly. But how do you ensure your website appears among its sources? David Bureš advises [focusing on high-quality, structured, and authoritative content](https://digichef.cz/jak-funguje-perplexity-ai-pro-a-jak-byt-na-nem-videt) – we even have an acronym for it: [EEAT](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/ai-serp). Perplexity primarily evaluates lists, reviews, domain authority, and well-optimized pages. Unlike the standard version, Perplexity Pro offers more precise answers and supports file handling. For SEO specialists, it is therefore important to adapt the website not only for Google but also for AI search engines. The future of search is answers, not clicks on links.

**Designing for unexpected situations.** When designing, we focus on common scenarios. We use phrases like happy day scenario, 80/20. But what if something doesn't go according to plan? Edge cases – rare or extreme situations – [will test the quality of the design and significantly impact the user experience](https://medium.com/@prajaktabirajdar00/designing-for-the-unexpected-handling-edge-cases-in-ui-ux-607a52ce38e4). Unexpected inputs, system errors, accessibility, and extreme user behavior are areas where design fails if not well thought out. An example of successful adaptation is Apple improving Face ID for masked users. Preparedness for the unexpected makes digital products more resilient and user-friendly.

**It's not enough to be first. You have to be better.** [Tuomas Artman](https://www.linkedin.com/in/tuomasartman/) on Linear's blog breaks down what it means to launch a product today. It's not always necessary to come up with a groundbreaking [MVP](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimum_viable_product), but rather with a leapfrog improvement over existing competition. Being first makes sense when conquering space, but in business, pioneers of new areas rarely survive long-term. Only disruptive startups create entirely new markets. Most companies, however, look for ways to improve existing products. Only when users stop asking for new features and are willing to pay for the product is it [time for a public launch](https://linear.app/blog/rethinking-the-startup-mvp-building-a-competitive-product).

[CRAP – a design concept anyone can master](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hqh31XvUpic)

Not every designer is a visual designer. Once we worked with a colleague who came to design from business analysis, and his wireframes and prototypes showed he had no formal graphic training. Fortunately, Jakub's partner took him under his wing and showed him how [CRAP](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/crap) works. Since then, our colleague's designs have been professionally tidy. If a business analyst can learn it, then anyone can.
