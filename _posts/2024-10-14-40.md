# Why is the INP threshold exactly 200 ms

**Why is the INP threshold exactly 200 milliseconds?** The INP metric ([Interaction to Next Paint](https://web.dev/articles/inp)) measures the time that elapses from a user action to the interface's response. It's one of the three [Core Web Vitals](https://web.dev/articles/vitals) introduced by Google. If the response arrives within 200 milliseconds, it's considered ideal. But why exactly 200 milliseconds? We tried to answer this question in a lecture at FrontKon.

For the answer, we must travel back in time to the first quarter of the 19th century. Then Jan Evangelista Purkyně [experimented](https://ziva.avcr.cz/jan-evangelista-purkyne.html#:~:text=Postup%20objektivn%C3%ADho%20vy%C5%A1et%C5%99ov%C3%A1n%C3%AD%20oka%20(1823)) with eye examination possibilities and discovered that [the reflection of a candle flame mirrors in the eye four times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purkinje_images). Purkyně used this effect to measure the curvature of the eye's lens, but at the same time laid the foundations for eye-tracking. Thanks to him, we can track where a person is looking.

A few years later, the Leipzig physician Ernst Heinrich Weber laid the foundations of experimental psychology when he described a phenomenon that today bears his name: [Weber's Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weber%E2%80%93Fechner_law). Weber found that the stronger the initial stimulus, the greater the difference must be for a person to perceive it. And vice versa. So with weak stimuli, we perceive even smaller differences more intensely.

And now we can jump to the 20th century. Users of computer terminals often had to wait a long time for a response on the monitor after pressing a key. Robert B. Miller from IBM found in 1968 that [anything that happens within a tenth of a second](https://yusufarslan.net/sites/yusufarslan.net/files/upload/content/Miller1968.pdf) is considered instantaneous. Ben Shneiderman elaborated on this finding [sixteen years later](https://www.cs.umd.edu/~ben/papers/Shneiderman1984Response.pdf) and established that a response above half a second affects productivity and user satisfaction. And exactly 500 milliseconds is the second INP threshold.

At the end of 2013, scientists from MIT found that [a person is capable of detecting changes](https://dspace.mit.edu/handle/1721.1/107157) in an interval of 13 milliseconds. However, possibly even shorter, as they didn't have the technology to display changes faster back then. Today we have displays with refresh rates up to 240 Hz, which represents an interval of 4.17 milliseconds.

Google already published in 2009 [experiments with search results display latency](https://services.google.com/fh/files/blogs/google_delayexp.pdf) in the order of tenths of a second. It found that if it took longer to display results, people reduced the number of searches. Similar results are also shown by recent Speedcurve measurements, which on mobile devices demonstrated [a correlation between INP and conversion rate](https://www.speedcurve.com/blog/core-web-vitals-inp-mobile/#:~:text=conversion%20correlated%20with%20mobile%20INP).

And that brings us to the answer to the initial question. INP has a threshold set at two-tenths of a second because that's a delay that the user already registers and has an impact on their willingness to continue interacting. For Google, this metric is essential also because it easily quantifies user experience, and thus can be [included in signals](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/12#%C2%A7zivot-s-technologiemi) for search result ranking.

**A professional can always clearly and understandably explain their procedures and results**

If a designer is not even able to describe their work process at a basic level, it should be a warning signal for you about potential complications in the future. You might be lucky, and in your specific case, they will intuitively do their job well.

But if problems arise, communication about their causes and remediation will be difficult because a designer who doesn't enlighten you about their way of thinking and work plan won't be able to help you effectively. As the classic says: If you can't explain it to a child, you don't understand it yourself.

Be wary of those who hide their procedures. Even though the creative process might seem like magic to less experienced clients, it really isn't. Creativity is a way of solving problems and a skill. A true professional informs you about the work status at any moment and continuously shows you results to ensure they are proceeding correctly and fulfilling your requirements precisely.
