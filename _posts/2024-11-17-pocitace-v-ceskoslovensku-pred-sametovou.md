# Computers in Czechoslovakia before the Velvet Revolution
> 

In 1989, only 1.8% of Czechoslovak households had a computer, write Petr Kočí and Honza Boček from the data team of Czech Radio. [Computers were luxury goods](https://www.irozhlas.cz/zpravy-domov/v-roce-1989-mely-pocitac-dve-domacnosti-ze-sta-dnes-je-obtizne-ho-nemit-_201411120544_pkoci), whose availability was limited by high customs and limited supplies. Domestic brands could not meet the demand.

While in the West, a technological revolution led by microprocessors was underway, the Eastern Bloc stagnated. This very [gap between the West's technical progress and socialist](https://plus.rozhlas.cz/zajimave-se-deje-na-zapade-zjistovali-nadsenci-do-pocitacu-v-ceskoslovensku-tak-8481228) reality fueled disillusionment, especially among young people. Computer games, miniaturization, and the growing availability of technologies paradoxically contributed to the regime's decay, which lost the ability to compete with dynamic development outside its borders.

In socialist Czechoslovakia, computers became a symbol of the regime's technical backwardness. Vít Pohanka on Czech Television shows how enthusiasts often [replaced scarce equipment with their own improvisations](https://art.ceskatelevize.cz/inside/artchiv-socialisticti-geekove-aneb-hrajeme-si-s-basicem-9MpXy), such as a mouse from medicine boxes or a joystick from a calculator.

Before the Velvet Revolution, [computing technology in Czechoslovakia](https://www.computerworld.cz/clanky/ceskoslovenske-kremikove-nebe-jak-vypadaly-tuzemske-pocitace-pred-rokem-1989/) focused on domestic products, such as the 8-bit computers [Didaktik Gama](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Didaktik_Gama), [IQ-151](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/IQ_151), or [PMD-85](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/PMD_85).
