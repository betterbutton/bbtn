# Improve Text Accessibility
> 

Accessibility on a website or in an app is not just a matter of technology or design. Content—the text that forms an important bridge between the user and your service—plays a major role. Accessible texts make orientation easier, increase comprehensibility, and help make your websites accessible to a wider range of users. In this article, you'll find practical tips on how to write texts that are understandable and useful for everyone.

## Basic Principles of Accessible Writing

1.  **Use simple language:** Write concisely and clearly. Avoid complex sentences, technical jargon, and rarely used words. If a technical term is necessary, explain it in simple words.

2.  **Write in the active voice:** Active sentences are direct and easy to understand. For example: "Fill out the form" instead of "The form should be filled out."

3.  **Clear structure:** Break text into shorter paragraphs and use headings and subheadings. This allows users to easily scan the text and find the information they need.

4.  **Unambiguous information:** Avoid ambiguities, idioms, or cultural references that may not be universally understood.

## How to Write Texts for Different User Groups

For users with cognitive impairments, it is crucial that texts are as simple and clear as possible. Use short sentences and avoid complex grammatical constructions, such as gerunds or less common word forms. Instructions should be clear and unambiguous. For example, instead of "Submit the filled-out form by pressing the button," write "Click the Submit button."

Deaf users often consider written language their second language, so texts should be as simple as possible. Avoid idioms and cultural references that may not be understood. Supplement text with visual elements, such as pictograms, icons, or illustrations, that help explain the message better. If you use technical or complex terms, include a brief explanation.

For users with visual impairments, it is important that texts are well-interpretable by screen readers. Buttons and links must have meaningful labels, for example, "Show more information" instead of "Click here." Provide alternative text for images, diagrams, and other visual content that briefly describes their meaning. ## Practical Examples of Accessible Texts

Links should be clear and informative. Instead of "Click here," use, for example, "More information about privacy protection." Similarly, buttons should contain a precise description of the action, such as "Submit form" instead of just "Submit." Error messages should not only inform the user about the problem but also offer a solution. For example, "Please enter a valid email address." is much more helpful than the generic "Error while filling out."

## How to Test Texts

Testing the accessibility of texts is an integral part of their creation. Involve real users with diverse needs, such as deaf, low-vision, or people with cognitive impairments. Their feedback will help you identify weaknesses and improve the content. Additionally, use tools like [WAVE](https://wave.webaim.org/) or [Axe](https://www.deque.com/axe/), which will reveal technical accessibility issues. Finally, it's a good practice to have texts reviewed by colleagues or people outside your team before publication, as they can offer a fresh perspective.

Accessible texts are the foundation of a good website or application. Texts written clearly and concisely will facilitate orientation, increase user comfort, and open your service to a wider audience. Follow these tips, test texts regularly, and learn from the feedback you receive. Every step toward greater accessibility counts!🎄15
