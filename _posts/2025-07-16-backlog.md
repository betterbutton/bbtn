# Backlog
> A list of tasks, features, bugs, ideas, or requirements that need to be addressed in the future. Without active care, it easily turns into a burden full of confusion and inaction.

[Backlog](https://www.atlassian.com/agile/scrum/backlogs) is meant to help the team focus on what's essential — it's [a list of tasks that are next in line](https://agile.prokoho.cz/sestaveni-backlogu/). Yet all too often, it becomes a dumping ground for ideas, outdated requirements, and forgotten tasks. Instead of providing clarity, it starts creating tension, indecision, and wasted energy.

In its ideal form, the backlog reflects current priorities. It emerges from a combination of product strategy, user feedback, and technical feasibility. In its worst form, it's just a pile of unvetted tasks that suffocates the team and undermines motivation.

The solution is to treat the backlog as editorial content, not a warehouse. The product manager should regularly prune, archive, and refine individual items. Every task should have a clear reason for being there and deliver real value. Backlog grooming then stops being a necessary evil and becomes meaningful editorial work.

A useful backlog isn't a prediction of the future. It's an overview of what's important right now. And if yours looks more like chaos than a compass, it's high time to start cleaning it up. Less noise, more direction. That's the difference between movement and real forward momentum.
