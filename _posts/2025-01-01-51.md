## Linkodrome

**UX for New Year's resolutions?** Why not, really. We UX designers have that superpower to apply our practices to any area. Robert Werkmeister recommends looking at yourself as a user and starting with an analysis of your own needs and goals. He continues with tactical advice on how to [mitigate the risk of failure](https://medium.com/design-bootcamp/how-to-approach-new-years-resolutions-from-a-ux-perspective-5a35095f061d) for the Resolution 2025 project. What we like most about it is the idea that we will adjust our resolutions continuously based on feedback.

> „This year, ditch the pressure to be perfect and focus on progress instead.“\
> **— [Christopher Nguyen](https://uxplaybook.org/articles/guide-to-realistic-new-years-resolutions-for-designers?srsltid=AfmBOopAIa2cbtATzsrh_OMxW2DbtOeWdX92PYwxP_ZfnoFWNPyGjtTt)**

**Focus on progress,** is the advice from Christopher Nguyen. He thinks the main problem with resolutions is that we view them as a perfect goal. **UX trends for 2025** are clearer than ever this time.

- **Accessibility** is heralded by the effective date of the [European accessibility law](https://www.zakonyprolidi.cz/cs/2023-424). It falls on Saturday, June 28, 2025.

- **Artificial intelligence** won't let us catch our breath. Perhaps the promised autonomous AI agents will help us with our work, and maybe we'll start [designing adaptive interfaces](https://www.questlabs.ai/blog/adaptive-user-interface), which have been talked about all year.

But that's just the beginning. [State of UX](https://trends.uxdesign.cc/) brings seven more insights into what awaits us. We will have to get used to prioritizing business goals over user needs. Empathy is giving way to data-driven decisions. Designers are losing control of the product in favor of growth teams and algorithms.

Mental model (Dall-e)

**Mental model** is people's idea of how things work. The image of how things *actually* work we call a conceptual model. When the conceptual model aligns with the mental model, we say the thing is usable.

Understanding users' [mental models](https://is.muni.cz/www/179593/mentalni_modely.htm) is crucial for designers. They use ethnographic observation methods to gain empathy, and they test early sketches and rough prototypes to verify how well the concept understanding aligns with users' mental models.

Individual users' mental models reflect their personal experience, and therefore often differ significantly. Context often matters a lot, and even the same person can apply a different mental model to the same thing or situation. People are not aware of their mental models; they kick in automatically. And because people gain new experiences constantly, the mental model changes over time. That's why a learning curve is also an important part of the concept.
