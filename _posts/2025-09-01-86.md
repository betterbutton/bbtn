**Display meaningful empty-states to users.** Accompany empty screens with a hint or a call to action.\
\
❌ A white page without text and a button\
✅ A message "Nothing to display yet" and a link to add\
\
👉 Users better understand the application's state\
👉 You motivate further steps\
\
An [empty-state](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/empty-state) is a hidden opportunity. 💡

**Zero-click is coming not only in search results, but on the web itself,** [Martin prophesies](https://www.lupa.cz/clanky/martin-kopta-web-uplne-nezmizi-bude-ale-pro-lidi-necim-co-se-pouzivalo-v-minulosti/) in an interview with David Slížek on Lupa. > “The experience is an emergent property of the exchanges of value.”\
> **— [Erika Hall](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7366227618330759169/)**

**Great usability alone is not enough.** In our glossary, we try to convince you that [sufficient design is good](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/good-enough), but Jon Daiello thinks that [good design is not sufficient](https://www.jondaiello.com/posts/why-good-ux-isnt-enough). Jon compares product design to an engine: the fuel in the form of great usability alone is not enough if other elements are missing. He explains [Peter Morville's honeycomb model](https://semanticstudios.com/user_experience_design/), where value is created by the interplay of six dimensions: usefulness, usability, findability, credibility, accessibility, and desirability. Failure of a single component can sink an otherwise well-designed product.

**The most common excuses for user research and their refutation.** Laura Klein summarizes why companies still skip user research—whether with the excuse that *there's no time*, *it's expensive*, or that *users don't know what they want*. She argues that the absence of research itself leads to wasting time, money, and energy. [Research doesn't have to be costly or time-consuming](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/why-organizations-dont-do-user-research/) and can prevent mistakes that hurt much more. A common hidden reason is fear of criticism or poorly set motivations, where delivering a new feature quickly is more important than creating a product people actually need.

**CSS magic for underlines.** Underlined links are more than just an old-fashioned habit—they are important for accessibility and usability. [CSS allows you to customize the underline](https://adactio.com/journal/22084): [offset it](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/CSS/text-underline-offset) from the text, [thicken or thin the link](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/CSS/text-decoration-thickness), change its [color](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/CSS/text-decoration-color), or [style](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/CSS/text-decoration-style). The result? Links remain clear while aesthetically blending with the design.

**Back to basics.** Joel Dare builds websites using only plain HTML and CSS. According to him, modern websites suffer from overcomplication, are slow, energy-intensive, and hard to maintain. In contrast, static pages are fast, secure, easy to host, and accessible. [Rolf Molich](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/rolf-molich) brought usability from heuristics to international UX standards

[Rolf Molich](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/rolf-molich) (\*1949) is one of the most influential figures in UX. Together with Jakob Nielsen, he [created heuristic evaluation](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/jak-vznikly-nielsenovy-heuristiky), which became a fundamental tool for usability assessment. His comparative [CUE studies](https://www.dialogdesign.dk/cue-studies/) highlighted major differences between teams and changed testing practices. Rolf is also co-author of several books, such as *E‑Commerce User Experience* and co-founder of the [CPUX certification](https://www.uxqb.org/ux-training-and-certification/).

For his work, he has received significant awards and even after forty years, he remains an inspiring critic who reminds us that usability is never final but requires patient testing and user involvement.

These days, you can meet Rolf at the ongoing conference [Mensch und Computer 2025](https://muc2025.mensch-und-computer.de/en/) in Chemnitz, Saxony.

## So we understand each other

[Pain points](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/pain-points) are usually small but crucial issues that disrupt the user experience. They are not always visible in analytics but manifest in user behavior. We uncover them through user research. Improving these points is not a cosmetic fix but a crucial step toward building trust and loyalty.

The [aesthetic-usability effect](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/aesthetic-usability) causes people to perceive visually attractive interfaces as more friendly and easier to use, even if they don't differ functionally from less attractive versions. Visual design is therefore not just decoration—a well-designed look supports orientation, reduces cognitive load, and increases satisfaction. However, the aesthetic layer must not mask deficiencies, as trust can be deceptive.

A [bookmarklet](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/bookmarklet) is a bookmark in a browser that runs JavaScript directly on the currently open page. It requires no installation or permissions, making it an ideal tool for developers and advanced users—for example, for testing, removing styles, or generating QR codes.

[Good enough](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/good-enough) in the context of design and development doesn't mean sloppy but thoughtful. It's about addressing essential matters without unnecessary feature bloat or perfectionism. This approach builds on principles like [KISS](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/kiss) or the Pareto rule. Users primarily want reliability and simplicity. And it is often *good enough* that leads to a faster, cheaper, and ultimately better outcome.
