## Linkodrome

**Is linkbuilding just an illusion?** Jono Alderson questions the concept of backlinks and warns against harmful simplification that turns a link into a tradable commodity. A link is not a commodity—it's a relationship between two contexts that gains meaning only within a network of trust, relevance, and meaning. Concepts like linkbuilding reinforce a flawed model of the web as a metric, not as a living structure of relationships. [It's not about link counts](https://www.jonoalderson.com/conjecture/no-such-thing-as-a-backlink/), but about whether you are part of a trustworthy, thematically connected ecosystem.

**How to fix bad user interface:** Bad UI often arises not from poor design but from ignoring the UI Stack—the five states any screen can have: [ideal, empty, error, partial, and loading](https://www.scotthurff.com/posts/why-your-user-interface-is-awkward-youre-ignoring-the-ui-stack/). Scott Hurff explains why designing just the beautiful full-content state isn't enough; you must think about exceptional and transitional situations. He emphasizes smooth transitions between states, working with user emotions, and collaboration between designers and developers.

**7 animations that improve your UI's impression.** Emil Kowalski offers seven practical tips to enhance the perception of motion in UI without complexity. Response to user actions, proper timing, initial scaling, suitable easing curves, conscious use of transform-origin, and considering interaction frequency—that's [Emil's recipe](https://emilkowal.ski/ui/7-practical-animation-tips). Even small tricks like subtle blurring help visually blend states and feel more natural. The overall impression is often the result of invisible details that accumulate over time.

**Jared Spool: UX is the foundation of digital marketing.** You cannot separate user experience from the product or service—and that applies to digital marketing too. Jared points out that analytics alone cannot reveal what works or doesn't from a UX perspective. [To understand user behavior, you must talk to them.](https://martech.org/why-ux-is-critical-to-digital-marketing/) A campaign's success depends not just on conversions but on the quality of the entire customer experience. Great UX simplifies marketing—it promotes itself through satisfied customers.

**How customizable user interfaces evolved.** The history of themeable UIs dates back to the 1970s with the Xerox Alto GUI. Even then, "themed" elements were created with limited resources—for example, in games like Galaxian or Super Mario Bros. In the 1990s, Windows allowed user theme changes and the web brought CSS. In the 2010s, thanks to Sass, Atomic Design, and design systems like Material Design, the idea of design tokens expanded. Today, Figma Variables and AI tools push the theme further, reacting to the reality of a "Multi-All-The-Things" [world with endless combinations of platforms, brands, and products](https://bradfrost.com/blog/post/the-history-of-themeable-user-interfaces/).

## To understand each other

[Hawthorne effect](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/hawthorne-effect) describes a situation where people change their behavior because they know they are being observed. It emerged from studies in the 1920s and remains relevant today, especially in user experience (UX) research. People in tests often try to "do things right," which skews results.

The abundance of choices in a digital environment often leads to confusion, decision postponement, or complete abandonment of a service. Psychologist Barry Schwartz described this as the [paradox of choice](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/overchoice)—the more options we have, the worse we decide and the less satisfied we are.

[Dot voting](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/dot-voting) is a simple way for a group to quickly identify the most important ideas. Each participant has a limited number of votes to allocate to proposals based on priority. The method encourages involvement from all team members and saves time in decision-making.

[Checklists](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/checklist) allow you not to forget crucial steps under stress, thereby increasing safety and reliability. Their power lies in simplicity and repeatability. In software development, they act as the team's collective memory, transforming experience into concrete action.
