**Three principles for presenting design more clearly and effectively.** Design presentation is not about an exhaustive explanation of every detail, but about the ability to focus on what the audience truly needs to hear. [Tanner Christensen recommends three approaches:](https://uxdesign.cc/three-ways-to-more-effectively-present-your-designs-cef559c3461f) orient the presentation to the listeners' needs, speak about specific design elements, and leave more room for questions and discussion than for your own monologue.

**How to customize Google search results using preferred sources.** Google introduced the Preferred Sources tool, which allows users to mark favorite news sites, like WIRED, and prioritize them in search results. It responds to criticism about the decline in result quality, where AI summaries often overshadow original journalism – sometimes with errors. [The guide shows how to add a site to your preferred sources](https://www.wired.com/story/wired-google-preferred-source/) via the settings page or directly from search results.

**Six buttons that shape the TV UX.** Milan Balać, in his two-part article, explains that the basics of TV interface design haven't changed for decades. The 10-foot experience (viewing from several meters away) determines the size and contrast of elements, while the remote control with a D-pad and six key buttons forms an unshakable interaction formula. [Milan shows](https://www.smashingmagazine.com/2025/08/designing-tv-evergreen-pattern-shapes-tv-experiences/?ref=sidebar) how, from the first remote controls through the NES controller to Apple TV, we keep returning to the same principle. [The second part](https://www.smashingmagazine.com/2025/09/designing-tv-principles-patterns-practical-guidance/) then offers practical tips on layout, typography, colors, focus management, and effective button mapping.

**Design thinking, after years of hype, proves to be a tool that promised more than it could deliver.** Organizations use it as a universal recipe for innovation, instead of addressing specific context, ethical frameworks, or limits. Its greatest value is not the methods, but collaboration and the ability to openly ask fundamental questions across teams. However, it lacks depth and critical thinking, so it often reinforces harmful systems or dead ends. Erika Hall argues that [design has not disappeared – it must be re-anchored in critical thinking and the courage](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7369899028375023616/) not just to solve problems but to question them.

**AI will shop for people sooner than you think.** Agentic commerce, an AI-agent-driven purchasing model, could change how people discover and acquire goods. Virtual assistants already help search and recommend products, but they will soon start completing orders themselves and acting based on user preferences. This will transform the traditional shopping funnel and [weaken the role of both e-shops and brick-and-mortar stores](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/industries/consumer-markets/library/agentic-commerce.html).

**How to work with a grid?** A well-designed grid is the foundation of every responsive interface. [Nick Babich offers four concrete steps](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7369680177263575042/) to improve layout consistency and scalability. The first step is choosing between a fluid grid for interactive content and a fixed grid for text. Next, it's important to thoughtfully set breakpoints based on window width, not container width. Navigation and sidebars should be part of the grid from the start. Finally, it's crucial to align content to the grid and use consistent spacing – this increases the credibility and readability of the design.

**CSS if() vs. media/container queries: when to use what.** The CSS Working Group approved the development of the `if()` function, which [brings declarative conditional values directly into properties](https://blog.logrocket.com/css-if-function-conditional-styling/). Instead of scattered `@media` blocks, container queries, or custom property tricks, logic can be written inline: property: `if(condition: value; else: fallback);`. `if()` supports three queries: `style()`, `media()`, and `supports()`, and improves readability and maintenance at the component level, especially for theming, state styles, and subtle responsiveness.

**How color interpolation works in CSS.** Color interpolation in CSS determines how colors mix between two points. Color spaces – Cartesian (e.g., sRGB) and polar (e.g., LCH, OKLCH) – and the hue change direction (which can be shorter, longer, increasing, or decreasing) play a crucial role. Using functions like color-mix() or linear-gradient(), [we can not only mix but also animate colors](https://css-tricks.com/what-you-need-to-know-about-css-color-interpolation/), creating smooth transitions and visually rich effects.

## Toolbox

The [href.li](https://href.li) service masks the referer, so the target website does not know where the visitor came from. At a time when most browsers and servers support the `Referrer-Policy` header, its benefit is weaker than before. The advantage of href.li is its simplicity; the disadvantage is dependence on a third-party domain and the risk that the service will shut down, like other similar projects. A more stable path is a custom redirector or simply properly configured referrer policy. Still, href.li has been working for 15 years and remains an option where you don't manage the environment.

## To Understand Each Other

[Deep links](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/deep-link) allow the user to open a specific page or screen in an app directly, thus shortening the path to the goal. Deep links increase engagement and conversions and are heavily used in campaigns, notifications, or emails.

[In-depth interviews](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/indepth-interview) are a qualitative method that helps uncover why users behave in certain ways. Unlike analytics or surveys, they provide insight into motivations, frustrations, and emotional barriers. The free structure of the conversation allows discovering topics that would otherwise remain hidden. It's not about statistics, but about the depth in which the greatest value of this method lies.

[The IKEA effect](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/ikea-effect) shows that people value outcomes they helped create more highly. This applies in research and design too. If we involve clients or stakeholders in formulating goals, user conversations, or data synthesis, their sense of ownership grows, as does the chance that the outputs will actually be used.

[SCAMPER](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/scamper) is a simple but effective method that fosters creativity through seven steps. Instead of free brainstorming, it offers a structured framework that helps find new angles on familiar problems.

[A screener](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/screener) is a set of short, targeted questions that allows researchers to quickly distinguish suitable respondents from unsuitable ones. In user research, it acts as an inconspicuous but crucial filter. It saves time and energy for both researchers and respondents and sets the right expectations.

[A pixel](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/pixel) is the basic unit of a digital image. Long ago, it was no longer just a physical point on a display. In modern design, we work with logical units like *CSS pixels* or *density-independent pixels*, which ensure a consistent look across different devices.
