# Href.li masks the referer in links
> The href.li service works simply: a click first goes through its domain, thereby preventing the target website from discovering where the visitor came from.

![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/8a099c63-3137-4b96-abca-c661e2cd8dac_1914x834.png "536x233")

[Href.li](https://href.li) – a game of hide and seek

The [href.li](https://href.li) service was created to remove referer information from links. Clicking a link via href.li ensures the target website does not see where the visitor is coming from. For online forums, communities, or social networks, this is a quick and convenient way to share links without revealing the visitor's origin.

Modern browsers and web servers already natively [support the](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/HTTP/Reference/Headers/Referrer-Policy) `Referrer-Policy` header or the corresponding `` [tag](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/HTML/Reference/Elements/meta/name/referrer). These allow website administrators to precisely determine whether and to what extent referer information is passed on. So, if you have control over your server settings or HTML structure, you have a more robust solution at your disposal than href.li or similar services like [anonym.to](https://anonym.to). Moreover, most environments now use stricter default settings, so the original benefit of href.li has significantly diminished.

However, sometimes you don't have control over the environment. Using href.li is simple; no configuration is needed, just insert the link. But this convenience comes at a cost. You are dependent on a third-party service that could shut down overnight. Similar services—like `dereferer.org` or `nullrefer.com`—already no longer exist, leaving thousands of broken links behind. Add to that the risk of potential manipulation, user distrust when clicking "masked" links, and the overall loss of control over the redirection.

There is also the option to create your own simple redirector on your domain. This ensures control, reliability, and user trust—and eliminates the middleman who might discontinue the service in the future.

Today, we actually see the opposite trend—various services like Chat GPT or Sidebar.io append either Google Analytics tracking parameters or a `ref` parameter to the end of URLs to signal where the user came from. Other services, like Tumblr, on the contrary, prioritize user privacy and mask the links instead.

If you have no way to influence the environment's settings and don't operate your own referer-masking redirector, then the href.li service has been reliably serving this purpose for fifteen years.
