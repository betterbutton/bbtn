# People Ignore Design That Ignores People
> A principle of user-centered design stating that people abandon interfaces that do not respect their goals, abilities, and context, rather than trying to overcome them.

> *You are opening the fifteenth window of the BBtn Advent Calendar for 2025. In this year's advent series, we'll look at popular UX designer sayings. Today's is:* *People ignore design that ignores people.*

The statement *People ignore design that ignores people* is most often attributed to the American designer and author *[Frank Chimero](https://frankchimero.com/)*. It aligns with his long-standing view of design as a human-centered activity, which he further develops in the book *[The Shape of Design](https://shapeofdesignbook.com/)*. Although the sentence often circulates as an aphorism, its meaning firmly stands on the principles of human-centered design.

> “An affection for the audience produces the care necessary to make the work well.”\
> **— [Frank Chimero](https://shapeofdesignbook.com/chapters/02-craft-and-beauty/#:~:text=an%20affection%20for%20the%20audience%20produces%20the%20care%20necessary%20to%20make%20the%20work%20well)**

In practice, this is not a metaphor but an observable phenomenon. On the web, ignoring manifests as [immediate abandonment](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/bounce-rate). Users abandon shopping carts, forms, and entire services the moment an interface starts creating obstacles. This typically involves unnecessary steps, unclear errors, forced registration, or interruption of workflow. Most people don't seek explanations or complain. They simply leave.

The same applies to accessibility. When a website is designed with the assumption of perfect vision, motor skills, or concentration, a portion of the audience is effectively excluded from using it. Empirical audits show that [most public websites contain basic barriers](https://webaim.org/projects/million/). For users who encounter them, the most reasonable reaction is to leave the page.

A special form of ignoring people is manipulative design patterns. Interfaces that nudge users toward decisions against their interests may work in the short term. In the long run, however, they destroy trust and lead to service avoidance.

Design that respects people reduces friction and resistance. Design that ignores them is not usually criticized. It is abandoned.
