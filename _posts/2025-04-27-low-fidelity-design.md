# Rough Draft
> Low-fidelity design – A quick and flexible way to test usability and user flow ideas early in web or app development without getting bogged down in unnecessary details.

Rough draft ([low-fidelity, lo-fi design](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/low-fidelity-prototypes)) helps quickly validate structure, information flow, user journeys, and product usability—all without wasting time on visual details. Rough drafts encourage honest feedback and help uncover problems before you pour a fortune into the project.

In the rough draft phase, simple tools are used: hand-drawn sketches, simple [wireframe models](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/jak-na-papirove-wireframy), or clickable prototypes made of rough shapes. The goal isn't to impress visually, but to verify that users can easily navigate the product and complete their tasks. The focus is on functionality and meaningfulness, not appearance.

Rough drafts directly invite comments and collaboration. It's easier to point out flaws because there's no strong emotional attachment to an unfinished design, and the presentation layer doesn't mask logical shortcomings. This allows for faster iterations.

Designing starts with low-fidelity design. You can gradually move to high-fidelity designs, where you'll tackle details like colors, typography, or micro-interactions, or you can work directly with an existing design system in code. If you skip the rough draft phase, you might end up with a beautifully crafted but poorly usable product. Fixes in later phases are much more expensive.

A rough draft is like the first dress rehearsal before the premiere: quick, flexible, and forgiving. It's where real innovation is born and where you can save time, money, and a lot of frustration.
