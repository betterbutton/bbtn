# User Shortcuts
> Desire path – an intuitive route, a shortcut that users naturally choose, even if it doesn't align with the intended design of user paths in the interface.

In UX design, the term [desire path](https://austinknight.com/writing/desire-paths-and-real-world-ux) describes a situation where people naturally choose a different way to use an interface than the one originally envisioned by the designer. In Czech, this concept is translated as "worn path," "footpath," or "shortcut." These organically formed routes show how users truly want to use a system – and often reveal hidden weaknesses or opportunities for improvement.

Once at Seznam for Mapy.com, we designed a feature for sharing shortened links because we thought the addresses in the address bar were long and complicated. However, during user testing, people consistently ignored this great feature and simply sent the addresses via email.

In another case, instead of browsing the category tree in Sbazar, respondents preferred to search again for *piano bench*, even though this meant missing out on ads from sellers who call a piano bench a *swivel chair for a piano*. Such behavior often indicates that the interface doesn't meet user expectations or needs. And that's exactly where there's room for adjustments that align with how people actually work.

We can monitor these worn digital paths in various ways – for example, using heatmaps, user testing, or analysis of real usage data. When a certain type of behavior repeats, it's a signal. You don't always need to immediately pave the path, but it's always worth understanding it. Sometimes, it's better to adapt the design than to force users to go elsewhere.

The term [desire path](https://medium.com/design-bootcamp/what-desire-paths-teach-us-about-ux-design-3aa6eeb56dff) originally came from urban planning. It describes a situation where people wear a footpath in the grass because it's shorter or more convenient than the official sidewalk. Sometimes the term [cow path](https://ultrathriving.com/articles/pave-the-cows-path/) (deer trail) is also used, referring to paths worn by wildlife in the landscape. In the digital world, it's the same thing. And anyone designing interfaces should pay attention to these shortcuts. They speak the language of experience, not just theory.
