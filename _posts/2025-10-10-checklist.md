# Checklist
> A simple and repeatable system that prevents human error and ensures reliable execution.

When complex procedures fail, it's usually not due to incompetence but forgetfulness. Someone skips an important step, fails to secure a dependency, or doesn't inform the team. And this is precisely what checklists ([checklists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Checklist)) are designed to prevent: small but critical errors that can halt an entire project.

In aviation, medicine, and software, checklists are a proven safeguard. *[Atul Gawande](https://atulgawande.com/)* in his book *[The Checklist Manifesto](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/6667514-the-checklist-manifesto)* describes how a standard preoperative checklist reduced complications by more than a third. It doesn't work by telling experts what to do, but by reminding them what not to forget under stress.

It is said that [the checklist was born on October 30, 1935](https://www.solist.blog/pilots-did-not-invent-checklists-in-1935-when-did-they-appear/), when a *Boeing B‑17* bomber, also known as the *Model 299* prototype or *Flying Fortress*, crashed shortly after takeoff in Dayton, Ohio, USA. Technicians had forgotten to disengage the flight control locks before takeoff. As a result, the first pre-flight checklist was created. However, the word *checklist* or *check‑list* had been in use since the mid-19th century.

Creators and operators of digital products can view checklists as the team's shared memory. A well-maintained checklist before deploying a new version might start with items like *"update the version"* or *"perform basic tests"* and gradually add more specific entries: *"verify analytics measurements"* or *"inform customer support"*. Each one is a reminder of a past mistake that will not be repeated.

Checklists are not bureaucracy but a design tool. They transform experience into reliability and help us accept that memory is imperfect. Thanks to them, we work more calmly and focused.

A good checklist doesn't delay—it provides certainty. It is a silent interface between a person and the complexity of a system, an invisible piece of design that will one day save you.
