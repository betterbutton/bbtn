# Three Lesser-Known UX Laws
> 

Priyadarshi took to Medium to [describe three lesser-known UX laws](https://medium.com/@priyadarshi2815):

1.  Bystander Effect

2.  Zeigarnik Effect (cliffhanger ending)

3.  Pelzman Effect (risk compensation)

The [Bystander Effect](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Efekt_p%C5%99ihl%C3%AD%C5%BEej%C3%ADc%C3%ADho), known from psychology, also influences the digital world and UX design. This phenomenon causes people in a collective to hesitate to act more often, expecting someone else to do it. This leads to unreported errors, toxic communities, and the accumulation of unresolved tasks in team tools. Designers can address this problem with personalized prompts, strengthening social proof, and rewarding active engagement. The key is to design an environment that motivates users toward responsibility and action.

The [Zeigarnik Effect](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/zeigarnik-effect) explains why we remember unfinished tasks better than completed ones. UX design leverages this psychological principle to maintain user attention. Progress bars, gamification with incomplete rewards, abandoned cart reminders, and content teasers effectively encourage users to return to unfinished tasks. Through this approach, designers can motivate user action and increase engagement rates.

The [Pelzman Effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risk_compensation) shows how safety features can paradoxically increase users' risk-taking behavior. The feeling of safety leads to over-reliance on features like "strong password" indicators or car autopilots, which can reduce caution. Users often ignore warnings, trust the system, and take risks. Solutions include transparent communication about limits, education on user responsibility, balancing simplicity and security, adaptive reminders, and abuse testing. Well-designed safety features should protect users without encouraging carelessness.
