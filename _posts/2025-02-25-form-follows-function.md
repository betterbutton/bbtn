# Form Follows Function
> The principle that form follows function is a design and architectural principle that subordinates the appearance and shape of objects to their function or purpose, even at the expense of aesthetics and other values.

In UX design, **function over form** ([function over form](https://medium.com/necdigitalstudio/a-brief-history-of-form-follows-function-6b6484c8c961)) is the governing rule, prioritizing usability over aesthetics. The most beautiful interface is useless if users cannot navigate it. Intuitive navigation, accessibility, and performance optimization are crucial factors.

The concept [form follows function](https://janmichl.com/cz.fff.html) originates from architecture, where it was formulated in the late 19th century by [Louis Sullivan](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Sullivan). He argued that a building's appearance should derive from its purpose, not from decorations. This idea influenced modernist architecture, [Adolf Loos](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Loos), the [Bauhaus](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bauhaus_(v%C3%BDtvarn%C3%A1_%C5%A1kola), and later designers like [Dieter Rams](https://www.vitsoe.com/eu/about/good-design#ten-principles-for-good-design) and early 21st-century Apple products.

Although the principle that form arises from function applies to website and app design, a strict focus on functionality can lead to visually unremarkable, minimalist products that fail to emotionally engage users. Research shows that aesthetically appealing design enhances perceived usability ([aesthetic-usability effect](https://lawsofux.com/aesthetic-usability-effect/)). Minimalism can also be counterproductive—extreme simplification can lead to ambiguity and frustration, for example by hiding controls ([mystery meat navigation](http://www.webpagesthatsuck.com/mysterymeatnavigation.html)).
