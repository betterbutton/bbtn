## From The Designer

![Example of a dialog with an unmarked default choice (left) and a marked default choice (right).](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/8c8d96dc-4fe9-4008-804e-f69c7825b08e_2160x1080.png "1456x728")

**Highlight the default choice.** When giving people a choice from multiple options, always mark the default choice. People should recognize the element that will bring them closer to the goal in multi-step processes, even if they are not currently focused on the text.

❌ Two buttons with the same appearance side by side\
✅ Highlighted button next to a regular button\
✅ Guideline icons next to text labels

👉 Mark as default the element that follows the previous step\
👉 Be fair to users and do not misuse highlighting for manipulation\
👉 Allow people to undo destructive choices

[Don't make users think.](https://www.google.com/search?q=Nenu%C5%A5te+u%C5%BEivatele+p%C5%99em%C3%BD%C5%A1let.)

**The future of design: smaller teams, broader roles, and greater impact.** Luděk Černocký wrote his vision [about the transformation of design in 2025](https://medium.com/design-bootcamp/the-shape-of-design-to-come-3947a6a85309): Smaller, more agile teams, the return of more versatile full-stack designers, and the democratization of design systems through AI and no-code tools. Tool development enables faster iterations and prototyping. The importance of designers with technical skills and strategic thinking is growing. However, soft skills, visual taste, and the ability to understand business impact are becoming equally important. Future designers will need to be not only skilled but also curious and adaptable.

**More trust, fewer timesheets.** Krispin Dolbear elaborated on his blog about [tracking time at the individual task level failing](https://www.krispindolbear.com/p/why-time-tracking-fails-designers) in managing creative teams – it doesn't provide reliable data, undermines trust, and hinders innovation. Design is not a linear process, and pressure to log minutes leads to defensive work rather than quality results. Krispin suggests moving from micromanagement to systems based on trust, outcomes, and feedback. *And what is your hourly rate?*

**Confused by levels A, AA, and AAA in accessibility?** Denis Deacon has prepared a brief overview for you of [what each accessibility level is for](https://www.dennisdeacon.com/web/accessibility/a-note-about-wcag-conformance-levels/). Level A is the minimum you should do for accessibility. Level AA is required by legal standards like the European Accessibility Act. The highest level, AAA, should be your goal if you target people with more severe disabilities with requirements like sign language or reading comprehension.
