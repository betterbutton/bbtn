# You Are Not The User
> The fundamental UX rule that a designer's instinct is unreliable – their experience differs fundamentally from the experience of ordinary users.

> *You are opening the tenth window of the BBtn Advent Calendar for 2025. In this year's advent series, we will look at popular proverbs of UX designers. Today's one is: You Are Not The User.*

![You Are Not The User](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/032d0bee-a4c8-4ac3-b696-5a09c3319e02_1536x1024.webp "478x318")

If someone doesn't understand it, [get them a shirt](https://www.redbubble.com/i/t-shirt/You-are-not-the-user-by-uxuiperu/50625518.NL9AC).

The first rule of user experience is also the one most often forgotten: *you are not the user* – [You Are Not The User](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/false-consensus/). Every designer's belief about what is *intuitive* or *obvious* reflects their expertise – not the reality of the people who use your website.

Most website creators belong to the small percentage of the most experienced computer users. OECD research from 2022 and 2023 showed that almost [a quarter of Czech adults struggle with very low basic skills](https://www.oecd.org/cs/publications/survey-of-adults-skills-2023-country-notes_ab4f6b8c-en/ceska-republika_6e4bb282-cs.html), and among the vocationally educated, it's two out of five. Similar ICDL research from last year also shows that [people with low skill levels are not aware of their incompetence](https://icdl.org/policy-and-publications/perception-reality/). What seems obvious to us is unreachable for many.

Psychology knows this fallacy well. *[The False Consensus Effect](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Efekt_fale%C5%A1n%C3%A9ho_konsenzu)* makes us think that others reason the same way we do. And the curse of knowledge prevents us from imagining what it's like to know nothing. Experiments by psychologist *Pamela Hinds* have proven that [experts consistently overestimate the abilities of novices](https://www.academia.edu/22882800/The_curse_of_expertise_The_effects_of_expertise_and_debiasing_methods_on_prediction_of_novice_performance) – even when warned in advance. Web designers are similarly misled: what once confused users now seems natural to us simply because we know it by heart.

Therefore, human-centered design principles – such as the standard *[ISO 9241­‑210](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_9241)* – have such a strict requirement: real users must be involved in the design. Empathy is not enough, observation is needed. Studies show that [testing with just five people can uncover a large portion of problems](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/5-users-find-85-percent-of-usability-issues) that experts miss.

Designer preferences are not proof. Watch how people work with the web, listen to how they speak, and learn from their mistakes. When someone can't find the button you use routinely, you understand that *good design begins where assumptions end* – with real users.
