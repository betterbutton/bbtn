# Hyphens and Dashes
> Typographers distinguish between hyphens, dashes, and minus signs for the aesthetic effect of text. Practices for their use vary between languages and cultures.

For ordinary mortals, there's no need to differentiate between a hyphen, a minus sign, and a dash, but in typography, visually distinct characters and complex rules have evolved for them. In handwriting, calligraphy, and lettering, punctuation marks are treated relatively freely and often become part of the author's style. However, for printing and later for computer use, both the forms and their usage gradually became standardized.

On a computer keyboard, you'll find the character *[hyphen-minus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyphen-minus)*. In ordinary texts, you can get by with it for writing hyphens, dashes, and minus signs. Computer keyboards inherited it from typewriters. Before text applications began helping authors, editors, and typographers distinguish between individual characters, it was also typographically common to use this very character for all purposes wherever speed and productivity mattered—for example, in newspaper publishing.

**Hyphen** ([hyphen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyphen)) is the character that forms a single expression from multiple parts. For example, the train station in the Prague district of Smíchov is *Praha­‑Smíchov* station, a translation dictionary from English to Czech is an *English­‑Czech* dictionary. Electronic mail is *e‑mail*. The book *75 Tips on How to Better Button* has the ISBN *978­‑80­‑251­‑5091‑7*. Unix time is counted from midnight on *1970‑01‑01.*

A hyphen is also used in *sla­‑bi­‑ko­‑vá­‑ní*, in writing parts of words such as the prefix *před-* or the suffixes *‑ly*, and in compound words (*bude­‑li*). At the end of a line, a hyphen indicates that a divided word continues on the next line.

In some fonts, the hyphen-minus and hyphen differ visually so that the former better serves all roles in ordinary texts. Some typographers, if the font allows, visually distinguish the hyphen from the division mark (they call it *divis*). From manuscript practice, the habit of writing the division mark slightly slanted (⹝) entered typography, but today it would seem outdated.

If you need to break a word at a hyphen at the end of a line, the new line should start with the hyphen, and the division mark should be at the end of the previous line. In practice, this is achieved by using a non-breaking hyphen ([non­‑breaking hyphen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyphen#Nonbreaking_hyphens)) instead of a regular hyphen in compound expressions and inserting a soft hyphen ([soft hyphen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soft_hyphen)) before it, which is normally invisible and only appears when breaking at the end of a line.

You'll also use a non-breaking hyphen when writing expressions like *e‑mail* to prevent the word from being split after a single letter at the end of a line. You can insert a soft hyphen into long words to allow word breaking at the end of a line even in environments that cannot divide words according to spelling rules (for example: *nej­ne­ob­ho­spo­da­řo­vá­va­tel­něj­ší­mi*).

We could continue with stylistic variants of the hyphen, but they are not commonly encountered in texts.

- hyphen­‑minus (-), U+002D
- hyphen (‐), U+2010
- non‑breaking hyphen (‑), U+2011
- soft hyphen (­), U+00AD

**Dash** ([dash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dash)) is used for sentence division instead of a comma or parentheses, for pauses in speech and hesitations (*Be careful, or —*), intervals and ranges (*in the years* *1970 – 2025*), in the sense of "to/until" (*Prague – Brno*), bullet points in lists, introducing direct speech, indicating the author of a quote (*— Jan Novák*), ratios (*performance – price*), pairs (*Voskovec – Werich*), and contrasts (Sparta – Slávia).

Dashes have length variants: The em dash ([em dash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dash#Em_dash)) should be as long as the font size (for a 12-point font, it will be 12 points long) or as the letter *„M“*, but this no longer holds true. The en dash ([en dash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dash#En_dash)) was supposed to be half or the width of the letter *„N“*.

In tables and lists with numbers, a figure dash ([figure dash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dash#Figure_dash)) may be used. Its length corresponds to the width of non-proportional digits. You can use it wherever you write digits vertically and want the rows to align in columns. In the USA, it is used to separate groups of digits in phone numbers (+1‒202‒456‒1111) and is not separated by spaces.

**In Europe**, using an en dash is more common. It is usually separated from the surrounding text by spaces on both sides. The space around a dash in the sense of "from/to" and in relationships should be a non-breaking space and ideally a narrow space ([U+202F](https://www.compart.com/en/unicode/U+202F)). If you cannot use a narrow space, omit it entirely. We use a full space ([U+00A0](https://www.compart.com/en/unicode/U+00A0)) where a dash replaces a comma, parentheses, quotation marks, hesitation, or a bullet point. If you must break a line at a dash, it's better to leave it at the end of the line.

Writing spaces around dashes is largely an aesthetic matter and can also be influenced by the specific font. Customs also differ among various languages and sometimes within a single language depending on culture (American typography differs from British). Using dashes instead of commas in a sentence feels like an Americanism, and Czech, under German influence, prefers parentheses over dashes.

A long dash in Czech text feels affected, and we therefore mostly avoid it. An exception is for introducing direct speech, indicating the author of a quote, or possibly for ranges or where we specifically want to emphasize affectation.

**In English typography**, it very much matters [which style manual you follow](https://jsp.ellpeck.de/#7120fe4e). The Oxford Style Manual, the editors of British newspapers *The Guardian* and *The Observer*, or Apple and Google do not use the long dash. The *Associated Press Stylebook* does not use the short dash. The *Chicago Manual of Style*, Microsoft, and Google do not separate the dash with spaces from the surrounding text, whereas the *Associated Press Stylebook*, *Oxford Style Manual*, and *The Guardian* do separate dashes.

The habits are also divided in English regarding the use of a dash in compound expressions. *Chicago*, *Oxford*, and *Microsoft* adhere to traditional typographic precision and use a dash if one part consists of multiple words—for example, *post–World War II*, but *post­‑war* or *New York–style*, but *Prague­‑based*. On the other hand, *AP*, *The Guardian*, *Apple*, and *Google* lean towards simplicity and readability and always use just a hyphen—*New York­‑style* and *post­‑World War II*.

- en dash (–), U+2013
- em dash (—), U+2014
- figure dash (‒), U+2012

**Minus** (minus) is a mathematical operator. Its length corresponds to the width of the plus sign (+), its stroke has the same thickness as the plus sign, and it is positioned at the same height as the horizontal stroke of the plus sign.

- minus sign (−), U+2012

Besides the hyphen, dashes, and minus sign, the Unicode character set also offers other horizontal lines. Worth mentioning is, for example, the **horizontal bar** ([horizontal bar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dash#Horizontal_bar)), which is used, for instance, to indicate the author of a quote or to introduce direct speech at the beginning of a line. For various stylistic and decorative purposes, such as separating footnotes, you can use a **two-em dash** or **three-em dash**.

- horizontal bar (―), U+2015
- two‑em dash (⸺), U+2E3A
- three‑em dash (⸻), U+2E3B
