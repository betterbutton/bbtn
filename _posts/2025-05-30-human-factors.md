# Human Factors
> The science of adapting systems to human abilities and limitations – the foundation of intuitive, safe, and effective user experience.

Human Factors ([human factors](https://humanfactors101.com/about/what-is-human-factors/)), also known as [ergonomics](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ergonomie), is a field that ensures technology and design truly serve people. It emerged in the 1940s when military engineers realized that many pilot errors were not due to human failure, but poor cockpit design.

Pioneers like *[Alphonse Chapanis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alphonse_Chapanis)* demonstrated that better arrangement of controls could prevent fatal mistakes—laying the groundwork for a field that now spans from digital interfaces to autonomous vehicles.

The fundamental goal of human factors is to reduce the gap between what the user wants to do and what the system allows them to do—the [gulf of execution](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/gulf-of-execution-and-evaluation), as named by *[Don Norman](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/don-norman)*. Equally important is making it understandable what happens after interacting with the system—the [gulf of evaluation](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/gulf-of-execution-and-evaluation). These two gulfs remain key to designing intuitive user interfaces.

Today, this field connects cognitive psychology, physical ergonomics, and [human–computer interaction](https://www.geeksforgeeks.org/introduction-to-human-computer-interface-hci/). Figures like Don Norman, *[Christopher Wickens](https://fabbs.org/about/in-honor-of/christopher-wickens-phd/)*, and *[Ben Shneiderman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ben_Shneiderman)* brought its principles into the digital age and profoundly influenced software, device, and service development.

Human Factors impacts UX design at every layer—from element layout and error message wording to how users form a [mental model](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/mentalni-model) of the system. It improves accessibility by considering diverse user abilities. It guides user testing with a scientific approach instead of assumptions.

In critical systems—from medical devices to autonomous controls—human factors determines both reliability and safety.

Foundational literature like *[Design of Everyday Things](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/85686691-the-design-of-everyday-things)*, *[Designing the User Interface](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/344725.Designing_the_User_Interface)*, and *[Human Factors in Engineering and Design](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/62849.Human_Factors_In_Engineering_and_Design)* remains essential equipment for designers and developers. So do professional journals like *[Human Factors](https://journals.sagepub.com/home/hfs)* and *[Ergonomics](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/terg20)*, where new standards and practices are born.
