# Molly Holzschlag: Good Fairy of the Open Web
> A pioneer of web standards, an advocate for accessibility, and an author who brought humanity to digital design.

![Molly Holzschlag, drawing generated from a photograph](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/568ef389-9cdd-4d54-95a3-45d7d71d1835_1536x1024.webp "588x392")

Molly Holzschlag was named one of the [25 top women of the web](https://web.archive.org/web/19980203173202/http://www.sfgate.com/technology/specials/1998/01/webgrrls.shtml) in 1998.

[Molly Miriam Esther Holzschlag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molly_Holzschlag) (January 27, 1963 — September 5, 2023), known as the *Good Fairy of the Web*, was one of the most influential figures of the early internet. Her emphasis on standards, accessibility, and human-centric design influenced entire generations of web creators.

She wrote or contributed to more than thirty books on web development and helped spread education on semantic HTML, CSS, and progressive enhancement. Her rallying cry, *“Accessibility first!”* became both an ethical and technical cornerstone for the entire industry.

> “Molly paved the way for web standards evangelists to follow, speaking eloquently about ***how*** we should perform our craft but also ***why*** we should follow these emerging best practices.”\
> **— [Rachel Andrew](https://rachelandrew.co.uk/archives/2023/09/07/why-are-we-not-still-using-tables-for-layout/#:~:text=Molly%20paved%20the%20way%20for%20web%20standards%20evangelists%20to%20follow%2C%20speaking%20eloquently%20about%20how%20we%20should%20perform%20our%20craft%20but%20also%20why%20we%20should%20follow%20these%20emerging%20best%20practices.)**

As a leading figure in the *[Web Standards Project](https://www.webstandards.org/)* (WaSP), she pressured browser makers to adhere to common technical standards. This made the web more open and reliable. She collaborated with the *W3C* consortium, browser developers, and was not afraid of sharp confrontation – for example with *Bill Gates* regarding Microsoft's approach to web accessibility.

> “I come from a Jewish background and am quite fascinated by the historic Jewish section of the \[Prague\] city. I also have family history there, as the writer Franz Kafka was a cousin in my maternal lineage. From a very young age I have been fascinated with Prague as a place of intrigue.”\
> **— [Molly Holzschlag](https://zdrojak.cz/clanky/molly-e-holzschlag-evangelist-and-educator/#:~:text=I%C2%A0come%20from%20a%20Jewish%20background%20and%20am%20quite%20fascinated%20by%20the%20historic%20Jewish%20section%20of%20the%20city.%20I%C2%A0also%20have%20family%20history%20there%2C%20as%20the%20writer%20Franz%20Kafka%20was%20a%20cousin%20in%20my%20maternal%20lineage.%20From%20a%20very%20young%20age%20I%C2%A0have%20been%20fascinated%20with%20Prague%20as%20a%20place%20of%20intrigue.)**

Beyond technical knowledge, she was also an exceptional mentor. She spoke at conferences like *SXSW*, *Webstock*, or *AccessU*, taught at universities, and wrote blogs with a human tone and understandable explanations. She believed the web stood not just on technology, but primarily on real people and their stories.

She also spoke publicly about her health and the fatigue that comes with relentless advocacy. Despite these difficulties, she remained active and determined until her final years. Her vision remains: An open and accessible web.
