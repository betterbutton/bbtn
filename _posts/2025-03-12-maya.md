# MAYA: Most Advanced Yet Acceptable
> The MAYA principle helps in UX design to balance innovation with familiarity, making products acceptable and easy to adopt.

The **MAYA** principle ([Most Advanced, Yet Acceptable](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/article/design-for-the-future-but-balance-it-with-your-users-present?srsltid=AfmBOor04nrilSProQhNr8bJs47L4EgaBC63TNpOXH9TrGDOA-hOLUh9) – as advanced as possible, yet acceptable) was introduced by industrial designer [Raymond Loewy](https://www.raymondloewy.com/biography/). According to him, a product should be innovative enough to attract attention, yet familiar enough for people to accept it.

Users often reject sudden changes. If innovation is too radical, it can lead to frustration and resistance. Successful design evolves gradually – it retains elements people are accustomed to and gently introduces novelties. This approach is used by companies like Apple, Google, or Amazon, and locally Seznam, which continuously improve their products without overwhelming users.

In UX, the [MAYA principle](https://substack.com/@itspatmorgan/note/c-59583344) manifests through gradual improvements. For example, artificial intelligence in e-commerce and search engines is introduced alongside traditional features before replacing them. Google's [Material Design](https://material.io/) constantly changes, but its core elements remain familiar.

An example is the evolution of the iPhone – Apple adds new features like Face ID or Dynamic Island without changing the key controls. Amazon and Shopify implement AI personalization gradually to avoid alienating customers. LinkedIn and Facebook change their interfaces slowly so users can adapt.

Loewy's reference shows how to combine modern technology with user-friendliness. By adhering to the MAYA principle, designers can create products that are innovative yet easily understandable, ensuring their success.
