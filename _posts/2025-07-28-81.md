## Everything Is as It Should Be

The Economist writes that [AI is killing the web](https://www.economist.com/business/2025/07/14/ai-is-killing-the-web-can-anything-save-it). SEO specialists are slowly coming to terms with the drop in traffic after the arrival of AI summaries. What's happening? Martin Michálek thinks that [the web is just transforming](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7352971256666677250/).

![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/3f3e392a-5bc7-4b3b-8ee2-29e869f100ac_608x627.jpeg "440x453")

AI is killing the web. Can anything save it? ([The Economist](https://www.economist.com/business/2025/07/14/ai-is-killing-the-web-can-anything-save-it))

Everything has already happened, even a structural change with a loss of traffic. Just remember how publishers raised the alarm when search engines and social networks refused to pay for bringing their users to news website articles and simply stopped displaying news summaries. Or when social networks started retaining traffic on their own websites and apps.

The end of the web was predicted when mobile apps took over and people started spending more time in them than in browsers before. It was similar when Tik Tok drove people crazy with short clips and people simply stopped going to the web and instead just scrolled through videos on the algorithmic feed.

The web, with its openness, ease of publishing and consuming content, and the joy of discovery, always found a way. We believe it will also cope with AI that sucks in content and delivers it to users without releasing them further to web pages. Just maybe there will be fewer ad-heavy websites that primarily get traffic from search engines.

[Everything is as it should be](https://www.justwatch.com/cz/vyhled%C3%A1n%C3%AD?q=vsechno%20je%20jak%20ma%20byt).

## Linkodrome

**Jon Kolko compares the evolution of the software industry to the advent of the assembly line** in Ford's era. From romantic experimentation, it became efficient production, where process is prioritized over creativity. According to him, UX design is heading toward the same fate: routine work with design systems and AI-generated interfaces. [Work for young designers will thus shrink to low-paid routine](https://www.jonkolko.com/writing/notes/henry-ford-and-ux), while real strategy remains in the hands of a select few.

**Most investments go only into development, while the UX research phase remains underfunded.** Designers often get involved late and lack the opportunity to influence project direction from the start. This signals a missed opportunity and a challenge to put greater emphasis on customer research and strategic design right from the beginning of product development. Simon Johnson claims that instead of a [double diamond](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/double-diamond), the [design process diagram is actually shaped like an arrow](https://www.userexperience.co.nz/2017/07/duped-by-the-double-diamond/).

**When design means only output.** Pavel Samsonov thinks designers undermined the profession themselves when they [reduced UX to output production](https://productpicnic.beehiiv.com/p/only-outputs-can-be-automated-9a43). Managers, obsessed with deadline management, don't see the difference between production and design – which is why UX is now easily replaceable. When the goal of the work isn't clear, only delivery speed is measured, not quality. AI then fills the space with *good enough* output. True value lies in conceptual integrity, not in meeting deadlines.

> “When UX doesn’t consider ALL users, shouldn’t it be known as ‘SOME User Experience’ or … SUX?“\
> **— [Billy Gregory](https://x.com/thebillygregory/status/552466012713783297)**

**Texts that readers actually finish.** Richard Dobiáš was annoyed that someone copied his copywriting training, so he is sharing his [system of seven copywriting principles](https://www.o-psani.cz/7-principu-copywritingu-zdarma) completely freely. The system is practical and memorable – it emphasizes simplicity, specificity, benefit, emotion, rhythm, surprise, and storytelling. Richard illustrates each principle with a concrete example and an improvement of the original text.

**JavaScript destroys the web and calls it progress.** The web as we know it has changed from a fast, stable, and accessible medium into a crowded, fragile, and confusing application. A few JavaScript frameworks, a build pipeline, and complex architectures replaced simple HTML pages – often without crucial necessity. [Development today optimizes for developers, not for users](https://www.jonoalderson.com/conjecture/javascript-broke-the-web-and-called-it-progress/), marketing, SEO, or performance. The result? Poor maintenance, delayed changes, frustration, and high costs, writes Jono Alderson. That's a rip-off, right?

> “Engineers vibe code features so fast that designers are cleaning up UX\
> to make them work with the product.”\
> **— [Luke Wroblewski](https://x.com/LukeW/status/1948825881109307902)**

**AI streamlines writing alternative text.** Jared Cunha shows how he [thought through a prompt for image description](https://jaredcunha.com/blog/writing-alt-text-with-ai). It must contain information about context, required length, and what should be emphasized. In our opinion, his outputs are unnecessarily long and are more suitable for `aria-description` than for `alt`.

**Automating marketing analyses with AI.** Marek Prokop describes how he [streamlined the work of a marketing consultant](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/jak-ai-zm%C4%9Bnila-mou-pr%C3%A1ci-marketingov%C3%A9ho-analytika-va%C5%A1i-marek-prokop-czzse/?trackingId=tlDUZfl9TC6%2FqxetEyqw8Q%3D%3D) using an AI agent, Claude Code. AI can independently create analyses, gather data, and generate reports. Marek emphasizes that the system works across technologies and that the key is a thoughtful way of working that allows AI to assist effectively.

> „Traffic, that is visits, ends up as the universal currency of the web.“\
> **— [Martin Michálek](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7352971256666677250/)**

**How to handle SVG.** Josh Comeau [clearly explains](https://www.joshwcomeau.com/svg/friendly-introduction-to-svg/) basic shapes like `<circle>`, `<rect>`, or `<path>` and shows how to style and animate them. SVG offers possibilities like path animation, drawing effects, or interactive elements. The author encourages readers not to be afraid to dive into the world of SVG, which combines aesthetics with technical elegance.

**A UX guide for beginners.** The Nielsen Norman Group has compiled [the ultimate list of articles and videos](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ux-basics-study-guide/) that explain the differences between UX and UI, the importance of simplicity, and why it's important to test with users. The guide covers planning, research, user interface design, and a career in UX. The approach is user-centered and promotes design based on data and empathy for end users.

Vojta Roček in the PPC Brainfood podcast addresses the question: **Does a PPC specialist still make sense in the age of AI?** Vojta says the role of PPC specialists is transforming – AI will gradually take over routine optimizations, but it won't replace people who understand business context, manage incomplete data, set strategic goals, and creative tests. Critical thinking, the ability to ask back, communicate, and reflect on the value of advertising activities will be key in the new era of PPC. People will become consultants for growth, not just campaign mechanics.

## Susan Kare Draws Icons Like She's Embroidering

[Susan Kare](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/susan-kare) was originally a graphic designer without experience in digital design. She created the iconic visual identity of the first Macintosh in the 1980s. With simple, humanly accessible icons like *Happy Mac* or the `⌘` symbol, she made computers accessible to the general public. She draws inspiration from the everyday world. Her work shows that design is primarily about empathy and understandability, not just aesthetics.

## So That We Understand Each Other

The [peak-end rule](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/mockup) was formulated by psychologist Daniel Kahneman. It says that people don't store experiences based on the average, but on the most emotionally intense moment and the ending. Any hassle can be forgiven if the experience ends well – or conversely, a mistake at the end can ruin everything.

[Selective attention](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/selective-attention) is the ability to focus on selected stimuli while ignoring others. People focus only on what helps them fulfill their task. For effective design, it's important to test where users are looking and not assume they intuitively notice everything.

[Bias blind spot](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/bias-blind-spot) is a distortion where we underestimate the influence of our own biases, even though we easily recognize those of others. Designers often subconsciously assume that others have similar experiences as they do and don't account for the needs of different users. The distortion cannot be completely eliminated, but it can be mitigated through team diversity, user testing, and openness to criticism.

[A UI mockup](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/mockup) is a detailed static design that visualizes the appearance of a digital product before development begins. It displays the layout of elements, colors, fonts, and graphics, helping to align expectations between designers, developers, and clients. It facilitates feedback, saves time, and minimizes the risk of costly errors. It serves not only as a visual document but also as a key communication tool in the project process.
