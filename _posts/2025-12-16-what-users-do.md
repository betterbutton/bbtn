# Watch What Users Do, Not What They Say
> A user experience design principle reminding that when making decisions, observing actual behavior is more reliable than verbal statements.

> *You are opening the sixteenth window of the BBtn Advent Calendar for 2025. In this year's advent series, we'll look at popular UX designer proverbs. Today's is: Watch What Users Do, Not What They Say.*

The well-known design rule says: *Watch what users do, not what they say.* It sounds provocative, but it summarizes experience validated by both research and practice. [When words and actions diverge](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/229060046_Telling_more_than_we_can_know_Verbal_reports_on_mental_processes), behavior is usually a more reliable guide. It's not cynicism; it's necessity.

Users mostly speak honestly, yet their descriptions of their own behavior are often inaccurate. People rationalize their actions retroactively, forget details, and [tend to describe themselves as they wish to be](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/attitudinal-behavioral/). In interviews, they claim to read carefully, weigh options, and appreciate complex features. In reality, they scan pages, choose the first acceptable option, and abandon tasks as soon as they encounter significant friction. Designing based on what they say means optimizing for intentions, not outcomes.

> “First Rule of Usability? Don’t Listen to Users”\
> **— [Jakob Nielsen](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/first-rule-of-usability-dont-listen-to-users/)**

Observing behavior tells a different story. Analytics data reveals where people actually drop off. Usability testing shows hesitation, errors, and improvised solutions. Experiments prove which [changes truly improve task completion](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/220451900_Controlled_experiments_on_the_web_Survey_and_practical_guide) or conversions. Behavior is shaped by real constraints—time, cognitive load, devices, and distractions—and thus exposes problems users cannot accurately name.

This doesn't mean ignoring users. Their words are valuable for understanding language, expectations, and perceived difficulties. Complaints often signal a real problem, even if the suggested solution misses the mark. A sensible approach is hierarchical. First, observe behavior and treat it as primary evidence. Only then ask questions to better understand what you've seen. Decide based on what people actually do on the web, not what they say they would do. Listening is important—but observing is more important.
