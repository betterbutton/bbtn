# PWDR: People Who Do Research
> A term for everyone who conducts research, even if their job title doesn't include the word 'researcher'.

PWDR – an acronym for [People Who Do Research](https://greatquestion.co/blog/people-who-do-research-our-favorite-term) – is a term that started being used for anyone who regularly performs research activities but isn't an official researcher. You may have already heard of this term. You might even be one. They are product managers, designers, marketers, developers, data analysts… basically anyone who occasionally sits down with users, tests a prototype, or runs an analysis and then adjusts the product based on it.

The term emerged around 2019 as part of the [ResearchOps](https://researchops.community/) movement, which aimed to unify support for research activities across companies. The label PWDR was introduced by [Kate Towsey](https://katetowsey.com/) as a way to talk about how [research happens outside of specialized research teams](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2On-vJ9puCg). In some companies, every other person does it, they just don't call it that.

It makes sense – researchers aren't inflatable and the demand for data and quick feedback is constantly growing. [Democratizing research](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/democratize-user-research/) isn't just a buzzword but a way to bring research closer to decision-making. Often it's faster and cheaper than waiting for a UX researcher to become available.

But it has its pitfalls. PWDRs without support can easily slide into unethical practices or superficial conclusions. If you don't know what selection bias is or how to recognize data saturation, research can easily turn into hypothesis confirmation. That's why PWDRs need tools, mentoring, and support – so it's not a punk move but a sustainable part of the culture.

If you've ever sent out a survey, done five interviews, or changed your onboarding based on Hotjar, you're a PWDR too. And that's okay. Just sign up for training and go for it with respect for the method.
