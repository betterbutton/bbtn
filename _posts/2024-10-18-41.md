# Linkodrome

**[Minimum Business Increment](https://www.pmi.org/disciplined-agile/da-flex-toc/minimum-business-increments) (MBI) helps with prioritization in agile product development.** MBI represents the smallest amount of work that needs to be completed to deliver specific business value. It includes only the essential changes that have a direct impact on business goals. It is specific in that it requires the resulting product to be immediately usable and to provide clearly defined value with an immediate and tangible benefit to the business.

While before launching a product to market you will likely work with the concept of [Minimum Viable Product](https://www.agilealliance.org/glossary/mvp/) (MVP), which focuses on the customer and their needs, during product development you will rather reach for MBI. The goal of an MVP is to deliver a minimal functional version of the product that enables feedback collection and testing of the core product hypothesis. Conversely, the goal of MBI is to deliver a feature or part of the product that brings direct business value and contributes to [achieving business goals](https://blog.logrocket.com/product-management/mvp-vs-mbi-strategy/).

**Typography underscores brand character.** *"When I started graphic design, there weren't as many quality free fonts, and fonts were pirated,"* says Martin at a gas station cafe on the way from [České Budějovice](https://www.ctvrtkon.cz/). People pirated [software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_free_and_open-source_software_packages), [movies](https://www.justwatch.com/), [music](https://open.spotify.com/), [photobank photos](https://unsplash.com/), [books](https://manybooks.net/)… Today we are accustomed to having everything we need for work available via subscriptions like [Adobe CC](https://www.adobe.com/creativecloud.html) and similar. For fonts, the collection of freely usable fonts [Google Fonts](https://fonts.google.com/) is a great help.

![Butcher shop sign from 1982](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/a49b8bb8-bbac-4855-bf63-4c51c564b33e_1951x1000.jpeg "596x305")

[Dana Kyndrová, p.f. 1983](https://livebid.cz/en/auction/artkunst_24/detail/351)

In this abundance, however, it's hard to navigate, and we slide into convenience. We keep using the same [proven fonts](https://blog.hubspot.com/website/web-safe-html-css-fonts) over and over. On websites, we reach again for [system fonts](https://modernfontstacks.com/) because they load quickly. [Logos are just names written in a familiar font.](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/38#:~:text=v%20roce%202028.-,Poznali%20jste%20Amazon,-%3F) We are thus returning to the aesthetics of the 1980s, when the font selection was very limited, and when you said butcher shop, newsstand, or delicatessen, every graphic designer immediately knew which font the shop sign would be done in.

![Magazine covers for Živel](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/6791fa6f-a6e3-490a-96cf-6ee8d0ae8d1f_2000x1501.png "524x393")

In the 1990s, something began to happen in typography as well. Digital typography suddenly unleashed the floodgates of creativity that wasn't possible with photographic reproduction or woodcut poster fonts. The initiative was taken by the generation of Aleš Najbrt and František Štorm. We devoured [magazines like Živel](https://www.facebook.com/Zivelmagazine/posts/pfbid0curyxJLzH2kn1BxAZsPoD4RfSqJ14Dk7bxdUz5VpRWQF1GP69jTaRvkwwYP1ymXel) and fonts from [experimental type foundries like Emigre](https://eyeondesign.aiga.org/emigre-type-foundry-pretty-much-designed-the-90s/). Film posters, [where graphic designers realized their work before the Velvet Revolution](https://www.ceskatelevize.cz/porady/10176269182-retro/211411000360012/), were replaced by [compact disc booklets](https://www.metal-line.cz/articles/12-obalu-ktere-stigmatizovaly-tuzemsky-metal-471).

Today, web typography has at its disposal a host of tools that until recently were only the domain of print graphic designers. We can control display at the character level, [we can animate texts](https://freefrontend.com/css-text-animations/), we have [alternative characters in OpenType](https://jecas.cz/font-feature-settings), we can [mask character shapes](https://css-tricks.com/how-to-do-knockout-text/), [wrap text around a masked image](https://tympanus.net/codrops/css_reference/shape-outside/), [write on a curve](https://css-tricks.com/snippets/svg/curved-text-along-path/)… Yet a large part of web typography still happens at the level of bitmap reproductions and SVG. Fortunately, one can also find [islands of positive deviation](https://webflow.com/blog/7-typography-trends-in-web-design) and [unbridled creativity](https://www.awwwards.com/websites/typography/).

The renowned type foundry Dalton Maag is also responding to diversity, offering design agencies [free access](https://www.daltonmaag.com/services/fontpass/for-creative-agencies.html) to its 1400 fonts via the FontPass platform. Creatives can use the fonts for their own branding and projects, and even for client commissions, albeit without the option to include the source font for free. Behind this generous offer is, of course, the expectation of selling licenses to clients of creative agencies, but even so, it's an opportunity to step out of the gray of commonly used fonts.

**Learn to recognize quality references**

Supplier references can dazzle at first glance, but don't be fooled just by big names. It often happens that someone mentions collaboration with a significant client without specifying what exactly they did on the project.

**Ask the right questions**

Find out what the supplier specifically created for that client. Was it a key role, or just a minor part? Verify how they proceeded, what challenges they overcame, and what their contribution was. Case studies can reveal a lot.

**Verify references**

Ask for contacts from previous clients and ask about their experience. Find out if the collaboration went smoothly and how the outputs function today.

**Don't be swayed by logos**

Don't be overwhelmed by big names. Instead, request specific samples that will help you assess the supplier's real added value and expertise.
