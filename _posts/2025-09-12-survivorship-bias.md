# Survivorship bias
> A logical fallacy where we draw conclusions from visible successes and overlook invisible failures, which often carry the most valuable information.

[Survivorship bias](https://www.masterclass.com/articles/survivorship-bias), also known as [survivorship bias](https://www.strafelda.cz/klam-prezivsich), distorts our view of reality. We only see those who succeeded—active users, popular features, or successful products. But we fail to notice those who failed, stopped using the service, or left quietly and without a trace. And it is often in these silent departures that the truth is hidden.

A typical example is World War II. Analysts wanted to reinforce the parts of the planes that were most frequently hit—based on those that returned. But statistician Abraham Wald, in his paper [A Method of Estimating Plane Vulnerability Based on Damage of Survivors](https://archive.org/details/DTIC_ADA091073/page/n7/mode/2up) in 1943, pointed out that the parts that showed no damage on the surviving planes should be reinforced. Planes that were hit in those areas never returned. Key data was missing.

The term was popularized by Nassim Nicholas Taleb in the book [Fooled by Randomness](https://archive.org/details/5FooledByRandomnessNassimTaleb) early in the century. In digital products, it's similar to the airplane example. When we look only at successful onboarding flows, we overlook the reasons why many failed. When we track only the most loyal users, we miss the causes of departure for others. We decide based on an incomplete picture.

To avoid the distortion of survivorship, don't focus only on those who succeeded. Track also those who disappeared. Find out why they didn't complete registration or stopped using the app. Their journey can show what doesn't work and what should be changed.

True insight doesn't come only from stories of success. It also comes from the silence after failure.
