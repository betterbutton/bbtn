## Linkodrome

**7 proven tips to improve UI animations** are shared by *Emil Kowalski*. The trick is in quick feedback, subtle scale changes, realistic initial states for animations, and delays on tooltips. He considers easing (the way movement accelerates and decelerates) the most important element and recommends custom curves instead of default CSS ones. He emphasizes that [even subtle differences affect the perception of smoothness and speed](https://emilkowal.ski/ui/7-practical-animation-tips).

**Good design doesn't happen by accident.** *Sarah Gibbons* and *Kelley Gordon* clearly explain how [visual principles influence both the look and the perceived functionality of a website](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/good-visual-design/). Using examples from real websites, they show how grids help in content organization, a typographic system builds hierarchy, a limited color palette supports readability, and high-quality photography adds to brand credibility.

**How to write error messages that help and don't harm.** *Rhiannon Jones* describes how she revamped error messages from chaotic legacy to a consistent and empathetic system. She categorizes errors by origin (systemic × user) and impact (partial × total). Every [error message received its context, tone, and priority](https://medium.com/deliveroo-design/how-to-write-any-error-message-7a3348cce594), depending on how severely it disrupts the user journey.

**A URL is not just an address, but a full-fledged interface.** *Ahmad Alfy* points out the often overlooked ability of URLs to carry application state. Using Google Maps or Figma as examples, he shows how to transfer state via URL. He emphasizes that a URL is not just an address – it's an interface, a tool for sharing, history, and bookmarks. He describes how to correctly store state in various parts of the URL (path, query, fragment) and adds [best practices and anti­‑patterns](https://alfy.blog/2025/10/31/your-url-is-your-state.html). A well-designed URL is a contract between the application and the user – clear, predictable, and robust.

**UX ambassadors and guerrilla tactics.** If you want to change an organization's culture toward a user-oriented approach, good design alone isn't enough – you also have to become a marketer. *Paul Boag* advises on how to use guerrilla internal marketing without a large budget to create a network of UX ambassadors across the company. Key tactics include newsletters, discussion forums, subtle PR events, internal conferences, and physical reminders of UX principles. The goal isn't to promote the UX team, but [to provide others with tools to advocate for a user-centered approach](https://boagworld.com/emails/marketing-ux/).

**UX without governance ends in the mud.** Instead of constantly delivering new features, digital products need care and maintenance – otherwise they turn into mud, i.e., unnecessary digital bloat. *Pavel Samsonov* calls for [the introduction of UX governance](https://productpicnic.beehiiv.com/p/ux-governance-vs-sludge): a discipline at the intersection of architecture and maintenance that cares for the quality and meaningfulness of the overall experience, not just its appearance.

“I like to refer to personas as the bright light under which we do surgery.” — [Alan Cooper](https://uxpod.com/episodes/personas-and-outrageous-software-an-interview-with-alan-cooper.html#:~:text=I%20like%20to%20refer%20to%20personas%20as%20the%20bright%20light%20under%20which%20we%20do%20surgery.)

[Alan Cooper](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/alan-cooper), the creator of *Visual Basic*, became famous primarily as a pioneer of user-oriented design. In the 1990s, he introduced a goal-directed design method using personas – fictional characters derived from real users.

In the book *[The Inmates Are Running the Asylum](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/44098.The_Inmates_Are_Running_the_Asylum)*, he criticized the practice where interfaces are designed by developers instead of designers. Thanks to his work and publications, he fundamentally influenced the approach to design in leading technology companies.

The book *[About Face](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/137333683-about-face)* remains, even after more than ten years, a fundamental UX design handbook. His approach remains relevant even in the era of AI and automation, where the human perspective is more important than ever.

## So We Understand Each Other

[Favicons](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/favicon), originally 16×16 pixel icons from 1999, have become an indispensable element of digital identity. Today they represent an adaptive set of icons in various formats used across devices and platforms. They ensure visual continuity, reinforce credibility, and help with SEO.

[Microformats](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/microformats) are simple HTML patterns that give content a structure understandable by machines without changing its appearance. They originated from the *“humans first, machines second”* approach and allow marking up contacts, events, or posts using common classes like `h‑card` or `h‑entry`.
