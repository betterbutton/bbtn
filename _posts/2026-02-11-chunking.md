# Chunking
> Structuring information and tasks into meaningful wholes that reduce cognitive load while maintaining clarity and orientation.

Chunking ([chunking](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/book/the-glossary-of-human-computer-interaction/chunking)) is not a rule about the number of items on a page. It is a way to reduce unnecessary mental effort. In [human‑centred design](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/human-cetered-design), it means organizing content and workflows into meaningful wholes that align with how people perceive, process, and remember information. When designed well, it increases comprehensibility, efficiency, and accessibility. When applied mechanically, it can instead fragment the experience and increase interaction costs.

The often-cited 7 ± 2 comes from a 1956 paper by *[George A. Miller](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/george-miller)*. However, his main finding does not state that navigation should have seven items. It shows that working [memory holds a limited number of chunks](https://labs.la.utexas.edu/gilden/files/2016/04/MagicNumberSeven-Miller1956.pdf) and that what we perceive as a single whole depends on meaning and prior experience. Newer research, such as the work of *[Nelson Cowan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nelson_Cowan)*, suggests that attention typically focuses on [about four such chunks](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/11830840_The_Magical_Number_4_in_Short-Term_Memory_A_Reconsideration_of_Mental_Storage_Capacity). Capacity is therefore not a universal number but a variable property of context.

For digital systems, the critical distinction is between the inherent complexity of a task and the burden created by its presentation. Cognitive load theory shows that the task's intrinsic complexity cannot be eliminated. However, unnecessary effort caused by poor structure can be reduced. Clear headings, logically grouped form fields, a process broken into steps, and progressive disclosure of details help focus on one meaningful whole at a time without losing orientation.

Visual grouping supports this process. Principles of proximity and similarity cause us to perceive elements as related. This facilitates quick scanning, which is more common on the web than continuous reading. However, chunking must be not only visual but also semantic. Structured headings, page regions, and proper markup ensure the grouping remains readable for assistive technologies as well.

Chunking operates on multiple levels. From formatting verification codes to designing entire information architecture. It is not about minimalism or a magic number. It is about respecting how people actually think and how they construct meaningful wholes.
