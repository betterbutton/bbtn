# Are you familiar with web tricks based on cognitive bias?
> 

Cognitive bias ([cognitive bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_bias)) are systematic errors in thinking, judgment, or decision-making. They arise from the limitations and simplifications of the processes by which we acquire and process information or how we use our knowledge.

Cognitive bias is used in the design of user interfaces with [dark patterns](https://cs.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_pattern). Designers thus influence user decisions and manipulate their behavior. As an unethical strategy, it is often used in marketing, e-commerce, or applications with the aim of increasing engagement, conversions, or sales.

Do you know any specific ways cognitive bias is used on the websites you visit?

**Anchoring Bias ([Anchoring Bias](https://thedecisionlab.com/biases/anchoring-bias)):** People tend to rely on the first piece of information they receive (the "anchor") when making decisions.

An e-shop displays a fictitious original price next to a reduced price, thereby increasing the perceived value of the offer. The user then perceives the discount as more advantageous.

**Foot-in-the-door Bias ([Foot-in-the-door Bias](https://www.psychologistworld.com/behavior/compliance/strategies/foot-in-door-technique)):** People tend to continue agreeing with further requests after agreeing with an initial, smaller request.

Interface design can first ask the user for simple, small actions, such as registering with an email. Once the user agrees, they are more likely to later perform more complex tasks, such as making a purchase or subscribing.

**Scarcity Bias ([Scarcity Bias](https://thedecisionlab.com/reference-guide/anthropology/scarcity)):** People tend to consider something more valuable if it is rare or limited.

Elements such as countdown timers, information about limited stock, or alerts about how many other people are currently considering the same product increase urgency and tempt the user to make a decision under pressure.

**Social Proof Bias ([Social Proof Bias](https://thedecisionlab.com/reference-guide/psychology/social-proof)):** Users tend to do what they see others doing, especially in situations of uncertainty.

Displaying reviews, ratings, or the number of people who have already purchased or used a product increases trust and can convince other users to behave similarly.

**Confirmation Bias ([Confirmation Bias](https://thedecisionlab.com/biases/confirmation-bias)):** People have a tendency to search for and prefer information that confirms their existing beliefs.

Personalized content that reflects the user's preferences based on their previous behavior can strengthen user engagement and keep them on the page longer.

**Contrast Effect ([Contrast Effect](https://effectiviology.com/contrast-effect/)):** The perception of value or attractiveness is influenced by comparison with other elements.

Displaying a more expensive option next to a cheaper one (e.g., different subscription tiers) can cause the cheaper option to appear more advantageous, thereby prompting the user to make a purchase.

**Commitment Bias ([Commitment Bias](https://thedecisionlab.com/biases/commitment-bias)):** People tend to persist in commitments they have made, even when it is not in their best interest.

Offering a free trial period with an subsequent automatic switch to a paid version often exploits this bias, as users tend to continue with the paid subscription once they have committed.

**Fear of Missing Out (FOMO) ([Fear of Missing Out](https://medium.com/convertize/cognitive-bias-of-the-week-fomo-8cdef7826b95)):** The fear of missing an opportunity or something important leads us to impulsive actions.

Using messages like "Last chance!" or "Only 2 hours left!" creates a sense of urgency and motivates the user to take action.

**Framing Effect ([Framing Effect](https://thedecisionlab.com/biases/framing-effect)):** Decision-making is influenced by how information is presented.

Offers highlight positive aspects ("Save 20%") or evoke a sense of loss ("You'll miss out on 20% if you don't buy now"). This framing influences how the user perceives the offer.

**Decoy Effect ([Decoy Effect](https://thedecisionlab.com/biases/decoy-effect)):** This involves introducing a third, less attractive option aimed at increasing the attractiveness of one of the original two options.

E-shops offer three tiers of products (e.g., small, medium, and large packages), where the medium package is designed to be the least advantageous, increasing the chance that the user will choose the more expensive or the cheapest variant.
