# Why do we skip the user research phase?
> 

Elenee from Bootcamp wonders, [why some projects skip user research](https://bootcamp.uxdesign.cc/tune-why-do-we-skip-the-research-phase-at-all-090de0fe1cb7). Imagine a small web development firm that gets a new project. The client provides detailed technical documents with required features and the deadline is tight. The project manager calls the team together and the UX designer, who is more of a generalist, immediately starts creating designs in Figma. The client wants to see what the result will look like as soon as possible. And so the developers start coding. Everything seems smooth, but one critical phase was skipped – user research.

Unfortunately, this scenario is not made up. This is how it often goes in practice. Web designers skip research due to limited budgets and short timelines. The result are products that don't meet the real needs of users, which increases costs and reduces satisfaction.

User research is key. It helps teams prioritize features, create user-oriented designs, and build products that resonate with the target audience. It brings informed decisions, better user experience, and reduces development costs.

What to do about it?

1.  **Stakeholder education:** Explain the long-term benefits of research, such as a more successful product and lower development costs.

2.  **Lean UX methods:** Use quick techniques like guerrilla testing and online surveys.

3.  **Leveraging existing data:** Mine data from previous projects or publicly available sources.

4.  **Close collaboration with clients:** Propose including research in the project plan and involve clients in the research process.

5.  **Iterative development:** Break the project into phases, start with a minimum viable product (MVP), and gradually improve it based on feedback.

Remember that skipping research can lead to product failure. Even with limited resources, you can conduct effective research, which leads to better and more successful products.
