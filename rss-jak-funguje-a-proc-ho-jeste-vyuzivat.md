# RSS: How It Works and Why You Should Still Use It
> 

RSS, or [Really Simple Syndication](https://www.rssboard.org/rss-specification), is a format that allows automatic tracking of changes on websites via [RSS readers](https://www.wired.com/story/best-rss-feed-readers/). Although RSS popularity has declined with the rise of social networks and personalized algorithms, it remains the ideal tool for creating your own information channel. [RSS is an alternative](https://www.makeuseof.com/what-is-rss-and-what-can-you-do-with-it/) to newsletters for those who don't want to leave information consumption to algorithms.

> “RSS puts you in control from the start and assures that the content delivered to you is what you want to see.”

Readers like [Feedly](https://feedly.com/) or [Inoreader](https://www.inoreader.com/) gather updates from favorite websites in one place, saving time and offering privacy without sharing personal data. If you like to keep things under control, you can [install an RSS reader](https://www.pikapods.com/apps#blog) or use a [community-hosted](https://freshrss.org/cloud-providers.html) variant. Adding RSS feeds is simple—just copy the URL and paste it into the reader. For many users, [RSS remains a reliable way to stay informed](https://www.makeuseof.com/what-is-rss-and-what-can-you-do-with-it/).

Some, like [Jeremy Keith](https://adactio.com/about/) (Adactio), are returning to RSS feeds: *„When I open my RSS reader to catch up on the feeds I’m subscribed to, it doesn’t feel like opening my email client. It feels more like opening a book.“* [Adactio praises](https://adactio.com/journal/21505) that, in addition to controlling information sources, no algorithm interferes with the selection and ordering of news.
