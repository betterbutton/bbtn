# Chrome browser turns 16
> 

Sixteen is a round number in binary (`0b10000`), and precisely that many years ago, on Tuesday, September 2, 2008, the first public beta version of [Google Chrome](https://www.google.com/chrome/) was released. The browser from Google actually got its true first release a few months later on December 11, but we're nerds, so we're celebrating now.

Less than twenty years ago, then Google board member [John Doerr denied](https://web.archive.org/web/20050113130759/https://networks.silicon.com/webwatch/0,39024667,39124736,00.htm) that Google was preparing its own browser, but immediately added with a laugh that, although he was on the company's leadership, it didn't mean he knew everything Google was doing. Google's stock was only just preparing for the stock market back then.

The original Google Chrome was built on the [Webkit](https://webkit.org/) engine, which is now developed by the open-source community but remains under strong Apple influence. With the release of the Chromium beta, the [Chromium](https://www.chromium.org/chromium-projects/) core split off from Webkit, which is also celebrating its sixteenth anniversary today. The complete separation of Chromium from Webkit happened in 2023 when the Webkit fork created the [Blink rendering engine](https://www.chromium.org/blink/). And while we're on anniversaries, the Chromium JavaScript engine [V8](https://v8.dev/) is also turning sixteen today. Besides the browser, it gave rise to the server-side technology [node.js](https://nodejs.org/en), which allows front-end developers to become [full-stack developers](https://www.coursera.org/articles/full-stack-developer).

Google didn't have an easy position from the start. The majority of users browsed the web with [Microsoft Internet Explorer](https://www.vzhurudolu.cz/prirucka/msie) and technically savvy individuals primarily preferred [Firefox](https://www.mozilla.org/cs/firefox/new/). Google supported its browser in every way possible, and in January 2011, the Czech Republic, and especially Prague, was hit by a [massive advertising campaign for Chrome](https://www.lupa.cz/clanky/za-tajemstvim-reklamni-kampane-na-google-chrome/).

Things change, however, so this summer, [Chrome had a 65% market share](https://gs.statcounter.com/browser-market-share) among browsers, leaving second-place Safari (18%) and third-place Microsoft Edge (5%) far behind. [Internet Explorer](https://www.vzhurudolu.cz/prirucka/msie) is thankfully already a thing of the past, and Firefox struggles between two and three percent, as does the once popular [Opera](https://www.opera.com/). Meanwhile, both Edge and Opera are built on Chromium, which is also favored by other alternative browsers like [Vivaldi](https://vivaldi.com/cs/), [Arc](https://arc.net/), [Brave](https://brave.com/), and [many others](https://alternativeto.net/category/browsers/chromium-based/).

So today, Google Chrome effectively defines the [web standard](https://www.w3.org/) much like MSIE did before. Because of this, Google is coming under scrutiny from market regulators—whether it's about supporting [technologies that enable user tracking](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/privacy-sandbox-results) or [monopolistic tendencies](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/google-nelegalni-monopol-usa).

With influence, however, comes responsibility, and so Google Chrome bears on its shoulders not only shaping the future of the World Wide Web but also the commitment to ensure the web remains open, secure, and accessible to all. How it will fulfill this role, we can perhaps say again in `0b10000` years.
