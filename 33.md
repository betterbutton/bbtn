# Linkodrome

**Tools don't make a designer.** The democratization of design is moving a step further. [Figma](https://www.figma.com/) put a design tool into the hands of people from many other professions, from product people to developers and testers. [Canva](https://www.canva.com/) went further and made design accessible to everyone, including children.

 [o Canvě wrote](https://www.samsobemarketerem.cz/p/nezaspi-dobu-a-delej-grafiku-chytre): *"And collaboration with a graphic designer can easily look like this: you prepare a rough draft, share it with them, and they fine-tune it to perfection. That's called teamwork."*

It sounds tempting, right? You'll save a lot of valuable time, simplify communication, speed up feedback, stop information from getting lost along the way, and as a client, you'll be more involved in the creation. On the other hand, however, you need the designer to work with the same tool, because otherwise it's likely they won't be able to use your materials and will have to recreate them.

The fine-tuning will also involve removing various errors and inappropriate practices that a craftsman wouldn't make. Communication can get stuck when you discover that the designer has replaced with their own idea the part you put the most effort into and are proud of. And then the arguing about who is to blame if hidden flaws appear in the final result. Should the designer have uncovered them?

We are experiencing in digital design something that client-designer collaboration has already gone through in fields like print or corporate identity. In those fields, the democratization of design led to designers starting to prepare templates, building blocks, and design kits instead of final outputs, from which anyone could then prepare the final outputs.

Even in digital design, design systems are already gaining ground. They are intended primarily for other professionals. It's time to ask designers for a component library for Canva or no-code tools like Webflow.

And in regular collaboration? It's rather counterproductive to do the designer's work and then just ask them for fine-tuning. Better to attach a rough sketch and a moodboard to the brief. A picture is worth a thousand words. And you can certainly prepare them in Canva or [Microsoft Designer](https://designer.microsoft.com/).

**As best as possible, but not better,** are you familiar with the [Four Agreements](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fVjqnDeyBRg)? Marek Prokop [recently wrote](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7229048745726750720/): *"I've been doing SEO for twenty-five years, and even after that quarter-century, I can't guarantee that everything I plan will succeed."* SEO is already like that. Full of unknowns, assumptions, a rapidly changing environment, and many players. In return, he adds that he always strives to, alongside uncertain optimization, improve the website's quality. At least something always works out.

As designers, we occasionally get asked how we know our designs will work. Not for nothing are the two most common designer answers: it depends and trust me. In design, an evidence-based approach is gaining ground – [evidence-based design](https://medium.com/we-are-systematic/evidence-based-design-6ada94c63e7a). Research, testing, feedback, and iterative work are our safeguards for reducing the risk of failure. Otherwise, it's just [a design game](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7226546121278431232/), and a designer who engages in it is [sawing off the branch](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7230174099921141760/) they're sitting on.

At the same time, however, we must admit that every software is broken. That's its nature. Every program has at least one unnecessary condition and every program contains at least one bug. Fixing bugs creates new ones. There's always work to be done.

Illustration: [Kateřina Čápová](https://www.katerinacapova.cz/) ([75 tips on how to ask for a web](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/kniha))

**Always add only one small functional part.**

Before deploying a new feature, agree with the supplier on carefully measuring its benefit, either through an A/B test, or evaluate the website's performance before and after deployment. If the change is for the worse, be hard on yourself and don't be afraid to discard the feature again.

If you're modifying your digital project, add new parts in a way that allows you to assess what caused the change. When you add too many things at once, you'll have a harder time figuring out what works and what doesn't.
