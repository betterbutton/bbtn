# Linkodrome

**Does Figma have an alternative?** Recently, [loud opinions](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/figma-se-odcizuje-designerum) have appeared that the development of [Figma](https://figma.com/) is going in the wrong direction. But do we have a choice? No, right now we don't mean Figma clones like [Penpot](https://penpot.app/), [Pixso](https://pixso.net/), [Creatie](https://creatie.ai/), [Siter](https://siter.io/), or [Motiff](https://motiff.com/). They are indeed a fraction of Figma's price, but often have a more limited feature set, slower development, and if you really want to escape Figma – they actually approach design in the same way as Figma.

Now we mean tools for professional web or app design that approach design differently from Figma. Before we all switched to Figma with the first lockdown, many of us designed in [Adobe XD](https://helpx.adobe.com/support/xd.html). Adobe later halted development, and it doesn't seem like development will continue after the failed acquisition of Figma.

macOS users worked with [Sketch](https://www.sketch.com/). It's still only for macOS, and we haven't had the courage to try the Windows clone [Lunacy](https://icons8.com/lunacy). The subscription price rose to $120 per year, but it only applies to designers. Sketch learned real-time collaboration, but only within the app. The prototype runs in a browser, and you can also comment there.

It still has its supporters: [Axure RP](https://www.axure.com/). It's good for interactive low-fidelity prototypes. So in the end, you'll still need to prepare the assets for production in another tool. But you might avoid the need to rework high-fidelity designs.

Fans of Axure have permanently sparred with designers in [Justinmind](https://www.justinmind.com/). It still exists, and today it's not entirely clear whether we should make better prototypes in it from Figma assets, or completely replace Figma.

Before Figma flooded everyone's pond, there was speculation that Sketch would benefit from acquiring Invision with its handoff tools. Then it wasn't clear for a while who was acquiring whom, until Invision created its own design tool and Sketch solved the handoffs. Today, Invision has the interactive whiteboard [Freehand](https://www.freehandapp.com/) and the prototyping tool [Prototype](https://www.invisionapp.com/product/prototype).

[Marvel](https://marvelapp.com/) was in a similar position to Invision. We still love it for the ability to snap a hand-drawn sketch and connect it into a prototype directly in the mobile app. It just annoyed us a bit when we told people with Android about it. Their app disappeared from Google Play, and they might not even have noticed.

As designers started complaining about Figma, they simultaneously began praising [Canva](https://canva.com/). In truth, however, we still don't know anyone who professionally designs in Canva anything other than personal and hobby websites. But that doesn't mean such people don't exist!

Otherwise, we report that during our service over the past five years, nothing special has happened in the field of design tools suitable for high-fidelity design and handoff to developers. Maybe it's still ahead of us. For now, however, we remain with Figma.

Czech Sign Language

Website accessibility is trending. It's thanks to the [European Accessibility Act](https://www.zakonyprolidi.cz/cs/2023-424), which will be effective from June 26 next year. The act follows the WCAG 2.2 standards. According to it, website content should be available through various sensory and input methods. This also includes the needs of users of Czech Sign Language. At the INSPO conference on Saturday, we [presented the findings of a survey](https://docs.google.com/presentation/d/1pkUESiaA5NKKnciwcfkZcSimJCIp8uDBkQx4Enak-V4/) we conducted for [Deaf Friendly](https://www.deaffriendly.cz/).

Users of Czech Sign Language have specific requirements. They exceed the standard A or AA levels of the [WCAG](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/) standard, which the law explicitly mandates. They aim for the highest AAA level. Although achieving the highest level of accessibility is not mandatory, it can fundamentally improve the user experience.

Because Czech Sign Language is a separate language version of the website, it should be genuinely addressed on the site. Czech Sign Language should be a separate language version of the website. The link to it should be in the top right corner in the same place and in the same form as we switch to English and other languages. To label the language version, it's important to use the Czech Sign Language character – just as we would label a Chinese version with a Chinese character. Do not use a wheelchair symbol or a crossed-out ear. It's also inappropriate to use the symbol for American Sign Language, which someone occasionally mistakenly downloads from icon libraries. For the sign language version, it's also not appropriate to use the [icon of Da Vinci's Vitruvian Man](https://www.designedtoinspire.com/blog/142365-searching-for-an-accessibility-symbol/).

Czech is often a foreign language for the deaf. This affects the way they read and perceive content. Users of Czech Sign Language have difficulty scanning text and need contextual captions and visual navigation. Visualizing concepts and conveying messages through images or animations makes understanding easier for them. Conversely, long texts confuse them. The most effective medium is very short videos. Ideally up to two minutes long. For better orientation, it's advisable to supplement the video in the background with branding and possibly a display of things and situations being discussed.

To ensure accessibility, it's necessary to break content into smaller units and remove unnecessary visual decorations, such as hero sections. Design and navigation should be as simple as possible, with content organized top-to-bottom in a single column.

Users of sign language also look for current information in their language version. If you create pages in Czech Sign Language only once and don't update them with news, it's advisable to point this out and possibly display the date of the last update.

Developing a section in Czech Sign Language isn't just about meeting standards. It's a step towards greater inclusion and removing barriers, which benefits the deaf and society as a whole.

# Tip From Our Book

**Mind the Correct Order of Work**

Project work doesn't always have to proceed strictly linearly, but a certain order of steps is crucial.

![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/b9df5e6d-a865-4d74-8f15-53f416472de6_4589x3442.png "652x489")

Illustration: [Kateřina Čápová](https://www.katerinacapova.cz/) ([75 Tips on How to Ask for a Website](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/kniha))

Sometimes a designer gets carried away and devotes too much energy to creating detailed outputs, while the main problem remains unsolved. Before you dive into specific solutions, make sure together that you have clarified all key concepts and well-defined problems you'll be working on.

This problem is common especially among less experienced designers, who tend to jump into details or focus on the visual fidelity of designs. It often stems from uncertainty, which they try to compensate for with creative distraction. The result, however, can be aesthetically successful designs that lack a solid foundation from earlier work phases.

Remember that the most expensive mistakes usually arise at the beginning – where they aren't immediately visible. And the longer it takes to uncover them, the harder they are to fix.
