# Linkodrome

**Apple finally didn't kill PWAs in iOS 17.4.** It's a marginal platform, but still, this [news is pleasing](https://9to5mac.com/2024/03/01/apple-home-screen-web-apps-ios-17-eu/). PWA is Progressive Web App, an application built on web technology that runs in the browser core. It saves the day where it doesn't pay off to develop for a specific platform.

**Google admitted it "tracks" users in Chrome's incognito mode.** Google failed in court with a motion to dismiss a lawsuit that [sought five billion dollars](https://www.engadget.com/the-morning-after-google-will-settle-5-billion-lawsuit-over-tracking-incognito-chrome-users-121651324.html?guccounter=1&guce_referrer=aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuZ29vZ2xlLmNvbS8&guce_referrer_sig=AQAAAEgzWQD9rXL_L1SFCupevvwiTJByE07HZzmIPv7RjhbwqjQ040KMSeH2jLFl0UHkrVuhg2nzFzWLBYV7pAsifJeOzD0gErFzQWcwUHgHFGP7rrmlEippdNANE_VhTgPEvxc3DtBbwtqfnXYiHRQbyogIC_IEfXL7rX91SbE0n9CK) in damages. Now, when you open incognito mode [in Chrome, you'll see a warning](https://eu.usatoday.com/story/tech/2024/01/17/google-chrome-incognito-mode-data-disclaimer/72260401007/#:~:text=The%20tech%20company%20has%20agreed,privacy%20browsers%20to%20collect%20information&text=Google%20Chrome%20has%20reportedly%20updated,a%20%245%20billion%20privacy%20lawsuit.) about what data Google records.

**Facebook is persuading advertisers to switch to IPv6.** It's not just out of enthusiasm for new technology, but mainly so it can better [track users](https://twitter.com/petrsoukup/status/1770012412827017527).

**Whiteboards have agreed on a common [format for canvases](https://x.com/orionreedone/status/1768292314802201087).** The Open Canvas Working Group currently operates without the biggest players like Miro, Mural, MS Whiteboard, or FigJam, but even so, the [canvas protocol](https://www.canvasprotocol.org/) looks promising. There are [tons of tools for infinite canvases](https://infinitecanvas.tools/gallery/), and a compatible format would be useful. Hopefully, the open canvas won't meet the same fate as the [attempt at Open Design](https://github.com/opendesigndev).

**I just arbitrarily label some images as UI and others as UX.** Eight years ago, [Sebastiaan de With shot us all down](https://twitter.com/sdw/status/709853249407361024), and he still laughs that his images appear in seriously meant presentations.

**Figma is unraveling design culture.** [Stéphanie Walter accuses the makers of the popular design tool](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/stephaniewalterpro_figmas-only-interest-is-revenue-and-it-activity-7167866045062463490-m45L) that in the pursuit of profit, they keep adding new features that grab attention, like FigJam or AI, and completely ignore fundamental things, like prototyping forms.

**When you say Design System, most people think of Figma.** A design system, however, should include not just a graphic library but also code. Figma owns the nice domain designsystems.com and [decided to fill it with useful content](https://www.designsystems.com/).

**Have you gotten the hang of autolayout?** Yasir Eryilmaz played around with it a bit and [it's really fun](https://twitter.com/yasirbugra/status/1762986388511678705). And since there's no catch, he also released the [source materials](https://www.figma.com/community/file/1344816368206098709).

**Design without a process means nothing.** At the agency 2FRESH, where Martin works, they claim that (their) process is a guarantee of consistently high-quality deliveries. [Pavel Samsonov goes even further](https://uxdesign.cc/design-without-process-or-the-form-factor-trap-scaling-the-design-ladder-part-2-37c3a6ffc928), saying that the true value of design doesn't lie in producing visual artifacts, but in the process that uses these artifacts to document and test design decisions. Design creates "negative value" if the process is simplified to merely creating visuals without deeper meaning or utility.

# Life with Technologies

In March, 25 years passed since the invention of the RSS feed format. [RSS is the predecessor of notifications.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RSS) Whenever new content appeared on a website, a link to it was added to the RSS feed. Content aggregators and [news readers](https://alternativeto.net/software/netnewswire/) regularly downloaded RSS feeds, and people thus learned about new content.

RSS evolved a bit chaotically, and so [Atom was soon created](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom_(web_standard), a similar format introduced [under the IETF umbrella](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc4287.html). And because periodically polling a bunch of subscribers to see if anything new was added to the feed isn't exactly efficient, a [push protocol for the web](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WebSub) was also created, now called WebSub. This notification protocol is also used by social networks like Mastodon or Diaspora, and its development is overseen by the [World Wide Web Consortium](https://www.w3.org/TR/websub/).

While RSS for distributing news on the web gradually [fell into near oblivion](https://openrss.org/blog/how-google-helped-destroy-adoption-of-rss-feeds), this [format for distributing podcasts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Podcast) came back to life. With the rise of podcasts, the renaissance of newsletters, and the move away from social networks, RSS feeds also returned to popularity a few years ago. Even before the COVID pandemic, Wired published an article [It's Time to Bring Back RSS](https://www.wired.com/story/rss-readers-feedly-inoreader-old-reader/). You can also find an RSS feed [on our website](https://reknisioweb.substack.com/feed) as an alternative distribution method to the newsletter.
