# Font Accessibility Guide
> 

Choosing the right font is crucial for improving text readability and accessibility, especially for people with poor vision, dyslexia, or low literacy. Typographic elements form the foundation for accessibility, but they must be combined with other best practices.

Gareth Ford Williams distilled the following [font accessibility recommendations](https://medium.com/the-readability-group/a-guide-to-understanding-what-makes-a-typeface-accessible-and-how-to-make-informed-decisions-9e5c0b9040a0):

- Use a font style that is suitable for your audience.

- Minimize the occurrence of letters that can be confused with others (Il1, O0).

- Minimize the occurrence of mirrored letter shapes (pq, bd).

- Letters should be easily distinguishable from one another.

- Humanist fonts ([dynamic renaissance antiqua](http://old.typo.cz/databaze/pravidla-a-nazvoslovi/klasifikace-pisem/ceskoslovenska-klasifikace/#:~:text=1.0.0.0%20DYNAMICK%C3%81%20ANTIKVA)) are generally more readable at smaller sizes than grotesque fonts.

- Ensure the font has sufficient spacing between letters.

- There should be a visible difference in height between uppercase letters and [ascenders](https://typomil.com/anatomie/).

- Test the suitability of any font/typeface in context.

Some fonts, like BBC Reith, have been developed with neuroscientific insights and user data. Key recommendations include using appropriate fonts, avoiding similar letter shapes, and ensuring sufficient spacing between letters. Other important aspects are letter distinguishability and testing the font in context.
